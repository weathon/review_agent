# Harsh Critic Review
## Section-by-Section Critical Review

### Title & Abstract
The title and abstract clearly signal a conceptual/philosophical intervention targeting the "black box" metaphor in deep learning. The central claim—that the black-box characterization rests on the false assumption that causal continuity necessitates individuated intermediary correlates—is stated upfront. However, the abstract overreaches by declaring this assumption "false" without scoping the claim to specific architectures, training regimes, or the mathematical definition of a "feature." For an ICLR submission, this framing risks being read as a categorical dismissal of mechanistic interpretability and representation learning unless carefully bounded. The abstract also claims explanations are "complete and without remainder" once the holistic system state is accounted for, which conflates *causal sufficiency* with *explanatory completeness* in a way that needs rigorous defense, particularly given ICLR's expectation that conceptual claims be grounded in the mathematical reality of neural networks.

### 1. Neural Network Architectures and Comprehensibility (Sections 1.1–1.3)
**1.1 Input and Output:** The section correctly frames the difficulty of tracing input features to output features in nonlinear systems. However, it treats "feature" monolithically and leans heavily on the distinction between natural vs. complex opacity (citing Zerilli, Chesterman) without engaging with how modern ML characterizes internal representations. Neural networks do not merely suffer from "complex opacity"; they possess *distributed*, *superposed*, and often *polysemantic* representations. The section implies that if we cannot cleanly map a single input feature to a single output feature via an individuated internal correlate, then no such correlate exists. This overlooks extensive work showing that correlates *do* exist, but they are linear combinations of activations, sparse directions in activation space, or identifiable circuits (e.g., mechanistic interpretability literature). The claim that "we cannot trace the causal sequence backward in time" is technically inaccurate for deterministic inference; the challenge is computational and representational, not temporal or logical.

**1.2 Is Opacity a Problem?:** Provides a fair survey of the trust vs. accuracy debate. The section appropriately notes that demands for explanation are domain-dependent and that human cognition itself often operates opaquely. This is well-positioned, but it does not yet bridge to the paper's core thesis. It would benefit from explicitly noting that many practitioners accept opacity *precisely because* empirical validation and robustness testing often substitute for granular causal tracing—a point that aligns with, rather than contradicts, the paper's later arguments.

**1.3 Secret Owls:** Relies on Cloud et al. (2025) to introduce a concrete case of "subliminal" bias transfer via semantically inert number sequences. The claim that "there are no semantically relevant features in the data set" is reasonable, but the paper equates *semantic irrelevance* with *absence of correlative structure*. In LLM training, statistical regularities in token sequences, positional encodings, and gradient trajectories absolutely carry causal influence, even if they lack human-interpretable semantics. Treating the three-digit number dataset as a "black box vehicle" without analyzing how SGD transforms these sequences into weight updates misses the mathematical machinery that actually mediates the causal chain. The section needs to clarify what kind of "feature" is meant: semantic? statistical? activation-directional? Without this, the owl example functions more as a rhetorical puzzle than a technical grounding.

### 2. Correlative Continuity in Distal Causation (Sections 2.1–2.3)
**2.1 The Habit of Expecting Continuity & 2.2 Wobbles Past and Present:** The core argument is introduced via the potter's clay analogy: causal continuity (wobble at t1 → wobble at t3) does not entail correlative continuity (no individuated feature at t2 maps to the oscillation frequency). The analogy is philosophically elegant but technically misaligned with neural networks. clay is a continuous, physical medium where stress/strain distributions *do* mathematically determine the second wobble; the correlate exists as a field of material deformation, even if humans cannot easily parse it. Neural networks, by contrast, are discrete, parameterized function approximators with explicit, fully accessible state representations at every timestep (weights, biases, optimizer states, activation vectors). The claim that an "omniscient god could not identify a feature in the still clay" is extended to NNs to argue that internal correlates are ontologically absent. This is a category error: NN internals are not ontologically hidden; they are high-dimensional and non-linearly entangled. The paper conflates *individuation difficulty* with *non-existence*, which contradicts empirical successes in circuit discovery, sparse autoencoder feature extraction, and representational similarity analysis.

**2.3 The View from Above:** Extends the clay example to argue that discontinuous correlation is an *ontological limit*, not an epistemic one, and contrasts it with phenomena like photic sneezing. The section rightly notes that correlative continuity is feature-dependent, not system-dependent. However, it does not address how this maps to actual NN architecture. If "the whole form of the clay" is the correlate at t2, then for NNs, the "whole form" is the weight matrix + architecture + activation geometry. These are fully specifiable mathematical objects. The paper's insistence that "there is no box" collapses the distinction between *distributed representation* and *absence of structure*. For ICLR, this section must grapple with the fact that modern interpretability research already accepts distributed, non-localized correlates; the debate is not whether correlates exist, but how to extract, align, and verify them.

### 3. Three Consequences (Sections 3.1–3.3)
**3.1 The Owls You See Are the Owls You Get:** Applies the thesis back to the Cloud et al. study, claiming the dataset's "overall form" is sufficient cause and explanation. The argument correctly identifies that demanding a single "owl-encoding" feature is misguided. However, stating that "the explanation is complete" because the whole dataset caused the shift is trivial from a scientific perspective. In ML safety and alignment, we need to know *which statistical properties* of the data drive undesirable dispositions to mitigate them. A holist explanation ("the whole set caused it") is causally accurate but practically vacuous. The section should clarify how XAI practitioners should adjust their goals if correlative discontinuity is accepted.

**3.2 Trust and Transparency:** Argues that trust debates relying on "hidden features" are mischaracterized if those features don't exist. This is a reasonable conceptual correction. However, the section underestimates why opacity matters for trust beyond the "hidden feature" assumption. Trust in ML often hinges on *robustness to distribution shift*, *adversarial susceptibility*, and *unintended generalization*—issues that persist regardless of whether correlates are ontologically continuous. The paper treats trust as primarily an epistemological/linguistic problem, but operational trust in ML depends on empirical reliability guarantees that conceptual reframing alone cannot replace.

**3.3 Language of Opacity:** Calls for abandoning "black box" rhetoric and suggests this will clarify XAI efforts. While conceptually appealing, the section does not articulate what replaces the current framework. If we stop assuming correlative continuity, do we abandon attribution methods? Do we shift from local explanations to global stability metrics? The paper leaves this entirely open. For ICLR, a conceptual paper should propose at least a preliminary research agenda or clarify how existing technical programs (e.g., circuit analysis, mechanistic probing, representation geometry) should be reinterpreted under the new framework.

### Technical Grounding & ICLR Positioning
The paper's philosophical rigor and clear prose are strengths, but its engagement with contemporary ML literature is too narrow. It primarily cites early conceptual XAI surveys and a single 2025 LLM study, while entirely omitting mechanistic interpretability, representation learning theory, and sparse representation literature. These fields directly address the "correlative continuity" question: they show that internal correlates exist but are distributed, superposed, and often non-monosemantic. By treating the lack of human-interpretable, localized correlates as evidence of ontological discontinuity, the paper misses the mathematical structure that actually defines NN opacity. Additionally, the definition of "feature" shifts between semantic intuitions, activation patterns, and holistic states without formal clarification. An ICLR submission in this space must either (1) formally define what constitutes a "feature" and "correlate" in NNs, or (2) explicitly position the argument against mechanistic interpretability's findings, explaining why distributed circuits do not count as the "correlative continuity" the paper disputes. Without this, the contribution reads as a philosophical critique of a straw-man version of modern interpretability.

### Overall Assessment
This paper raises a genuine and valuable philosophical point: the assumption that every causal chain in a neural network must pass through individuated, human-comprehensible internal correlates is unwarranted, and conflating epistemic difficulty with ontological absence can mislead XAI research. However, as submitted to ICLR, the paper's technical grounding is underdeveloped. It relies on a physical analogy (the clay) that does not map cleanly onto the discrete, parameterized, and mathematically precise nature of neural networks, and it overlooks extensive work showing that internal correlates *do* exist, just in distributed and superposed forms. The consequences for trust, transparency, and XAI practice are asserted but not operationalized, leaving the field without clear guidance on how to adjust methodological priorities. To meet ICLR's bar, the paper must rigorously define its core terms (feature, correlate, continuity), directly engage with mechanistic interpretability and representation geometry literature, and clarify how its holist stance interacts with existing technical workflows. The conceptual insight is promising, but it currently functions more as a critique of early XAI rhetoric than a forward-looking contribution aligned with the current state of ML research.

# Neutral Reviewer
## Balanced Review

### Summary
This paper argues that the "black box" characterization of neural networks rests on a tacit fallacy: the assumption that causal continuity between inputs and outputs necessarily entails correlative continuity (i.e., the existence of identifiable, intermediate features that map to specific causes or effects). Drawing on physical/biological analogies and a recent LLM "subliminal learning" study, the author contends that explanations of network behavior can be causally complete without requiring granular internal feature attribution. This reconceptualization is used to challenge prevailing discourse in Explainable AI (XAI), trust, and the language of opacity surrounding deep learning systems.

### Strengths
1. **Clear identification of a pervasive conceptual assumption in XAI:** The paper accurately pinpoints a widely held but often unstated premise in interpretability research—that hidden intermediate correlates *must* exist and be discoverable if we just look deeper or develop better methods (Sec 1.1, 2.1). This provides a clean philosophical target for critique.
2. **Accessible argument structure and effective pedagogical analogies:** The pottery wheel/clay wobble example (Sec 2.2) and the photic sneeze reflex effectively illustrate how causal continuity can persist without discrete, isolatable correlates. These analogies successfully ground the abstract claim about neural networks in intuitive, real-world systems.
3. **Direct engagement with contemporary ML phenomena:** By applying the framework to the recent Cloud et al. (2025) "secret owls" study (Sec 1.3, 3.1), the author grounds the philosophical argument in a concrete, timely large-language-model behavior study, demonstrating relevance to current ML discussion.
4. **Nuanced treatment of trust and transparency debates:** The paper correctly avoids overclaiming by acknowledging that dissolving the "opacity" myth does not automatically resolve trust disputes; rather, it reorients the foundational assumptions of those debates (Sec 3.2). This shows careful conceptual reasoning.

### Weaknesses
1. **Lacks technical formalization or empirical grounding expected at ICLR:** The argument remains entirely conceptual. ICLR prioritizes contributions with formal definitions, mathematical proofs, algorithmic proposals, or rigorous empirical validation. The paper does not formalize "causal vs. correlative continuity" in terms of representation theory, information geometry, or computational graphs (Sec 2 is purely prose-driven).
2. **Overstates novelty within existing philosophical literature:** The decoupling of causal history from mechanistic/correlative explanation is extensively discussed in philosophy of science (e.g., mechanistic explanation, functional decomposition, systems biology). The paper does not adequately situate itself in this literature or clarify how neural networks present a philosophically distinct case beyond standard holistic vs. reductionist debates (Sec 2.1, 2.3).
3. **Engages loosely with current mechanistic interpretability research:** The claim that no intermediate correlates exist asserts a negative that is not tested against state-of-the-art circuit discovery, sparse autoencoders (SAEs), or activation patching. The paper declares the "box" a myth without demonstrating that modern representation analysis actually fails to find correlates in cases like the "owls" dataset (Sec 3.1).
4. **Weak practical recommendations for ML research:** Apart from suggesting that the field drop the term "black box," the paper does not propose what XAI researchers should do instead. If intermediate feature attribution is ontologically unnecessary, what methodological or evaluation frameworks should replace it? The discussion remains at the meta-level without actionable technical guidance (Sec 3).

### Novelty & Significance
- **Novelty:** Low-Moderate in ML; Low-Moderate in philosophy of AI. The conceptual move is clearly articulated but largely recapitulates existing arguments in philosophy of mind and complex systems regarding holism vs. reductionism. Its specific application to NN opacity is novel in phrasing but not in core theoretical machinery.
- **Clarity:** High. The prose is well-organized, the central claim is stated early, and the logical flow from premise to implications is easy to follow. Mathematical notation is used consistently where present, though underutilized for formal modeling.
- **Reproducibility:** Not Applicable / Low. As a purely conceptual paper, it contains no code, datasets, or experimental protocols. While reproducibility requirements are relaxed for theoretical/conceptual work at some venues, ICLR typically expects at least a formal framework, simulated experiments, or reproducible analysis to accompany conceptual claims.
- **Significance:** Moderate for interdisciplinary AI ethics/philosophy; Low for core ML research. The paper may stimulate valuable debate about how researchers frame XAI and trust, but it does not propose testable hypotheses, new interpretability methods, or evaluation benchmarks that would shift technical practice. Fit for ICLR is weak; the contribution aligns more closely with venues like FAccT, AIES, Philosophy & Technology, or interdisciplinary AI tracks at AAAI/IJCAI.

### Suggestions for Improvement
1. **Formalize the core claim within a computational framework:** Translate the philosophical argument into terms familiar to ML researchers. Use causal graphs over activations, information bottleneck theory, or representation similarity analysis to define "correlative discontinuity" mathematically, clarifying under what architectural or training conditions it should be expected.
2. **Empirically test or rigorously analyze the claim:** Instead of asserting that intermediates lack correlates, apply modern mechanistic interpretability tools (e.g., SAEs, activation patching, causal mediation analysis) to the Cloud et al. (2025) case. Either demonstrate a systematic failure to find stable correlates (supporting the thesis) or refine the claim by showing *when* correlates emerge vs. dissolve.
3. **Deepen the scholarly context and precisely delineate novelty:** Engage directly with literature on mechanistic explanation, functional decomposition in neuroscience/CS, and debates over whether NNs implement "distributed" vs. "local" representations. Clarify exactly what is philosophically or technically new beyond existing holism/reductionism discussions.
4. **Provide actionable consequences for XAI and ML practice:** If intermediate correlates are not ontologically required, propose concrete alternatives: e.g., shift evaluation from feature attribution to behavioral specifications, counterfactual robustness, specification testing, or verification-based trust. Give ML researchers a clear methodological roadmap derived from the conceptual shift.
5. **Target venue alignment or adapt structure accordingly:** For ICLR, integrate at least one experimental or theoretical proof component that demonstrates empirical or formal validity. If retaining a purely conceptual form, consider submission to an ethics/philosophy-focused AI venue where conceptual contributions to framing and terminology are standard.

# Final Consolidated Review
## Summary
The paper argues that characterizing neural networks as "black boxes" relies on a false assumption: that causal continuity between inputs and outputs necessitates correlative continuity (i.e., the existence of individuated, identifiable intermediate features). Using analogies like a potter's clay and applying the framework to a recent study on "subliminal" bias transfer in LLMs, the author contends that neural network opacity is often an ontological reality rather than an epistemic limit, and that explanations citing the complete global state of a system are causally sufficient. This reconceptualization is applied to debates surrounding Explainable AI (XAI), algorithmic trust, and the field's terminology.

## Strengths
- **Clear identification of a pervasive conceptual target in XAI:** The paper accurately isolates a widely held but often unstated premise in interpretability discourse—that hidden intermediate correlates *must* exist and be discoverable if we simply look deeper or develop better methods. This provides a precise philosophical challenge to common intuitions about transparency.
- **Accessible argumentation grounded in contemporary phenomena:** The central thesis is effectively structured and supported by pedagogical analogies (the clay wobble) that make abstract causal concepts tangible. Applying this framework to a timely LLM phenomenon (the transmission of behavioral dispositions via semantically inert data) successfully grounds the philosophical argument in current machine learning discussions.

## Weaknesses
- **Conflation of epistemic limits with ontological non-existence in discrete computational systems:** The paper argues that because intermediate features are difficult to individuate or lack human-interpretable semantics, they ontologically do not exist (e.g., claiming "there is simply no box"). However, neural networks are fully specified, deterministic mathematical objects where inputs, weights, activations, and optimization states are perfectly accessible and explicitly defined. The mathematical correlate of an input-output mapping *is* the complete parameter state and its activation geometry. By framing distributed, high-dimensional computation as an "ontological discontinuity," the paper conflates the absence of *localized, human-semantic features* with the ontological absence of *any correlate*. For a technical audience, opacity in neural networks arises from computational nonlinearity and representational entanglement, not from a fundamental ontological void as argued for continuous physical media.
- **The "holist" explanation is practically vacuous for XAI and alignment research:** The paper concludes that accounting for the "whole form" of a system or dataset constitutes a causally complete and sufficient explanation. While philosophically coherent, this renders the explanation practically useless for applied machine learning. AI safety, robustness verification, and mechanistic analysis fundamentally require identifying the specific statistical properties, circuits, or parameter directions responsible for a behavior in order to mitigate harms, ensure generalization, or verify alignment. Declaring the global state a complete explanation bypasses the granular diagnostic needs of actual ML practice without offering a functional alternative.
- **Lack of technical formalization misaligned with venue standards:** The central thesis is entirely prose-driven and relies heavily on physical/biological analogies without formalizing "causal vs. correlative continuity" within computational graphs, structural causal models, or information-theoretic frameworks. The definition of "feature" shifts loosely between semantic intuitions, dataset statistical regularities, and holistic system states. Because the paper seeks to redefine the nature of opacity in mathematical learning systems without engaging their formal structure, the core argument remains a philosophical assertion ungrounded in the technical architecture it critiques.

## Nice-to-Haves
- Formalize the argument using structural causal models, representation similarity analysis, or information bottleneck theory to precisely specify boundary conditions where correlative discontinuity genuinely emerges versus where standard circuit mapping recovers intermediate features.
- Test the "no correlate" claim empirically using modern attribution methods on the "subliminal learning" case to demonstrate where feature individuation genuinely fails due to system architecture rather than current methodological limitations.
- Propose concrete alternative evaluation frameworks for trust and XAI that operate under the proposed paradigm (e.g., shifting from granular attribution to behavioral specification testing or counterfactual robustness metrics).

## Novel Insights
The paper effectively challenges the reification of the "black box," but its core insight—that causal chains in complex systems can be fully accounted for by global state dynamics without requiring localized, intermediate explanatory variables—recapitulates standard holist positions in philosophy of science and complex systems. Applied to neural networks, it correctly identifies that the demand for monosemantic, human-readable intermediate features is often misplaced, but framing distributed mathematical computation as "ontological discontinuity" does not yield a fundamentally new technical or philosophical insight beyond existing discussions of superposition and distributed representation in ML.

## Suggestions
- Ground the conceptual claims directly in the formal mathematics of neural networks (e.g., explicitly model weights, activation manifolds, and gradient flow as the causal vehicle) rather than relying on analogies to continuous physical media. Clarify how the proposed framework reinterprets, rather than dismisses, existing technical programs in mechanistic interpretability, and specify what methodological shifts should follow from accepting correlative discontinuity in practical XAI workflows.
