# Harsh Critic Review
## Section-by-Section Critical Review

### Title & Abstract
- **Does the title accurately reflect the contribution?** Yes. "Learning Robust Diffusion Models from Imprecise Supervision" accurately captures the scope (diffusion models) and the problem setting (imprecise supervision encompassing partial, noisy, and unlabeled data).
- **Does the abstract clearly state the problem, method, and key results?** The abstract is well-structured: it identifies the misalignment caused by imprecise conditioning, introduces DMIS as a likelihood-based unified framework decomposing into generative and classification objectives, and summarizes the empirical scope (generation, weakly supervised learning, dataset condensation).
- **Are any claims in the abstract unsupported by the paper?** The claim that DMIS is the "first systematic study within diffusion models" is slightly overstated given that concurrent/adjacent works on label-noise-robust diffusion (e.g., TDSM, CAD) and diffusion-based classifiers already exist. The authors acknowledge some of these in Appendix F.2, but the abstract should more precisely qualify the novelty (e.g., "first unified framework handling heterogeneous imprecise supervision without external priors").

### Introduction & Motivation
- **Is the problem well-motivated? Is the gap in prior work clearly identified?** Yes. Real-world conditional generation suffers from noisy, ambiguous, or incomplete labels. The gap is clearly articulated: prior diffusion adaptations are either task-specific or rely on strong external priors (e.g., noise transition matrices, confidence scores), limiting practical applicability.
- **Are the contributions clearly stated and accurate?** The four contributions are clearly enumerated. However, the novelty of the generative objective (Eq. 10) closely mirrors established self-training/EMA-stabilized label refinement techniques from discriminative learning (PRODEN, ELR). The introduction should more explicitly clarify what is fundamentally new in adapting these to score matching versus what is a principled translation of existing weak-supervision paradigms.
- **Does the introduction over-claim or under-sell?** Slightly over-claims on "uniqueness" without sufficiently differentiating from two-stage pipelines (label correction → standard diffusion training). It would strengthen the paper to explicitly state why a joint score-matching formulation is preferable over sequential correction, both theoretically and empirically.

### Method / Approach
- **Is the method clearly described and reproducible?** The mathematical formulation is rigorous. The ELBO decomposition (Eq. 7), weighted denoising score matching (Eq. 10), and the diffusion classifier formulation (Eq. 11-12) are clearly derived. Reproducibility is supported by detailed appendices and implementation hyperparameters. However, the exact training algorithm (pseudocode or step-by-step loop detailing when the EMA classifier is updated, when $\phi$ is synchronized, and how gradients flow between classification and generation terms) is missing from the main text.
- **Are key assumptions stated and justified?** The class-conditional independence assumption ($p(Z|X,Y) = p(Z|Y)$) is standard and justified. The uniform prior assumption is later relaxed in Appendix C.2, which is appropriate.
- **Are there logical gaps in the derivation or reasoning?** The theoretical link between Theorem 1 and Proposition 1 is sound. However, a practical gap exists in Eq. 10: optimizing the weighted score matching loss requires computing $p(y|x_t, z)$ at every timestep $t$ and every training step. Computing this via the diffusion classifier (Eq. 11-12) requires estimating $\log p_\theta(x_t|y)$ across all $c$ classes via Monte Carlo ELBO evaluation. This is computationally prohibitive for large $c$ or high-resolution models. While Sec. 5 addresses inference efficiency, it does not fully resolve the training-time bottleneck, creating a disconnect between theory and practical scalability.
- **Are there edge cases or failure modes not discussed?** Early-training posterior collapse is not discussed. If the diffusion classifier yields highly inaccurate posteriors in early epochs, the weighted score targets in Eq. 10 become severely biased, potentially destabilizing the reverse process. The reliance on EMA (Eq. 7) mitigates this but does not eliminate risk of error accumulation under extreme noise or highly skewed candidate sets.
- **For theoretical claims: are proofs correct and complete?** Proofs in Appendices B.6-B.9 are complete. Theorem 1 correctly exploits the linearity of the score function under mixture distributions. Proposition 1 is a straightforward calculus result on convex combinations. Theorems 2 & 3 correctly characterize the optimal subinterval for expectation approximation under a log-normal timestep distribution. The math is sound.

### Experiments & Results
- **Do the experiments actually test the paper's claims?** Yes. Image generation tests distribution fidelity under imprecision (Table 1/10). Weakly supervised learning tests downstream classification accuracy using the learned diffusion model (Table 2). Dataset condensation tests data utility (Table 3). All align with claims.
- **Are baselines appropriate and fairly compared?** The baselines are extensive and well-chosen for discriminative weak supervision. However, the generative comparison primarily uses a naive "Vanilla" baseline trained directly on imprecise labels (Eq. 8). Missing are comparisons to practical "correction-then-train" pipelines (e.g., train a robust classifier, correct labels/pseudo-label, then train standard conditional diffusion). Appendix F.2 includes two robust diffusion methods, but a systematic comparison against strong label-correction baselines would better validate the necessity of the joint formulation.
- **Are there missing ablations that would materially change conclusions?** Yes. Computational cost metrics (training wall-clock time, FLOPs, memory footprint, number of classifier evaluations per step) are entirely absent. The paper claims efficiency gains from timestep sampling (Sec. 5), but without quantitative overhead reporting relative to Vanilla or prior robust methods, the efficiency claim remains qualitative. Additionally, an ablation on the EMA decay rate or the weight of the classification term in Eq. 7 would clarify sensitivity.
- **Are error bars / statistical significance reported?** Yes, standard deviations are reported across multiple runs in Tables 2, 3, 5-9. This meets ICLR standards.
- **Do the results support the claims made, or are they cherry-picked?** Results consistently favor DMIS, which supports the claims. The authors honestly note that Vanilla sometimes outperforms several discriminative baselines (Sec. 6.2), acknowledging diffusion's implicit regularization. No obvious cherry-picking detected.
- **Are datasets and evaluation metrics appropriate?** Standard benchmarks (CIFAR-10, ImageNette, Fashion-MNIST) are appropriate for proof-of-concept. Metrics (FID, IS, CW-FID, Density, Coverage) are standard for generation; accuracy is standard for classification. For dataset condensation (Task 3), evaluating downstream ResNet-34 accuracy on condensed data is standard, but noting that DMIS acts as a *generative* condenser rather than an optimization-based distiller (like MTT) would contextualize the comparison better.

### Writing & Clarity
- **Are there sections that are confusing or poorly explained?** The transition from the variational lower bound (Eq. 7) to the practical implementation of $p_\phi(Y|X,Z)$ is somewhat abrupt. The paper uses $p_\phi$ as the EMA model but does not explicitly detail how the diffusion classifier parameters are synchronized with the generator during joint training. A brief training algorithm pseudocode would resolve this.
- **Are figures and tables clear and informative?** Figure 1 effectively motivates the timestep subinterval choice. Figure 2 qualitatively supports generation quality. Tables contain rich data, though the parser artifacts obscured some formatting; conceptually, they are well-organized. Table 3's comparison across IPC values is clear, but a brief discussion on how condensation quality degrades under higher noise rates would add depth.

### Limitations & Broader Impact
- **Do the authors acknowledge the key limitations?** Partially. The computational cost of the diffusion classifier is noted and partially addressed via timestep reduction. However, the paper does not explicitly discuss limitations regarding scalability to high-dimensional label spaces (e.g., text conditioning) or high-resolution generation where per-class ELBO estimation becomes intractable even with sub-interval sampling.
- **Are there fundamental limitations they missed?** The method assumes the imprecise supervision mechanism is class-conditional rather than instance-dependent. While Appendix F.4 tests instance-dependent noise empirically, the theoretical framework (Theorem 1) does not account for $x$-dependent label corruption. This limits theoretical guarantees under complex, realistic noise. Additionally, the framework's reliance on a fully enumerated label space $Y$ for the classifier restricts direct application to open-vocabulary or continuous conditioning without significant modification.
- **Are there failure modes or negative societal impacts not discussed?** Standard generative model risks (e.g., amplifying biases present in noisy web data, misuse of high-quality synthetic data) are not discussed. For ICLR, a brief acknowledgment in the conclusion or appendix is expected, though not a primary technical weakness.

### Overall Assessment
This paper presents a theoretically sound and broadly applicable framework (DMIS) for training conditional diffusion models under heterogeneous imprecise supervision. The decomposition of the likelihood maximization into a weighted generative objective and an EMA-stabilized diffusion classifier is mathematically well-founded and effectively bridges imprecise label learning with score-based modeling. The proposed timestep sub-interval sampling strategy is a practical innovation that addresses a real computational bottleneck. The experimental scope is comprehensive, covering generation, weakly supervised classification, and the novel application of noisy dataset condensation. Despite these strengths, the paper has notable gaps regarding computational efficiency quantification and baseline comparisons. The training-time overhead of per-timestep, per-class ELBO estimation is substantial, yet no FLOP or wall-clock comparisons are provided against simpler correction-then-train pipelines. Additionally, while the theory elegantly adapts self-training and weighted loss principles to diffusion, the novelty over two-stage approaches needs sharper empirical justification. If the authors can provide rigorous efficiency ablations, clarify the training loop dynamics, and more carefully contextualize the "unified" claim against concurrent robust diffusion works, the contribution will strongly meet ICLR's bar for technical soundness, novelty, and practical relevance.

# Neutral Reviewer
## Balanced Review

### Summary
This paper introduces DMIS, a unified framework for training conditional diffusion models under imprecise supervision, encompassing noisy-label, partial-label, and semi-supervised settings. By treating the true label as a latent variable and decomposing the marginal likelihood into generative and classification components, the authors derive a weighted denoising score matching objective complemented by an optimized timestep sampling strategy for efficient posterior inference. Extensive experiments across image generation, weakly supervised classification, and a novel noisy dataset condensation task demonstrate consistent improvements over naive baselines and discriminative weak-supervision methods.

### Strengths
1. **Theoretically Grounded Objective Derivation:** The paper provides a rigorous probabilistic foundation by proving (Theorem 1) that imprecise-label conditional scores form a convex combination of clean-label scores weighted by posterior probabilities. This directly motivates the weighted denoising score matching loss (Eq. 10) and Proposition 1, offering a principled alternative to ad-hoc noise correction heuristics.
2. **Comprehensive Multi-Task Evaluation:** The framework is thoroughly validated across three distinct learning paradigms: conditional image generation (Table 1), weakly supervised classification (Table 2), and noisy dataset condensation (Table 3). The consistent outperformance of both discriminative weak-supervision baselines and the "Vanilla" diffusion baseline demonstrates strong versatility and generalization capability.
3. **Well-Motivated Computational Efficiency Strategy:** Section 5 identifies the suboptimal nature of uniform timestep sampling for diffusion classifiers and formally characterizes an optimal median-centered subinterval (Theorems 2 & 3). Figure 1 empirically confirms that concentrating ELBO computations around the median timestep maintains classification accuracy while reducing inference overhead.
4. **Pioneering a New Benchmark Setting:** The introduction of "noisy dataset condensation" addresses a highly practical, previously unexplored problem. Results in Table 3 show that DMIS-generated synthetic datasets significantly outperform gradient/distribution-matching condensation methods under severe label noise, establishing a valuable new direction for data-efficient robust training.

### Weaknesses
1. **Lack of Computational Overhead Quantification:** Despite the timestep sampling optimization, the training loop still requires estimating a per-class ELBO approximation via a diffusion classifier at each step. The paper omits wall-clock training time, FLOPs, or GPU memory comparisons against the Vanilla baseline and robust diffusion methods (e.g., CAD, TDSM), making it difficult to assess practical feasibility or scalability for larger training budgets.
2. **Insufficient Component-Wise Ablations:** The unified objective jointly optimizes the weighted generative loss (Eq. 10) and multiple classification losses (Eqs. 13–15). However, no systematic ablation isolates the contributions of each component (e.g., generative-only vs. classifier-only, uniform vs. proposed timestep sampling, EMA update frequency impact). This limits interpretability regarding which mechanism drives the reported performance gains.
3. **Limited Scale and Architecture Validation:** All experiments are conducted on small-scale datasets (Fashion-MNIST, CIFAR-10, ImageNette) with standard U-Net/DDPM++ architectures. For a venue like ICLR, where robustness claims are increasingly expected to hold on larger models (e.g., latent diffusion on ImageNet-1k or Stable Diffusion backbones), the absence of higher-resolution or LDM experiments restricts the perceived scalability and real-world impact.
4. **Marginalized Key Baseline Comparisons:** Comparisons against existing noise-robust diffusion models (Na et al., 2024; Dufour et al., 2024) are relegated to Appendix F.2 (Table 11). Given the paper's core contribution is *robust diffusion modeling*, these diffusion-specific comparisons should be elevated to the main text to properly contextualize how DMIS avoids their reliance on external noise transition matrices or risk scores.

### Novelty & Significance
The paper presents a conceptually meaningful extension of weak/uncertain label learning into the diffusion model domain. The mathematical bridge linking imprecise supervision to clean-label score decomposition is novel for generative score matching and provides a principled, prior-free alternative to existing robust diffusion methods that require external noise estimators. While the underlying EM/self-training intuition is well-established in discriminative learning, its rigorous adaptation to conditional diffusion training and ELBO-based posterior inference is a solid technical contribution. The significance is notable for the generative modeling community, directly addressing the pervasive bottleneck of imperfect web-scale training data. The impact would be substantially amplified, however, by demonstrating computational trade-offs explicitly and validating scalability beyond 32×32/64×64 resolutions.

### Suggestions for Improvement
1. **Report Computational Profiling Metrics:** Add a dedicated subsection or table in Section 6 reporting training time per epoch, total FLOPs, and peak GPU memory for DMIS versus Vanilla and robust diffusion baselines. This will empirically ground the theoretical efficiency gains of the timestep sampling strategy.
2. **Conduct Systematic Ablations:** Introduce ablation experiments isolating: (a) the weighted generative loss vs. classification loss, (b) uniform vs. median-centered timestep sampling, and (c) different EMA decay rates for the classifier target ϕ. This will clarify component contributions and hyperparameter sensitivity.
3. **Elevate Diffusion-Specific Baselines to Main Text:** Move the comparison against CAD and TDSM (currently Appendix Table 11) into Section 6.1 or 6.2. Explicitly discuss how DMIS achieves robustness without requiring noise transition matrices, confidence scores, or external teacher models.
4. **Discuss Scalability to Latent Diffusion Models:** Given ICLR's focus on modern architectures, add a discussion or preliminary experiment on adapting DMIS to latent diffusion models (e.g., Stable Diffusion). Address potential bottlenecks in latent-space ELBO evaluation and how techniques like top-k truncation (Appendix F.3) or classifier-free guidance approximations could mitigate them.
5. **Clarify Assumption Robustness:** Theorem 1 assumes conditional independence between the imprecise signal _Z_ and input _X_ given true label _Y_. Briefly discuss how violations (e.g., instance-dependent noise correlated with visual features) might affect convergence, and explicitly connect this discussion to the instance-dependent noise results in Table 13 to demonstrate practical resilience.

# Final Consolidated Review
## Summary
This paper introduces DMIS, a unified framework for training conditional diffusion models under imprecise supervision (noisy, partial, and supplementary-unlabeled labels). By treating true labels as latent variables, the authors derive a likelihood-based objective that decomposes into a weighted denoising score matching generative term and an EMA-stabilized classification term for posterior inference. The method is complemented by a theoretically grounded median-centered timestep sampling strategy to reduce classifier overhead. Extensive experiments across image generation, weakly supervised classification, and a novel noisy dataset condensation task demonstrate consistent improvements over naive baselines and discriminative weak-supervision methods, establishing a principled, prior-free alternative to existing robust diffusion approaches.

## Strengths
- **Theoretically principled objective derivation:** The paper rigorously proves (Theorem 1) that imprecise-label conditional scores decompose into a convex combination of clean-label scores. This elegantly bridges discriminative imprecise-label learning with score-based generative modeling, justifying the weighted denoising score matching loss (Eq. 10) without relying on external noise transition matrices or risk estimators.
- **Comprehensive multi-task validation:** The framework is thoroughly evaluated across three distinct paradigms: conditional generation, weakly supervised classification, and dataset condensation. The consistent outperformance of both "Vanilla" diffusion baselines and established discriminative weak-supervision methods demonstrates strong versatility and validates the implicit label-refinement capability of the generative process.
- **Well-motivated computational efficiency strategy:** The paper correctly identifies that uniform timestep sampling is suboptimal for diffusion classifier ELBO estimation. The derivation of a median-centered subinterval (Theorems 2 & 3) provides a formal, empirically validated strategy to concentrate computation where classification signal is most informative, addressing a real practical bottleneck in diffusion-based posteriors.

## Weaknesses
- **Missing computational profiling metrics:** While Section 5 optimizes classifier inference, the training loop still requires estimating per-class ELBO approxiations via a diffusion classifier at each step. The paper lacks quantitative reporting of training wall-clock time, FLOPs, or memory overhead compared to the Vanilla baseline or robust diffusion competitors. Without this, claims of practical feasibility and efficiency remain qualitative and difficult to benchmark against real-world training constraints.
- **Insufficient component-wise ablations:** The joint optimization couples a weighted generative loss with task-specific classification objectives (Eqs. 13–15). The absence of systematic ablations isolating the classification term vs. the generative term, or ablating the proposed timestep sampling strategy, limits interpretability. It remains unclear how much of the robustness stems from the novel score weighting versus standard EMA self-training dynamics already prevalent in discriminative learning.
- **Diffusion-specific baselines relegated to appendix:** Direct comparisons against recent noise-robust diffusion models (e.g., TDSM, CAD) that use external priors are placed in Appendix F.2. Given the paper positions itself as a unified framework for robust diffusion training, these comparisons must be in the main text to properly contextualize the advantage of the prior-free approach and justify the architectural/training differences.
- **Limited scale and architecture validation:** Empirical validation is restricted to low-resolution datasets (up to 64×64) with standard U-Net backbones. While adequate for theoretical validation, the per-class ELBO estimation and weighted score matching will face exponentially growing computational and representational challenges on high-resolution latent diffusion models. The paper lacks discussion or preliminary results demonstrating how the framework scales or adapts under higher capacity regimes.

## Nice-to-Haves
- Provide a strict hyperparameter sensitivity analysis for the EMA decay rate and classification loss weighting, as the generative-classification balance appears dataset-dependent and will influence user adoption.
- Include a brief denoising trajectory visualization for mislabeled samples to illustrate qualitatively where and how the label posterior correction manifests during the reverse diffusion process, complementing the aggregate FID/accuracy tables.
- Discuss potential adaptations for continuous or open-vocabulary conditioning (e.g., text prompts), where enumerating the label space $Y$ for posterior computation is intractable, even if this falls outside the current categorical scope.

## Novel Insights
The paper reveals a fundamental structural property of conditional diffusion models under weak supervision: the imprecise-label conditional score is mathematically equivalent to a convex combination of clean-label scores, with weights defined by the model's own posterior. This insight transforms robust diffusion training from an external noise-correction problem into an internal self-consistency problem, showing that generative models can implicitly denoise and disambiguate labels through weighted score matching without auxiliary networks or hand-crafted noise priors. Furthermore, extending this framework to noisy dataset condensation demonstrates that the generative process doesn't just survive label corruption; it actively filters it, producing compact synthetic datasets that outperform explicit gradient and distribution-matching distillation methods. This reframes diffusion models not just as passive samplers of corrupted data, but as active regularizers that can distill cleaner representations from weaker supervision.

## Suggestions
1. Move the comparison against robust diffusion baselines (TDSM, CAD) from Appendix F.2 to Section 6.1, and explicitly discuss how DMIS achieves competitive or superior robustness without requiring external noise estimators or confidence scores.
2. Add a dedicated table or paragraph in the experiments section reporting training/inference wall-clock time, peak VRAM usage, and approximate FLOPs for DMIS vs. Vanilla and robust diffusion baselines to empirically ground the efficiency claims.
3. Introduce component ablation studies: (a) generative-only vs. joint generative+classification training, (b) uniform vs. median-centered timestep sampling for the classifier, and (c) varying EMA decay rates. This will clarify which mechanisms drive the reported gains.
4. Qualify the "first systematic study" claim in the abstract/introduction to accurately reflect that while several task-specific robust diffusion methods exist, DMIS is the first unified, prior-free framework handling heterogeneous imprecise supervision under a single likelihood objective.
