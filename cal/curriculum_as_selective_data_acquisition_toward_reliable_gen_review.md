# Harsh Critic Review
## Section-by-Section Critical Review

**Title & Abstract**
The title promises "toward reliable generalization in goal-conditioned RL," but the abstract and subsequent results only demonstrate marginal, task-specific improvements on hardcoded edge goals in a small deterministic grid. The claim that curricula "reduce approximation error" is stated in the abstract and Section 3.1, yet no quantitative approximation error metrics (e.g., value MSE, OOD generalization error, or calibration plots) are actually reported. The abstract's concluding sentence linking the work to "persistent and open-ended agents" is speculative and not operationalized or tested in the paper. At an ICLR venue, titles and abstract claims must be tightly bounded by the empirical evidence presented.

**1. Introduction & Motivation**
The motivation to reframe curriculum learning as a distribution-shaping mechanism (i.e., selective data acquisition) is conceptually sound, but the claim that "far less attention has been paid to its effect on the distribution of training data itself" does not align with the broader GCRL and RL literature. Prior methods like Hindsight Experience Replay (Andrychowicz et al., 2017), Active Goal Exploration/ALP-GMM (Forestier et al., 2017), automated curriculum generation (Portelas et al., 2020; Racanière et al., 2020), and prioritized experience replay all explicitly model and manipulate training distributions. The paper needs to more precisely differentiate its perspective: is the contribution a novel algorithmic framework, or primarily an empirical validation that manual distribution biasing improves UVFA fitting on edge goals? The connection to open-ended learning (Hughes et al., 2024) is cited as motivation but remains entirely untested, leaving the introduction's ambition misaligned with the paper's actual scope.

**2. Methods**
- **2.1 Environment:** The GridWorld dimensions, obstacle configuration, start-state distribution, and goal space cardinality are unspecified. These details directly affect whether "edge goals" are inherently harder due to geometry or simply artifactually under-sampled.
- **2.2 UVFA Training Objective:** The paper trains the UVFA via *mean squared error regression against discounted returns-to-go* collected from rollouts. This is supervised value regression, not reinforcement learning. Standard GCRL relies on temporal difference (TD) or policy gradient methods with bootstrapping and on-policy/off-policy exploration. By freezing data collection and using supervised regression, the paper explicitly removes exploration dynamics, credit assignment over time, and policy optimization. This is a valid experimental design choice for isolating data distribution effects, but it must be clearly framed as such. Otherwise, it risks misrepresenting how curricula function in actual RL training loops.
- **2.3 PBRS:** The use of Manhattan distance potential with λ=0.5, c=0.01, and γ=0.99 creates a near-perfect shaping signal in a deterministic grid. Under PBRS, a greedy policy trivially follows the value gradient. Combined with supervised UVFA training on these rollouts, the "learning problem" reduces to curve-fitting a smooth value surface rather than solving sparse reward or exploration challenges typical of GCRL. The paper must clarify whether the observed benefits are due to curriculum-driven data selection or simply to the inductive bias of training on PBRS-shaped trajectories where value gradients align with optimal paths.
- **2.4 Curriculum Design & 2.5 Training Protocol:** The edge-biased sampling mechanism is described only at a high level. Is the bias implemented via rejection sampling, weighted cross-entropy in data loading, or trajectory-level weighting? How is the "weighted curriculum" parameterized exactly? The protocol states 1000 episodes are collected via *greedy* action selection. If the policy is already greedy under a shaping signal that makes optimal paths obvious, the resulting dataset lacks exploration diversity. Curricula in RL typically interact with an evolving policy; here, the data acquisition policy is static and deterministic. This fundamentally limits the generalizability of the conclusions to interactive RL settings.

**3. Results**
- **Statistical Rigor:** Results are reported across only three seeds with large standard deviations (e.g., Edge-Goal Success: 0.060 ± 0.055 vs. 0.143 ± 0.107). The intervals overlap substantially, and no statistical significance tests (e.g., paired t-test, bootstrap CIs, or Mann-Whitney U) are provided. Given the modest effect sizes (∆overall ≈ +0.021), claims of consistent improvement are not statistically substantiated.
- **Missing Baselines:** The paper compares only uniform vs. manual edge-weighted sampling. It omits standard GCRL baselines that inherently manipulate goal distributions, most notably Hindsight Experience Replay (HER), which is the dominant paradigm for handling sparse/edge goal coverage. It also omits automated curriculum methods (ALP-GMM, GoalGAN, or teacher-student frameworks). Without these, it is impossible to determine whether the observed gains are specific to the "selective data acquisition" framing or simply replicable by existing, more principled methods.
- **Metrics & Consistency:** The claim that approximation error is reduced appears multiple times but is never quantified. Only policy success rates are reported. Additionally, the captions for Figure 1, Figure 2, and Figure 3 are nearly identical and refer to overlapping experimental conditions, which obscures the actual ablation structure. The paper needs a clear train/validation distribution plot, explicit value error metrics, and a precise breakdown of how the weighted curriculum shifts the sampling distribution mathematically.

**4. Discussion & Limitations**
The discussion correctly notes that benefits are non-uniform and that manual curricula may trade off performance on well-covered goals. However, it misses the most critical limitation: the evaluation setup decouples data acquisition from policy optimization. By using a static greedy data collector and supervised UVFA training, the paper sidesteps the core feedback loop of RL (policy improvement → new data distribution → further learning). Consequently, the findings cannot be directly extrapolated to "reliable generalization in GCRL" or open-ended systems, where agents must discover and adapt to novel goals autonomously. The mention of teacher-student or adversarial frameworks in the limitations section acknowledges this gap but frames future work as incremental rather than addressing the methodological disconnect in the current study.

**5. Conclusion**
The conclusion restates the premise that curriculum reshapes training distributions and improves UVFA approximation on targeted goals. However, the leap to "promising pathway toward more persistent and open-ended agents" is unsupported by the narrow, deterministic, and supervised experimental setup. The conclusion should be tightened to reflect the actual contribution: empirical evidence that manual biasing of goal sampling distributions can improve supervised value function fitting on underrepresented regions in simple, deterministic environments.

### Overall Assessment
This paper proposes a conceptually reasonable reframing of curriculum learning as selective data acquisition, emphasizing how goal-sampling distributions shape UVFA generalization in GCRL. However, the empirical evaluation falls short of ICLR's standards for methodological rigor and claim-evidence alignment. The setup reduces GCRL to supervised value regression on deterministic rollouts generated by a greedy policy under perfect potential-based shaping, which removes exploration, bootstrapping, and interactive policy optimization—the core dynamics that make curriculum learning impactful in RL. Results rely on only three seeds, lack statistical significance testing, omit critical baselines like HER and automated curricula, and claim unreported metrics like approximation error reduction. While the discussion honestly acknowledges the small scale and manual design, the paper would benefit from either (1) scaling to an interactive RL loop with standard algorithms (DQN/PPO/SAC with HER curricula), rigorous statistical analysis, and comparison to existing distribution-shaping methods, or (2) repositioning as a conceptual/position paper with clearly bounded preliminary evidence. As currently written, the contribution is preliminary and does not meet the novelty and empirical depth expected for ICLR acceptance.

# Neutral Reviewer
## Balanced Review

### Summary
This paper reframes curriculum learning in goal-conditioned RL as a mechanism of selective data acquisition that deliberately shifts the training distribution toward under-represented, difficult goals. Using Universal Value Function Approximators trained on offline trajectories in a GridWorld environment, the authors show that edge-biased sampling modestly improves value approximation and policy success on hard-to-reach goals compared to uniform sampling. The work positions curricula as structural guides for function approximation inductive biases, proposing a conceptual bridge toward open-ended and persistent agent learning.

### Strengths
1. **Clear conceptual framing:** The paper successfully articulates a useful shift in perspective, treating curricula not merely as exploration heuristics but as deliberate data acquisition strategies that reshape state-goal distributions and inductive biases. This is logically grounded and well-motivated by recent open-ended learning literature.
2. **Controlled experimental design:** By fixing dataset size, network architecture, training epochs, and using a supervised regression objective on PBRS-shaped returns, the authors cleanly isolate the impact of sampling bias. This allows for a direct comparison of how data distribution shifts affect value estimation quality.
3. **Ablation on curriculum intensity:** The comparison between the baseline edge-biased curriculum and the difficulty-weighted variant (Curr-W) demonstrates a dose-response relationship: stronger bias toward underachieved goals yields larger success deltas on edges. This supports the core hypothesis with empirical granularity.
4. **Transparent and cautious reporting:** Results include standard deviations across seeds, and the authors explicitly acknowledge modest absolute gains and high variance, avoiding overclaiming while maintaining scientific honesty.

### Weaknesses
1. **Limited environment complexity and task scope:** Evaluation is confined to a small, deterministic GridWorld with trivial state representations (x,y coordinates). This severely simplifies core GCRL challenges (e.g., function approximation capacity limits, partial observability, exploration-exploitation tradeoffs), making it difficult to generalize the findings to standard RL domains.
2. **Non-RL training paradigm bypasses policy dynamics:** Data is collected via greedy rollouts under potential-based shaping, and the UVFA is trained offline via supervised regression for 50 epochs. This reduces the problem to distance-regression rather than evaluating how curriculum interacts with actual policy optimization, bootstrapping, or off-policy replay dynamics.
3. **Marginal empirical impact with high variance:** The reported gains are small (Overall +0.02, Edge +0.08) and exhibit large standard deviations relative to means (e.g., Edge Curr: 0.143 ± 0.107 at H=16). In several cases, the mean success is not statistically distinguishable from uniform baselines, weakening claims of reliable generalization.
4. **Missing established baselines and literature context:** The paper does not compare against standard GCRL sampling methods (e.g., HER, prioritized HER/PER) or automated curriculum learners (e.g., ALP-GMM, Teacher-Student). Without these baselines, it's unclear whether the modest gains stem from a fundamental advantage or are subsumed by existing adaptive sampling techniques.
5. **Confounding effect of PBRS on generalization claims:** Because the reward is directly proportional to Manhattan distance, the UVFA essentially learns a distance heuristic. Curriculum benefits may simply reflect better interpolation of a known metric rather than improved RL generalization or robust policy learning, blurring the mechanistic interpretation.

### Novelty & Significance
**Novelty:** The reframing of curricula as selective data acquisition is conceptually sound and clearly presented, but it closely overlaps with established ideas in adaptive goal sampling, prioritized experience replay, and offline RL dataset curation. The novelty lies primarily in the explicit framing rather than in algorithmic or theoretical advances.
**Clarity:** The manuscript is well-structured, with a logical flow from motivation to methods, results, and discussion. However, incomplete table captions (e.g., "Pc"), missing reference placeholders, and the lack of quantitative function approximation error metrics (despite being claimed) slightly detract from clarity.
**Reproducibility:** Core hyperparameters (MLP size, learning rate, batch size, epochs, PBRS coefficients) are provided, suggesting strong theoretical reproducibility. However, the absence of grid dimensions, obstacle configurations, environment code, and a public repository limits practical replication, while the high variance across only three seeds raises questions about robustness.
**Significance:** For ICLR, which expects methodological rigor, broad relevance, and strong empirical validation, the current contribution falls short. The toy setting, offline supervised training paradigm, marginal and high-variance gains, and lack of standard RL baselines limit immediate impact on the GCRL or open-ended learning communities. Significance would improve substantially if validated on standard benchmarks with modern RL algorithms and paired with mechanistic analysis of representation learning.

### Suggestions for Improvement
1. **Integrate with standard RL pipelines & benchmarks:** Evaluate the curriculum framework within online/offline RL algorithms (e.g., SAC+HER, TD3) and test on widely adopted GCRL benchmarks (e.g., Minigrid, PointMaze, or Procgen) to demonstrate transferability beyond supervised value regression in GridWorld.
2. **Include strong, relevant baselines:** Compare against HER, prioritized HER/replay, and automated curriculum methods (e.g., ALP-GMM, ACL) to contextualize results and determine whether the manual curriculum offers distinct advantages in sample efficiency or final performance.
3. **Quantify function approximation error directly:** Replace or supplement success rates with explicit metrics on value prediction error (e.g., MSE/MAE on held-out state-goal pairs, error heatmaps, out-of-distribution extrapolation bounds) to substantiate the claim that curricula "reduce approximation error."
4. **Improve statistical rigor:** Conduct formal significance testing (e.g., paired bootstrap or t-tests across runs), increase the number of seeds/environments to reduce variance, and report effect sizes alongside deltas to clarify whether improvements are robust or stochastic.
5. **Clarify deployment in practical RL settings:** Discuss how the curated distribution would be leveraged in practice (e.g., as initial replay buffer sampling, dynamic priority weighting, or periodic policy resets) and analyze how curriculum benefits interact with distribution shift during online training.
6. **Polish manuscript elements:** Resolve placeholder citations, ensure all table/figure captions are complete, and explicitly state grid dimensions and obstacle configurations to meet publication-ready standards.

# Final Consolidated Review
## Summary
This paper proposes reframing curriculum learning in goal-conditioned RL as a mechanism of selective data acquisition that deliberately biases the training distribution toward underachieved goals. Using a controlled offline setup in a deterministic GridWorld, the authors demonstrate through supervised UVFA regression that edge-biased sampling yields modest, targeted improvements in policy success on hard-to-reach goals compared to uniform sampling. The work positions distributional shaping as a structural inductive bias, offering a conceptual bridge toward persistent and open-ended agent design.

## Strengths
- **Controlled isolation of distribution effects:** By decoupling data collection (fixed greedy rollouts under PBRS) from model training (supervised UVFA regression), the design cleanly isolates the impact of sampling distribution shifts on value estimation, removing confounding variables like exploration noise, bootstrapping drift, and policy-data feedback loops.
- **Empirical validation of the "dose-response" hypothesis:** The ablation between a fixed edge-biased curriculum and a difficulty-weighted curriculum demonstrates that stronger sampling bias toward underachieved regions yields proportionally larger success gains in those regions (+0.18 edge delta for weighted vs +0.05 for baseline). This directly supports the paper's hypothesis regarding selective data acquisition.
- **Honest and scoped reporting:** The authors transparently report high variance and modest absolute gains (+0.02 overall, +0.08 on edges), and explicitly limit claims to preliminary evidence in simple environments. This cautious tone appropriately bounds the ambitious framing around open-ended learning.

## Weaknesses
- **Core claim of "reduced approximation error" lacks quantitative support:** The abstract, introduction, and discussion repeatedly state that curricula reduce approximation error, yet the results section reports only downstream policy success rates. Without direct metrics on the UVFA's prediction quality (e.g., value MSE, MAE, or residual heatmaps across state-goal regions), the central mechanistic assertion remains inferred rather than measured.
- **Missing environment and curriculum specifications impede reproducibility:** The paper refers to a "GridWorld" but omits critical details: grid dimensions, obstacle configurations, goal space cardinality, and the exact mathematical formulation of the weighted sampling distribution. Without these, it is impossible to verify whether the "edge" difficulty stems from geometric constraints or is an artifact of underspecification, nor can the experiment be faithfully reproduced.
- **Supervised offline setup limits extrapolation to interactive GCRL:** While the use of static, greedy-rollout datasets and supervised regression is a methodologically sound choice for isolating distribution shifts, it fundamentally diverges from interactive reinforcement learning. In real GCRL, policy updates alter the visitation distribution, and bootstrapping/TD introduces different error propagation dynamics than MSE regression on shaped returns. Consequently, the paper's framing of these results as a "pathway toward reliable generalization in GCRL" overextends what the experimental setup can actually demonstrate.
- **Underpowered statistical evaluation and omitted GCRL baselines:** Results are averaged over only three seeds with large standard deviations that substantially overlap between conditions, and no statistical significance tests are provided. Given the small effect sizes, the claim of "consistent improvements" is not rigorously substantiated. Additionally, the absence of comparison to standard GCRL goal-relabeling and adaptive sampling methods (e.g., HER, prioritized replay, or automated curricula) makes it difficult to position this manual approach relative to established solutions to goal sparsity.

## Nice-to-Haves
- Adding formal statistical testing (e.g., bootstrapped confidence intervals or Wilcoxon signed-rank tests) and increasing the random seed count to ≥5 would strengthen the robustness claims.
- Explicitly quantifying the distributional shift between uniform and curriculum sampling using a formal metric (e.g., KL-divergence, Wasserstein distance, or empirical coverage scores) would rigorously ground the "selective data acquisition" terminology.
- Discussing how the proposed sampling strategy could be practically integrated into an active RL loop (e.g., as an offline buffer initializer, a dynamic replay priority, or a periodic curriculum reset) would better bridge the conceptual gap to applied GCRL systems.

## Novel Insights

The paper provides a clear conceptual pivot by stripping curriculum learning down to its data-acquisition function: rather than treating curricula primarily as exploration heuristics or reward-sparcity workarounds, it frames them as structural reweighting mechanisms that align training visitation distributions with the representational bottlenecks of a function approximator. The observation that manually skewing sampling toward hard regions produces a predictable, dose-dependent improvement in localized value estimation suggests that simple, hand-designed sampling biases can act as computationally cheap surrogates for complex, adaptive teacher-student or adversarial systems.
