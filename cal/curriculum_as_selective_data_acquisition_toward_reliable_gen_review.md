# Harsh Critic Review
## Section-by-Section Critical Review

### Title & Abstract
- **Title accuracy:** The title accurately reflects the core premise (curriculum as selective data acquisition in GCRL) but promises a level of generality ("reliable generalization") that the experimental scope does not support.
- **Abstract clarity:** The abstract clearly states the lens (data selection), method (UVFA + PBRS in GridWorld), and direction of results (improved coverage on hard goals). However, it claims that curricula "reduce approximation error," yet the body of the paper never reports or visualizes approximation error (e.g., MSE or L1 validation loss on a held-out test set). This claim is unsupported by the presented results.
- **Claim support:** The abstract implies meaningful gains, but Section 3 and Table 1 show extremely marginal overall improvements (+0.02 overall, +0.08 on edge goals with standard deviations overlapping zero). While directionally consistent, the magnitude and statistical confidence do not match the assertive tone of the abstract for an ICLR venue.

### Introduction & Motivation
- **Motivation & gap:** The motivation to reframe curriculum learning as *distributional data selection* rather than purely an exploration heuristic is conceptually interesting and well-articulated. The connection to Hughes et al. (2024) and open-ended learning (OEL) sets a high-level ambition.
- **Over-claiming:** The introduction heavily positions the work as a stepping stone toward "persistent, open-ended agents." However, the paper only evaluates static, hand-crafted biasing in a deterministic GridWorld with a fixed horizon. The leap from this setup to OEL is speculative and under-justified. The introduction would benefit from tempering claims to align strictly with what is empirically demonstrated: manual resampling can shift visitation distributions and slightly alter value estimates in simple environments.
- **Contribution clarity:** The contributions are clear, but read more like a position/perspective piece paired with a preliminary experiment than a full technical contribution suitable for ICLR. The paper should clarify whether it is primarily making a methodological proposal, an empirical observation, or a theoretical framing.

### Method / Approach
- **Reproducibility & clarity:** The environment, architecture, and hyperparameters (Sections 2.1–2.5) are reasonably specified. However, the data collection protocol raises significant methodological concerns for an RL/GCRL paper. Section 2.5 states: *"For each seed, we roll out 1000 episodes with greedy action selection under PBRS shaping."* Collecting training data exclusively via greedy rollouts on a shaped MDK fundamentally alters the learning regime. The agent will simply follow the potential gradient (Manhattan distance) and produce near-optimal trajectories. This yields a dataset dominated by short, successful paths with minimal exposure to recovery, exploration, or off-policy states. Training a UVFA on such a dataset is closer to supervised value regression or imitation learning than to standard curriculum GCRL.
- **Assumptions & justification:** The use of PBRS with $\phi(s,g) = -d(s,g)$ effectively trivializes the MDP. In deterministic GridWorld, the optimal policy under this potential is known analytically. The curriculum's marginal impact may simply reflect overfitting to slightly longer paths rather than meaningful generalization. The assumption that edge-biased sampling alone will improve UVFA quality overlooks that the bottleneck here is likely *data diversity* and *exploration*, not just goal frequency.
- **Logical gaps & failure modes:** The paper does not address how policy extraction works from the learned $V(s,g)$. Section 2.5 mentions zero-shot evaluation with greedy policies, but without a $Q(s,a,g)$ formulation, model-based planning, or explicit policy optimization (e.g., PPO/DDPG), it is unclear how "policy success" is measured during training vs. evaluation. Are edges simply selected by moving to states with highest predicted value? This needs formalization. Additionally, catastrophic interference or degradation on interior goals is mentioned only briefly in Discussion, with no analysis of trade-offs.

### Experiments & Results
- **Testing the claims:** The experiments do measure success rates under different sampling strategies, and they confirm distributional shifts (Figs 2–3). However, they do not evaluate the *rate* of data collection, sample efficiency, or function approximation quality, despite these being central to the framing.
- **Baselines & ablations:** The study only compares against uniform sampling. In GCRL, Hindsight Experience Replay (HER) is a standard baseline for handling sparse rewards and goal resampling. Even simple variants like future goal replay or ALP-GMM are standard curriculum/GCRL baselines. Their absence makes it impossible to assess whether the observed gains come from the curriculum idea or simply from breaking the uniform sampling baseline known to be pathological in multi-goal settings. No ablations are provided for curriculum strength, shaping coefficients ($\lambda, c$), network capacity, or training epochs.
- **Statistical rigor:** Results average over only 3 seeds. Table 1 and Figures 1–3 report mean ± 1 standard deviation. For edge-goal success, the intervals are $0.060 \pm 0.055$ vs $0.143 \pm 0.083$. These distributions heavily overlap. The paper claims "consistent improvements," but no statistical significance test (e.g., t-test, bootstrap, or Wilcoxon) is provided. Given the small $N$ and high variance, the conclusions are not statistically robust.
- **Metrics & datasets:** Success rate at fixed horizon is appropriate, but the exclusive use of a small, deterministic GridWorld environment limits external validity. More critically, the abstract's claim of "reduced approximation error" is never quantified. No validation MSE, gradient norm analysis, or generalization gap metrics are presented.

### Writing & Clarity
- **Conceptual clarity:** The pipeline from data collection → UVFA regression → policy evaluation is conceptually underspecified. As noted, treating value learning as supervised regression on greedy PBRS trajectories blurs the line between RL planning and function approximation. Readers expecting a standard RL training loop (env interaction, target updates, exploration) will find the method opaque.
- **Figures & Tables:** Figures 1–3 conceptually convey the intended message (distribution shift → targeted improvement). However, the axis labels, legend clarity, and distinction between train vs. eval distributions are not explicitly defined in the captions. Table 1's caption contains parser artifacts ("Pc"), and the reported numbers in the table ($H=16$) slightly mismatch the text ($H=16$ values in Section 3.1). These are likely extraction issues, but they highlight a lack of careful cross-referencing between text and results.

### Limitations & Broader Impact
- **Acknowledged limitations:** Authors appropriately note the simplified environment, manual curriculum design, and modest/occasional inconsistency of gains.
- **Missed fundamental limitations:** The most critical limitation unaddressed is the *data generation process itself*. Because rollouts are greedy under a dense potential function, the collected dataset lacks the off-policy, exploratory, or failure-recovery trajectories necessary for robust UVFA generalization. A curriculum mechanism that cannot influence exploration (since action selection is fixed to greedy PBRS gradients) is inherently capped. Additionally, the connection to OEL remains purely rhetorical; no mechanism for persistence, growth of state-action space, or adaptive difficulty scheduling is evaluated.
- **Societal impact:** Standard AI safety/generalization considerations apply. No unique negative impacts are introduced by this specific framing.

### Overall Assessment
This paper proposes an interesting conceptual reframing: treating curriculum learning as selective data acquisition that shifts the state-goal visitation distribution to improve UVFA coverage. However, in its current form, the submission falls well below ICLR's acceptance bar on methodological rigor, empirical robustness, and technical contribution. The learning regimen (greedy rollouts under PBRS on a deterministic grid) effectively reduces the problem to supervised regression of a known distance function, removing the core RL challenges of exploration, credit assignment, and off-policy learning. The empirical results are marginal (+2% overall, +8% edge success) with highly overlapping error bars over only 3 seeds and no statistical testing, and crucial baselines (e.g., HER, automated curricula) are omitted. Additionally, key claims in the abstract (reduction in approximation error, pathway to OEL) are not substantiated in the experiments or analysis. To meet ICLR standards, the work would require a complete redesign: a proper interactive RL training loop, standard GCRL/curriculum baselines, rigorous statistical validation, ablations on curriculum strength and data quality, and either deeper theoretical grounding or evaluation in more complex environments where function approximation truly bottlenecks learning. As it stands, this reads as an early-stage workshop study rather than a conference-level contribution.

# Neutral Reviewer
## Balanced Review

### Summary
This paper investigates curriculum learning in goal-conditioned reinforcement learning (GCRL) by reframing it as a mechanism for selective data acquisition, where biasing goal sampling toward underachieved regions reshapes the training distribution and improves value function approximation. Using a deterministic GridWorld environment and Universal Value Function Approximators (UVFAs), the authors compare uniform goal sampling against manually edge-weighted and difficulty-weighted curricula. Results show modest aggregate improvements but consistent gains in policy success on harder, peripheral goals, supporting the authors' claim that curricula structurally guide data acquisition rather than merely mitigate exploration or sparsity issues.

### Strengths
1. **Clear Conceptual Framing:** The paper articulates a coherent perspective that curricula function by reshaping the state–goal visitation distribution to prioritize underrepresented or difficult goals, explicitly linking data distribution shifts to function approximation quality.
2. **Transparent Experimental Isolation:** The study design effectively isolates the effect of sampling bias by holding the UVFA architecture, reward function, and dataset size constant across conditions. Hyperparameters are explicitly reported (MLP with 64 hidden units, Adam lr=$10^{-3}$, batch size 256, 50 epochs), facilitating reproducibility of the supervised fitting phase.
3. **Dose-Response Evidence:** The comparison between the baseline curriculum and the weighted curriculum demonstrates a logical amplification of effects (e.g., $\Delta$edge $\approx +0.18$ at $H=16$), empirically validating the hypothesis that stronger redistribution toward harder goals yields greater improvements in those regions.

### Weaknesses
1. **Limited Empirical Scope for ICLR Standards:** The evaluation is restricted to a small, deterministic GridWorld with a shallow MLP. While useful for isolation, ICLR typically expects validation in environments with higher complexity (e.g., obstacle-rich grids, continuous control, or pixel-based observations) to demonstrate that the "selective data acquisition" insight scales beyond trivial function fitting.
2. **Static Dataset & Absence of Online RL Dynamics:** The 1000 trajectories per seed are collected via greedy rollouts and treated as a static offline dataset. This reduces the study to offline supervised value regression rather than capturing the dynamic feedback loop inherent to curriculum learning in RL. Without online policy updates influencing future sampling, the core interactive benefits of curricula in mitigating distributional shift and improving exploration cannot be assessed.
3. **Statistical Fragility:** Results are averaged over only 3 seeds, with high variance relative to means (e.g., Table 1 edge success: $0.143 \pm 0.107$). The reported overall gain is modest ($\Delta +0.021$), and without statistical significance testing (e.g., t-tests or bootstrap confidence intervals), it is difficult to confirm that these improvements are reliable rather than noise.
4. **Reward Shaping Contradicts Sparsity Motivation:** The introduction cites sparse reward challenges and references HER, yet the experiment uses dense Potential-Based Reward Shaping (PBRS) with a terminal bonus. Dense rewards significantly simplify value estimation and policy learning; consequently, the demonstrated marginal benefit of the curriculum may not translate to truly sparse or long-horizon settings where curricula are often claimed to be most critical.
5. **Missing Baselines & Field Positioning:** The paper lacks comparisons to established GCRL and curriculum learning methods (e.g., Hindsight Experience Replay, Automatic Goal Generation, ALP-GMM, or PAIRED). Without these baselines, it is unclear whether the proposed manual edge-weighting offers competitive or complementary performance relative to the current state-of-the-art.

### Novelty & Significance
**Novelty:** Low to Moderate. The intuition that curricula reshape data distributions to focus on hard tasks is established in the GCRL and intrinsic motivation literature. This paper's primary novelty lies in explicitly naming and formalizing this mechanism as "selective data acquisition" and empirically demonstrating the weighting-difficulty relationship. However, it does not introduce a new algorithm, theoretical bound, or architectural advancement.

**Significance:** Currently Low for an ICLR acceptance. While the connection to open-ended learning and the "zone of proximal development" provides compelling high-level motivation, the empirical evidence is preliminary. The combination of a toy environment, static greedy data collection, lack of online RL dynamics, and minimal competitive baselines limits the paper's immediate impact on the broader RL community. To reach ICLR's significance threshold, the work needs to demonstrate that this perspective yields measurable advantages in more realistic or demanding GCRL benchmarks and interacts meaningfully with standard RL training loops.

### Suggestions for Improvement
1. **Incorporate Online RL Learning Dynamics:** Replace or supplement the static offline training with an online RL loop (e.g., PPO, SAC, or DQN) where the curriculum actively influences policy exploration over time. This will allow you to assess whether "selective data acquisition" improves sample efficiency, prevents policy collapse, and sustains long-term learning.
2. **Expand Environments & Add Standard Baselines:** Evaluate on more complex benchmarks (e.g., Minigrid with random walls, AntMaze, or FetchReach). Compare the curriculum approach against standard methods like Hindsight Experience Replay (HER), Automatic Curriculum Learning (e.g., PLR/ALP), or simple adaptive replay strategies to contextualize the practical value of your approach.
3. **Strengthen Statistical Reliability:** Increase the number of random seeds to at least 5–10 and apply statistical significance tests. Report p-values or confidence intervals to determine whether the modest gains on edge goals are robust across variations in initialization and data collection.
4. **Evaluate in Sparse Reward Settings:** Remove PBRS or introduce sparse reward variants to demonstrate where your curriculum provides the most critical advantage. This would directly address the sparsity motivation framed in the introduction and show whether data acquisition compensates for the lack of intermediate signals.
5. **Formalize the Data Selection Perspective:** To elevate the contribution beyond a case study, provide a brief theoretical analysis or empirical scaling law showing how the goal sampling distribution $P(g)$ directly influences UVFA approximation error or generalization bounds. Linking the empirical findings to a principled analysis would significantly strengthen the paper's academic rigor.

# Spark Finder Review
## How to Improve This Paper

### Missing Experiments (top 3-5 only)
1. Add standard GCRL baselines (specifically HER and Reverse Curriculum) because comparing only against naive uniform sampling creates a strawman baseline that undermines the claim that curricula provide meaningful RL gains.
2. Test in at least one continuous or obstacle-rich environment (e.g., MiniGrid or MuJoCo goal-conditioned tasks) because proving "selective data acquisition" improves generalization in deterministic GridWorld with PBRS does not credibly scale to real GCRL challenges.
3. Implement online RL training loops instead of fixed offline datasets because the current setup isolates data sampling from policy improvement, making it impossible to determine whether the reported gains survive standard off-policy updates.

### Deeper Analysis Needed (top 3-5 only)
1. Report explicit value approximation metrics (MSE/L1 across distances) because the core claim that curricula reduce approximation error is entirely asserted without quantitative evidence beyond policy success rates.
2. Perform rigorous statistical significance testing across ≥30 seeds because standard deviations exceed the mean improvement deltas (e.g., ±0.125 on edge gains), meaning the results could easily be seed noise rather than a true method effect.
3. Analyze the bias-variance tradeoff of the sampling weights because the paper acknowledges curricula can hurt easy goals but never quantifies the Pareto frontier, leaving it unclear whether the edge weighting is optimally tuned or just arbitrarily chosen.

### Visualizations & Case Studies
1. Plot 2D value function heatmaps for uniform vs. curriculum models on the same fixed goal because visualizing learned value smoothness and generalization boundaries directly reveals whether curriculum fixes representational holes or merely causes localized overfitting.
2. Provide explicit training data density plots (state-goal histograms or KDEs) instead of bar charts labeled "training distributions" because readers cannot verify the alleged distributional shift without seeing the actual sampling divergence between methods.
3. Show side-by-side trajectory rollouts for matched goals where only one condition succeeds because case studies expose whether the curriculum genuinely improves long-horizon planning or simply biases the agent toward geometrically trivial edge-hugging behaviors.

### Obvious Next Steps
1. Integrate a simple adaptive/automated curriculum mechanism (e.g., coverage-based goal weighting or teacher-student sampling) because hand-tuning edge bias admits the method is a heuristic and fails to support the claim of a generalizable structural principle.
2. Include a capacity and dataset-size ablation because fixed N=1000 and small MLPs risk underfitting uniform training, meaning the curriculum advantage might vanish entirely with larger models or more data.
3. Run a continual/open-ended loop where goals are incrementally uncovered over time because heavily invoking Hughes et al. (2024) on open-ended learning without even a toy demonstration of persistent skill acquisition makes those claims speculative.

# Final Consolidated Review
## Summary
This paper reframes curriculum learning in goal-conditioned reinforcement learning (GCRL) as a mechanism of selective data acquisition that deliberately biases the state-goal visitation distribution toward underachieved goals. Using Universal Value Function Approximators trained on static, greedily-collected trajectories in a deterministic GridWorld with potential-based reward shaping, the authors demonstrate that manual edge-weighting yields modest but dose-responsive improvements in policy success on harder goals compared to uniform sampling.

## Strengths
- **Clear conceptual framing:** The paper effectively articulates a structural perspective of curricula as distributional priors that reshape training data to prioritize underrepresented regions, explicitly linking sampling shifts to targeted improvements in function approximation quality.
- **Transparent experimental isolation & dose-response evidence:** The study design rigorously controls for architecture, hyperparameters, and dataset size, isolating sampling bias as the sole variable. The comparison between baseline and weighted curricula demonstrates a logical amplification effect, empirically validating that stronger redistribution toward difficult goals yields greater targeted gains.

## Weaknesses
- **Misaligned offline/greedy protocol:** Section 2.5 specifies that data is collected exclusively via 1000 episodes of *greedy action selection* under a dense Manhattan potential function. This fundamentally trivializes the deterministic MDP, producing a dataset dominated by near-optimal trajectories with no exposure to exploration, recovery, or off-policy states. Training on this static dataset resembles supervised regression on a known distance function rather than curriculum RL, severing the interactive feedback loop where data acquisition and policy improvement co-evolve.
- **Statistically fragile & unsubstantiated core metrics:** Results are averaged over only three seeds, with reported gains of +0.021 (overall) and +0.083 (edge goals) accompanied by standard deviations (~0.055 and ~0.107) that heavily overlap. No significance tests are provided. Furthermore, the abstract explicitly claims curricula "reduce approximation error," yet the results section never reports MSE, L1 loss, or any direct function approximation error metric, leaving this central quantitative assertion entirely unsupported.
- **Absence of standard GCRL/Curriculum baselines:** The evaluation compares solely against naive uniform sampling. Established methods that directly manipulate goal distributions or relabel experience to handle similar bottlenecks, such as Hindsight Experience Replay (HER, Andrychowicz et al., 2017) or automated curriculum strategies (e.g., ALP-GMM), are absent as baselines. Without these comparisons, it is unclear whether the proposed biasing provides competitive value or merely exploits a strawman baseline.
- **Overextended claims relative to empirical scope:** The introduction and conclusion heavily invoke "reliable generalization" and open-ended learning (citing Hughes et al., 2024), yet experiments are confined to a tiny, discrete grid with fixed, hand-crafted edge biasing. The empirical evidence does not demonstrate persistence, adaptive difficulty scheduling, or scaling to representational bottlenecks, making the leap to open-ended systems purely rhetorical in the current work.

## Nice-to-Haves
- Include explicit validation error plots (MSE or L1 loss vs. goal distance/difficulty) to directly visualize how curriculum alters the approximation landscape.
- Provide 2D value function heatmaps or KDEs of training distributions to visually distinguish whether curriculum repairs global representational holes or induces localized overfitting.
- Add capacity and dataset-size ablations to verify that baseline performance is not artificially capped by underfitting (e.g., confirming that uniform sampling wouldn't match curriculum with a larger network or more data).
- Explore a sparse-reward variant of the environment to test whether selective acquisition compensates for the lack of dense shaping signals, aligning the experiment more closely with the sparsity motivation introduced in Section 1.

## Novel Insights
The paper offers a shift in perspective rather than a new algorithm: by treating curriculum as a structural data acquisition prior that intentionally skews the empirical visitation measure, it highlights how simple distributional biases can systematically patch holes in generalization where uniform sampling starves the approximator. This framing usefully decouples the concept of "curriculum" from heuristic exploration aids, positing it instead as a tunable regularization of the training distribution, though its practical utility currently remains constrained by static, offline data collection and must be integrated into adaptive, interactive loops to realize its proposed connection to open-ended learning.

## Potentially Missed Related Work
- Andrychowicz et al. (2017) **Hindsight Experience Replay** — While cited in the text, it is not used as a baseline; HER effectively performs implicit selective data acquisition via relabeling and is necessary to contextualize whether explicit biasing offers distinct advantages over experience relabeling.
- Forestier et al. (2017) / Graves et al. (2017) **Automatic Curriculum Learning** — Relevant for contrasting manual biasing with algorithmic coverage-based or self-play curricula that dynamically adjust goal difficulty.

## Suggestions
- **Incorporate online RL dynamics:** Replace or supplement the static greedy dataset with an interactive training loop (e.g., DQN or PPO in a goal-conditioned setting) where the sampling distribution updates based on the agent's current capabilities, allowing evaluation of how selective acquisition interacts with policy updates over time.
- **Benchmark against HER and automated curricula:** Re-run the core comparison including Hindsight Experience Replay and at least one automatic curriculum method to determine if the proposed edge-biasing provides complementary or superior gains on harder goal distributions.
- **Substantiate error claims and strengthen statistics:** Add direct function approximation metrics (validation MSE across goal subsets) to the results to support the abstract's claim, increase the number of random seeds to at least 5–10, and report statistical significance tests (e.g., bootstrap CIs or t-tests) to verify robustness.
