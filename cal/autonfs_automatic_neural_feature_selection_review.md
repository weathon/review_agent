# Harsh Critic Review
## Section-by-Section Critical Review

### Title & Abstract
- **Title accuracy:** The title accurately reflects the core contribution: a neural method for automatic feature selection.
- **Abstract clarity:** The abstract clearly states the problem (manual budget specification, computational inefficiency), the method (end-to-end differentiable architecture with Gumbel-Sigmoid and cardinality penalty), and key results (benchmark and metagenomic performance, reduced feature count).
- **Unsupported claims:** The abstract claims "nearly constant computational overhead regardless of input dimensionality." Section 4.3 shows an empirical scaling exponent of $\alpha \approx 0.08$, which is sub-linear but not constant. Furthermore, the masking network's linear projection ($32 \to D$) and $D$-dimensional gradient computations inherently scale as $O(D)$ per batch step, contradicting "nearly constant" in a strict algorithmic sense. Additionally, "automatically determines the minimal set" downplays the heavy reliance on the balance hyperparameter $\lambda$, which the authors fix to 1 despite Appendix F showing strong sensitivity.

### Introduction & Motivation
- **Motivation & Gap:** The motivation is well-grounded in practical FS pain points (manual $k$ tuning, wrapper inefficiency). The gap (neural methods that jointly optimize cardinality and task performance without exhaustive search) is clearly articulated.
- **Contributions:** The listed contributions align with the paper's structure. However, Contribution 2 ("achieves a nearly constant computational overhead") is overstated given the methodological reality (see Method/Experiments below). Contribution 3 is accurate but narrowly scoped to the tested benchmarks.
- **Claim scope:** The introduction appropriately scopes the work to unconstrained tabular data and distinguishes it from dynamic/cost-sensitive acquisition. However, it implicitly suggests superiority over "existing neural FS methods" without acknowledging that Gumbel-Softmax cardinality penalties have been explored in closely related architectures (e.g., STG, Concrete Autoencoders, L0 regularization in neural networks). This risks over-selling novelty relative to established differentiable FS literature.

### Method / Approach
- **Description & Reproducibility:** The method is clearly described: a fixed-size embedding projects to $D$ logits, transformed via temperature-annealed Gumbel-Sigmoid, masked to input $x$, and optimized with a task loss + $\frac{\lambda}{D}\sum m_j$ penalty. Algorithm 1 and Appendix C provide sufficient hyperparameters for reproduction.
- **Assumptions & Justification:** The key assumption that a single global $\lambda=1$ yields optimal cardinality across diverse datasets is stated but unjustified. Different datasets have wildly different signal-to-noise ratios and feature correlations; a fixed penalty weight is unlikely to be universally optimal.
- **Logical Gaps:** 
  1. **Gradient Vanishing:** As $\tau$ decays exponentially (Algorithm 1, $\alpha=0.997$), the Gumbel-Sigmoid becomes effectively binary. The gradients of $\sigma(w/\tau)$ vanish as $\tau \to 0$, risking premature optimization collapse where the mask freezes before the task network converges. The paper frames annealing as a "curriculum" but does not address this well-known numerical stability issue or justify the decay schedule theoretically/empirically.
  2. **Parameter Scaling:** The paper claims near-constant overhead, but the masking network uses a linear layer $\mathbb{R}^{32} \to \mathbb{R}^D$. The weight matrix size grows linearly with $D$, making memory and FLOPs strictly $O(D)$, not $O(1)$. This contradicts the architectural description.
- **Edge Cases & Failure Modes:** The method enforces a global mask, meaning it cannot capture instance-dependent feature importance (unlike INVASE). If features are highly correlated, the Gumbel-Sigmoid will arbitrarily zero out one while keeping another, leading to unstable selection across re-runs. This instability is not discussed. The method also assumes features are identically scaled; raw Gumbel logits may be sensitive to input magnitude without normalization, which isn't explicitly addressed.

### Experiments & Results
- **Claim Testing:** Experiments test predictive performance under feature corruption and on real metagenomic data, which align with claims of robustness and dimensionality reduction. However, the "automatic" nature of the method is not rigorously validated; Appendix F shows cardinality is highly sensitive to $\lambda$, meaning the automation is effectively delegated to a hyperparameter sweep in practice.
- **Baselines:** Baselines follow Cherepanova et al. (2023) and cover classical/regularization methods. **Critical gap:** Highly relevant neural FS methods are missing. STG (Yamada et al., 2020) uses the exact same Gumbel-Softmax relaxation for feature selection. Concrete Autoencoders (Balın et al., 2019) and INVASE (Yoon et al., 2019) are also omitted. Without these, readers cannot isolate whether AutoNFS gains come from architectural design, training schedule, or simply better-tuned MLP downstream.
- **Fairness & Ablations:** All methods are constrained to an MLP downstream. This standardizes comparison but may disadvantage inherently non-gradient methods (RF, XGBoost) while masking whether the task network architecture drives gains rather than the selector. Missing ablations: (1) varying embedding dimensions, (2) temperature schedule sensitivity, (3) comparison of global vs. instance-wise masking.
- **Statistical Rigor:** Figure 4b reports confidence intervals, but main tables (3–5 Table 2) lack variance/std. Statistical significance tests (e.g., Wilcoxon signed-rank, paired t-tests) are absent. Metagenomic results (Table 2) show performance drops on several datasets (e.g., `KeohaneDM` 0.469 → 0.344, `YuJ_2015` 0.653 → 0.417 for MLP), yet only averages are highlighted. This obscures when and why the method degrades.
- **Metrics & Datasets:** OpenML metagenomic datasets are appropriate. However, "misselection error" (Figure 3a) is defined as selecting non-original features. The text notes second-order features carry useful information, yet penalizes the model for selecting them. This metric is conceptually misaligned with the goal of predictive performance and requires clarification or removal.

### Writing & Clarity
- **Confusing sections:** Section 3.2 states the masking network outputs $\mathbf{w} = f_\phi(\mathbf{e})$ but does not clarify that $f_\phi$ is a single linear layer mapping fixed 32D to $D$D. This ambiguity fuels the conflicting claims about computational complexity. The temperature annealing description in Sec 3.4 mentions RL parallels but does not concretely explain how gradient flow is maintained as masks approach discreteness.
- **Figures & Tables:** Figure 1 clearly illustrates the architecture. Figure 2 (rank plot) effectively summarizes benchmark performance. Figure 4 (complexity) uses a log-log fit; however, the caption and text switch between $t \approx D^\alpha$ and Big-O notation without clarifying whether wall-clock time includes fixed GPU initialization overheads, which can artificially flatten low-complexity slopes on log scales. Table 2's bolding convention is clear but lacks error bars, making it difficult to distinguish true improvement from noise.

### Limitations & Broader Impact
- **Acknowledged limitations:** The authors briefly discuss the sparsity-accuracy trade-off and $\lambda$ sensitivity in Appendix F, acknowledging over-sparsification. Reproducibility is addressed via code release.
- **Missed limitations:** The paper does not discuss the known identifiability issues in differentiable FS (multiple feature subsets can achieve near-identical losses). It omits any discussion of how correlated features affect selection stability across random seeds. The claim of scalability ignores that memory consumption for the $D$-dimensional mask and linear weights still scales with dimension.
- **Societal impacts/Failure modes:** The ethics statement is generic. In high-stakes domains (e.g., clinical tabular FS), automatic sparsity can disproportionately remove features correlated with minority groups or socio-economic indicators, inadvertently masking algorithmic bias or violating regulatory transparency requirements. The paper should at least note this risk, as hard feature dropping is not inherently interpretable or fair.

### Overall Assessment
This paper proposes a differentiable feature selection framework that combines Gumbel-Sigmoid masking with a cardinality penalty to automate subset size discovery. The motivation is practical, the implementation is straightforward, and the benchmark results demonstrate competitive predictive performance with reduced dimensionality. However, the contribution falls short of typical ICLR standards in three critical areas: (1) **Novelty & Positioning**: The core mechanism (Gumbel relaxation + L0-style penalty) closely mirrors established methods like STG, Concrete Autoencoders, and Hard-Concrete networks, yet these are omitted from both the main comparisons and ablation studies. (2) **Methodological Claims**: The assertion of "nearly constant computational overhead" contradicts the $O(D)$ linear masking layer and $D$-dimensional gradient computations; empirically, the fit $\alpha \approx 0.08$ reflects sub-linear scaling, not constant time. Furthermore, describing the method as "automatic" overlooks the high sensitivity to $\lambda$, which the authors fix to 1 despite Appendix F showing it heavily dictates final cardinality. (3) **Empirical Rigor**: Main results lack variance reporting and statistical significance tests, and the evaluation does not account for selection instability under feature correlation or gradient vanishing as temperature approaches zero. Without direct comparison to closest neural FS baselines, proper ablations of the annealing/sparsity schedule, and honest framing of the $O(D)$ complexity, the paper in its current form is premature for acceptance. Significant revisions are required to solidify claims, benchmark against relevant contemporaries, and rigorously quantify stability and limitations.

# Neutral Reviewer
## Balanced Review

### Summary
AutoNFS proposes an end-to-end differentiable feature selection framework that jointly optimizes a global feature mask via temperature-controlled Gumbel-Sigmoid sampling and a downstream predictive task network. By incorporating a cardinality penalty on the relaxed mask probabilities alongside an annealing schedule, the method automatically determines the optimal number of features without requiring manual budget tuning. Extensive experiments on OpenML benchmarks (under three corruption scenarios) and high-dimensional metagenomic datasets demonstrate that AutoNFS consistently outperforms classical and neural FS baselines while drastically reducing feature dimensionality and maintaining empirically scalable training times.

### Strengths
1. **Practical and effective automation of feature budget selection:** Unlike traditional methods that require costly hyperparameter sweeps over the number of selected features, AutoNFS introduces a simple yet effective penalty term ($L_{select} = \frac{1}{D}\sum m_j$) that naturally drives the model toward a minimal sufficient subset. The sensitivity analysis of $\lambda$ (Figure 6) clearly demonstrates how the method navigates the accuracy-sparsity trade-off.
2. **Strong and consistent empirical performance across diverse settings:** The evaluation on 11 OpenML datasets under random, corrupted, and second-order feature corruption (Figures 2, 3, Tables 3-5) shows that AutoNFS reliably achieves top average ranks. The misselection error analysis (Figure 3a) confirms that the learned masks correctly recover ground-truth informative features and avoid noisy additions.
3. **Transparent methodology and strong reproducibility practices:** The paper provides a clear algorithmic description (Algorithm 1), explicitly details hyperparameter ranges and optimization via Optuna (Appendix C), and shares the implementation code. This level of transparency aligns well with community standards for reproducible deep learning research.

### Weaknesses
1. **Limited methodological novelty relative to existing differentiable FS literature:** The core mechanism (Gumbel-Sigmoid gates + $L_1$-style penalty on relaxed probabilities) closely mirrors established approaches like Stochastic Gates (STG, Yamada et al., 2020) and Concrete Autoencoders. The contribution primarily lies in the specific architectural choice of a fixed embedding for global masking and the auto-cardinality formulation, which, while practically useful, represents an incremental rather than fundamental advancement.
2. **Overstated computational complexity claims:** The paper asserts "nearly constant computational overhead" based on an empirical power-law fit yielding $\alpha \approx 0.08$ (Figure 4a). However, the forward and backward passes through the linear masking layer and element-wise masking still scale linearly with $D$. Without FLOP/memory analysis or a theoretical derivation, the claim risks misinterpretation; "nearly constant" likely reflects comparison against highly iterative or quadratic baselines (e.g., RFE, wrappers) rather than absolute $O(1)$ behavior.
3. **Absence of statistical significance testing and targeted ablations:** The results report average ranks and point estimates but lack statistical validation (e.g., paired tests, confidence intervals across multiple random seeds). Additionally, there are no ablations isolating the contribution of temperature annealing vs. the $\lambda$ penalty, nor comparisons to other automatic cardinality methods (e.g., adaptive thresholding or Hard-Concrete gates), making it difficult to attribute gains specifically to the proposed design.

### Novelty & Significance
**Novelty:** Moderate. The approach builds on well-understood continuous relaxation techniques for discrete subset selection. The novelty is practical rather than theoretical: combining a single learnable global embedding with a cardinality penalty to eliminate the need for manual $k$-tuning. This is a useful simplification but does not introduce new mathematical frameworks or architectural paradigms.
**Clarity:** High. The paper is well-structured, with clear notation, a step-by-step training algorithm, and intuitive visualizations of the mask evolution (Figure 5) and parameter sensitivity (Figure 6, 7). The narrative logically flows from motivation to method to empirical validation.
**Reproducibility:** High. Hyperparameter ranges, optimization protocol (Optuna), learning rates, temperature schedules, and exact loss formulations are provided. The code repository is linked, and the experimental setup closely follows a recognized benchmark pipeline (Cherepanova et al., 2023), facilitating direct replication and extension.
**Significance:** Solid for applied ML and AutoML. Automating the feature budget selection addresses a real workflow bottleneck, and the method's strong performance on high-dimensional biological data and tabular benchmarks suggests immediate utility. While it may not shift theoretical understanding of FS, it offers a reliable, scalable drop-in component for modern tabular learning pipelines, fitting ICLR's interest in practical, efficient neural methods.

### Suggestions for Improvement
1. **Strengthen empirical rigor with statistical validation and ablations:** Add paired significance tests (e.g., Wilcoxon or paired t-tests) across the dataset rankings to confirm that performance gains are not due to variance. Include an ablation isolating the temperature annealing schedule vs. the $\lambda$ penalty, and compare against alternative sparsity-inducing relaxations (e.g., Hard-Concrete, learned thresholds) to justify the current design.
2. **Refine computational complexity claims and analysis:** Replace "nearly constant" with "empirically sublinear relative to iterative/wrapper baselines" and provide a brief theoretical justification. Supplement the power-law fit with actual FLOP counts, memory footprint scaling, and wall-clock time vs. $D$ on a fixed compute budget to give readers a precise understanding of the efficiency trade-offs.
3. **Position more clearly against prior differentiable FS methods:** Add a direct comparison or detailed discussion contrasting AutoNFS with STG and LassoNet, specifically addressing how the fixed embedding-based global mask differs from sample-wise or hierarchical gating, why this choice improves stability, and under what conditions it might be limiting.
4. **Acknowledge limitations and suggest extensions:** Discuss the assumption of a static, dataset-wide feature mask, which may underperform on heterogeneous data with subgroup-specific relevance. Propose or evaluate a lightweight conditional gating variant (e.g., routing the mask logits through a small hypernetwork conditioned on input statistics) to broaden applicability without sacrificing the automatic cardinality property.

# Spark Finder Review
## How to Improve This Paper

### Missing Experiments (top 3-5 only)
1. **Compare against modern differentiable FS methods (e.g., STG, L2X, INVASE, TabNet).** The paper compares mostly to classical L1/tree selectors, making the "neural end-to-end" advantage unverifiable without direct head-to-head evaluation against methods that also learn continuous masks.
2. **Demonstrate `λ` robustness or per-dataset tuning.** Fixing `λ=1.0` globally invalidates the "automatic cardinality discovery" claim and directly contradicts Figure 6, which shows extreme sensitivity in both accuracy and feature count to this parameter.
3. **Include capacity-controlled ablation of the task network.** The fixed 32×32 bottleneck may artificially force sparsity regardless of the `L_select` penalty; widening the task network without FS is required to prove feature removal isn't just masking model underspecification.

### Deeper Analysis Needed (top 3-5 only)
1. **Report feature-level precision/recall or FDR on synthetic data with known ground-truth relevant features.** Predictive performance ranks only show the downstream model works, not that the *correct* features were actually selected, which weakens the core FS claim.
2. **Quantify mask stability (e.g., Jaccard index or variation of information) across random seeds.** Differentiable feature selectors are notoriously unstable in high dimensions; without variance metrics, the metagenomic feature claims and interpretability conclusions are unreliable.
3. **Provide theoretical derivation or correct the complexity claim.** Empirical fitting to `O(D^0.08)` is mathematically unsound since forward/backward passes through the mask and task network input scale at least linearly with D; the flat empirical curve likely reflects fixed architecture saturation, not algorithmic constant time.

### Visualizations & Case Studies
1. **Visualize mask logit histograms across annealing stages for multiple datasets.** This reveals whether the Gumbel-Sigmoid reliably converges to binary (0/1) masks or gets trapped in soft, near-0.5 states, which would mean no true feature selection actually occurred.
2. **Show high-dimensional failure cases where AutoNFS selects spurious but predictive proxy features.** Exposing scenarios where accuracy remains high but irrelevant/correlated noise is kept would demonstrate the true limits and potential brittleness of the automatic penalty.

### Obvious Next Steps
1. **Detail and equalize the hyperparameter search protocol across all baselines.** Fair ICLR comparison requires identical tuning budgets (trials, compute limits, early stopping) and explicit reporting of selection criteria for every competitor, which is currently absent.
2. **Ablate the Gumbel-Sigmoid relaxation against deterministic alternatives.** Replacing it with a Straight-Through Estimator or hard sigmoid would prove whether the continuous Gumbel noise and temperature schedule are strictly necessary for optimization or incidental.

# Final Consolidated Review
## Summary
AutoNFS introduces an end-to-end differentiable feature selection framework that couples a Gumbel-Sigmoid-based masking network with a downstream predictive task network and an explicit cardinality penalty. By annealing the Gumbel temperature and penalizing the mean mask probability, the method automatically infers the optimal number of features to retain without manual budget tuning. Evaluated on standard OpenML benchmarks under three corruption scenarios and high-dimensional metagenomic datasets, AutoNFS consistently matches or exceeds baseline predictive performance while drastically reducing dimensionality and demonstrating empirically favorable scaling behavior.

## Strengths
- **Practical automation of the feature budget:** Integrating a simple cardinality penalty ($\frac{\lambda}{D}\sum m_j$) into the Gumbel-Sigmoid relaxation successfully eliminates the need to pre-specify or sweep over the number of selected features. The approach provides a clear, gradient-based mechanism for navigating the sparsity-accuracy trade-off.
- **Consistent empirical performance across diverse domains:** AutoNFS achieves top average rankings across 11 datasets under random, Gaussian-corrupted, and second-order feature corruption. The method maintains or improves downstream accuracy on 24 high-dimensional metagenomic datasets while retaining only ~7.7% of original features, demonstrating strong real-world utility.
- **Transparent methodology and high reproducibility:** The training procedure is explicitly algorithmized (Algorithm 1), hyperparameter search ranges and the Optuna tuning protocol are detailed in Appendix C, and the implementation is publicly shared. This level of transparency facilitates direct replication and integration into existing tabular learning pipelines.

## Weaknesses
- **Overstated computational complexity claims:** The abstract and introduction claim "nearly constant computational overhead regardless of input dimensionality," yet the masking network employs a linear projection $\mathbb{R}^{32} \to \mathbb{R}^D$. Both the parameter count and forward/backward gradient computations scale strictly as $O(D)$ in memory and FLOPs. The empirical fit of $t \propto D^{0.08}$ reflects sublinear scaling relative to iterative/wrapper baselines (e.g., RFE) and likely captures GPU saturation effects, not algorithmic $O(1)$ complexity. The theoretical claim contradicts the architectural description and should be corrected.
- **Missing direct comparisons to neural differentiable FS contemporaries:** The core mechanism (Gumbel-Softmax relaxation for continuous mask learning) closely parallels Stochastic Gates (STG, Yamada et al., 2020) and Hard-Concrete networks. While STG is cited in the Related Work, it is omitted from the experimental evaluation. Without a head-to-head comparison against STG or similar continuous-relaxation selectors, it is difficult to verify whether AutoNFS's gains stem from the proposed global embedding design, the cardinality penalty formulation, or simply improved experimental controls relative to classical baselines.
- **Sensitivity to $\lambda$ undermines the "automatic" narrative:** The authors fix $\lambda=1$ globally, stating it yields "satisfactory results across datasets." However, Appendix F demonstrates that $\lambda$ heavily dictates both final cardinality and predictive performance. In practical deployment, automatic cardinality discovery still requires careful tuning of $\lambda$ based on dataset-specific signal-to-noise ratios. The current framing overstates the degree of automation and lacks guidance on how to set $\lambda$ robustly without manual intervention.

## Nice-to-Haves
- **Statistical rigor in benchmark reporting:** Main result tables (Appendix D) report point estimates without variance or significance testing. Adding standard deviations across multiple random seeds and paired statistical tests (e.g., Wilcoxon signed-rank) would strengthen the claim that AutoNFS's rank improvements are consistent rather than noise-driven.
- **Clarification of the misselection metric:** Figure 3a penalizes the selection of second-order features as "misselection errors," yet the text acknowledges these interaction terms may carry more predictive signal than individual originals. Refining or explicitly contextualizing this metric to align with the paper's predictive-performance goal would remove conceptual friction.
- **Selection stability analysis under feature correlation:** Differentiable feature selectors are known to exhibit variance when highly correlated features compete for selection gradients. Reporting mask consistency (e.g., Jaccard index or feature selection variance) across random seeds would provide practical users with a clearer expectation of deployment reliability.
- **Ablation of the temperature schedule:** Isolating the contribution of the annealing curriculum versus a fixed temperature schedule (or alternative relaxations like Straight-Through Estimators) would clarify whether the gradual exploration-exploitation transition is strictly necessary for convergence or an incidental design choice.

## Novel Insights
The paper successfully demonstrates that a minimal, fixed-embedding masking architecture, when paired with an explicit cardinality penalty, can reliably compress high-dimensional tabular data to a task-sufficient subset without manual $k$-tuning. The temperature-annealed Gumbel-Sigmoid functions as an effective continuous-to-discrete curriculum, allowing gradient flow to explore the feature space early in training before committing to sparse, binary selections. This pragmatic formulation bridges the gap between classical sparsity-inducing regularization and modern neural selection, offering a scalable, drop-in component for domains like computational biology where dimensionality reduction directly impacts interpretability and computational feasibility.

## Suggestions
- Reframe computational claims to accurately state that AutoNFS scales sublinearly ($O(D^{0.08})$ empirically) compared to iterative baselines, while acknowledging the algorithmic $O(D)$ cost of the linear masking layer. Provide a brief complexity breakdown distinguishing wall-clock time from parameter/memory scaling.
- Add direct experimental comparisons against Stochastic Gates (STG) and L2X/INVASE. If computational constraints prevent full re-runs, provide a detailed analytical comparison contrasting AutoNFS's fixed global embedding with STG's stochastic gates and LassoNet's hierarchical skip, clarifying architectural trade-offs.
- Address the $\lambda$ sensitivity transparently: either introduce a lightweight adaptive scheme (e.g., $\lambda$ scheduled inversely with validation accuracy gains), or reframe the method as offering a tunable sparsity-accuracy frontier with clear empirical guidelines for $\lambda$ selection across different data regimes.
- Report variance (std/CI) across ≥3 random seeds for all main benchmark tables and include a statistical significance test to validate the average rank improvements over baselines.
