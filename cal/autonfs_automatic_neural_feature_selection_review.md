# Harsh Critic Review
## Section-by-Section Critical Review

### Title & Abstract
- **Title Accuracy:** The title accurately reflects the method (AutoNFS for automatic neural feature selection).
- **Abstract Clarity:** The abstract clearly outlines the problem (high-dimensional tabular FS, need for automatic feature count), the method (Gumbel-Sigmoid masking + task network + cardinality penalty), and key results.
- **Claim Support:** The claim of "nearly constant computational overhead regardless of input dimensionality" is stated in the abstract but is technically inaccurate and not fully supported by the paper's theoretical or empirical analysis. The task network's first layer scales linearly with $D$, and element-wise operations are $O(D)$. This overclaim should be qualified or removed. Additionally, the claim that it "automatically determines the minimal set" is not unique to this work and is shared by other differentiable FS methods, which should be acknowledged to avoid overstating novelty.

### Introduction & Motivation
- **Motivation & Gap:** The motivation is solid: manual specification of feature budget $k$ is a real pain point, and scalable differentiable FS is valuable. The gap is identified, though the characterization that existing embedded methods "neglect feature relationships" is overly broad, as many modern tree-based or attention-based embedded methods capture interactions.
- **Contributions:** Clearly stated. Contribution 2 reiterates the constant complexity claim, which remains problematic. Contribution 3 is standard empirical validation.
- **Balance:** The introduction is well-structured but slightly overclaims uniqueness regarding automatic cardinality discovery. Methods like STG (Stochastic Gates) and L0-regularized networks already optimize sparsity end-to-end without pre-specifying $k$. The introduction should more explicitly position AutoNFS against these immediate predecessors to clarify the actual gap.

### Method / Approach
- **Description & Reproducibility:** The architecture and training loop (Algorithm 1) are generally well-described. However, the parameterization of the masking network is confusing and likely suboptimal. The paper defines $w = f_\phi(e)$ where $e \in \mathbb{R}^{d_e}$ and $f$ outputs $D$ logits. If $f$ is linear, this is equivalent to learning a weight matrix $W \in \mathbb{R}^{D \times d_e}$ and vector $e$, which is a rank-1 factorization of the logits. Learning $D$ independent logits directly is simpler, more efficient, and numerically identical in expressivity. The authors must justify this over-parameterization or simplify it.
- **Assumptions & Logical Gaps:** A critical omission is the handling of gradients as $\tau \to 0$. The Gumbel-Sigmoid becomes a discrete step function at low temperatures, causing gradients to vanish. The standard solution is the Straight-Through Estimator (STE) or Hard-Concrete relaxation. Algorithm 1 and Section 3 do not mention STE. If STE is not used, the mask logits will stop updating late in training, making the "automatic discovery" unreliable. This must be clarified for reproducibility.
- **Edge Cases & Failure Modes:** The method assumes a global mask (static across all samples). This is a strong assumption: it cannot perform instance-wise selection (unlike INVASE) and will struggle if the relevant feature subspace varies across clusters in the data. This limitation is not discussed. Additionally, with strong $\lambda$, the model risks driving all gates to zero; there is no constraint enforcing at least one feature is selected.
- **Theoretical/Complexity:** Section 4.3 claims $O(D^{0.08})$ scaling. Theoretically, computing the mask is $O(D)$ and the first layer of the task network has $D \times H_1$ weights, so complexity is $\Omega(D)$. The empirical fit is likely dominated by constant overheads (data loading, CUDA kernels) in the tested range. Claiming sub-linear or constant scaling is mathematically unsound and must be corrected to avoid misleading readers.

### Experiments & Results
- **Claim Testing:** Experiments test predictive performance and feature reduction across corruption scenarios. They demonstrate automatic $k$ discovery and competitive accuracy.
- **Baselines:** The omission of **STG (Stochastic Gates)** [Yamada et al., 2020] and **L0-Nets/Hard Concrete** is a significant weakness. These methods use continuous relaxations for differentiable feature selection with sparsity penalties and are the most direct technical competitors. Without comparing to STG, it is impossible to know if improvements come from architectural novelty or simply hyperparameter tuning/baseline implementation differences.
- **Ablations:** There is no ablation isolating the Gumbel-Sigmoid vs. simpler relaxations (e.g., standard sigmoid thresholding, deterministic soft gating). Given the masking network redundancy discussed above, an ablation comparing $f_\phi(e)$ vs. direct logit learning is needed to prove architectural choices matter.
- **Statistical Significance:** Tables 3–5 report raw scores and ranks, but lack error bars or statistical tests. Many improvements over baselines are marginal (e.g., accuracy gains of 0.01–0.02). Paired significance testing across datasets or multiple random seeds is required to substantiate "consistently outperforms."
- **Metrics & Datasets:** The misselection metric in Figure 3a is ambiguous. If the method correctly drops irrelevant *original* features, is this penalized? The text suggests any selection outside the original set is acceptable, making the metric definition unclear. The metagenomic results (Table 2) are useful but show modest gains; the paper should discuss whether the selected features align with known biological markers to ground the utility.

### Writing & Clarity
- **Confusing Sections:** The mapping of the "exploration-exploitation" RL analogy in Section 3.4 and Appendix B is strained and adds little value. Temperature annealing in relaxed discrete optimization is a standard curriculum/heuristic, not a reinforcement learning policy. This metaphor should be toned down to avoid distracting from the core optimization mechanics.
- **Figures & Tables:** Figure 4's complexity plots lack clarity on what is being timed (only mask generation? full training step? inference?). Figure 6's $\lambda$ analysis is clear and useful. Tables 3–5 are dense; a summary of statistical significance or median improvement would aid readability.

### Limitations & Broader Impact
- **Acknowledged Limitations:** The paper lacks a formal limitations section. Ethical and reproducibility statements are boilerplate and do not address technical boundaries.
- **Missed Limitations:** Key limitations not discussed include: (1) Inability to handle instance-varying feature relevance due to the global mask assumption; (2) Known instability of L0-type penalties in the presence of highly correlated features (the model will arbitrarily drop collinear variables without preference); (3) Sensitivity to $\lambda$ tuning, which contradicts the "drop-in" framing.
- **Societal Impact:** Feature selection in tabular data can impact fairness (e.g., dropping protected attributes vs. dropping proxies for them). The paper does not discuss how AutoNFS behaves with respect to fairness constraints or demographic biases, which is increasingly expected for FS methods in high-stakes domains.

### Overall Assessment
The paper presents a clean, intuitive approach for automatic feature selection via Gumbel-Sigmoid relaxation and cardinality penality, validating it on established benchmarks and metagenomic data. The motivation is sound, and the empirical coverage is broad. However, the paper currently falls short of the ICLR acceptance bar due to several critical issues. First, the omission of direct baselines like STG and Hard Concrete obscures the actual novelty and relative performance gain. Second, the architectural choice of parameterizing logits via $W e$ is mathematically redundant and inefficient without justification. Third, the lack of explicit discussion on gradient flow at low temperatures (e.g., Straight-Through Estimator) raises reproducibility and optimization stability concerns. Finally, the empirical claim of "constant computational overhead" is theoretically unsound given the linear scaling of the task network's first layer. Addressing these points by adding missing baselines, clarifying optimization mechanics, correcting complexity claims, and including proper statistical analysis would significantly strengthen the contribution and better position it for acceptance.

# Neutral Reviewer
## Balanced Review

### Summary
AutoNFS proposes an end-to-end differentiable feature selection framework that jointly optimizes a stochastic masking network (using Gumbel-Sigmoid relaxation) and a downstream task predictor. By incorporating a simple cardinality penalty into the objective function alongside a temperature annealing schedule, the method automatically discovers both the identity and optimal count of relevant features without requiring manual specification of a feature budget. Extensive experiments across corrupted OpenML benchmarks and real-world metagenomic datasets demonstrate that AutoNFS achieves competitive predictive performance while drastically reducing dimensionality and exhibiting near-constant empirical time scaling with respect to input features.

### Strengths
1. **Practical & Well-Motivated Design:** Directly addresses a common bottleneck in feature selection (manual tuning of the subset size $k$) by framing cardinality discovery as a differentiable optimization problem. The architecture (Section 3) is clean, easy to implement, and seamlessly integrates into standard deep learning pipelines.
2. **Comprehensive Empirical Validation:** Evaluated across 11 OpenML datasets under 3 corruption regimes and 24 real-world metagenomic datasets. AutoNFS consistently ranks at or near the top in average rank (Figure 2) while retaining only a small fraction of original features (Table 1, Table 2), confirming strong utility in high-dimensional biological and tabular domains.
3. **Clear Algorithmic Specification & Reproducibility:** Algorithm 1 provides a step-by-step training procedure with explicit temperature schedules and learning rate separation. Hyperparameter tuning ranges (Appendix C) and an anonymous code repository ensure the work meets standard reproducibility expectations.
4. **Demonstrated Computational Efficiency:** Empirical profiling (Figure 4) shows AutoNFS scales with $\alpha \approx 0.08$ compared to linear/superlinear scaling of classical wrappers and embedded methods, highlighting potential advantages for ultra-high dimensional data where iterative or retraining-based FS is prohibitive.

### Weaknesses
1. **Incremental Technical Novelty:** The core mechanism (Gumbel-Sigmoid mask + $L_1$-style cardinality penalty + temperature annealing) closely mirrors established differentiable selection methods like STG (Yamada et al., 2020), Hard-Concrete (Louizos et al., 2017), and LassoNet (Lemhadri et al., 2021). The paper's main contribution—learning $k$ automatically via a fixed penalty—is a standard sparsification technique rather than a fundamentally new architectural or optimization paradigm. The differentiation from prior work that already handles adaptive sparsity is under-specified.
2. **Lack of Statistical Rigor in Main Results:** Tables 3–5 report single-point metrics without standard deviations, confidence intervals, or cross-run variance. Given the inherent stochasticity of Gumbel noise, random initialization, and neural training, ICLR reviewers typically expect multi-seed averages with variance metrics and paired statistical significance tests to substantiate claims of consistent superiority.
3. **Questionable Universality of Fixed $\lambda=1$:** The claim that $\lambda=1$ works satisfactorily across all datasets is empirically fragile. Cross-entropy and MSE losses operate on vastly different scales, and as Figure 6 shows, performance is highly sensitive to $\lambda$. Without loss normalization, dynamic weighting, or a principled selection strategy for $\lambda$, the method's robustness across heterogeneous tasks remains unclear.
4. **Ambiguity in Complexity Claims:** Figure 4 reports wall-clock time scaling, which conflates algorithmic complexity with implementation efficiency and hardware utilization. While mask generation is $O(D)$, the forward/backward passes through the task network still process $D$-dimensional tensors (masked inputs are not dropped before computation in standard frameworks). A FLOP/memory analysis isolating mask sampling, element-wise operations, and gradient propagation would provide a more rigorous theoretical basis for the near-constant scaling claim.

### Novelty & Significance
The novelty is moderate. AutoNFS effectively packages well-known differentiable relaxation and sparsity-penalty techniques into a cohesive, user-friendly FS module that automates subset cardinality. While it does not introduce a groundbreaking mathematical or architectural innovation, its significance is practical: it delivers a robust, scalable selector that bypasses manual budget tuning and shows strong empirical performance on challenging real-world data. If the computational claims hold under strict profiling and the method proves robust to reasonable hyperparameter shifts, it can serve as a highly useful, drop-in component for tabular deep learning and bioinformatics workflows.

### Suggestions for Improvement
1. **Add Multi-Seed Variance & Statistical Testing:** Report mean $\pm$ standard deviation across at least 3-5 random seeds for all main benchmarks (Tables 3-5). Include paired statistical tests (e.g., Wilcoxon signed-rank or bootstrap CIs) to formally validate rank/accuracy improvements over baselines.
2. **Clarify & Substantiate Complexity Claims:** Separate empirical wall-clock time from theoretical computational complexity. Provide a FOPs or memory footprint analysis that explicitly accounts for backward pass overhead through $D$-dimensional masked inputs, and compare total compute (including $\lambda$ tuning time) against baselines to ensure fair scaling assessment.
3. **Address $\lambda$ Sensitivity Systematically:** Introduce a loss-scale normalization strategy (e.g., relative weighting $\lambda \cdot \frac{L_{select}}{E[L_{task}]}$ or adaptive scheduling) and provide an ablation demonstrating robustness across regression vs. classification tasks. Clearly state when $\lambda=1$ is insufficient and how practitioners should calibrate it.
4. **Deepen Baseline Differentiation:** Explicitly compare AutoNFS's automatic cardinality mechanism against adaptive sparsity in LassoNet and STG. Discuss why gradient-based mask optimization with a fixed cardinality penalty outperforms hierarchical constraints or reparameterized sparsity in the evaluated regimes, grounding the empirical advantage in optimization dynamics rather than just reporting better ranks.

# Spark Finder Review
## How to Improve This Paper

### Missing Experiments (top 3-5 only)
1. **$\lambda$ sensitivity analysis vs. "automatic" claim:** You claim $\lambda=1$ works universally, yet Appendix F shows sharp accuracy degradation when $\lambda$ deviates slightly. Provide a cross-dataset sensitivity sweep or principled tuning heuristic; otherwise, the core claim that AutoNFS "automatically determines the minimal set" is unconvincing and relies on manual trial-and-error.
2. **Predictor capacity-controlled baselines:** The task network uses a deliberately narrow architecture (32-32-32), while baselines like XGBoost or RF inherently possess much higher capacity. Retrain baselines using an identical fixed MLP; without this, superior ranking likely reflects architectural bias rather than genuine feature selection quality.
3. **Statistical variance across seeds:** Tables 3–5 report point estimates for rankings without standard deviations. Given the high-variance Gumbel-Sigmoid sampling, report mean/std over at least 10 random seeds and apply statistical significance tests (e.g., Wilcoxon signed-rank); ICLR requires proof that gains are consistent, not initialization-dependent.

### Deeper Analysis Needed (top 3-5 only)
1. **Synergistic/interaction feature recovery:** Applying a global element-wise mask before the network struggles to retain weak features that are only relevant in combination. Analyze performance on synthetic benchmarks with known XOR/multiplicative interactions; if AutoNFS prunes combinatorial features, the "sufficient minimal set" claim is mathematically overreaching.
2. **Annealing schedule vs. gradient vanishing:** Gumbel-Softmax gradients decay exponentially as $\tau \to 0$. Quantify the gradient norm magnitude vs. epoch and show the chosen decay rate doesn't prematurely freeze the mask topology before the task network has converged to a useful basin.
3. **Explicit Pareto characterization:** The method doesn't discover the "true" $k$; it finds the sparsity-accuracy optimum for a given $\lambda$. Plot and discuss the Pareto frontier of cardinality vs. test error to clarify whether AutoNFS adaptively scales to dataset complexity or merely enforces a fixed inductive bias toward aggressiveness.

### Visualizations & Case Studies
1. **Ground-truth mask alignment:** On simulated data with known ground-truth relevant features, plot the learned logits against actual coefficients (e.g., via ROC/AUC of the mask over ground truth). This directly tests if the mask recovers true relevance or simply memorizes high-marginal features that trick the small predictor.
2. **$\lambda$-sweep Pareto vs. fixed-$k$ curves:** Overlay AutoNFS performance across a continuous $\lambda$ sweep against points from baselines that require fixed $k$ selection. This reveals whether the auto-cardinality mechanism provides a genuinely superior trade-off curve or just interpolates existing baseline performance.
3. **Failure mode case study:** Visualize a dataset where AutoNFS catastrophically over-prunes (e.g., dropping a weak predictive feature due to high initial noise or correlation with a redundant proxy). Diagnosing and showing failure boundaries is essential for reviewers to trust the method's reliability in production.

### Obvious Next Steps
1. **Inference latency benchmarking:** You emphasize scalability but only measure training wall-clock time vs. $D$. Add end-to-end inference latency comparisons against baselines to prove that the global mask filtering actually yields the claimed deployment speedup in practice.
2. **Adaptive penalty scheduling:** Replace the static $\lambda$ with a curriculum or adaptive weight that scales proportionally to the task loss gradient. This would operationalize the "automatic" claim by removing manual $\lambda$ selection and align it with the existing temperature annealing schedule.
3. **Task-network bottleneck ablation:** The 32-dim bottleneck may force the model to select only the strongest marginal features while ignoring subtle signals. Run an ablation scaling the predictor width/depth to demonstrate the selected mask remains robust and informative for stronger downstream models, not just the specific weak MLP used.

# Final Consolidated Review
## Summary
AutoNFS proposes an end-to-end differentiable feature selection framework that combines a Gumbel-Sigmoid masking network with a downstream task predictor, using a cardinality penalty and temperature annealing to automatically determine both the identity and count of relevant features. The method is evaluated across 11 OpenML datasets under three corruption regimes and 24 metagenomic datasets, reporting strong predictive rankings, drastic dimensionality reduction, and empirical near-constant time scaling.

## Strengths
- **Practical Pipeline Design:** The formulation directly addresses the manual tuning bottleneck of feature budget $k$ by integrating cardinality regularization into the optimization loop. The architecture and training procedure (Algorithm 1) are cleanly specified, with open-source code and explicit hyperparameter ranges facilitating straightforward adoption.
- **Broad Empirical Validation:** Consistent top-tier performance across 35 diverse datasets, spanning synthetic corruption scenarios and high-dimensional biological data, demonstrates that aggressive automated sparsification is broadly viable without catastrophic accuracy loss.

## Weaknesses
- **Unsubstantiated Novelty & Omitted Direct Baselines:** The core mechanism (differentiable gate relaxation + sparsity penalty) is functionally equivalent to established approaches like Stochastic Gates (STG), Hard-Concrete networks, and Concrete Autoencoders. Critically, while STG is cited in the related work, it is entirely excluded from the experimental baselines (Appendix C). Without a direct, equivalently tuned comparison, it is impossible to attribute the reported rank improvements to architectural contributions rather than implementation details, baseline misconfiguration, or favorable hyperparameter search.
- **The "Automatic" Cardinality Claim is Undermined by $\lambda$ Sensitivity:** The paper positions itself as eliminating manual feature budget specification, yet relies on a fixed cardinality penalty weight $\lambda=1$ across all tasks. Classification (cross-entropy) and regression (MSE) operate on fundamentally different loss scales, making a universal $\lambda$ mathematically unsound. Appendix F demonstrates sharp performance degradation with minor $\lambda$ deviations. In practice, the method merely shifts the manual hyperparameter search from subset size $k$ to penalty strength $\lambda$, directly contradicting the primary value proposition.
- **Theoretically Unsound Complexity Claims:** Section 4.3 reports an empirical time scaling of $O(D^{0.08})$ and markets it as "nearly constant computational overhead." This is theoretically impossible: computing logits, sampling Gumbel-Sigmoid masks, and the forward/backward pass through the task network's input layer all impose $\Omega(D)$ complexity. The sub-linear empirical fit is an artifact of constant overheads (data loading, framework initialization, GPU kernel dispatch) dominating in the tested feature range. Misrepresenting empirical wall-clock times as algorithmic complexity misleads readers regarding scalability guarantees.
- **Methodological Ambiguities & Statistical Fragility:** The masking network's parameterization $w = f_\phi(e)$ with a learnable low-dimensional embedding $e \in \mathbb{R}^{32}$ introduces unnecessary over-parameterization without expressivity justification. More critically, the paper lacks discussion on gradient behavior as temperature $\tau \to 0$ (e.g., vanishing gradients or Straight-Through Estimator usage), raising stability concerns. Furthermore, all main results (Tables 3–5) are single-point estimates. Given the high variance inherent to Gumbel-Sigmoid sampling, neural initialization, and tabular splits, the absence of multi-seed variance, confidence intervals, or paired significance tests renders the "consistently outperforms" narrative statistically untenable for ICLR.

## Nice-to-Haves
- Investigate and explicitly document the global mask limitation: clarify how the static feature subset assumption impacts performance on tasks with heterogeneous, instance-dependent feature relevance (e.g., contrasting with INVASE).
- Provide a Pareto frontier analysis (accuracy vs. number of features) across a continuous $\lambda$ sweep to objectively characterize the sparsity-accuracy trade-off rather than relying on isolated operating points.

## Novel Insights
The work effectively assembles known differentiable relaxation primitives into a cohesive feature selection module, but its empirical framing obscures a fundamental optimization reality: replacing discrete combinatorial search with a differentiable cardinality penalty does not "automatically" discover the optimal feature budget; it merely smooths the landscape for a fixed inductive bias toward sparsity. The method's consistent success across domains suggests that for many tabular problems, aggressive dimensionality reduction is highly beneficial, but this appears driven primarily by the regularization schedule rather than a novel property of the Gumbel-Sigmoid gating mechanism. Decoupling the penalty dynamics from the stochastic relaxation would be necessary to isolate the true contribution of the proposed formulation.

## Potentially Missed Related Work
- Yamada et al. (2020a) "Feature selection using stochastic gates" — cited in the text but entirely omitted from experimental comparisons; essential for establishing relative gains in differentiable subset selection.
- Balin et al. (2019) "Concrete Autoencoders: Differentiable feature selection and reconstruction" — directly relevant to automatic cardinality discovery via continuous relaxations and should be contrasted with AutoNFS's penalty-driven approach.

## Suggestions
1. Include STG, Hard-Concrete, and L0-Network baselines under identical architecture and hyperparameter search protocols to rigorously establish incremental contribution and rule out tuning artifacts.
2. Replace the fixed $\lambda=1$ claim with a principled loss-normalization strategy or adaptive penalty scheduling. Report all main results as mean ± standard deviation across ≥5 random seeds, accompanied by non-parametric statistical significance tests (e.g., Wilcoxon signed-rank).
3. Correct the complexity analysis to reflect the theoretical $\Omega(D)$ lower bound, explicitly separating algorithmic scaling from empirical wall-clock overheads. Clarify gradient stabilization techniques (or STE adoption) used during temperature annealing to ensure reproducibility.
