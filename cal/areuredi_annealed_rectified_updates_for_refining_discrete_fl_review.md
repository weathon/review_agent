# Harsh Critic Review
## Section-by-Section Critical Review

**Title & Abstract**
The title accurately reflects the method's components, and the abstract clearly states the problem and contributions. However, the claim of "theoretical guarantees of convergence to the Pareto front" is too strong for the abstract given the experimental implementation. The main text explicitly introduces a monotonicity sampling constraint (Section 4) that alters the underlying MCMC dynamics, which means the *actual* algorithm run in experiments does not satisfy the theoretical assumptions. The abstract should either temper the guarantee claim or explicitly note that experiments use a practical constraint that deviates from the theoretical formulation.

**Introduction & Preliminaries (Sections 1–2)**
The motivation is well-grounded in biomolecular engineering, and the gap in discrete-space multi-objective optimization is clearly articulated. The transition from continuous flows to ReDi is logical. The preliminaries concisely define discrete flow matching, conditional total correlation (TC), and the ReDi rectification update. One minor conceptual clarification: ReDi's TC reduction is presented as a monotonically decreasing guarantee, but Appendix B (Table 4) shows TC *increases* after the first rectification round before decreasing. The authors attribute this to distributional shift from model-generated couplings, but this warrants a brief theoretical or empirical discussion in Section 2 to reconcile the provable guarantee with the observed behavior.

**Method & Theoretical Guarantees (Section 3 & Appendix A)**
The core methodology (annealed Tchebycheff scalarization, locally balanced proposals, MH updates) is mathematically sound and builds correctly on established reversible MCMC literature. The target distribution $\pi_{\eta,\omega}(x) \propto p_1(x) \exp(\eta S_\omega(x))$ and the detailed balance proof (Appendix A.2, Step 1) are correct under fixed $\eta$. However, several critical gaps undermine the theoretical contribution as presented for ICLR:
1. **Non-Stationary Target Distribution**: The annealing schedule (Eq. 3) makes $\eta_t$ increase over time. Standard MH invariance (Appendix A) assumes a fixed target. A non-stationary chain with finite steps does not inherit the stationary distribution guarantees unless mixing is infinitely fast at each step. The paper should explicitly address how annealing impacts the convergence claim, likely by reframing it as an asymptotic statement or acknowledging a theory-practice gap.
2. **Coverage vs. Single-Run Design**: The Coverage Theorem (Appendix A) requires sampling $\omega$ from a full-support distribution over the simplex. However, the main experiments use fixed, equal weights (Appendix F: "applied the same weight for each objective"). A single run with fixed $\omega$ can only target *one* scalarized optimum, not the full Pareto front. The claim of "full coverage" is thus not demonstrated empirically and needs explicit procedural clarification (e.g., multiple runs with varied $\omega$, or explicit weight scheduling).
3. **Monotonicity Constraint Contradiction**: Section 4 states: "we introduce a monotonicity constraint that accepts only token updates that increase the weighted sum of the current objective scores... This monotonicity constraint was involved in all the following experiments." This overrides the MH acceptance probability (Eq. 5), explicitly breaking reversibility and detailed balance. Consequently, Theorem 1 (Invariance) and the Pareto concentration proof do not apply to the algorithm actually evaluated. The authors must either: (a) remove the constraint to align with theory, (b) remove/adjust the theoretical claims, or (c) formally analyze how the constraint affects convergence and mixing.

**Experiments & Results (Section 4 & Appendices C–G)**
The experimental design covers relevant biomolecular tasks and includes useful ablations, but several methodological choices fall short of ICLR standards for rigorous generative evaluation:
1. **Surrogate Model Reliability**: The half-life predictor is trained on only 105 unique entries after filtering (Appendix E.3). While transfer learning from a larger stability dataset helps, optimizing a generative model against such a narrow, high-variance surrogate risks adversarial overfitting to surrogate artifacts rather than true biochemical properties. The hemolysis/non-fouling/solubility classifiers achieve validation F1 scores of 0.58, 0.71, and 0.68 (Appendix E.1), which are modest for guiding de novo design. The paper needs a robustness check (e.g., Oracle cross-validation, perturbation analysis, or external validation) to show that AReUReDi isn't simply exploiting surrogate noise.
2. **Missing Standard MOO Metrics**: Table 1 reports *average* scores across 100 samples. In multi-objective optimization, front quality is measured by metrics like Hypervolume, Inverted Generational Distance (IGD), or Spread. Averages obscure front geometry, trade-off density, and dominance relationships. Without these, claims of "balancing trade-offs" and "superior Pareto trade-offs" remain under-supported.
3. **Algorithm Initialization Inconsistency**: Algorithm 1 (Line 4) initializes $x_0$ uniformly at random. However, Section 4.3 states that for SMILES generation, "initial sequences $x_0$ are sampled from the pre-trained SMILESReDi$^{[1]}$". This discrepancy should be resolved in the algorithm pseudocode or methodology text.
4. **Objective Mismatch in Constraint**: The method optimizes the Tchebycheff scalarization $S_\omega(x) = \min_n \omega_n \tilde{s}_n(x)$, but the practical monotonicity constraint (Section 4) accepts updates only if they increase the *weighted sum* of objectives. These are fundamentally different optimization landscapes. The constraint may inadvertently reject valid Tchebycheff-improving moves or accept Pareto-dominating moves that hurt the bottleneck objective, complicating interpretation of the results.
5. **Computational Efficiency**: Table 2 shows AReUReDi requires ~50–200 seconds per binder, vs. ~2–37s for baselines. The wall-clock matching in Table 11 mitigates this somewhat, but the method's heavy per-token proposal evaluation limits scalability to longer sequences (e.g., antibodies or full proteins). A discussion on algorithmic complexity and potential acceleration (e.g., parallel coordinate updates, learned proposal caching) is needed.

**Limitations & Discussion (Section 6)**
The limitations section is overly brief. It acknowledges computational cost and future modalities but omits critical failure modes: surrogate dependency, the monotonicity constraint's impact on theory, poor mixing in rugged landscapes, and lack of MOO front metrics. ICLR expects a candid discussion of where the method breaks down and how the theoretical guarantees degrade under practical constraints. Expanding this section would significantly strengthen the manuscript.

### Overall Assessment
AReUReDi presents a conceptually elegant integration of rectified discrete flows with MCMC-based multi-objective guidance, addressing a meaningful gap in discrete biomolecular design. The methodological building blocks (locally balanced proposals, Tchebycheff scalarization, MH updates) are well-motivated and the ablation studies thoughtfully probe key design choices. However, the paper currently suffers from a significant theory-practice disconnect: the empirical implementation uses a monotonicity constraint and annealed $\eta_t$ that invalidate the stated invariance and convergence guarantees. Additionally, the reliance on weak surrogate models (particularly the 105-sample half-life dataset) and the absence of standard Pareto front evaluation metrics (Hypervolume, IGD) weaken the empirical claims. To meet ICLR's acceptance bar, the authors must (1) reconcile or explicitly bound the gap between the theoretical MCMC formulation and the constrained sampling used in experiments, (2) replace/augment average-score reporting with rigorous MOO front metrics, and (3) address surrogate robustness to ensure optimized sequences reflect genuine biochemical trade-offs rather than model artifacts. With these revisions, the work would form a strong, theoretically grounded contribution to discrete generative modeling for scientific design.

# Neutral Reviewer
## Balanced Review

### Summary
This paper introduces AReUReDi, a multi-objective optimization framework that extends Rectified Discrete Flows (ReDi) to generate discrete biological sequences satisfying multiple competing objectives. By integrating annealed Tchebycheff scalarization, locally balanced proposals, and Metropolis-Hastings updates, the method steers sampling toward the Pareto front while theoretically preserving the generative prior's distributional invariance. Extensive experiments on wild-type peptide and chemically modified SMILES generation demonstrate that AReUReDi achieves superior multi-property trade-offs compared to evolutionary MOO baselines and recent discrete diffusion models.

### Strengths
1. **Rigorous Theoretical Guarantees:** Appendix A provides clear, standard MCMC-based proofs for distributional invariance, asymptotic convergence to the Pareto front as $\eta \to \infty$, and full front coverage under weight randomization. This establishes a strong formal foundation rarely seen in applied discrete generative modeling.
2. **Systematic and Multi-faceted Empirical Validation:** The paper evaluates across diverse tasks (8 protein targets for peptides, 5 for SMILES) with up to 5 simultaneous objectives. Results consistently show improved trade-offs over classical MOO algorithms (NSGA-III, SMS-EMOA, etc.) and recent diffusion baselines (PepTune). Tables 7, 8, 13, and 14 thoroughly validate objective targeting and weight sensitivity.
3. **Principled Integration of Prior and Guidance:** The locally balanced proposal mechanism (Section 3.3) elegantly blends the ReDi marginal prior with multi-objective guidance. Using balancing functions like Barker's to guarantee reversibility is mathematically sound, and the time-dependent bond-aware noising schedule for SMILES (Appendix C.2) demonstrates thoughtful adaptation to domain-specific constraints.

### Weaknesses
1. **Practical Deviation from Theoretical Guarantees:** Section 4 explicitly states that all main experiments use a "monotonicity constraint" that only accepts token updates increasing the weighted objective sum (supported by Table 6). This greedy acceptance rule explicitly breaks detailed balance and invalidates the stated invariance and convergence proofs in the practical regime. The paper does not adequately reconcile or analyze this discrepancy.
2. **Dependence on Moderate-Accuracy Surrogate Models:** The optimization entirely relies on pre-trained property predictors with modest validation metrics (e.g., XGBoost hemolysis F1=0.58, affinity predictor Spearman=0.64; Appendix E). Without robustness checks or uncertainty quantification, it is unclear whether reported Pareto improvements reflect true biological superiority or artifacts of surrogate optimization (reward hacking/over-optimization on noisy predictors).
3. **Limited Core Algorithmic Novelty:** While the application to discrete flows is new, the optimization backbone (Tchebycheff scalarization + locally balanced proposals + Metropolis-Hastings) is a well-established MCMC paradigm. The paper could better articulate what is fundamentally novel beyond composing these known techniques with ReDi, particularly compared to existing guided MCMC or diffusion guidance literature.

### Novelty & Significance
**Novelty:** Moderate-High. The core contribution lies in the principled adaptation of classical guided MCMC techniques to the discrete flow matching setting, with specific proposals leveraging ReDi's rectified marginals. While the optimization components themselves are established, their integration, theoretical packaging for discrete token spaces, and application to biomolecular design represent a meaningful advance.
**Significance:** High. Multi-objective discrete generation is a critical bottleneck in AI for science and computational biology. Delivering a method that outperforms strong baselines while providing convergence guarantees addresses a clear community need. The work is highly relevant to ICLR, particularly for researchers in discrete generative models, constrained optimization, and scientific machine learning.
**Clarity:** High. The paper is well-structured, notation is consistent, and the methodology is cleanly separated from theory and experiments.
**Reproducibility:** High. Detailed training configurations, hyperparameters, dataset sources, and sampling procedures are provided. The promised code release will solidify reproducibility, though reliance on custom surrogate scoring models may require additional documentation for exact score replication.

### Suggestions for Improvement
1. **Reconcile Theory and Practice Regarding the Monotonicity Constraint:** Clearly separate the theoretical algorithm (pure MH updates) from the practical variant (monotonic constraint) in both text and Algorithm 1. Provide a dedicated ablation or theoretical note analyzing how the constraint impacts mixing rates, diversity, and Pareto coverage. Consider framing it explicitly as a heuristic acceleration that trades asymptotic guarantees for finite-step efficiency, and report results where the constraint is *disabled* to verify theoretical behavior.
2. **Add Surrogate Robustness & Uncertainty Analysis:** Acknowledge the moderate accuracy of the scoring predictors. Include a sensitivity analysis showing how AReUReDi's Pareto performance degrades under perturbed or noisy objective evaluations. Ideally, incorporate a lightweight uncertainty estimation (e.g., ensemble variance or predictive variance weighting) to downweight unreliable objectives during sampling.
3. **Sharpen Novelty Positioning vs. Guided MCMC Literature:** In Section 5 and the introduction, explicitly contrast AReUReDi with prior guided MCMC/diffusion methods. Highlight discrete-flow-specific advantages (e.g., how ReDi's reduced conditional total correlation yields higher-quality proposal distributions compared to standard factorized priors, or how the discrete topology changes LBP design).
4. **Expand Compute-Quality Trade-off Discussion:** Table 11 offers a brief matched wall-clock comparison. Expand this into a systematic analysis: plot property scores vs. wall-clock time across baselines, discuss the computational bottleneck (re-evaluating all candidates vs. top-p pruning), and propose scalable approximations (e.g., stochastic coordinate updates, surrogate-accelerated proposals) to make the method more practical for longer sequences like proteins or RNA.

# Final Consolidated Review
## Summary
AReUReDi introduces a multi-objective framework for discrete biological sequence generation by integrating Rectified Discrete Flows (ReDi) with Metropolis-Hastings sampling. The method combines annealed Tchebycheff scalarization and locally balanced proposals to steer discrete flows toward the Pareto front, claims formal convergence guarantees, and demonstrates improved property trade-offs on peptide and chemically-modified SMILES design tasks across multiple objectives.

## Strengths
- **Principled adaptation of guided MCMC to discrete flows:** The integration of locally balanced proposals with ReDi's rectified marginals is mathematically sound and directly addresses the challenge of navigating high-dimensional discrete spaces without continuous relaxations or token-level embedding distortions.
- **Comprehensive empirical validation and ablations:** The evaluation spans 8 peptide targets and 5 SMILES targets with up to 5 simultaneous objectives. The ablation studies on rectification rounds, guidance annealing, and weight vectors (Tables 7-10, 13-14) provide strong evidence that the method responds predictably to objective trade-offs and that ReDi's reduced conditional total correlation meaningfully improves proposal quality.
- **Formal theoretical framing:** Appendix A provides clear, standard proofs for distributional invariance, asymptotic Pareto concentration as $\eta \to \infty$, and front coverage under weight randomization. This establishes a rigorous baseline for discrete guided sampling rarely matched in applied generative modeling.

## Weaknesses
- **Theory-practice disconnect via the monotonicity constraint:** The paper explicitly states that all empirical experiments use a monotonicity constraint that "accepts only token updates that increase the weighted sum of the current objective scores" (Section 4). This greedy rule explicitly breaks the reversibility and detailed balance required for the Invariance and Convergence theorems in Appendix A. Furthermore, the constraint optimizes a weighted *sum* landscape, while the proposal mechanism and target distribution are derived from a Tchebycheff ($\min_n$) scalarization. This mismatch means the evaluated algorithm operates under fundamentally different dynamics than the theoretical guarantees claim, undermining the core argument for provable Pareto convergence in practice.
- **Absence of standard multi-objective evaluation metrics:** Claims of "superior trade-off navigation" and "Pareto optimality" are supported exclusively by average scalar scores across 100 samples (e.g., Table 1). In multi-objective optimization, averages obscure front geometry, dominance relationships, sparsity, and mode collapse. Without standard metrics like Hypervolume, Inverted Generational Distance (IGD), or Pareto front density analyses, it is impossible to rigorously determine whether AReUReDi genuinely outperforms baselines in front approximation or merely shifts marginal means upward.
- **High sensitivity to low-fidelity surrogate models:** The optimization relies on pre-trained property predictors with modest validation metrics (hemolysis F1 ~0.58, half-life trained on only 105 unique entries after filtering). Optimizing an MCMC sampler against such noisy, low-capacity surrogates carries a high risk of Goodhart's law, where reported gains reflect exploitation of predictor artifacts rather than genuine biochemical improvements. The paper lacks robustness checks (e.g., external cross-validation, predictor ensemble averaging, or perturbation analysis) to confirm that the generated sequences generalize beyond the specific scoring models used for training.

## Nice-to-Haves
- Clarify the initialization step in Algorithm 1 (currently uniform random) to match the SMILES experiments, which initialize $x_0$ from a pre-trained SMILESReDi model.
- Provide 2D scatter plots of key objective pairs (e.g., Affinity vs. Half-Life) with baseline outputs overlaid to visually substantiate trade-off navigation claims.
- Discuss the theoretical impact of top-$p$ candidate pruning on the proposal symmetry and stationary distribution, or explicitly frame it as a finite-computation approximation with bounded bias.
- Expand the limitations section to candidly address the impact of the monotonicity constraint on mixing rates and front coverage, and clarify how the method degrades on highly rugged or noisy objective landscapes.

## Novel Insights
Beyond the direct contributions, the paper highlights an important structural insight: reducing factorization error via rectification (lowering conditional total correlation) is not merely an efficiency trick for discrete generation, but a prerequisite for effective high-dimensional discrete optimization. When inter-dimensional dependencies are poorly calibrated, gradient-free guidance signals (like locally balanced proposals) fail to propagate coherently across sequence positions, leading to mode collapse or surrogate exploitation. By using ReDi to straighten discrete probability paths and minimize TC, AReUReDi effectively provides a well-conditioned prior that acts as a stable reference measure for MCMC steering. This suggests that future discrete optimization methods should prioritize prior calibration and transport cost reduction alongside guidance mechanisms, particularly in combinatorial scientific domains where local token moves must respect global structural constraints.

## Suggestions
- **Explicitly decouple theory from practice in the methodology and algorithm pseudocode:** Present the "pure" MH algorithm as the theoretically grounded variant, and the monotonicity-constrained version as a practical heuristic. Add a brief analysis or ablation showing mixing behavior and front coverage *without* the constraint to empirically validate the asymptotic claims and quantify the trade-off between finite-step efficiency and theoretical guarantees.
- **Incorporate standard Pareto front metrics:** Compute and report Hypervolume or IGD for at least one primary wild-type task. This will replace ambiguous average-score comparisons with standardized quantification of front quality, dominance, and spread.
- **Add surrogate robustness analysis:** Run a sensitivity experiment where scoring models are perturbed with noise, or evaluate a subset of top AReUReDi designs using an independent, externally trained predictor architecture. This will strengthen confidence that the optimized properties generalize beyond the specific training artifacts of the self-developed scoring pipelines.
