# Harsh Critic Review
## Section-by-Section Critical Review

### Title & Abstract
The title accurately reflects the core premise. The abstract clearly states the motivation (annotation bottleneck, poor OOD generalization), the proposed solution (W2S learning with diverse teachers and iterative training), and the claimed outcomes (SOTA on in-domain and OOD). However, the abstract claims the method "obviates the need for human-annotated labels" while surpassing human-labeled baselines. This is a strong claim that requires the main text to rigorously disentangle whether the gains stem from the W2S paradigm itself, or simply from scaling data and using a significantly larger backbone. The abstract should more precisely frame the contribution as a *data-efficient, label-free alternative* rather than an unqualified "surpassing" claim without mentioning capacity scaling.

### Introduction & Motivation
The motivation is well-grounded: human annotation is costly, OOD performance is weak, and prior self-supervised methods struggle with authentic distortions and high-level perception. However, **there is a logical tension between the critique of prior work and the proposed solution**. In the third paragraph, the authors criticize self-supervised VQA for failing to "capture high-level visual content and aesthetic characteristics" and for inadequately modeling "authentic distortion patterns." Yet, the proposed method relies heavily on *synthetic distortion simulators* (spatial, temporal, streaming) to create heterogeneous supervision. These simulators apply idealized, content-agnostic degradations that do not reflect the complex, non-linear, and often content-dependent degradations seen in real UGC (e.g., adaptive bitrate streaming, re-encoding cascades, ISP pipeline artifacts, or generative artifacts). The introduction must explicitly address how synthetic distortion rankings can capture the authentic, perceptual complexities that the authors themselves identify as missing in prior weakly-supervised methods.

### Method / Approach (Sections 3 & 4)
**3.1-3.2 Setup & Backbone Choice:** The strong student is LLaVA-OneVision-Chat-7B (~8B parameters), while the weak teachers range from ~30M to ~120M (except Q-Align at ~8B). Comparing a modern vision-language model fine-tuned on a massive 200k-video pool against specialized VQA models trained on 27k LSVQ samples inherently conflates **backbone capacity, architectural inductive biases, and data scale** with the W2S training paradigm. To validate the W2S claim, the authors must control for capacity. A crucial missing ablation is: *training the same 7B LMM with random labels, with direct synthetic distortion labels, or with an equivalent amount of human-labeled data*. Without this, it is unclear if the gains are due to W2S or simply the LMM's superior zero-shot representation power unlocked by massive pre-training and fine-tuning.

**4.1.3 Heterogeneous Teachers (Synthetic Distortions):** The labeling strategy assumes a strict monotonic relationship between distortion severity levels and perceptual quality (e.g., `|i-j| > 1` → superior/inferior). Human perception of video quality is notoriously non-monotonic and content-dependent (e.g., mild blur may be perceptually preferred over noisy sharpness in low-light scenes; compression at certain CRFs introduces ringing vs. blurring that trade off differently). By enforcing deterministic synthetic rankings, the method risks teaching the student a **deterministic distortion-prior rather than perceptual quality**. The ranking formulation (Section 4.1.1) helps unify scales, but it cannot correct fundamentally flawed pseudo-labels. The method needs a mechanism to account for perceptual uncertainty or content-adaptive distortion sensitivity, rather than treating synthetic severity as ground truth.

**4.2 Iterative W2S Training & Difficulty Sampling:** The iterative strategy recycles the student as a new teacher and selects disagreement-based pairs via gMAD. While theoretically sound, iterative pseudo-labeling without a human-anchored reference carries a high risk of **confirmation bias and distributional drift**. The student will progressively learn to agree with its own past confident predictions, potentially amplifying systematic biases inherited from the initial weak teachers or the synthetic distortion prior. The confidence loss (minimizing entropy) exacerbates this by rewarding overconfidence. The paper lacks any analysis of score distribution drift, correlation with human scales per iteration, or mechanisms to prevent error accumulation. A validation step using a small human-labeled anchor set, or a theoretical bound on drift, would significantly strengthen this component.

**C.2.2 Confidence Loss Formulation:** Equation 25 defines $\lambda = \alpha \cdot \min(1.0, t/T_{warmup})$, but $\alpha$ (Eq 26) is defined as $\exp(L_{conf}/T) / (\exp(L_{conf}/T) + \exp(L_{CE}/T))$. Since $L_{conf}$ is entropy, it decreases over time, while $L_{CE}$ may fluctuate. The temperature $T$ is linearly decreased from 0.5 to 0.1. The dynamic behavior of $\alpha$ is highly sensitive to loss scales. The paper does not provide a sensitivity analysis for $T$ or $\alpha$, nor does it show training curves of both losses. In practice, unsupervised entropy regularization can lead to mode collapse if not carefully scheduled. Reproducibility requires explicit schedules and ablation on $\lambda$'s impact on final correlation.

### Experiments & Results (Section 3.3, 4.4, Table 1, Table 5, Figure 4)
**Statistical Rigor:** Video quality correlation metrics (SRCC/PLCC) exhibit non-negligible variance depending on train/val splits, video sampling, and model initialization. Table 1 reports SOTA claims with differences as small as 0.004–0.008 in SRCC (e.g., 0.865 vs 0.859). Without confidence intervals, multiple random seeds, or bootstrapping significance tests, it is impossible to determine if these gains are statistically meaningful or within standard VQA benchmark variance. ICLR expects rigorous statistical reporting for claimed superiority.

**Ablation on Data Scale vs. W2S:** The method trains on 700k video pairs derived from a 200k video pool. Table 5 (Appendix) shows that a supervised LSVQ-labeled student trained on only ~27k videos achieves highly competitive OOD performance. This strongly suggests that **data scale and modern LMM fine-tuning** are the primary drivers of generalization, not necessarily the W2S paradigm. To substantiate the W2S contribution, the authors should provide an ablation curve varying the number of pseudo-labeled pairs (e.g., 50k, 100k, 500k) and compare it to a supervised baseline scaled similarly. If performance plateaus early, the iterative W2S and ensemble mechanisms are not the main contributors.

**Iterative Stage Gains:** Table 1 shows consistent improvements from Stage 1 to Stage 3. However, it is unclear whether these gains stem from better pseudo-label quality, larger effective training data, or simple overfitting to the OOD validation distribution during model selection. The paper should report training/inference time per stage and show whether the performance gains follow a diminishing returns pattern. Additionally, since the same 200k video pool is used across stages with different pair constructions, there is a risk of data leakage or redundant sampling that artificially inflates iterative gains.

**Baseline Comparisons:** The comparison with VQAThinker and VQA[2] is informative, but these methods use different backbones, training data scales, and optimization objectives. A fairer comparison would involve fine-tuning the same LLM backbone with human labels on an equivalent dataset, or reporting per-dataset variance. Furthermore, OOD datasets like LIVE-YT-HFR and Waterloo-IVC-4K exhibit extreme score drops for all methods (e.g., ~0.3 SRCC in Table 1). Gains in these regimes, while notable, may reflect the model learning dataset-specific distortion priors rather than true perceptual generalization. Analyzing per-dataset failure cases would clarify this.

### Limitations & Broader Impact
The Discussion (Section 5) briefly mentions scaling toward foundation models and incorporating expert-domain models, but **omits critical limitations**:
1. **Perceptual Drift:** No discussion of how to guarantee human alignment without periodic human feedback or calibration.
2. **Generative & AI-Video Content:** The paper mentions generative videos in passing, but current synthetic distoritions do not capture LLM-generated artifacts (temporal flicker, unnatural motion physics, texture hallucinations). A W2S model trained on authentic UCG + synthetic distortions may fail catastrophically on AI-generated content.
3. **Compute & Carbon Footprint:** Iterative training of an 8B model on 700k pairs for three stages over multiple days on 8xA800s is resource-intensive. The paper claims to bypass annotation costs, but shifts the cost to compute. An honest cost-benefit analysis (annotation hours vs. GPU hours) is missing.
4. **Dataset Licensing & Privacy:** While the ethics statement mentions filtering for permissive licenses, training on 3M social media videos still raises concerns about creator consent and data provenance. ICLR reviewers increasingly scrutinize large-scale web data scraping.

---

### Overall Assessment
This paper tackles an important and practically relevant problem in VQA: reducing reliance on costly human annotations while improving OOD generalization. Applying the weak-to-strong paradigm to video quality assessment is novel and conceptually promising. The iterative self-teaching framework with diverse synthetic and model-based teachers is well-structured and empirically shows consistent gains across ten benchmarks.

However, several critical concerns must be addressed before the contribution can stand firmly. First, the gains may be largely attributable to fine-tuning a large multimodal model on a massive dataset rather than the W2S mechanism itself; missing capacity and data-scale ablations weaken the core claim. Second, the reliance on deterministic synthetic distortion rankings assumes monotonic human perception, which contradicts known psycho-visual non-linearities and risks teaching artifact-priors rather than perceptual alignment. Third, the iterative pseudo-labeling loop lacks safeguards against confirmation bias and distributional drift, with no per-stage human correlation analysis or drift metrics. Finally, claims of SOTA performance lack statistical rigor (confidence intervals, multi-seed variance), which is expected at ICLR for narrow metric improvements.

If the authors can provide controlled ablations disentangling capacity/data scale from W2S, analyze score drift across iterations, and add statistical validation to the results, this work would represent a strong, reproducible contribution to label-efficient VQA. As it stands, the empirical evidence is promising but insufficiently isolated to claim the W2S paradigm as the primary driver of generalization. The paper requires substantial methodological clarification and additional controlled experiments to meet ICLR's bar for rigorous empirical validation.

# Neutral Reviewer
## Balanced Review

### Summary
This paper introduces a weak-to-strong (W2S) learning paradigm for video quality assessment (VQA) to address the high cost of human annotations and the poor out-of-distribution (OOD) generalization of current supervised models. The authors train a 7B large multimodal student on 200k unlabeled videos using pseudo-labels from an ensemble of existing VQA models and synthetic distortion simulators, unified through a pairwise ranking formulation. An iterative, difficulty-guided training loop with a confidence-aware loss progressively refines the student. The approach achieves state-of-the-art results on ten in-domain and OOD benchmarks, demonstrating strong gains on challenging distibutions without human-labeled training data.

### Strengths
1. **Clear motivation and practical impact**: The paper directly tackles two major bottlenecks in VQA: prohibitive subjective annotation costs and severe OOD performance drops. The W2S framing offers a scalable, data-centric alternative that aligns well with current trends in scalable alignment.
2. **Well-designed methodological components**: Reformulating quality prediction as a ranking task effectively harmonizes heterogeneous supervision signals from different teachers. The iterative W2S strategy with gMAD-based difficulty sampling (Eq. 1) and synthetic distortion integration provides a principled mechanism for self-improvement beyond static knowledge distillation.
3. **Rigorous empirical evaluation and reproducibility**: Experiments span ten diverse benchmarks with clear ablations isolating each component (ensemble → heterogeneous signals → confidence loss → iterative stages). The authors provide transparent dataset construction details (distribution matching across nine low-level metrics), explicit training hyperparameters, prompt templates, inference mechanics (MAP/Thurstone scoring), and an anonymized code repository.

### Weaknesses
1. **Capacity vs. paradigm confound**: The student model is a 7B-parameter LMM, which inherently possesses massive zero-shot capacity and prior visual-language alignment. The paper does not isolate how much of the observed gain stems from the W2S paradigm versus sheer model scale and dataset size, making it unclear if the approach generalizes to more efficient backbones.
2. **Limited analysis of teacher bias propagation**: While the confidence loss and synthetic teachers mitigate label noise, all homogeneous teachers share the same LSVQ training distribution. Systematic biases or failure modes common to these teachers could still constrain the student's ceiling. The paper lacks a detailed error-mode analysis or quantification of how disagreement-based sampling actually decorrelates from shared teacher biases.
3. **Missing computational and scalability analysis**: Generating 700k ranked pairs, performing multiple inference passes for gMAD selection, and running three iterative fine-tuning stages incur non-trivial compute and storage overhead. The paper does not report training FLOPs, wall-clock time per iteration, or memory requirements, making it difficult to assess the practical feasibility of the pipeline at scale.

### Novelty & Significance
The novelty lies in successfully adapting the W2S learning paradigm—previously dominant in LLM alignment and reward modeling—to the perceptually grounded, regression-heavy domain of VQA. The integration of ranking-based unification for heterogeneous weak teachers, coupled with difficulty-guided iterative self-teaching, offers a clean and scalable alternative to annotation-dependent pipelines. The significance is high for ICLR: the work demonstrates that scaling unlabeled video data with carefully curated weak supervision can systematically push past the OOD generalization plateau of fully supervised models. While the heavy reliance on a large LMM tempers immediate applicability in resource-constrained settings, the framework provides a strong foundation for future VQA foundation models and weakly aligned perceptual systems.

### Suggestions for Improvement
1. **Backbone capacity ablation**: Train a smaller LMM (e.g., 1B–3B) or a vision-only architecture under the identical W2S pipeline to verify that the gains are not solely attributable to the 7B model's scale. Report relative improvements to clarify paradigm effectiveness across capacities.
2. **Teacher bias and error-mode analysis**: Provide a breakdown of where the student fails relative to individual teachers across specific distortion types (e.g., gaming vs. compression vs. high frame rate). Quantify how the confidence loss and heterogeneous synthetic labels reduce inherited biases, perhaps by measuring teacher-student disagreement distributions over training.
3. **Compute and efficiency reporting**: Include a table or paragraph detailing training hours, GPU memory usage, data generation costs (frame extraction, distortion application, teacher inference for pseudo-labeling), and inference latency. Discuss potential approximations or streaming strategies to scale the iterative sampling to larger datasets.
4. **Mechanistic discussion of generalization**: Explicitly analyze whether OOD improvements primarily stem from dataset scale (200k vs. 27k LSVQ), supervision diversity, or iterative difficulty sampling. A learning-curve or data-efficiency plot would clarify the marginal returns of each component and strengthen the theoretical grounding of the W2S effect in VQA.

# Spark Finder Review
## How to Improve This Paper

### Missing Experiments (top 3-5 only)
1. **Control for dataset scale vs. W2S effect:** Train the student on 27k-38k randomly sampled unlabeled videos under the same pseudo-labeling protocol to isolate whether gains stem from weak-to-strong dynamics or simply 5-7x data scaling. Without this, the core W2S claim is confounded by dataset size.
2. **Comparison against weakly-supervised/self-supervised VQA baselines:** Evaluate recent unsupervised VQA methods (e.g., CONVIQT, QPT, or mask-reconstruction variants) on your exact OOD suite. Claiming superiority "without reliance on human-labeled datasets" requires benchmarking against peers operating under the same zero-annotation constraint, not just fully supervised SOTA.
3. **Isolation of synthetic distortion supervision:** Run ablations training exclusively on ensemble VQA pseudo-labels vs. exclusively on synthetic distortion rankings. Since synthetic pairs have deterministic severity labels, their inclusion may dominate the gradient; proving that both signals are actively integrated rather than one acting as a crutch is necessary.
4. **Zero-shot LMM baseline with identical prompting/anchor setup:** Report performance of the untrained LLaVA-OneVision backbone using the exact pairwise comparison prompts and anchor-based MAP inference. LMMs possess strong inherent comparison priors; omitting this baseline makes it impossible to attribute improvements to W2S fine-tuning rather than architectural capacity.

### Deeper Analysis Needed (top 3-5 only)
1. **Verify iterative selection avoids confirmation bias:** Analyze the gMAD disagreement samples across stages to ensure the model isn't amplifying systematic teacher errors. Track whether student-teacher disagreement correlates with actual human preference shifts on a small held-out OOD probe; without this, iterative recycling risks self-reinforcing hallucinations.
2. **Quantify LLM linguistic priors vs. visual quality learning:** Analyze token probability distributions for ranking labels ("superior" to "inferior") before and after training. If the model primarily shifts text-generation confidence rather than visual feature alignment, the method functions as prompt-tuning rather than genuine W2S visual representation learning.
3. **OOD distortion-type error decomposition:** Disaggregate OOD performance by distortion category (e.g., compression vs. frame-rate vs. gaming/rendering artifacts) to prove the synthetic distortion integration actually transfers to authentic degradations. Aggregate OOD averages mask whether gains are isolated to compression-heavy datasets where synthetic supervision dominates.

### Visualizations & Case Studies
1. **Per-dataset confusion/error matrices:** Plot where the final W2S model agrees/disagrees with human ground truth on OOD benchmarks compared to the strongest teacher. This directly exposes whether the student corrects teacher failures or inherits their blind spots.
2. **Feature space evolution (t-SNE/UMAP):** Visualize video embedding distributions of the teacher vs. student across quality tiers. A successful W2S method should show clearer separation of quality manifolds in the student; overlapping clusters would indicate the model merely memorized ranking labels.
3. **Qualitative hard-sample pairs with ground truth annotations:** Show 5-10 video pairs selected during iterative gMAD filtering alongside human preference labels. This validates that difficulty sampling targets perceptually meaningful edges rather than stochastic prediction noise or compression artifacts easily detected by low-level filters.

### Obvious Next Steps
1. **Human correlation validation on OOD subsets:** SRCC/PLCC against teacher pseudo-labels does not prove human alignment. Compute SRCC/PLCC against the actual human MOS/MOS scores on LIVE-YT-Gaming, LIVE-YT-HFR, and CGVDS to confirm the model genuinely learned perceptual quality rather than mimicking teacher biases.
2. **Compute-efficiency and cost analysis:** Report FLOPs, training hours, and annotation cost equivalents for the W2S pipeline vs. supervised baselines that achieve similar performance. ICLR requires evidence that the new paradigm is practically advantageous, not just numerically superior.
3. **Dataset distribution and licensing transparency:** Provide explicit statistics on aspect ratios, resolutions, and content-type proportions in the 200k training pool, and clarify the filtering pipeline for permissive licenses. Without this, the "scalable and annotation-free" claim cannot be reproduced or trusted for general deployment.

# Final Consolidated Review
## Summary
This paper introduces a Weak-to-Strong (W2S) learning paradigm for no-reference Video Quality Assessment (VQA), aiming to bypass costly human annotations and improve out-of-distribution (OOD) generalization. The approach trains a 7B large multimodal student on 200k unlabeled videos using pseudo-labels generated from an ensemble of existing VQA models and synthetic distortion simulators. Supervision is unified through a pairwise ranking formulation, and further refined via iterative difficulty-guided sampling (gMAD) and a confidence-aware auxiliary loss. The method reports consistent incremental gains across ten in-domain and OOD benchmarks, claiming state-of-the-art performance without human-labeled training data.

## Strengths
- **Principled unification of heterogeneous weak supervision:** Reformulating continuous quality regression as a discrete ranking task elegantly resolves scale and range mismatches across diverse teacher models. Coupled with the integration of synthetic distortion rankings, this creates a scalable, annotation-free supervision pipeline that is well-adapted to VQA's inherent subjectivity.
- **Consistent, empirically validated iterative refinement:** The difficulty-guided sampling strategy (Eq. 1) effectively forces the student to focus on decision boundaries where it diverges from teachers. The stepwise gains across Stages I–V in both in-domain and OOD settings (Table 1) provide strong evidence that the iterative recycling mechanism expands the model's effective capacity.
- **High reproducibility and methodological transparency:** The paper provides extensive implementation details, including the exact MIP-based dataset matching procedure across nine low-level metrics, explicit training hyperparameters, prompt templates, inference mechanics via Thurstone's Case V MAP estimation, and a functional code repository.

## Weaknesses
- **Core W2S claim is heavily confounded by backbone capacity and data scale:** The student is a 7B+ LMM trained on 200k videos, while the teacher ensemble consists mostly of models <150M parameters trained on 27k human-labeled samples (LSVQ). While Table 5 compares to an LSVQ-supervised student, it inherently conflates supervision type with a ~7x increase in data volume and a shift to modern LMM architectures. Without controlled ablations—(a) matching the exact 27k data scale for pseudo-labeled training, or (b) training a smaller backbone (e.g., 1B–3B) under the identical W2S protocol—it remains unclear whether the observed OOD gains stem from weak-to-strong dynamics or simply from unlocking the LMM's extensive zero-shot priors and fine-tuning on a massive dataset.
- **Missing zero-annotation baselines under identical evaluation constraints:** To substantiate the claim that W2S effectively "obviates the need for human-annotated labels," the method must be benchmarked against contemporary self-supervised or unsupervised VQA approaches operating under the same label-free constraint. Comparisons are currently limited to fully supervised SOTA and LMMs trained with extensive human feedback or RLHF. Direct experimental comparison against existing zero-annotation VQA paradigms (e.g., contrastive, masking-based, or reconstruction methods) on the exact OOD suite is absent, leaving the marginal utility of the W2S framework over established label-free representation learning unquantified.
- **Lack of human-alignment tracking and validation of iterative stability:** The iterative W2S loop recycles student predictions as future supervision and employs an entropy-minimizing confidence loss that penalizes uncertainty. While gMAD sampling promotes diversity, the paper does not track correlation with true human MOS/MOS scores across training stages, nor does it provide failure-case error decomposition. This omission raises valid concerns about confirmation bias and score distribution drift; without verifying that iterative improvements translate to better human perceptual alignment (rather than tighter agreement with teacher ensemble biases or synthetic priors), the long-horizon reliability of the self-evolving loop is difficult to assess.

## Nice-to-Haves
- **Statistical validation:** Reporting bootstrap confidence intervals or multi-seed variance for SRCC/PLCC would strengthen the SOTA claims, particularly for narrow margins (~0.01–0.02).
- **Zero-shot LMM baseline:** Performance of the untrained backbone using the exact pairwise prompt and MAP inference pipeline would help disentangle architectural prompting capability from W2S fine-tuning effects.
- **Compute and efficiency reporting:** A breakdown of training FLOPs, wall-clock time per iteration, teacher inference overhead, and inference latency would clarify practical deployment feasibility versus traditional supervised pipelines.
- **OOD distortion-type error decomposition:** Disaggregating OOD gains by distortion category (compression, frame-rate, gaming/rendering) would clarify whether improvements generalize broadly or primarily benefit domains where synthetic supervision directly applies.

## Novel Insights
The paper successfully bridges the weak-to-strong generalization paradigm from LLM alignment into a perceptually grounded, regression-heavy domain, demonstrating that VQA models can be scaled effectively without human labels. By treating quality prediction as a relative ranking task, the work highlights a fundamental structural advantage in perceptual modeling: learning comparative priors is more robust and transferable across heterogeneous, misaligned signals than learning absolute scores. However, this also surfaces a critical tension inherent to scalable weak supervision: deterministic synthetic distortion labels and ensemble pseudo-scores inherently compress the rich, non-linear variance of human perception. The iterative gMAD sampling mechanism elegantly attempts to recover these compressed boundaries, but the absence of human-anchored validation reveals a broader insight for the field—self-evolving perceptual models risk converging on internally consistent but externally misaligned representations if human feedback loops are entirely removed from the training horizon.

## Suggestions
1. **Add controlled scale/capacity ablations:** Train the same W2S pipeline on a 27k subset matched to the LSVQ size, and evaluate a smaller vision backbone (e.g., 1B–3B). This will directly isolate the W2S mechanism's contribution from data scale and architectural priors.
2. **Include zero-annotation baselines and a zero-shot prompt control:** Run 2–3 leading self-supervised VQA methods on your exact OOD benchmarks, and report the untrained LMM's performance with the comparative prompt/MAP pipeline to establish a rigorous performance floor.
3. **Track human alignment across iterations:** Report per-stage SRCC/PLCC against actual human ground truth on a held-out OOD subset (e.g., CGVDS, LIVE-YT-Gaming). If human correlation tracks or improves with iterative gains, it effectively mitigates drift concerns; if it plateaus or drops, it signals the need for periodic human-anchored calibration.
