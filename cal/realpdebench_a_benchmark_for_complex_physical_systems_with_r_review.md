# Harsh Critic Review
## Section-by-Section Critical Review

### Title & Abstract
- **Title accuracy:** The title accurately reflects the core contribution: a benchmark integrating real-world measurements with simulated data for complex physical systems. It clearly signals the domain and scope.
- **Abstract clarity:** The abstract effectively states the problem (scientific ML relies on simulated data, creating a sim-to-real bottleneck), the method (RealPDEBench with paired datasets, 3 tasks, 9 metrics, 10 baselines), and key results (significant sim-real discrepancies, sim pretraining consistently improves real-world accuracy and convergence).
- **Unsupported claims:** The claim of being the "*first* benchmark for scientific ML that integrates real-world measurements with paired numerical simulations" is slightly overstated given the concurrent work cited in Section 2 (REALM, Mao et al., 2025). While REALM focuses on multiphysics reactive flows and your benchmark spans broader fluid/combustion regimes with explicit paired trajectories, the absolute "first" claim should be softened or explicitly differentiated from concurrent submissions to avoid novelty disputes at ICLR. The claim that pretraining "*consistently* improves both accuracy and convergence" is largely supported by Table 1 and Figure 3b, but should note any minor exceptions if they exist in the full tables.

### Introduction & Motivation
- **Problem motivation & gap:** The motivation is well-grounded in a recognized bottleneck: scientific ML is bottlenecked by evaluation/training on idealized simulations, hindering sim-to-real transfer and real-world deployment. The gap is clearly identified and contextualized with references to measurement costs, modalities, and numerical errors.
- **Contributions clarity:** The fourfold contribution structure (data, tasks, metrics, baselines) is explicit and accurately maps to Sections 3.1–3.4.
- **Over-claim/under-sell:** The introduction appropriately positions the work without over-claiming methodological novelty. However, it under-sells the experimental engineering effort. The detailed PIV setup, laser sheet alignment, and CFD turbulence/chemistry modeling (Section B.2) represent substantial domain expertise that is only hinted at in the introduction. Briefly highlighting the experimental rigor earlier would strengthen the perceived value. The differentiation from prior real-data collections (e.g., Kang et al., Casey & Wintergerste) is clear, but a sharper contrast with REALM's design philosophy (e.g., trajectory length, parameter coverage, modality pairing) in the introduction would better anchor the novelty.

### Method / Approach
- **Clarity & reproducibility:** The methodology is well-structured. Data formats (HDF5), PIV/CL imaging pipelines, CFD solvers (Lilypad/Waterlily/STAR-CCM+), and baseline architectures are described with sufficient detail for reproduction. The unified `RealDataset` and `Model` modules (Section C.3–C.4) further support reproducibility.
- **Assumptions stated & justified:** Key assumptions are stated, notably: (1) adding Gaussian noise and masking unmeasured modalities during simulated training bridges the modality distribution gap (Section 4.2, D.1), and (2) evaluation is strictly on real-world data to measure real-world fidelity. These are well-justified for scientific ML workflows.
- **Logical gaps:** A notable gap exists in **Section 3.1 (Task Definition)** regarding the train/validation/test split. The text defines the split as $n$ real samples for training, $(N-n)/2$ for validation, and $(N-n)/2$ for testing, but never specifies the actual value of $n$ per dataset or the total $N$. Without explicit split ratios or sample counts, reproducibility of the training protocol is compromised. Appendix B.1 gives total trajectory counts (e.g., 92 × 2 for Cylinder), but the training subset $n$ must be explicitly stated in the main text.
- **Edge cases/failure modes:** The paper discusses autoregressive error accumulation (Section 4.5, Figure 3c) and handles unmeasured modalities via zero-padding/masking. However, it does not explicitly discuss how the framework handles *temporal misalignment* between real and simulated trajectories, or how initial condition mismatches in real experiments (e.g., slight variations in flow establishment) might propagate. A brief discussion on trajectory synchronization/initialization protocols would address this edge case.
- **Theoretical claims:** The paper is empirical, so no theoretical proofs are expected or missing.

### Experiments & Results
- **Test claims:** The experiments directly test the stated claims: they quantify the sim-real gap (Section 4.2), demonstrate sim-to-real transfer benefits (Section 4.3), compare baselines (Section 4.4), and evaluate long-horizon stability (Section 4.5).
- **Baselines appropriateness:** The selection is comprehensive and appropriate, spanning linear methods (DMD), convolutional (U-Net, CNO), spectral/wavelet operators (FNO, WDNO, MWT), attention-based (GK-Transformer, Transolver), and foundation models (DPOT-S/L). This covers the current scientific ML landscape adequately for ICLR.
- **Missing ablations:** A critical missing ablation is the **sensitivity to real data quantity ($n$)**. Since real-world data is scarce, the paper should evaluate how fine-tuning performance scales with $n = \{10\%, 20\%, 50\%, 100\%\}$. Additionally, the masking probability and noise scale added to simulated data (Section D.1) are not ablated; reporting sensitivity would strengthen the sim-to-real strategy claims.
- **Error bars / significance:** No error bars, standard deviations, or statistical significance tests are reported across multiple random seeds. Figure 3a mentions "statistics of 10 values" but visualizes only means. ICLR standards require reporting variance across at least 3 seeds for all model training results to confirm robustness, especially for fine-tuning experiments.
- **Results support claims / cherry-picking:** Results broadly support the claims, but the Combustion dataset introduces an unquantified confounder. **Section D.2** states that because real combustion data only measures light intensity $I$, a U-Net surrogate is trained to map simulated species/temperature to $I$. This means "simulated training" for Combustion actually uses *model-generated proxies*, not raw simulation outputs. The surrogate's prediction error compounds the sim-to-real evaluation, and this compounding error is not isolated or reported. Furthermore, while Table 1 shows improvements, the lack of normalized training compute budgets across baselines (e.g., DPOT-L-FT vs. U-Net) makes direct performance comparisons partially confounded by model scale and optimization steps.
- **Datasets/Metrics appropriateness:** Metrics are well-chosen and standard in CFD/ML literature (RMSE, $R^2$, fRMSE, FE, KE, MVPE). The inclusion of Update Ratio (Section 3.3.1) is a practical and novel contribution for assessing data efficiency. The datasets cover relevant physical regimes (wake, control, FSI, 3D foil, reactive flows) and are appropriate for evaluating scientific ML.

### Writing & Clarity
- **Confusing/poorly explained sections:** Section 3.1's task definition would benefit from a concrete table specifying $N$, $n$, validation, and test counts per dataset. The description of the mask-training strategy (Section 4.2) and noise injection is deferred to Appendices D and D.1; a 2-3 line summary of the exact masking/noise protocol in the main text would improve flow and clarity without sacrificing space.
- **Figures/Tables clarity:** Figure 4 (RMSE vs. FE trade-off) effectively summarizes multi-metric performance across datasets. Figures 3b and 5 provide clear visualizations of fine-tuning convergence and long-horizon MVP fidelity. Figure 1 is informative for demonstrating modality gaps. The main tables in the text are structurally sound, though the appendix tables (2–6) should explicitly state evaluation settings (e.g., 1-round vs. autoregressive) in their captions to avoid reader confusion when cross-referencing.

### Limitations & Broader Impact
- **Acknowledge key limitations:** The authors acknowledge scope limitations (only fluid/combustion), lack of combustion-specific metrics, and absence of strong OOD regime exploration (Appendix F).
- **Missed fundamental limitations:** The paper does not adequately address the **ground-truth uncertainty** of the "real-world" measurements. PIV and CL imaging have inherent spatial resolution limits, particle slip errors, and laser sheet thickness artifacts (e.g., 2mm sheet for 2D approximation of 3D flows). Additionally, the LES simulations themselves rely on turbulence/chemistry closures (EDC model, 38-species mechanism) that introduce systematic biases. Quantifying or at least discussing these experimental and numerical uncertainties would provide necessary context for the benchmark's "ground truth" quality. The surrogate mapping for Combustion (D.2) is also a fundamental limitation that should be elevated to the main Limitations section.
- **Societal impacts/failure modes:** The ethics statement appropriately cautions against safety-critical deployment without validation. A brief discussion on potential negative impacts, such as misuse of the benchmark to justify overconfident deployment of ML surrogates in engineering design without uncertainty quantification, would strengthen the broader impact statement.

### Overall Assessment
RealPDEBench presents a timely and valuable contribution to the scientific ML community, addressing a critical bottleneck in sim-to-real evaluation through carefully collected, paired real-world and simulated datasets. The benchmark design, encompassing diverse fluid/combustion scenarios, well-motivated tasks, and a comprehensive metric suite, aligns well with ICLR's standards for impactful empirical work. However, the submission currently lacks several rigor elements expected for top-tier acceptance: explicit train/val split counts ($n$ values), reporting of variance across random seeds, ablation on real data scarcity ($n$ scaling), and clarification of fair compute/tuning budgets across disparate baseline architectures. Furthermore, the combustion surrogate mapping and unquantified experimental/simulation uncertainties introduce confounders in the sim-to-real claims that require explicit acknowledgment or isolation. Addressing these methodological transparency issues, alongside tightening the novelty claims relative to concurrent work, would significantly strengthen the paper's readiness for publication.

# Neutral Reviewer
## Balanced Review

### Summary
The paper introduces RealPDEBench, the first scientific ML benchmark pairing real-world experimental measurements with numerically simulated data across five complex physical systems (fluid dynamics and combustion). It establishes three standardized training tasks (real-only, simulation-only, and sim-pretrain/real-finetune), nine evaluation metrics spanning data fidelity and physical consistency, and benchmarks ten representative models ranging from traditional reduced-order models to large PDE foundation models. Experiments quantify the simulation-to-reality gap and demonstrate that simulated pretraining consistently accelerates convergence and improves prediction accuracy on real-world test sets.

### Strengths
1. **Directly Addresses a Critical Bottleneck in Scientific ML:** The community's heavy reliance on synthetic data limits real-world applicability and hinders sim-to-real research. By providing >700 paired trajectories across diverse regimes (laminar-to-turbulent flows, active control, fluid-structure interaction, 3D foil effects, and reacting flows), the benchmark enables rigorous evaluation of models under realistic noise, measurement limitations, and physical complexity.
2. **Comprehensive & Systematic Evaluation Protocol:** The three-task design isolates the value of simulation vs. real data. The metric suite thoughtfully combines standard ML errors (RMSE, Rel $L_2$) with physics-aware diagnostics (fRMSE, Frequency Error, MVPE, Update Ratio). Benchmarking ten diverse architectures under a unified framework provides a clear, reproducible reference point for future work.
3. **Exceptional Reproducibility & Experimental Transparency:** The paper details hardware setups (water tunnel specs, PIV processing, chemiluminescence imaging), CFD solvers (Lilypad, Waterlily, STAR-CCM+), and post-processing pipelines. Releasing a modular PyTorch codebase, configuration scripts, partial raw data, and calibration files strongly aligns with ICLR’s reproducibility expectations.
4. **Actionable Empirical Findings:** Results consistently show that models trained purely on simulation degrade on real data (Table 1, Sec 4.2), while sim-pretraining followed by real finetuning significantly reduces the optimization steps needed to match real-only performance (Update Ratio < 1, Fig 3b). This provides practical guidance for leveraging cheap synthetic data in experimental pipelines.

### Weaknesses
1. **Limited Dataset Scale & Lack of Statistical Rigor:** Several datasets are relatively small (e.g., 30 trajectories for Combustion, 51 for FSI, per Table 7 in App B.1), which constrains training large foundation models and may inflate variance. Key claims like "9.39% to 78.91% improvements" (Sec 4.2) and metric comparisons in Table 1 lack reported standard deviations, multiple random seeds, or statistical significance tests.
2. **Superficial Architectural Analysis:** The discussion of why certain models excel (e.g., U-Net for local features, MWT for periodicity, DPOT-L for overall scale) remains largely correlational. There is no ablation on inductive biases, attention vs. convolution trade-offs, or explicit analysis of how architectural choices interact with real-world measurement noise versus numerical discretization error.
3. **Underutilized Out-of-Distribution (OOD) Framework:** While the `RealDataset` module explicitly supports `in_dist`/`out_dist` and `seen`/`unseen` evaluation modes (Sec C.3), the main experiments only test within the parameter grid used for training/validation. Given ICLR's emphasis on generalization, the omission of rigorous OOD extrapolation tests is a notable missed opportunity.
4. **Ambiguous Handling of Modality Mismatch in Combustion:** Real-world combustion experiments only capture OH* chemiluminescence intensity, while simulations track dozens of thermochemical fields. The paper employs a surrogate U-Net to map simulated fields to the real modality (Sec D.2, App D), but provides minimal architectural details, loss weighting, or error propagation analysis. This raises concerns about how much downstream performance stems from physically grounded mapping versus model hallucination.

### Novelty & Significance
The primary novelty is resource-driven rather than algorithmic: the construction of a rigorously paired real/sim dataset, a physics-aware metric suite, and a standardized evaluation protocol for sim-to-real transfer in SciML. This is highly significant for the neural operator and scientific computing communities, as it shifts the evaluation paradigm from synthetic fidelity to real-world applicability. For ICLR, benchmark papers must demonstrate exceptional empirical depth and clear community value; this paper delivers on both fronts but falls slightly short on statistical rigor and generalization analysis. With stronger variance reporting and OOD testing, it would be a clear fit for top ML venues focused on scientific applications.

### Suggestions for Improvement
1. **Incorporate Statistical Validation:** Report results as mean ± std across ≥3 random seeds. Add confidence intervals or non-parametric tests for key comparisons (e.g., real-only vs. sim-pretrain+ft in Table 1) to solidify claims of consistent improvement.
2. **Execute Rigorous OOD/Extrapolation Experiments:** Utilize the existing `test_mode` framework to evaluate performance on unseen Reynolds numbers, angles of attack, or equivalence ratios. Analyze how sim-to-real transfer degrades under distribution shift and whether pretraining improves OOD robustness compared to real-only training.
3. **Deepen Mechanistic Analysis & Ablations:** Instead of observational correlations, run controlled ablations (e.g., varying noise scales, masking rates, or training data fractions) to isolate which properties of simulated data drive finetuning gains. Provide a clearer architectural and loss specification for the combustion surrogate model, including a sensitivity analysis of its mapping error on downstream physics metrics.
4. **Clarify Practical Scaling Guidance:** Discuss the minimum number of real-world trajectories/steps required for effective finetuning across datasets. This would transform the benchmark from a static evaluation suite into a practical guide for experimentalists deciding how much real data to collect.

# Spark Finder Review
## How to Improve This Paper

### Missing Experiments (top 3-5 only)
1. Report explicit out-of-distribution (OOD) generalization results using held-out parameter regimes, because the claim of evaluating models across "distinct operating conditions" is unsubstantiated without showing performance on unseen physical parameters.
2. Run compute-matched comparisons between real-world training from scratch and simulation pretraining plus finetuning, because the current Update Ratio metric conflates sample efficiency with arbitrary optimization trajectories, making true data-efficiency claims unconvincing.
3. Evaluate standard domain adaptation baselines (e.g., AdaBN, feature alignment, or discrepancy minimization) instead of relying solely on noise injection and modality masking, otherwise the claim that RealPDEBench facilitates rigorous "sim-to-real transfer" research is functionally empty.

### Deeper Analysis Needed (top 3-5 only)
1. Quantify real-world measurement noise floors through repeated experimental trials or instrument specifications and subtract them from model errors, because without separating sensor noise from algorithmic mistakes, claims of high-fidelity prediction are statistically ambiguous.
2. Analyze how the combustion surrogate model's mapping error propagates into downstream spatiotemporal predictions, because predicting real chemiluminescence via a learned transform from mismatched simulation fields introduces an unquantified confounder that invalidates the combustion results.
3. Evaluate physical conservation violations (e.g., mass continuity, momentum balance, or vorticity transport errors) rather than only statistical similarity metrics, because ICLR expects scientific ML models to be validated on physical consistency, not just noisy data拟合.

### Visualizations & Case Studies
1. Provide spatial residual heatmaps showing prediction errors across the flow domain for each regime, because aggregate scalar metrics hide critical localized failure modes like boundary layer separation or wake misalignment.
2. Visualize strictly aligned real-world and simulated trajectories from identical initial conditions, because the claimed "sim-to-real gap" is currently only supported by reported error tables rather than direct visual evidence, weakening the core motivation.
3. Show ablation trajectory plots comparing predictions with and without the modality masking strategy, because this would directly prove whether the masking mechanism actually transfers simulated physics knowledge to real data or merely acts as implicit regularization.

### Obvious Next Steps
1. Release fixed, documented train/validation/test parameter splits with explicit trajectory assignments, because parameter-level splitting without published boundaries prevents strict reproducibility and independent OOD benchmarking by the community.
2. Replace the ad-hoc combustion surrogate pipeline with proper modality alignment in simulation or direct experimental intensity tracking, because a benchmark claiming "paired data" cannot rely on an unvalidated intermediate network to bridge fundamentally different physical observables.
3. Conduct a hyperparameter sensitivity analysis for the pretraining/finetuning stage (learning rates, layer freezing ratios, and data mixing ratios), because foundation model adaptation is notoriously unstable, and reported gains may simply reflect careful tuning rather than inherent benchmark utility.

# Final Consolidated Review
## Summary
RealPDEBench introduces a scientific ML benchmark that pairs real-world experimental measurements with numerically simulated data across five complex fluid and combustion regimes. It standardizes evaluation through three training paradigms, nine physics- and data-aware metrics, and ten diverse baselines, empirically quantifying the simulation-to-reality gap and demonstrating consistent accuracy and convergence gains when pretraining on simulated data followed by real-world fine-tuning.

## Strengths
- **Directly addresses a critical community bottleneck by delivering rigorously paired real/sim data.** The benchmark provides >700 trajectories spanning laminar-to-turbulent flows, active control, fluid-structure interaction, 3D effects, and reactive flows. The extensive documentation of experimental hardware (water tunnels, PIV processing, chemiluminescence imaging) and numerical solvers (Lilypad, Waterlily, STAR-CCM+) establishes a high bar for methodological transparency in scientific ML.
- **Systematic, extensible evaluation protocol.** The three-task design cleanly isolates the contribution of synthetic vs. real data, while the metric suite thoughtfully pairs standard ML errors with physics-aware diagnostics (e.g., fRMSE, MVPE, Update Ratio). The unified PyTorch `RealDataset`/`Model` modules ensure straightforward reproducibility and community extension.
- **Actionable empirical findings with clear practical value.** The experiments convincingly show that models trained purely on simulation degrade on real test sets, while simulation pretraining followed by real fine-tuning consistently reduces optimization steps and improves fidelity. The observed error accumulation in long-horizon autoregression and architecture-specific frequency responses (e.g., MWT for periodicity, CNO for local features) provide concrete guidance for future scientific ML development.

## Weaknesses
- **Unquantified confounder in the Combustion benchmark:** Appendix D.2 explicitly states that because real-world combustion only measures OH* chemiluminescence intensity while simulations output dozens of thermochemical fields, a separate U-Net surrogate is trained to map simulated states to the real modality. This means "simulated training" on Combustion relies on model-generated proxies, not raw simulation outputs. The surrogate's prediction error is neither isolated nor reported, meaning downstream performance conflates physical modeling gains with surrogate hallucination. This fundamentally compromises the validity of sim-to-real conclusions for the combustion regime.
- **Missing statistical rigor and reproducibility parameters:** Table 1 reports single-point metrics across nine baselines and three tasks without standard deviations, confidence intervals, or results over multiple random seeds. Furthermore, Section 3.1 defines the protocol as "$n$ real-world samples for training, $(N-n)/2$ for validation, and $(N-n)/2$ for testing," but the exact value of $n$ per dataset is never provided. Without explicit split ratios or variance reporting, the claimed "consistent" improvements and baseline rankings cannot be independently verified or statistically validated, falling short of ICLR standards for benchmark papers.
- **Compute- and capacity-confounded baseline comparisons:** The benchmark directly compares lightweight architectures (e.g., U-Net, ~1-10M parameters) with massive foundation models (DPOT-L, 509M parameters) without normalizing for parameter scale, FLOPs, or optimization budgets. The leaderboard dominance of DPOT-L is largely driven by model capacity rather than benchmark insights. The absence of compute-matched comparisons or capacity-stratified analysis weakens the practical utility of the evaluation and obscures true architectural vs. scaling benefits.

## Nice-to-Haves
- Fully utilize the built-in `test_mode` (detailed in Sec C.3) to report out-of-distribution (OOD) generalization on held-out Reynolds numbers, angles of attack, or equivalence ratios, which would stress-test sim-to-real transfer under distribution shift.
- Provide a real-data scarcity ablation ($n$-scaling) to quantify how fine-tuning performance degrades with fewer trajectories, offering experimentalists concrete guidance on minimum data collection requirements.
- Incorporate explicit domain adaptation or feature-alignment baselines alongside the current noise-injection strategy to broaden the benchmark's utility for transfer learning research.

## Novel Insights
The benchmark successfully reframes operator learning evaluation from synthetic fidelity to empirical realism, revealing that simulation data serves as a highly effective inductive prior for real-world dynamics when leveraged via fine-tuning rather than direct transfer. More importantly, the divergence between architecture performance on local pixel-level metrics versus global physics-aware diagnostics (e.g., spectral frequency errors) highlights a critical design lesson: architectural inductive biases interact non-trivially with real-world measurement artifacts. Wavelet-based operators capture periodicity better, while convolutions excel locally but suffer faster error accumulation autoregressively. This suggests that future scientific ML cannot treat all experimental noise as generic Gaussian corruption; instead, models must be co-designed with the specific spectral and spatial characteristics of the measurement modality, shifting the field from chasing lower MSE to building noise-robust physical priors.

## Potentially Missed Related Work
- Multi-fidelity and bias-correction frameworks in CFD (e.g., Conti et al., *PNAS* 2024) that explicitly learn simulation-to-experiment offset terms rather than relying on direct parameter fine-tuning.
- Recent uncertainty quantification literature on PIV/CL measurement noise floors, which could help contextualize the irreducible error baseline of the benchmark's "ground truth."

## Suggestions
1. Explicitly report the training subset size $n$ for each dataset and provide mean ± std (across ≥3 seeds) for all fine-tuning results to meet statistical reporting standards.
2. Quantify the combustion surrogate's mapping error on a held-out validation split and clearly separate its contribution from the main model's performance in Table 1 or via a dedicated ablation, or replace it with direct modality alignment if feasible.
3. Normalize baseline comparisons by parameter count/FLOPs or explicitly discuss capacity scaling effects in Section 4.4 to prevent conflating architectural transfer benefits with brute-force model size.
