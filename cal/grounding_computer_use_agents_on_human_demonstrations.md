# Harsh Critic Review
## Section-by-Section Critical Review

### Title & Abstract
- The title is broadly accurate: the paper is about grounding computer-use agents using human demonstrations, and that is indeed the core contribution.
- The abstract clearly states the problem, introduces GROUNDCUA and GROUNDNEXT, and gives the main empirical claims.
- However, several abstract claims are stronger than the evidence presented in the main paper:
  - “state-of-the-art results across five benchmarks” is supported by Tables 2 and 3, but the margins are mixed and the comparison set is selective in places.
  - “requires less than one-tenth the training data of prior work” is plausible for the SFT setup, but this is only meaningful against selected baselines and would benefit from a more careful apples-to-apples comparison, especially since training data composition and task definitions differ.
  - The claim that GROUNDNEXT attains “comparable or superior results” in OSWorld-Verified with o3 as planner is strong, but Table 4 shows it is only marginally behind JEDI-7B overall (50.6 vs. 51.0), so “comparable” is fair, while “superior” is not uniformly true across categories or against all relevant baselines.
  - The abstract also claims strong cross-platform generalization, but the evidence in Tables 13 and 14 is mixed: the model is strong on some splits, but not consistently best across mobile/web settings.

### Introduction & Motivation
- The problem is well-motivated. The paper correctly identifies grounding as a bottleneck for computer-use agents, especially on dense desktop interfaces.
- The gap in prior work is reasonably identified: desktop grounding datasets are limited, and automated pipelines may miss small or visually ambiguous elements.
- The contributions are stated clearly, especially the dataset scale, the model family, and the SFT/RL training pipeline.
- The introduction sometimes over-claims:
  - It repeatedly asserts that GROUNDCUA is the “largest” and “most diverse” expert-annotated desktop grounding dataset. The paper does provide support relative to the cited datasets, but the claim would be stronger with a more rigorous accounting of what counts as “expert-annotated” and whether comparable datasets may exist outside the cited set.
  - The claim that high-quality data can “outperform larger, less precise datasets” is supported directionally, but the comparison is not fully controlled because different datasets differ not just in quality but also in platform coverage, instruction style, and annotation semantics.
- ICLR-level expectation: the introduction succeeds in framing a meaningful contribution, but it should more carefully qualify its strongest generalization claims.

### Method / Approach
- The dataset creation pipeline is understandable at a high level: human demonstrations, keyframe extraction, exhaustive element annotation, then instruction generation from dense labels.
- The instruction generation scheme is well-motivated and appears reproducible in spirit, especially the three instruction types in Section 3 and Appendix B.
- The main methodological concern is the lack of enough detail to fully verify the quality and consistency of the constructed instruction-tuning set:
  - Section 3 says instructions are generated by prompting a multimodal LLM with annotations, but the exact prompt design, filtering criteria, and failure modes are only loosely described.
  - Appendix B.6 reports a 4% error rate on 100 instructions, which is useful but too small to validate a 700K-sample synthetic instruction set.
- The RL formulation in Section 4.1 is reasonable, but the description is not fully airtight:
  - The paper uses a discrete reward based on normalized signed distance to the target box. This is intuitive, but the derivation of the normalization is somewhat confusing in the main text and appendix.
  - The reward depends on whether the prediction lies inside the box and on distance from the box boundary, but the exact behavior for degenerate boxes, overlapping elements, or multiple valid targets is not discussed.
  - The paper states it excludes reward-model-based approaches due to unreliability of judges. That is a reasonable choice, but the paper should be clearer about whether the reward is computed from ground truth boxes only and how this interacts with noisy or ambiguous labels.
- Important missing methodological detail: the final task is phrased as point prediction inside a box, but many computer-use benchmarks ultimately care about full actions, sequences, or target selection under context. The paper partially addresses this with OSWorld-Verified, but the grounding model itself is still evaluated only on point-in-box accuracy.
- The theoretical side is not a major paper contribution, so the formula issues are not fatal, but the method would benefit from a cleaner exposition of the reward and the RL objective.

### Experiments & Results
- The experiments do test the paper’s main claims:
  - SFT performance on five grounding benchmarks (Table 2).
  - RL improvements over SFT and over RL baselines (Table 3).
  - Agentic usefulness in OSWorld-Verified with an external planner (Table 4).
  - Data quality comparisons and ablations in Figures 3, 8, and Tables 7–10, 17, 18.
- Baselines are largely appropriate and fairly broad, including both SFT and RL-tuned methods.
- That said, several evaluation issues materially affect confidence:
  - The paper compares against many models but not always under fully matched settings. Some baselines are “agent mode,” some are not; some use different planners or RL recipes; some results appear taken from the literature rather than re-run.
  - There is no statistical significance testing or confidence intervals anywhere. For a benchmark paper with often modest margins, this is a notable omission.
  - The paper does not report variance across seeds. This matters especially for RL gains, which are presented as modest but consistent.
  - The comparison in Table 4 is informative, but because it uses o3 as planner, the result is a system-level comparison rather than a clean assessment of the grounding model alone. That is fine, but the paper should be explicit that the planner is doing substantial work.
- Missing ablations that would materially strengthen the claims:
  - A direct ablation of dense annotations vs. sparsified annotations, holding application coverage fixed.
  - A study of whether instruction generation itself, rather than the human demos, is the main driver of gains.
  - A comparison to training on the same number of samples from alternative desktop datasets under identical instruction-generation and filtering pipelines.
  - An analysis of whether model gains come mostly from OCR/text-heavy cases or genuinely from visual grounding of small icons and controls.
- The results generally support the paper’s central claim that data quality matters, but the evidence is strongest for in-domain desktop grounding and less conclusive for the broader “general-purpose computer-use agents” framing.
- Dataset and metric choice are appropriate for grounding accuracy, but the paper’s own limitation section admits it does not evaluate end-to-end task completion, which is a significant gap for the overall computer-use-agent narrative.

### Writing & Clarity
- The paper is mostly understandable, and the section structure is sensible.
- The strongest clarity issues are not grammar but conceptual:
  - The reward definition in Section 4.1 / C.4 is hard to parse and appears duplicated in slightly different forms. The intent is understandable, but the presentation obscures the exact algorithm.
  - The distinction between the 700K SFT subset, the 10K RL subset, and the full 700K/900K/3.56M construction could be stated more crisply. As written, it is sometimes hard to tell whether the 700K instruction set is unique samples, generated prompts, or element-level instances.
  - The paper alternates between claims about “desktop grounding,” “computer-use agents,” and “cross-platform generalization.” These are related but not identical claims, and the transitions between them could be tighter.
- Figures and tables are generally informative:
  - Table 1 is useful for positioning the dataset.
  - Tables 2–4 directly support the main claims.
  - Tables 7–10 are good ablations.
  - Figure 3 and Figure 8 are helpful for understanding RL behavior.
- A small but important clarity issue: some table narratives describe averages “without UI-Vision” or “with UI-Vision,” and this should be emphasized more consistently because it changes the conclusion materially.

### Limitations & Broader Impact
- The paper does include a limitations section and it is better than average for this area.
- It acknowledges several key limitations:
  - Bias toward common applications.
  - Static keyframe-based annotations missing dynamics.
  - Annotation inconsistency and scalability limits.
  - Lack of end-to-end agentic task-completion evaluation.
- However, it misses some important limitations:
  - The dataset is restricted to open-source applications, which helps licensing but limits representativeness for real-world desktop usage.
  - The human demonstrations may overrepresent expert or semi-expert task behavior rather than novice user behavior.
  - The dataset generation pipeline relies on a proprietary or external labeling vendor and on synthetic instruction generation, which may introduce hidden biases.
  - The broader societal impact of better computer-use agents is underdeveloped. There is some ethics text, but not much discussion of misuse risks such as unauthorized UI manipulation, phishing-like workflows, or automation of harmful actions.
- The ethics statement is reassuring on privacy, but it does not deeply address security or dual-use risks, which are highly relevant for computer-use agents.

### Overall Assessment
This is a substantial and timely paper with a genuinely meaningful dataset contribution: GROUNDCUA seems to fill a real gap in dense, human-annotated desktop grounding data, and the empirical gains are strong enough to take seriously. The main limitation is not the existence of results, but the degree to which the paper overgeneralizes from grounding benchmarks to broader computer-use-agent capability, while relying on selective comparisons and limited statistical analysis. The dataset and model are likely valuable to the community, and the contribution stands, but for ICLR’s bar I would want more rigorous controlled ablations, clearer evidence about the unique value of human demonstrations versus instruction generation, and a more cautious framing of the “general-purpose” and “state-of-the-art” claims.

# Neutral Reviewer
## Balanced Review

### Summary
This paper introduces GROUNDCUA, a large-scale desktop GUI grounding dataset built from human expert demonstrations, and GROUNDNEXT, 3B/7B grounding models trained on it via SFT and RL. The core claim is that dense, human-verified annotations and carefully generated instruction data enable substantially better data efficiency than prior desktop grounding efforts, yielding strong results on several grounding benchmarks and competitive agentic performance on OSWorld-Verified.

### Strengths
1. **Substantial dataset contribution with genuine desktop coverage.**  
   GROUNDCUA contains 55,568 screenshots, 87 applications, and 3.56M+ annotated UI elements, with dense annotations averaging 64 elements per screenshot. Compared with prior datasets, this is a strong contribution in both scale and annotation density, and the paper clearly positions it as filling a gap in expert-annotated desktop grounding.

2. **High annotation quality and realistic data collection pipeline.**  
   The dataset is derived from human demonstrations rather than synthetic traversal or accessibility-tree scraping, which the paper argues yields more realistic interface states. The authors also describe multi-stage quality assurance, annotator training, and a human validation study of generated instructions with a reported 4% error rate, which supports the claim of high-quality supervision.

3. **Strong empirical performance and data efficiency claims.**  
   GROUNDNEXT-3B and -7B achieve strong benchmark results across ScreenSpot-Pro, OSWorld-G, MMBench-GUI, ScreenSpot-v2, and UI-Vision. The paper repeatedly shows that 700K SFT samples outperform much larger prior corpora such as JEDI’s 9M training samples, which is a meaningful result if the comparisons are apples-to-apples.

4. **Interesting analysis of instruction types and RL design.**  
   The paper does more than report final numbers: it ablates direct, functional, and spatial instruction types, compares reward granularities, and studies GRPO vs. RLOO. This is useful for the community because it helps explain what kinds of supervision matter for GUI grounding.

5. **Open release commitment and reproducibility-oriented framing.**  
   The authors promise release of the dataset, model weights, code, evaluation scripts, and annotation guidelines. For ICLR, this is a positive signal because strong reproducibility and community utility matter, especially for large benchmark/dataset papers.

### Weaknesses
1. **Novelty is somewhat incremental relative to the current GUI grounding literature.**  
   The paper’s main advance is primarily dataset scale/quality plus straightforward SFT/RL training, rather than a new modeling method or learning principle. While the dataset is valuable, the modeling side relies on a standard base model (Qwen2.5-VL-Instruct) and common SFT/RL machinery, so the methodological novelty may be limited for ICLR’s bar.

2. **Several claims appear stronger than the evidence provided.**  
   The paper claims broad cross-platform generalization and strong agentic utility, but the training data is desktop-centric and the gains on mobile/web are mixed. Some benchmark tables show strong results, yet the paper sometimes extrapolates from benchmark accuracy to “reliable computer-use agents” without full end-to-end task completion evidence.

3. **Evaluation may be vulnerable to benchmark overlap and benchmark-selection bias.**  
   The authors note that perfect train-test separation is not always possible due to annotation differences and repetitive desktop software. That acknowledgment is honest, but it also means the reported benchmark gains may partly reflect benchmark proximity to the training distribution, especially on in-domain desktop tasks.

4. **The RL contribution is modest and somewhat underwhelming.**  
   The paper emphasizes RL post-training, but the gains are generally small. The authors themselves describe RL as incremental and attribute limited improvement to a strong SFT ceiling. That is a fair analysis, but it weakens the significance of the RL component as a core contribution.

5. **Some important dataset/method details remain underspecified in the main paper.**  
   The paper references many appendix details, but the main text leaves unanswered questions about annotation cost, exact task selection criteria, deduplication thresholds, the balance of application coverage, and the full human demonstration protocol. For a dataset paper, these details are important for assessing scale, bias, and reuse.

6. **Limited evidence on robustness and real-world deployment.**  
   The paper explicitly says it does not perform full end-to-end agentic testing for task completion. Given ICLR’s interest in learning systems that generalize reliably, this is a meaningful gap: grounding accuracy alone is not sufficient to establish agent usefulness.

### Novelty & Significance
**Novelty:** Moderate. The dataset itself is a strong and timely contribution, especially because it is expert-annotated, dense, and desktop-focused. However, the learning recipe is conventional: SFT on curated data plus RL with shaped rewards. That makes the work more of a high-value systems/data contribution than a fundamentally novel ML method.

**Significance:** High. Desktop computer-use agents are an important and emerging area, and high-quality grounding data is genuinely scarce. If the release is as complete and clean as promised, GROUNDCUA could become a useful benchmark/training resource for the community.

**Clarity:** Good overall, though somewhat overloaded. The paper clearly states its high-level goals and results, and it provides extensive ablations. However, the many claims, benchmark tables, and appendix-dependent details make the main narrative somewhat diffuse.

**Reproducibility:** Above average for a paper of this type, assuming the promised release happens. The paper includes training details, RL design, and validation protocols, but full reproducibility still depends on access to the dataset, model weights, and exact benchmark evaluation setups.

**ICLR significance assessment:** Likely borderline-to-strong for a dataset/benchmark paper, but weaker if judged primarily as a modeling paper. ICLR typically expects either a clear algorithmic advance or a dataset that enables a distinctly new research direction; this work has the latter potential, but the modeling novelty is not especially high.

### Suggestions for Improvement
1. **Strengthen the end-to-end evaluation.**  
   Add or expand task-completion experiments, not just grounding accuracy, so the paper better supports claims about computer-use agents in realistic settings.

2. **Provide stronger controls for potential benchmark leakage or proximity.**  
   Report more detailed overlap analysis between GROUNDCUA and each benchmark, including application/version overlap and prompt/template similarity, to make the gains easier to trust.

3. **Clarify dataset construction and selection bias.**  
   Describe how tasks were chosen across the 87 applications, how representative they are of real desktop usage, and whether certain categories are overrepresented. A concise coverage/bias analysis would help.

4. **Make the RL contribution more rigorous or de-emphasize it.**  
   Since RL gains are modest, either deepen the RL study with more informative comparisons or reposition RL as a secondary refinement rather than a headline result.

5. **Report annotation cost and scaling implications.**  
   For a dataset paper, ICLR readers would benefit from knowing annotation effort, cost, throughput, and quality-control overhead. This would help assess the practicality of replicating or extending the approach.

6. **Add stronger robustness analyses.**  
   Evaluate across UI version changes, unseen applications, resolution shifts, and noisy instructions. This would better align with the paper’s claims about real-world reliability.

7. **Tighten the narrative around generalization.**  
   The paper should more carefully distinguish what is demonstrated on desktop from what is inferred for mobile/web. Present the cross-domain results as promising transfer, not broad generalization.



# Spark Finder Review
## How to Improve This Paper

### Missing Experiments (top 3-5 only)
1. Add a true data-source ablation that controls for scale, application diversity, and instruction format: train on equally sized subsets from GROUNDCUA, OS-Atlas, JEDI, Aguvis, and UGround with the same 700K/100K budgets. Right now the “high-quality data beats more data” claim is not fully credible because the compared corpora differ simultaneously in annotation density, task construction, and domain coverage.

2. Add an ablation isolating what matters in GROUNDCUA: dense human boxes vs. human demonstrations vs. generated instructions vs. OCR/context vs. category metadata. Without this, the paper cannot support the claim that the dataset’s expert demonstrations and dense annotations are the causal reason for the gains.

3. Add benchmark contamination and overlap analysis, especially for UI-Vision, ScreenSpotPro, OSWorld-G, and MMBench-GUI. Since the paper trains on open-source app families that overlap semantically and sometimes structurally with benchmark apps, the current state-of-the-art claims are vulnerable to leakage concerns.

4. Add stronger RL baselines and reward ablations on the same model/dataset setting, including GRPO, binary reward, continuous reward, and a comparable judge-based setup if feasible. The current RL section shows only a modest gain, so the claim that GROUNDCUA is particularly effective for RL is not convincing without broader and fairer comparisons.

5. Add a task-completion agentic evaluation on more than OSWorld-Verified, with the same planner and identical execution budget across baselines. ICLR-level claims about “computer-use agents” require showing that better grounding actually improves end-to-end task success, not just point prediction accuracy.

### Deeper Analysis Needed (top 3-5 only)
1. Analyze performance by element type, target size, resolution, and spatial relation difficulty. The central claim is that dense high-resolution desktop annotations help fine-grained grounding, but the paper does not quantify where the gains come from or whether they concentrate on tiny icons and dense screens.

2. Add a calibration/error analysis for “near misses” versus true failures, ideally with success under relaxed IoU or distance thresholds. The paper argues many errors are acceptable localization misses, but without a principled analysis the accuracy metric may understate or mischaracterize actual grounding quality.

3. Report variance over multiple random seeds for SFT and RL. With only single-run results, especially for RL, it is hard to tell whether the gains are robust or just seed-sensitive, which matters for an ICLR claim about a training recipe.

4. Quantify the effect of open-source app similarity on generalization. The paper speculates that LibreOffice-like apps help, but it does not measure transfer by application family, which is necessary to support the claim that the dataset improves broad desktop robustness.

5. Explain why UI-Vision improves dramatically while some cross-domain scores remain weaker. The current discussion is narrative; what is missing is a systematic transfer analysis that shows whether GROUNDCUA primarily improves desktop grounding, icon recognition, or instruction following.

### Visualizations & Case Studies
1. Show side-by-side examples where competing models miss small targets but GROUNDNEXT succeeds, especially for icons, menus, and dense toolbars. This would directly test the claim that dense annotations improve fine-grained desktop grounding.

2. Add failure case visualizations for the main error modes: near misses, wrong semantic target, and spatial-relation failures. Without these, the reader cannot tell whether the model is truly grounding better or just benefiting from easy benchmark structure.

3. Provide qualitative examples of the instruction-generation pipeline, including both successful and flawed generated instructions. Since generated instructions are a major part of the training set, examples of failure modes are needed to judge whether the data construction is reliable.

4. Visualize benchmark overlap or similarity between training apps and evaluation screenshots. This would reveal whether gains reflect real generalization or memorization of app UI patterns.

### Obvious Next Steps
1. Build a unified grounding + action dataset that links the dense screenshot annotations to actual task execution traces. The paper currently stops at grounding accuracy; for ICLR, the obvious next step is showing that the dataset can train agents that complete tasks.

2. Extend the dataset beyond open-source desktop apps to proprietary and more dynamic environments. The current scope is too narrow to justify “general-purpose” computer-use claims without demonstrating transfer to the broader desktop ecosystem.

3. Design richer reward signals using the dense annotations already collected. The paper itself admits the RL reward is simplistic; the next step should be a principled reward grounded in box geometry, element type, and task semantics rather than a coarse distance binning.

4. Study continual adaptation to app updates and new versions. Since desktop UIs drift frequently, a serious grounding dataset paper should test whether models trained on GROUNDCUA remain useful under version changes, not just on static benchmarks.

# Final Consolidated Review
## Summary
This paper introduces GROUNDCUA, a large-scale desktop grounding dataset built from human demonstrations and dense UI element annotations, together with GROUNDNEXT, 3B/7B grounding models trained with SFT and a small RL post-training stage. The dataset is genuinely substantial for desktop GUI grounding, and the empirical results are strong on several in-domain benchmarks, but the paper repeatedly stretches its claims beyond what the evidence cleanly supports.

## Strengths
- **The dataset is a real contribution for desktop grounding.** GROUNDCUA covers 55,568 screenshots, 87 applications, and 3.56M+ UI element annotations, with very dense labeling (about 64 elements per screenshot on average). Compared to prior desktop grounding corpora, this is meaningfully larger and much denser, and the human-demonstration collection pipeline is a better fit for realistic desktop states than accessibility-tree scraping or synthetic UI generation.
- **The paper provides solid evidence that this data is useful for training.** GROUNDNEXT-3B and -7B achieve strong results on the grounding benchmarks the paper targets, and the ablations on instruction types, reward granularity, and dataset scaling are useful. The fact that 700K SFT samples can outperform much larger prior corpora is a credible and important finding, even if the comparison is not perfectly matched.

## Weaknesses
- **The paper overclaims generalization and “computer-use agent” capability.** Most of the evidence is still about point grounding accuracy, not end-to-end task completion. The OSWorld-Verified result is useful, but it relies on an external planner and is still only one system-level evaluation; it does not justify broad claims of reliable computer-use agents or robust cross-platform generalization.
- **The causal story behind the gains is under-controlled.** The paper argues that high-quality human demonstrations and dense annotations are the reason GROUNDCUA works better, but it does not isolate that effect cleanly from other factors like application coverage, instruction generation, OCR/context usage, or benchmark proximity. The “high-quality data beats more data” claim is directionally plausible, but not fully proven by the current experiments.
- **The RL component is weaker than the framing suggests.** The RL gains are modest, and the authors themselves acknowledge that the SFT stage already creates a strong ceiling. That makes RL look more like a minor refinement than a core advance, so it should not be presented as a headline contribution.
- **Evaluation confidence is limited by missing robustness analysis.** There is no statistical testing, no seed variance, and little analysis of benchmark overlap or leakage risk. Given that several reported improvements are not huge, this makes the state-of-the-art claims less secure than the tables alone suggest.

## Nice-to-Haves
- A clearer breakdown of where the gains come from: small icons vs. text-heavy elements, dense screens vs. sparse screens, and desktop-only vs. cross-domain cases.
- More explicit overlap analysis between GROUNDCUA and the evaluation benchmarks, especially for desktop applications that may share UI structure or semantics.
- More thorough reporting of annotation cost, throughput, and selection criteria for the 87 applications, since that would help readers assess how scalable the dataset construction process really is.

## Novel Insights
The most interesting insight is that for desktop grounding, annotation density may matter as much as raw scale: the dataset is not just large, it is packed with small, fine-grained targets that standard automated pipelines tend to miss. The paper also suggests a practical training recipe for this domain: strong SFT on carefully curated demonstrations gives most of the benefit, while RL mostly polishes the model rather than transforming it, which is an important reality check for current GUI-agent training.

## Potentially Missed Related Work
- **Grounding datasets derived from accessibility trees or synthetic UI generation** — relevant as the closest comparison class for desktop grounding; the paper cites several, but a fuller discussion of their annotation noise and coverage tradeoffs would help contextualize GROUNDCUA’s advantage.
- **Recent RL-for-GUI-agent papers such as GUI-G1 / InfiGUI-G1 / GTA1** — relevant because they frame the same post-training design space and help assess whether GROUNDCUA’s RL findings are actually distinctive or just consistent with existing trends.

## Suggestions
- Add at least one stronger controlled experiment that holds model, instruction format, and sample count fixed while swapping only the data source, so the “data quality” claim is more convincing.
- Add a more direct end-to-end agentic evaluation beyond OSWorld-Verified, or at minimum present the current agentic result more cautiously as a planner-assisted system test rather than evidence of general computer-use competence.
- Include a benchmark overlap / similarity audit and a seed-variance report to make the performance claims more trustworthy.
