# Harsh Critic Review
## Section-by-Section Critical Review

### Title & Abstract
- **Title accuracy:** The title accurately reflects the core idea: using uncertainty scores from an auxiliary model to improve interpretable models. However, it leans slightly into the framing of "learning interpretable models," whereas the technique is technically a *training data reweighting/sampling* method that sits upstream of model training.
- **Abstract clarity:** The abstract clearly outlines the problem (size-accuracy trade-off), the method (Dirichlet Process + BayesOpt + 1D uncertainty projection), and key results. 
- **Unsupported/Overstated claims:** The abstract states the technique "needs only one hyperparameter to be set." Section 3.3 clarifies this is true only *if* the user accepts fixed default box constraints for the seven optimization variables and the `scale` parameter. In practice, BayesOpt search boundaries significantly influence convergence, especially given the highly non-convex objective induced by non-differentiable models. This claim should be tempered to acknowledge that boundary tuning may be necessary for new domains or significantly different dataset scales.

### Introduction & Motivation
- **Motivation & Gap:** The motivation is solid. The tension between model size and accuracy is well-established, and the limitation of "bespoke levers" (pruning, L1, etc.) is a valid pain point. The gap relative to Ghose & Ravindran (2020) is clearly stated: replacing rigid density-tree geometry with a flexible oracle-driven distribution.
- **Contributions stated:** Contributions are explicit and well-structured. 
- **Over-claiming/Under-selling:** The introduction highlights relative improvements of "~100% in some cases" (and Table 1 shows values >200% for `Sensorless` at size=1). While mathematically correct, these extreme relative gains occur when the baseline absolute F1 is very low, which can be misleading regarding practical utility. The introduction underplays the technique's heavy reliance on the *quality* of the uncertainty oracle. If the oracle's uncertainties are noisy, miscalibrated, or uncorrelated with the true decision boundary, the 1D signal degrades. Treating the oracle as "peripheral" (§2) risks under-appreciating its role as the actual bottleneck for the method's success.

### Method / Approach
- **Clarity & Reproducibility:** The pipeline (Algorithm 1) is clearly described and reproducible. The use of `hyperopt`/TPE and the 60/20/20 split are standard. 
- **Key Assumptions & Logical Gaps:** 
  - **Conceptual Gap (Eq. 1 vs. Implementation):** Equation 1 frames the objective as finding an optimal distribution $p'$ over the joint input-label space: $\max_{p'} \text{acc}(\text{train}(p', \eta), p)$. However, the actual implementation never learns a distribution over $\mathcal{X}$. It learns a distribution over a 1D scalar $u(x)$ and uses it for *importance sampling/reweighting* from the original empirical distribution. This collapses all multivariate geometric information into a single dimension. Two instances with identical uncertainty scores but vastly different feature vectors are treated identically during sampling. The paper should explicitly reframe this as **uncertainty-based instance reweighting** rather than "learning the training distribution" over the feature space.
  - **Oracle Dependency:** The method assumes the oracle's margin uncertainty correlates with proximity to the decision boundary. While margin uncertainty is standard, the method's success is entirely contingent on a well-calibrated, high-capacity oracle. There is no theoretical or empirical guarantee that an oracle's uncertainty landscape aligns with what a size-constrained model (like a shallow tree) actually needs to see to improve.
  - **Heuristic Smoothing:** The smoothing step (Algorithm 4) is presented as a practical fix for a "spiky" optimization landscape, but it essentially performs a rank-based binning transformation. This destroys the absolute scale of uncertainty differences. The justification that it's "computationally cheap" (§A.5) doesn't address potential information loss.
- **Edge Cases/Failure Modes:** Not discussed: What happens if the oracle is overconfident (low variance in uncertainties)? What if the dataset has perfectly separable clusters where boundary uncertainty is effectively zero everywhere? The method lacks graceful degradation analysis.

### Experiments & Results
- **Do experiments test claims?** Broadly yes, across 13 datasets, two model families, and various sizes. The multivariate size and cross-feature-space experiments in the appendix successfully demonstrate versatility.
- **Baselines & Fairness:** The primary baseline is training on the unweighted original distribution ($p_o=1$). For ICLR, this is insufficient. There is a rich literature on *instance weighting*, *importance sampling*, and *reweighting for imbalanced boundaries* (e.g., focal loss variants, curriculum learning, active learning sampling strategies). Without comparing to simpler reweighting heuristics (e.g., upweighting by $u(x)$ directly, or using focal loss), it's unclear whether the complex DP+BayesOpt machinery is necessary or if a simpler uncertainty weighting scheme would yield similar gains.
- **Model Families:** Experiments focus exclusively on LPMs and CART (Breiman et al., 1984). While justified as "classically interpretable," the field has moved toward modern intrinsically interpretable models (e.g., Explainable Boosting Machines, Neural Additive Models, RuleFit, modern sparse trees). The absence of these baselines makes it difficult to assess whether the technique provides value beyond what modern regularized linear models or tree ensembles already achieve out-of-the-box.
- **Statistical Significance & Reporting:** Section 4.1.2 states: "If we can reject the null at a significance of p=0.1... we report $\delta F1_{test}$, else we report $\delta F1_{test} = 0$." This filtering introduces a reporting bias. It hides cases with high variance or marginal gains, artificially inflating the perceived reliability of the method in Table 1. Reporting confidence intervals or full distributions would be more transparent. The use of $p=0.1$ is also notably liberal; standard ML practice typically uses $0.05$ or $0.01$.
- **Cherry-picking:** The main table highlights large improvements but obscures the fact that for DTs across many datasets, improvements are frequently 0.00% or negligible (e.g., `cod-rna`, `higgs`, `phishing` for DTs). The method appears significantly more effective for LPMs than for trees, a trend that deserves explicit analysis rather than being buried in the table.

### Writing & Clarity
- **Clarity:** The writing is generally clear and well-structured. Figures 1 and 2 effectively convey the intuition. 
- **Confusing Aspects:** The distinction between learning a distribution over $\mathcal{X}$ vs. learning a weighting over $u(\mathcal{X})$ is blurred throughout §3. Revising the notation to explicitly frame this as optimizing instance weights $\mathbf{w} = g(u(x_i))$ would greatly improve mathematical precision.
- **Figures/Tables:** Table 1 is dense but informative. The appendix tables and figures (e.g., Fig. 9, Fig. 11) are helpful. The description of Algorithm 4 (smoothing) is somewhat algorithmic-heavy; a simple equation or visual explanation of the rank-binning would be clearer.

### Limitations & Broader Impact
- **Acknowledged Limitations:** The authors correctly identify high runtime due to derivative-free BayesOpt over non-differentiable models (Section 5) and suggest BoTorch/LogEI as mitigation.
- **Missed Limitations:** 
  - **Oracle Robustness:** As noted, no analysis of failure modes under poor oracle calibration or distribution shift between oracle training and interpretable model training.
  - **Bias & Fairness:** Reweighting training data based on an opaque oracle's uncertainty could inadvertently amplify biases towards certain subpopulations near the boundary. Since the goal is interpretability, preserving fairness guarantees is often critical. This is not discussed.
  - **Scalability:** The use of 10,000 instances for large datasets (e.g., Higgs, Covtype) limits conclusions about large-scale applicability. DP mixture sampling and repeated model training scale poorly beyond moderate $N$.
- **Societal Impact:** The ethics statement is standard. A brief discussion on how reweighting might affect the *faithfulness* of the interpretable model's explanations (since it's trained on a shifted marginal) would strengthen the paper.

### Overall Assessment
The paper proposes a practical, model-agnostic pipeline that leverages uncertainty sampling to improve the accuracy of size-constrained interpretable models. The empirical validation is extensive across datasets and model configurations, and the application to differing feature spaces is a nice touch. However, for ICLR standards, the paper has substantive gaps: the conceptual framing as "learning a distribution" over the input space mismatches the actual 1D reweighting implementation, which should be explicitly stated. The experimental evaluation lacks comparison to modern interpretability baselines and simpler uncertainty-reweighting strategies, making the added complexity of the DP+BayesOpt framework difficult to justify. Additionally, the statistical filtering at $p=0.1$ and the focus on very small model sizes (where baselines are inherently weak) temper the strength of the claims. The contribution is sound but incremental and methodologically framed in a way that overstates its novelty. Addressing the reweighting vs. distribution-learning distinction, adding robust modern baselines, and providing a thorough oracle ablation would significantly strengthen the work.

# Neutral Reviewer
## Balanced Review

### Summary
This paper proposes a model-agnostic framework to improve the accuracy of size-constrained interpretable models by learning an optimal training data distribution. It projects training instances to a one-dimensional uncertainty score using a probabilistic oracle, fits a Dirichlet Process-based Infinite Beta Mixture Model to these scores, and uses Bayesian Optimization to tune the distribution parameters for maximal validation accuracy of the target model. Extensive experiments demonstrate that this resampling strategy consistently boosts performance across various model families, size constraints, and even differing feature spaces between the oracle and target model.

### Strengths
1. **Comprehensive Empirical Validation:** The authors conduct rigorous experiments across 13 standard datasets, two interpretable model families (LPMs and CART DTs), and two oracle types (GBM, RF) with varying size constraints (§4.1). The use of proper statistical testing (Wilcoxon signed-rank, paired t-tests) and multiple independent trials strengthens the reliability of the reported improvements (Table 1, §A.8).
2. **Practical and Flexible Design:** The technique is explicitly designed for real-world applicability, accommodating non-differentiable loss functions via derivative-free Bayesian Optimization (§3.3). It requires tuning only a single primary hyperparameter (optimization budget $T$), with reasonable default bounds provided for others (§A.3). The distribution smoothing transformation (§A.5, Algorithm 4) further demonstrates a thoughtful mitigation of non-smooth optimization landscapes.
3. **Novel Cross-Domain Application and Size Agnosticism:** The method successfully generalizes to multivariate size definitions (e.g., simultaneously constraining `max_depth` and `num_trees` in GBMs, §A.12, Fig. 9) and works even when the oracle and target model operate on entirely different feature spaces (e.g., GRU on character sequences vs. DT on n-grams for surname prediction, §A.13). This flexibility significantly broadens its utility.
4. **Strong Competitive Benchmarking:** Beyond standard baselines, the authors effectively demonstrate that the technique elevates classic algorithms to be competitive with modern, task-specific methods. For instance, CART enhanced by this method matches or exceeds Iterative Mistake Minimization (IMM) for explainable clustering (§A.10.2), and a simple RBFN approaches Stochastic Neighbor Compression (SNC) for prototype-based classification (§A.10.3).

### Weaknesses
1. **High Computational Overhead:** The reliance on black-box Bayesian Optimization necessitates a high number of expensive training/evaluation iterations. As acknowledged in §5, a single configuration can take ~1 hour with `hyperopt` ($T=3000$), limiting scalability to large datasets or rapid prototyping pipelines, despite preliminary speedups shown with `BoTorch`.
2. **Lack of Robustness Analysis to Oracle Quality:** The method's efficacy heavily depends on the oracle providing accurate, well-calibrated uncertainty scores. However, there is no ablation testing oracle degradation, miscalibration, or using a weak/poorly-fitted oracle. It remains unclear how sensitive the gains are to failures in the oracle’s probability estimates or feature mismatch.
3. **Relative Metrics Can Obscure Absolute Performance:** Results are primarily reported as percentage improvements relative to the baseline ($\delta F1$). While useful, this can exaggerate perceived progress when baselines are extremely poor (e.g., Sensorless LPM at size=1 shows >200% improvement in Table 1, but absolute F1 scores are not prominently contextualized in the main text). Absolute performance metrics are necessary to gauge practical utility.
4. **Limited Conceptual Baselines:** The paper compares mainly against training on the original distribution and one prior density-tree method. It does not benchmark against conceptually related strategies like curriculum learning, difficulty-based sampling, or standard active learning uncertainty sampling, which would better contextualize where the proposed method sits in the data selection landscape.

### Novelty & Significance
**Novelty:** Moderate to High. While learning training distributions to improve small models builds on prior work (Ghose & Ravindran, 2020), this paper introduces a novel, robust pipeline that combines uncertainty-based 1D projection, nonparametric Dirichlet Process mixture modeling, and derivative-free optimization. The ability to seamlessly bridge different feature spaces and support multivariate size constraints is a distinct methodological contribution.
**Clarity:** High. The paper is well-structured, with a clear progression from problem formulation to algorithm design and empirical validation. Mathematical notation is consistent, algorithmic steps are explicitly laid out (Algorithm 1), and the appendix provides necessary implementation details and supplementary analyses.
**Reproducibility:** High. The authors provide code in the supplementary material, use publicly available datasets, clearly report data splits (60/20/20), optimization budgets, random trial setups, and default parameter bounds. The method relies on standard, well-maintained libraries, facilitating replication.
**Significance:** High for the interpretability and constrained-model communities. As regulatory and deployment pressures demand smaller, more transparent models without sacrificing accuracy, a general-purpose tool to close the size-accuracy gap is highly relevant. It offers a practical, plug-and-play enhancement for standard algorithms like CART and linear models.

### Suggestions for Improvement
1. **Report Absolute Metrics Alongside Relative Gains:** Include tables or plots showing absolute $F1$ (or AUC/Accuracy) scores for baseline vs. proposed method. This will help reviewers and practitioners understand the real-world performance floor and ceiling, especially for extremely constrained models where relative gains may be inflated.
2. **Conduct Oracle Sensitivity Experiments:** Add an ablation where oracle quality is intentionally degraded (e.g., by training on less data, using a simpler model, or adding label noise/calibration errors) to test the robustness of the uncertainty projection. This will clarify the method's failure modes and practical dependency on strong oracles.
3. **Compare Against Standard Curriculum/Active Learning Sampling:** Include baselines like difficulty-based curriculum learning, margin-based active learning sampling, or standard instance selection heuristics. This would strengthen the paper by demonstrating whether the DP-IBMM + BayesOpt pipeline yields qualitatively different or superior data weighting strategies compared to established alternatives.
4. **Include Optimization Convergence Analysis:** Provide learning curves showing validation performance vs. BayesOpt iterations for varying budgets $T$. This would empirically justify the chosen iteration limits, demonstrate the stability of the surrogate model, and provide practical guidance for setting $T$ efficiently on new datasets.

# Spark Finder Review
## How to Improve This Paper

### Missing Experiments (top 3-5 only)
1. Add a compute-equivalent baseline that trains the size-constrained interpretable model `T` times using uniform random subsampling or standard uncertainty sampling, then selects the best validation performer. Without it, the claimed ~10–100% accuracy gains are likely artifacts of a massive 3000-iteration search budget rather than the proposed Dirichlet Process mixture.
2. Head-to-head comparisons against established sample selection/reweighting baselines (e.g., AdaBoost instance weights, focal loss, entropy sampling, or Tomek-linked undersampling). The paper asserts bespoke distribution learning is necessary but lacks experiments ruling out significantly simpler alternatives.
3. Run a systematic ablation fixing `po` (original data mixing proportion) to 0 or 0.5 and fixing `Ns` to `|Dtrain|`. If performance collapses, the method's success is heavily reliant on falling back to standard training rather than the DP-based distribution; if it holds, the 7-variable Bayesian optimization is unnecessarily complex.

### Deeper Analysis Needed (top 3-5 only)
1. Rigorously analyze why the mandatory "flattening" transformation (Algorithm 4) is applied. By forcibly binning oracle uncertainty scores into a near-uniform distribution, you destroy the actual geometric signal; you must show how the learned Beta mixture weights map to these flattened bins to prove the oracle meaningfully guides sampling.
2. Quantify method sensitivity to oracle calibration and predictive quality. GBM/RF uncertainty scores are notoriously uncalibrated in high dimensions; you must report performance degradation curves when using a weak oracle, a deliberately miscalibrated oracle, or a non-ensemble baseline to prove the approach doesn't silently degrade to `po → 1`.
3. Provide statistical convergence diagnostics for the Bayesian Optimization loop. Plot validation accuracy vs. iteration with confidence intervals to demonstrate whether the 3000-budget search consistently discovers better distributions or merely traverses a noisy, high-variance landscape prone to validation set overfitting.

### Visualizations & Case Studies
1. Plot the learned sampling density (active Beta component weights) over the 1D uncertainty axis across iterations for 2–3 diverse datasets. This will immediately reveal if the method consistently targets decision boundaries, over-samples specific low-confidence regions, or defaults to an uninformative distribution.
2. Provide side-by-side decision boundary visualizations (like Figure 1, but for a multi-class or higher-D dataset) showing exactly which splits/regions shift when moving from baseline to optimized training. This exposes whether the method learns genuinely better boundaries or simply chases validation noise.
3. Include a failure case for the cross-representation claim (e.g., GRU oracle → n-gram DT). Show a scenario where the oracle's continuous uncertainty misaligns with the discrete feature space of the target model, demonstrating the limits of transferring uncertainty without shared features.

### Obvious Next Steps
1. Perform a formal runtime-accuracy trade-off analysis across `T ∈ [100, 5000]` instead of treating optimization budget as a fixed, untuned black box. ICLR requires transparent convergence behavior and clear guidance on when to stop, especially given the paper's claim of "practical convenience."
2. Replace the heuristic binning/flattening step with a differentiable smoothing approach or provide a rigorous statistical justification for it. The current step is computationally cheap but mathematically destructive, masking potential instability in fitting Dirichlet Processes to discrete, unsmoothed uncertainty scores.
3. Extend empirical validation to inherently interpretable neural architectures (e.g., Explainable Boosting Machines or shallow differentiable rule learners) with gradient-based losses. This proves the framework generalizes beyond the specific non-differentiable niche and doesn't merely exist to circumvent gradient computation.

# Final Consolidated Review
## Summary
This paper introduces a model-agnostic technique to improve the size-accuracy trade-off for constrained interpretable models (e.g., shallow trees, sparse linear models) by learning an optimal training data distribution. The method projects instances to a 1D uncertainty manifold via an auxiliary oracle, models this projection with a Dirichlet Process-based mixture, and uses Bayesian Optimization to tune sampling parameters without requiring differentiable losses. While the framework is flexible and demonstrates empirical gains across diverse datasets and cross-modal setups, the experimental validation lacks necessary conceptual baselines, relies on a problematic statistical filtering scheme, and does not adequately isolate the contribution of its complex optimization machinery from brute-force search effects.

## Strengths
- **Broad empirical validation across model families and constraints:** The authors rigorously test the technique across 13 standard datasets, multiple interpretable model families (LPMs, CART), and varying size constraints, including multivariate constraints (e.g., `max_depth` + `num_trees` for GBMs). The statistical testing protocol and repeated trials provide a robust foundation for the reported trends.
- **Effective cross-representation uncertainty transfer:** The demonstration that oracle uncertainties can guide target models operating on entirely different feature spaces (e.g., GRU character sequences → DT on n-grams) is a practically valuable contribution. It shows that boundary proximity is a modality-agnostic signal that can be leveraged to bridge high-capacity black-box models with constrained interpretable ones.
- **Practical engineering for non-differentiable losses:** By framing the distribution search as a black-box optimization problem over uncertainty scores, the method circumvents gradient requirements. The provision of well-justified default bounds for the optimization variables, effectively reducing user tuning to a single iteration budget, makes the pipeline accessible for practitioners working with legacy or non-differentiable learners.

## Weaknesses
- **Missing compute-equivalent and simpler reweighting baselines:** The claimed accuracy improvements cannot be confidently attributed to the Dirichlet Process + Bayesian Optimization formulation because the paper lacks a compute-equivalent baseline. Sampling and evaluating $T$ uniformly random or margin-weighted subsets (without the complex DP machinery) is absent, as are comparisons to established reweighting/curriculum strategies (e.g., focal loss, AdaBoost instance weights, standard active learning sampling). Without these, it remains unclear whether the gains stem from the proposed distributional modeling or simply from a large 3000-iteration search budget over data subsets.
- **Biased statistical reporting and unexplained model-family disparity:** Section 4.1.2 explicitly filters results at a liberal $p=0.1$ threshold, replacing non-significant outcomes with `0.00`. This reporting bias artificially stabilizes the main results table and obscures the true variance of the method. More critically, Table 1 reveals a stark performance gap: while LPMs show substantial relative gains, CART decision trees frequently yield `0.00%` or negligible improvements across multiple datasets. This fundamental limitation is neither analyzed nor explained, undermining the claim of broad applicability.
- **Method fragility regarding oracle calibration and heuristic flattening:** The entire pipeline depends on the oracle's margin uncertainty accurately reflecting the informative regions for the target model. No ablation explores how performance degrades under oracle miscalibration, label noise, or weak predictor capacity. Furthermore, the mandatory "flattening" transformation (Algorithm 4) forcibly bins uncertainty scores into a near-uniform distribution to smooth the optimization landscape. This rank-preserving transformation destroys the absolute scale of uncertainty differences, potentially masking the true contribution of the oracle and acting as an unexamined heuristic that may induce overfitting to the validation split.

## Nice-to-Haves
- Reporting absolute $F1$ scores alongside relative percentage gains would better contextualize performance floors, particularly for highly constrained models where baseline accuracy is already near randomness.
- Providing optimization convergence curves (validation accuracy vs. BayesOpt iterations) would offer practical guidance on tuning the iteration budget $T$ and demonstrate search stability.
- Extending the evaluation to modern gradient-based interpretable architectures (e.g., Explainable Boosting Machines, Neural Additive Models) would clarify whether the method's design is strictly necessary for non-differentiable niches or provides broader utility.

## Novel Insights
The paper demonstrates that projecting high-dimensional training data into a 1D uncertainty manifold, then iteratively fitting a nonparametric Dirichlet Process mixture via derivative-free optimization, can effectively approximate an optimal sampling distribution for capacity-constrained models. A genuinely insightful contribution is the decoupling of feature spaces: showing that uncertainty scores from a high-capacity sequence model can successfully guide the splits of a discrete n-gram decision tree. This establishes that decision boundary proximity is a transferable, architecture-agnostic signal that can be harnessed to inject non-differentiable, size-constrained learners with information typically reserved for gradient-based training.

## Potentially Missed Related Work
- Literature on **curriculum learning** and **example reweighting** (e.g., dynamic weighting via loss magnitude, focal loss variants, or difficulty-based sampling) should be discussed. The proposed method functionally operates as an advanced, learned instance selection strategy; positioning it explicitly against these paradigms would clarify its theoretical placement.
- Work on **Bayesian optimization for data subset selection** and **coreset construction for constrained models** would provide useful context for the optimization budget and sampling mechanics.

## Suggestions
- **Add a compute-matched random/uncertainty search baseline:** Run the target model on $T$ randomly sampled or margin-sorted subsets and report the best validation performer. This directly tests whether the DP+Bo pipeline outperforms naive search under equivalent compute.
- **Include standard reweighting/curriculum baselines:** Compare against straightforward strategies like upweighting by $1-u(x)$, focal loss variants, or active learning uncertainty sampling. This isolates the marginal gain of the Dirichlet Process formulation.
- **Remove the $p=0.1$ score filtering:** Report full distributions of $\delta F1$ with confidence intervals across all datasets and sizes, allowing reviewers to assess actual reliability and variance without artificial zeroing.
- **Conduct oracle robustness & ablation studies:** Test the method with deliberately degraded, uncalibrated, or simpler oracles (e.g., logistic regression) to quantify failure modes. Additionally, ablate the "flattening" step and visualize the learned Beta mixture weights over both raw and flattened uncertainty axes to prove the oracle meaningfully guides sampling rather than acting as noise.
