# Harsh Critic Review
## Section-by-Section Critical Review

### Title & Abstract
- **Accuracy & Reflection:** The title accurately reflects the core mechanism (uncertainty oracle + training distribution learning) and target (interpretable models). The abstract clearly outlines the problem (size-accuracy trade-off), the method (Dirichlet Process + BayesOpt over oracle uncertainty scores), and key results (versatility, single hyperparam claim, cross-feature-space applicability).
- **Potential Overstatement:** The claim that the method "only needs one hyperparameter to be set" (Abstract) is misleading. While $T$ (iterations) is the *explicit* budget, the BayesOpt wrapper requires box constraints for 7 variables ($\alpha, a, b, a', b', N_s, p_o$). The authors acknowledge in §A.3 that reasonable defaults exist, but for a practitioner, tuning these bounds (or validating them on new domains) is non-trivial. This should be tempered in the abstract to avoid overclaiming ease-of-use.

### Introduction & Motivation
- **Problem Motivation & Gap:** The size-accuracy trade-off in XAI is well-motivated with strong references. The gap identified (bespoke hyperparameter levers being insufficient, and prior density-tree methods being limited to CART) is clearly stated and justifies the proposed extension.
- **Contributions:** Clearly enumerated. However, the introduction underplays a critical dependency: the quality and calibration of the *uncertainty oracle*. The method assumes oracle uncertainty reliably proxies instance difficulty/boundary proximity (citing Lewis & Catlett, 1994). If the oracle is poorly calibrated, biased, or overfits distributional artifacts, the learned sampling distribution may degrade rather than help the interpretable model. This assumption deserves explicit qualification in the introduction.
- **Calibration of Claims:** The claim of making "fairly old techniques competitive with recent task-specialized techniques" (§4.2) is supported in the appendix, but the introduction frames it as a primary outcome. ICLR reviewers typically expect the main paper to contain the core competitiveness comparison rather than relegate it to §A.10.

### Method / Approach
- **Clarity & Reproducibility:** Algorithm 1 and the step-by-step breakdown in §3.1/3.3 are clear. The use of a DP-based Infinite Beta Mixture Model (IBMM) over 1D uncertainty scores is a sound way to parameterize a flexible 1D density. Appendix sections A.2-A.4 provide necessary implementation details (CRP sampling, initialization, multiset sampling).
- **Assumptions & Justification:** The choice of margin uncertainty (Eq 2) is justified (§A.1), but the broader assumption that optimizing sampling distribution parameters via black-box BayesOpt will yield a unimodal, smooth optimization landscape is questionable. The authors correctly identify this as a practical issue and introduce a non-differentiable, rank-based "flattening" transform (§A.5, Algorithm 4). While pragmatically effective, this is an ad-hoc heuristic that breaks the differentiable link between $\Phi$ and the objective. A more principled alternative (e.g., kernel density estimation on scores, or directly learning instance weights via differentiable approximations) is not discussed.
- **Logical Gaps:** Equation 1 formalizes the objective as finding $p'$ to maximize $\text{acc}(\text{train}_{F,f}(p', \eta), p)$. In practice, $p'$ is never explicitly constructed or compared to a baseline distribution; it is implicitly represented by the sampling weights induced by the IBMM. The disconnect between the formal objective (distribution $p'$) and the actual implementation (sampling with replacement from a finite set weighted by IBMM probabilities) is minor but mathematically imprecise. The paper should clarify that $p'$ is an *empirical* training distribution induced by the sampling process, not a continuous density over the original space.

### Experiments & Results
- **Testing Claims:** The experimental suite is extensive: 13 datasets, two model families (LPM, DT), two oracles (GBM, RF), and multiple size constraints. This thoroughly tests the versatility claim.
- **Baselines & Missing Ablations:** Comparisons to the density-tree approach (§A.9) and task-specific methods (IMM, ExShallow, SNC, ProtoNN) are appropriate. However, a critical missing ablation is against **simpler instance weighting/reweighting strategies**. For example, does this complex DP+BayesOpt pipeline outperform basic hard-example mining, loss-based instance weighting, or uniform subsampling with cross-validation? Without this, it's unclear if the gains stem from the DP parameterization or simply from *any* non-uniform training distribution. Appendix A.6 only ablates the smoothing step.
- **Statistical Reporting & Significance:** The paper reports relative percentage improvements $\delta F_1$ (Eq 3). This metric is highly sensitive to the baseline absolute score. For example, Table 1 shows a $216.47\%$ improvement for `Sensorless lpm gbm` at size=1. If the baseline $F_1$ is ~0.1, this means +0.21 absolute improvement, which is meaningful but context is missing. **Reporting absolute $F_1$ scores alongside relative improvements is necessary for ICLR standards.** Additionally, the significance threshold of $p=0.1$ (§4.1.2) is unusually lenient for machine learning literature; $p<0.05$ or proper confidence intervals on the relative gains would strengthen the claims.
- **Cherry-picking/Robustness:** The authors fairly report negative improvements (~1.88% of cases in §A.8) and explain why improvements diminish at larger sizes. This transparency is commendable.

### Writing & Clarity
- **Clarity Issues:** The exposition of the Dirichlet Process parameters ($\alpha, a, b, a', b'$) and their mapping to active components is standard for Bayesian nonparametrics but may be dense for a general ML audience. A brief intuitive mapping of how changing $\alpha$ or the shape parameters affects the sampling behavior (e.g., "higher $\alpha$ encourages more modes, allowing the sampler to focus on multiple distinct uncertainty regions") would improve readability.
- **Figures/Tables:** Table 1 is information-dense but difficult to interpret without baseline absolute scores. Figure 2 is excellent and clearly communicates the pipeline. The reliance on the appendix for critical experiments (e.g., full results, competitiveness, multivariate sizes, different feature spaces) is heavy; ICLR prefers main-paper coverage of at least the primary empirical contributions, with the appendix as supplementary evidence.

### Limitations & Broader Impact
- **Acknowledged Limitations:** Runtime is honestly addressed (§5 and §A.11). The authors correctly identify TPE (`hyperopt`) as a bottleneck and demonstrate that modern GP-based BayesOpt (`BoTorch` with `LogEI`) yields ~20-30x speedups with 200 iterations. This is a strong practical contribution.
- **Missed Limitations:** 
  1. **Oracle Dependency & Failure Modes:** What happens when the oracle fails? E.g., on noisy, non-separable, or adversarially perturbed data where uncertainty scores do not correlate with class boundaries. The method has no fallback mechanism discussed.
  2. **Distribution Shift Risk:** Training on a learned $p'$ that diverges from the original data distribution risks learning spurious correlations or rules that don't generalize to the true test distribution, even if $D_{val}$ and $D_{test}$ are i.i.d. in the current setup. Deploying such models in OOD settings could amplify failures. This is not discussed.
  3. **Interpretability of the Sampling Process:** The technique improves accuracy but obscures *why* certain rules were chosen. Since the training data is resampled via a black-box optimizer, the final interpretable model's decision boundaries are indirectly shaped by the oracle's uncertainty landscape, not the true data manifold. This subtle shift in interpretability semantics should be acknowledged.
- **Broader Impact:** The ethics statement is standard. Given the focus on "interpretable" models, a brief discussion on the risk of *plausible but incorrect* rule generation (due to oracle-guided resampling) in high-stakes domains would align with ICLR's expectations for responsible AI research.

### Overall Assessment
This paper presents a coherent and empirically validated technique for improving the accuracy of size-constrained interpretable models by learning an adaptive training distribution via Bayesian Optimization. The core idea is sound, the extension beyond prior density-tree work is meaningful, and the extensive evaluation across multiple model families, datasets, and even cross-feature-space settings demonstrates practical versatility. However, the contribution faces several substantive hurdles for ICLR acceptance: (1) the reliance on relative improvement metrics without absolute baselines obscures real-world impact, (2) critical ablations against simpler instance weighting strategies are missing, making it unclear if the DP+BayesOpt complexity is justified, (3) the "single hyperparameter" claim is technically inaccurate given the need to set valid search bounds for seven variables, and (4) failure modes related to oracle calibration and distributional shift are not addressed. The method is practically useful and the runtime mitigation via BoTorch is a strong addition, but the paper would benefit from reporting absolute scores, adding a baseline comparison to standard hard-example mining or importance weighting, and more rigorously qualifying the oracle dependency. With these revisions, it would be a strong candidate for acceptance; in its current form, it is borderline, pending clarification on the novelty of the distribution learning mechanism relative to simpler resampling heuristics.

# Neutral Reviewer
## Balanced Review

### Summary
This paper proposes a model-agnostic framework for improving the accuracy of size-constrained interpretable models (e.g., shallow decision trees, sparse linear models) by learning an optimal training data distribution through Bayesian Optimization. The method projects high-dimensional training data onto a single dimension using uncertainty scores from a probabilistic auxiliary model, then fits a Dirichlet Process-based Infinite Beta Mixture Model to guide resampling, effectively accommodating non-differentiable loss functions with minimal hyperparameter tuning. Across 13 datasets and multiple model-family combinations, the approach consistently yields notable accuracy gains for small models and elevates classical algorithms to competitive standing against modern specialized techniques.

### Strengths
1. **Rigorous and Broad Empirical Validation:** The technique is evaluated across 13 diverse datasets, two interpretable model families, and two oracle types, with results averaged over five trials and supported by statistical testing (Wilcoxon signed-rank tests). Table 1 and Appendix Table 4 clearly demonstrate consistent, often substantial relative F1-macro improvements, particularly at strict model size constraints.
2. **High Flexibility and Practical Convenience:** By leveraging Bayesian Optimization, the method natively handles non-differentiable objectives (like CART) and requires only a single primary hyperparameter ($T$, the optimization budget). The decoupling of the oracle's feature space from the target model's, experimentally validated in the cross-modal surname classification task (§A.13), significantly broadens its applicability beyond standard data-weighting schemes.
3. **Strong Benchmarking Against Specialized Baselines:** The paper effectively demonstrates real-world utility by showing that standard CART, when augmented with this sampling strategy, competes with modern explainable clustering methods like IMM (§A.10.2) and that a simple RBFN can match Stochastic Neighbor Compression (§A.10.3). This convincingly positions the method as a general-purpose enhancer for classical interpretable models.

### Weaknesses
1. **High Computational Cost and Preliminary Optimizations:** The reliance on $T=3000$ BayesOpt iterations, each requiring full model training and validation, leads to substantial runtimes (e.g., ~1 hour per configuration, §5). While the authors note potential speedups using BoTorch (~200 iterations), this mitigation is presented as preliminary. For ICLR, where scalability and optimization efficiency are paramount, this remains a significant practical bottleneck.
2. **Lack of Oracle Sensitivity and Robustness Analysis:** The entire pipeline hinges on the quality and calibration of the uncertainty oracle. The experiments exclusively use high-performing, calibrated GBMs and RFs without evaluating performance degradation under weaker, mis-specified, or uncalibrated oracles. A sensitivity analysis or ablation study is missing, making it unclear how fragile the method is to oracle failure.
3. **Unconventional Statistical Reporting:** In §4.1.2, the authors report $\delta F_1 = 0$ whenever a $t$-test fails to reject the null at $p=0.1$, rather than reporting the observed value or confidence interval. This censored reporting artificially truncates the result distribution and may bias aggregated performance metrics upward. Standard practice would be to report all raw deltas and use appropriate aggregation or non-parametric confidence intervals.
4. **Heuristic Smoothing and Limited Mechanistic Insight:** The "flattening" transformation (Algorithm 4, §A.5) is applied universally to all datasets simply because it is "computationally cheap," without verifying whether the original uncertainty distributions actually require smoothing. Coupled with the fixed `scale=10000` parameter and absence of theoretical analysis on *why* DP-IBMM optimization over uncertainty scores specifically benefits small models (beyond the boundary proximity heuristic), the methodology appears somewhat engineered for the optimizer rather than grounded in learning theory.

### Novelty & Significance
The paper presents a solid incremental advancement over prior data-distribution learning techniques, primarily by generalizing from density trees to a flexible uncertainty oracle + DP-IBMM + BayesOpt pipeline. The integration of these components into a truly model-agnostic framework with a single-tuning requirement is a meaningful engineering contribution. However, for ICLR, the significance is moderately constrained by its focus on classical ML paradigms (CART, linear models, tabular datasets) and lack of connection to contemporary deep learning or large-scale representation learning challenges. The work would strongly fit venues specializing in interpretable ML, AutoML, or statistical learning, but requires clearer justification of its relevance to ICLR's core audience (e.g., connections to differentiable interpretable architectures, scaling to modern datasets, or integration with deep learning pipelines) to meet the conference's typical bar for novelty and broad impact.

### Suggestions for Improvement
1. **Conduct Oracle Quality & Calibration Ablations:** Systematically evaluate how varying oracle accuracy, calibration errors, and architecture choices impact downstream performance. Establishing robustness bounds would strengthen claims of practical reliability.
2. **Adopt Standard Statistical Reporting Practices:** Remove the censored $\delta F_1 = 0$ reporting rule. Present the full distribution of improvements across trials with confidence intervals or use bootstrap aggregation to ensure transparent, unbiased statistical claims.
3. **Analyze the Learned Sampling Distributions:** Provide qualitative and quantitative analysis of the optimal $\Phi_*$ parameters and the resulting sampled instance profiles across different model sizes. Demonstrating *what* the optimizer actually learns (e.g., class-balanced boundary sampling vs. noise filtering) would greatly improve mechanistic interpretability and theoretical grounding.
4. **Clarify ICLR Relevance and Scalability Pathways:** Explicitly discuss how this framework could extend to modern differentiable interpretable models (e.g., neural additive models, soft decision trees) or integrate with gradient-based meta-learning/bilevel optimization to bypass the runtime bottleneck. Connecting the method to current trends in efficient/edge deployment or deep model compression would better align it with ICLR's expectations.

# Spark Finder Review
## How to Improve This Paper

### Missing Experiments (top 4 only)
1. Add baselines comparing against standard importance weighting or sample reweighting (e.g., focal loss, margin-based weighting, or direct thresholding of oracle uncertainty); without this, the claim that the Dirichlet Process + BO machinery is necessary rather than just complex overfitting is unconvincing.
2. Report absolute F1/Accuracy scores alongside relative improvements; claiming >100% relative improvement is statistically meaningless when baseline scores are near random, and this omission obscures whether the models actually reach useful performance levels.
3. Include ablation experiments with weak, uncalibrated, or random oracles; results demonstrating graceful degradation are required to prove that gains derive from the oracle's boundary signal rather than the stochastic nature of the optimization loop.
4. Compare against spending an equivalent compute budget on hyperparameter tuning of the interpretable model itself (e.g., class weights, pruning thresholds); this is required to validate the core claim that learning the training distribution is a more effective use of resources than direct model optimization.

### Deeper Analysis Needed (top 3 only)
1. Analyze the statistical properties of the final sampled training distributions (e.g., class balance, entropy, distance to boundaries); without characterizing what Φ actually learns, improvements could simply be attributed to naive class oversampling rather than sophisticated boundary-aware sampling.
2. Quantify the information loss from collapsing the feature space to 1D via uncertainty scores; projecting multi-modal confusion structures onto a single scalar risks ignoring distinct failure modes that a higher-dimensional density would resolve, undermining the method's theoretical foundation.
3. Conduct sensitivity analysis on the Bayesian Optimization search bounds; the claim of "only one hyperparameter" is misleading if performance heavily depends on fixed `scale` values and box constraints, which are notoriously critical to BO convergence.

### Visualizations & Case Studies
1. Plot the learned Beta mixture densities over uncertainty scores for key datasets to visually confirm whether the method actually discovers multi-modal sampling structures or merely degenerates into up-weighting the highest-uncertainty points.
2. Visualize and compare the decision splits of baseline vs. oracle-guided trees on a 2D dataset; this would expose whether the method yields genuinely optimal geometric partitions or merely shifts decision thresholds without improving structural fidelity.
3. Provide a detailed case study on configurations that yield negative improvements; exposing failure modes (e.g., oracle miscalibration, label noise, or sparse true boundaries) is essential to establish the method's operational limits.

### Obvious Next Steps (top 3 only)
1. Remove the arbitrary statistical filtering (zeroing δF1 if p > 0.1) and report full distributions or standard confidence intervals; this thresholding artificially cleans the results and violates the statistical reporting rigor expected at ICLR.
2. Integrate the BoTorch/LogEI runtime optimizations into the main experimental narrative rather than relegating them to the Appendix; claiming "practical convenience" while acknowledging hour-long runtimes per configuration severely undermines the paper's applicability.
3. Formally position the method against established literature on importance sampling and dataset distillation; defensively asserting "this is not distillation" without theoretical or empirical bridging leaves the contribution's novelty ambiguous and invites dismissal as reinvented weighting.

# Final Consolidated Review
## Summary
This paper proposes a model-agnostic framework to improve the accuracy of size-constrained interpretable models (e.g., shallow decision trees, sparse linear models) by learning an optimal training data distribution. It projects high-dimensional training instances to a 1D space using prediction uncertainty scores from an auxiliary "oracle" model, then fits a Dirichlet Process-based Infinite Beta Mixture Model parameterized by $\Phi$. Bayesian Optimization is used to maximize validation accuracy of the target model, natively accommodating non-differentiable loss functions. Extensive experiments across 13 datasets demonstrate consistent relative improvements for small models and show that classical algorithms, when augmented with this technique, can compete with modern specialized methods in explainable clustering and prototype-based tasks.

## Strengths
- **Broad and rigorous empirical validation:** The method is evaluated across 13 diverse datasets, two interpretable model families (DTs, LPMs), and two oracle types (GBMs, RFs), with results averaged over multiple trials and supported by statistical testing. The consistent, often substantial gains at strict size constraints robustly support the core effectiveness claim.
- **High flexibility and practical decoupling:** By leveraging black-box Bayesian Optimization, the framework handles non-differentiable objectives without modification. The experimental demonstration that the oracle and target model can operate on entirely different feature spaces (e.g., GRU on character sequences vs. DT on n-grams) significantly broadens its utility beyond traditional data-weighting schemes.
- **Meaningful competitive benchmarking:** The paper convincingly shows that enhancing classical, highly interpretable algorithms (CART, RBFN) with this sampling strategy elevates their performance to rival modern specialized techniques like IMM and SNC. This positions the method as a strong general-purpose enhancer for legacy interpretable pipelines.

## Weaknesses
- **Absence of simpler reweighting/mining baselines:** The paper claims that optimizing a DP-mixture distribution via BayesOpt yields superior accuracy, yet it lacks comparisons against standard, computationally cheaper alternatives such as hard-example mining, uncertainty thresholding, or class/importance weighting. Without these baselines, it is unclear whether the gains stem from the sophisticated non-parametric machinery or simply from any non-uniform resampling strategy.
- **Censored statistical reporting distorts empirical claims:** Section 4.1.2 artificially reports $\delta F_1 = 0$ whenever a paired $t$-test fails to reject the null at $p=0.1$. This truncation artificially cleans the result distribution, masks the variance of improvements, and violates standard empirical reporting practices. Full distributions or proper confidence intervals are required to accurately assess consistency.
- **Lack of absolute performance context:** Table 1 reports only relative percentage improvements, with several exceeding 100-200%. Without baseline absolute $F_1$ scores, the practical significance of these gains is obscured; large relative improvements can easily stem from baseline models performing near random guessing on difficult datasets.
- **No analysis of oracle sensitivity or failure modes:** The entire pipeline hinges on the oracle's uncertainty scores accurately proxying instance difficulty/boundary proximity. The experiments exclusively use well-calibrated, high-performing GBMs and RFs. There is no ablation analyzing performance degradation under weaker, mis-specified, or uncalibrated oracles, leaving the method's robustness and operational limits uncharacterized.

## Nice-to-Haves
- Integrate the promising BoTorch/LogEI runtime mitigations (§A.11) more prominently into the main experimental narrative to substantiate claims of practical convenience given current hour-scale runtimes.
- Provide qualitative analysis of the learned $\Phi^*$ parameters (e.g., visualizing optimal Beta mixture densities over uncertainty scores) to elucidate what sampling strategies the optimizer actually discovers (e.g., boundary-focused vs. class-balanced sampling).
- Clarify the practical impact of fixed box constraints for the 7 optimization variables, or provide brief guidance on when default bounds might require adjustment for atypical datasets.

## Novel Insights
The paper effectively reframes the interpretability-accuracy trade-off not as an inherent architectural limitation, but as a data selection problem. By decoupling uncertainty estimation from model training and leveraging a non-parametric density optimized via black-box methods, it circumvents the differentiability constraints that typically bottleneck interpretable ML optimization. This creates a versatile "drop-in" enhancement that bridges classical, highly transparent algorithms with modern performance standards. The explicit demonstration of cross-modal oracle-target compatibility further highlights a paradigm where interpretability no longer requires sacrificing complex feature representations during the guidance phase.

## Suggestions
- Add baseline comparisons against standard instance weighting strategies (e.g., focal loss, margin-based thresholding, or direct hard-negative mining) to empirically justify the added complexity of the DP-IBMM and BayesOpt pipeline.
- Replace the censored $\delta F_1 = 0$ reporting rule with the full distribution of improvements across trials. Report standard confidence intervals or use non-parametric bootstrap aggregation, and adopt $p<0.05$ or report exact $p$-values to align with standard empirical rigor.
- Augment Table 1 and relevant appendix tables with baseline absolute $F_1$ scores alongside relative improvements to properly contextualize the magnitude and practical relevance of the reported gains.
- Conduct a sensitivity analysis using progressively weaker, randomly initialized, or intentionally miscalibrated oracles. Characterizing degradation patterns will establish practical robustness bounds and guide practitioners on oracle selection.
