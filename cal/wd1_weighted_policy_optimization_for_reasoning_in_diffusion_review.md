# Harsh Critic Review
## Section-by-Section Critical Review

### Title & Abstract
- **Does the title accurately reflect the contribution?** Yes. "Weighted Policy Optimization" accurately captures the core algorithmic shift from ratio-based importance sampling to advantage-weighted log-likelihood maximization, and "Diffusion Language Models" correctly bounds the scope.
- **Does the abstract clearly state the problem, method, and key results?** Yes. The problem (intractable dLLM likelihoods causing high variance/approximation error in policy ratio estimation for RL) is clearly stated. The method (`wd1`, ratio-free weighted log-likelihood requiring only current-policy likelihood approximation) is concisely introduced. Results (`wd1` outperforms `d1` with lower compute; `wd1++` achieves SOTA math scores with 20 steps) are explicitly quantified.
- **Are any claims in the abstract unsupported by the paper?** The claim "requiring only a single approximation for the current parametrized policy likelihood" requires nuance. While the *optimization objective* (Eq. 8) only needs $\log \pi_\theta$, the sampling procedure in Algorithm 1 (line 5) and Section 3.2 relies on sampling from a geometric mixture $\pi_{\text{oldref}} \propto \pi_{\text{old}}^\lambda \pi_{\text{ref}}^\beta$. Section 3.2 explicitly states they approximate $\log \pi_{\text{oldref}} \approx \lambda \log \pi_{\text{old}} + \beta \log \pi_{\text{ref}}$. This means reference and old policy forward passes (or logit evaluations) are still incurred during rollout generation if $\beta > 0$, contradicting the strict "single approximation" claim. The abstract should clarify that the *loss computation* is single-approximation, or note that $\beta=0$ in practice removes the reference overhead.

### Introduction & Motivation
- **Is the problem well-motivated? Is the gap in prior work clearly identified?** Yes. The motivation is strong. The introduction correctly identifies that applying GRPO-style ratio clipping to dLLMs requires approximating $\log \pi_\theta$, $\log \pi_{\text{old}}$, and $\log \pi_{\text{ref}}$, which compounds bias and variance (Figure 1 illustrates this well). The gap (ratio estimation instability + high compute in diffusion RL) is clearly articulated.
- **Are the contributions clearly stated and accurate?** Yes. The four contributions are accurately summarized: novel WLL objective, theoretical link to energy-guided diffusion + unlearning, empirical gains over `d1` without SFT, and the `wd1++` stepwise extension.
- **Does the introduction over-claim or under-sell?** Slight over-claim on computational advantage without qualification. The introduction frames the method as bypassing likelihood ratio estimation entirely, which is true for the gradient step. However, as noted, the geometric mixture sampling still requires evaluating or approximating previous/reference policy distributions unless $\beta=0$ and caching is perfect. The introduction should explicitly acknowledge this dependency to avoid misrepresenting the pipeline's true FLOP requirements.

### Method / Approach
- **Is the method clearly described and reproducible?** Mostly. Algorithm 1 provides a clear high-level flow. However, there is a **critical inconsistency**: Algorithm 1, line 4 states `oi ~ π_old(·|q)`, while Section 3.2 explicitly states "We first obtain G completions sampled from geometric mixture $\pi_{\text{oldref}}$". This contradiction makes reproduction ambiguous. Are rollouts drawn from $\pi_{\text{old}}$ or $\pi_{\text{oldref}}$? If $\pi_{\text{oldref}}$, how is sampling implemented efficiently for discrete masked diffusion? (Sec B.3 mentions it requires multiple forward passes or shifting the advantage, which reintroduces approximation). This must be reconciled.
- **Are key assumptions stated and justified?** The derivation assumes the closed-form optimal policy $\pi^*$ from Eq. 5 is well-defined and that minimizing $D_{KL}(\pi^* \parallel \pi_\theta)$ via importance sampling yields Eq. 7. The assumption that the normalization constant in Eq. 7 can be dropped (since it's independent of $o$) is valid. The use of reverse-KL over forward-KL is justified by monotonic improvement (Theorem 2), a standard result in RL literature, appropriately adapted.
- **Are there logical gaps in the derivation or reasoning?** The jump from Eq. 6 to Eq. 8 introduces $w^{[-]}$ heuristically to address vanishing weights and likelihood inflation for negative samples. While empirically motivated, the theoretical grounding for the *exact* form of $w^{[-]}$ in Eq. 9 is less rigorous than the AWR derivation in Prop 2. It's presented as a practical balancing mechanism, which is acceptable, but the paper should clarify whether this modification preserves the original reverse-KL constrained optimization guarantee or if it breaks theoretical alignment with Eq. 4.
- **Are there edge cases or failure modes not discussed?** The method assumes group-relative advantage estimation is meaningful. In sparse reward settings (e.g., binary 0/1 correctness for math), if all samples in a group are correct or all are incorrect, $A_i = 0$, leading to $w^{[+]} = w^{[-]}$ and halted updates. This is acknowledged in Sec 3.2, but the impact on training stability for extremely sparse or dense reward landscapes is not empirically tested.
- **For theoretical claims: are proofs correct and complete?** Theorem 1 (AW-D-CSM) and Remark 1/2 provide a compelling link to energy-guided diffusion. The proofs in Appendix A.3 follow standard score-matching and Bayes rule manipulations correctly. However, the equivalence $L_{WDLL} \Leftrightarrow L_{AW-DCE}$ (Remark 1) hinges entirely on the DCE likelihood approximation being a valid surrogate for ELBO, which the authors accept from prior work but do not independently verify for their off-policy weighted objective.

### Experiments & Results
- **Do the experiments actually test the paper's claims?** Yes. The main tests compare `wd1` to `d1` and `diffu-GRPO` across reasoning benchmarks, validating sample efficiency and compute reduction. The ablation on $w^{[-]}$ (Table 4) directly tests the necessity of the negative sample penalty.
- **Are baselines appropriate and fairly compared?** Baselines (`d1`, `diffu-GRPO`, `MDPO`, `SDPO`) are highly relevant and current. The comparison to `d1` is fair, as both use the same likelihood approximation and LoRA setup. However, MDPO and SDPO are full fine-tuning methods, while `wd1` uses LoRA (except `wd1` (full) which is close to MDPO). Comparing LoRA-optimized `wd1` to full-parameter baselines in Table 3 could be seen as favorable to `wd1` in terms of FLOPs/memory, though the authors report `wd1` (full) for a fairer comparison.
- **Are there missing ablations that would materially change conclusions?** 
  1. **Ablation on Likelihood Approximator:** The paper attributes gains to the ratio-free objective, but uses the exact same biased approximation from `d1` for $\log \pi_\theta$. An ablation comparing `wd1` with ELBO-based approximation (varying MC sample size $t$) would clarify if the gains come purely from the objective structure or from mitigating the specific bias of the $t=1$ approximation.
  2. **Ablation on $\beta$ in Sampling:** Since the abstract claims single-approximation efficiency, showing training dynamics and compute cost with $\beta=0.04$ (reference regularization active) vs $\beta=0$ would validate the practical sampling overhead discussed in Sec B.3.
- **Are error bars / statistical significance reported?** **No.** This is a significant omission for an ICLR submission. RL training, especially with off-policy updates and advantage estimation, is highly sensitive to seed, rollout batch composition, and temperature. Table 1 reports single deterministic values. Figure 4 (right) shows one alternative seed run, but main results lack variance reporting or statistical significance testing across multiple seeds.
- **Do the results support the claims made, or are they cherry-picked?** Results strongly support the claim that `wd1` improves reasoning accuracy over `d1` without SFT. The "10x fewer rollouts" claim in Table 3 (right) needs careful interpretation. `wd1++` achieves this partly by leveraging *intermediate denoising steps* as additional training signals (Sec 3.3, Eq. 10). This is effectively trajectory-level data augmentation. The paper should explicitly separate how much sample efficiency comes from the weighted loss vs. how much comes from extracting $L$ training steps per single rollout.
- **Are datasets and evaluation metrics appropriate?** Yes. Standard math (GSM8K, MATH500) and planning benchmarks (Sudoku, Countdown) with pass@1 accuracy are appropriate for reasoning RL.

### Writing & Clarity
- **Are there sections that are confusing or poorly explained?** Section 3.1's derivation from Eq. 5 to Eq. 7 is dense. While Proposition 2 justifies the importance sampling shift, the transition to the geometric mixture distribution $\pi_{\text{oldref}}$ is not intuitively motivated in the main text. Readers might ask: why mix with $\pi_{\text{ref}}$? The text should briefly explain that this mixture stabilizes off-policy learning and enforces the regularization implicitly during sampling, rather than just in the loss.
- **Are figures and tables clear and informative?** Yes. Table 2 clearly breaks down compute savings. Figure 2 effectively shows the ablation on combined weights. The only minor clarity issue is Figure 1, which plots "Example policy ratio value", but doesn't specify if this is post-clipping or pre-clipping, nor the sample size of $t$ used for the ELBO baseline, making it slightly hard to reproduce the variance claim quantitatively.

### Limitations & Broader Impact
- **Do the authors acknowledge the key limitations?** Yes, Section D acknowledges collapse under uniform rewards, text-only scope, and reliance on the biased `d1` approximation.
- **Are there fundamental limitations they missed?** The reliance on group-relative advantage assumes that within-batch variance is a good proxy for global advantage. In early training when the model is weak, or on tasks with very sparse rewards, this advantage estimator can be extremely noisy. The paper does not discuss how `wd1` interacts with reward shaping or process reward models, which are becoming standard for math reasoning. Additionally, the "unlearning" interpretation (Remark 2) assumes the negative samples are distributed according to a Boltzmann distribution scaled by $e^{-A}$. In practice, with small batch sizes $G=6$, this distributional assumption is highly approximate, which could lead to optimizing against arbitrary noise rather than true "negative" trajectories.
- **Are there failure modes or negative societal impacts not discussed?** Standard RL failure modes (reward hacking, verbosity) are not discussed. Given the focus on math/reasoning, there's minimal societal risk, but a brief note on potential over-optimization of format rewards at the expense of robustness would strengthen the impact statement.

### Overall Assessment
This paper presents a well-motivated and theoretically grounded approach to RL for diffusion-based LLMs, successfully sidestepping the high-variance policy ratio estimation that plagues GRPO-style methods in this domain. The derivation connecting the weighted log-likelihood objective to energy-guided diffusion and negative sample unlearning is insightful and aligns well with recent trends in efficient RL. Empirically, the method demonstrates strong performance gains, particularly in eliminating the need for a costly SFT warmup phase and reducing training overhead. However, the contribution would benefit from addressing several key issues before publication: (1) resolving the contradiction between Algorithm 1 (sampling from $\pi_{\text{old}}$) and the text (sampling from $\pi_{\text{oldref}}$), which impacts reproducibility and the validity of the compute-savings claims; (2) providing statistical variance/error bars across multiple random seeds for RL results, which is essential for ICLR acceptance; and (3) clarifying whether the dramatic sample efficiency of `wd1++` stems primarily from the loss formulation or from the implicit data augmentation of intermediate denoising steps. With these clarifications and minor additional ablations, the paper makes a solid, scalable contribution to efficient diffusion model fine-tuning and is well-positioned for acceptance.

# Neutral Reviewer
## Balanced Review

### Summary
This paper introduces `wd1`, a ratio-free reinforcement learning algorithm for diffusion-based large language models (dLLMs) that circumvents the high variance and computational overhead of importance sampling ratios by reformulating the RL objective as a weighted log-likelihood loss balanced by positive and negative advantage terms. The authors formally derive the method from reverse-KL policy optimization, theoretically linking it to energy-guided discrete diffusion training and negative sample unlearning. Empirically, `wd1` and its stepwise extension `wd1++` achieve substantial accuracy improvements over diffusion-GRPO baselines like `d1` on reasoning and math benchmarks while significantly reducing training time, FLOPs, and required rollouts.

### Strengths
1. **Well-Motivated Solution to a Core dLLM Bottleneck:** The paper directly targets the intractable likelihood function of dLLMs, which forces existing methods to compute noisy, biased policy ratios. By eliminating ratios entirely and relying on a single likelihood approximation, `wd1` offers a principled path to stable policy updates. The theoretical error analysis in Appendix A.1 clearly quantifies how ratio-based methods exponentially amplify approximation bias, whereas `wd1`'s error scales linearly.
2. **Strong Theoretical Grounding & Novel Interpretation:** The derivation from reverse-KL constrained optimization (Theorem 2) to weighted log-likelihood is rigorous and aligns with established continuous-control literature but is novelly adapted to masked diffusion. Furthermore, Theorem 1 and Lemma 1 provide valuable insights by reframing the objective as energy-guided discrete diffusion sampling combined with data unlearning, enriching the theoretical understanding of RL fine-tuning for diffusion models.
3. **Comprehensive Efficiency Metrics & Ablation Studies:** Beyond standard accuracy tables, the authors report runtime, FLOPs, and function evaluations (Table 2, Table 8, Appendix B.5), providing ICLR-caliber transparency on compute costs. The ablations effectively isolate key design choices: removing SFT is validated (Table 4, Eq. 8 vs Eq. 10), the necessity of the negative weight term `w[-]` is demonstrated (Figure 2, Table 9), and the sensitivity to the exponential scaling factor `ψ` is carefully analyzed (Figure 4).
4. **Clever `wd1++` Extension:** Leveraging intermediate denoising steps as additional training samples (Section 3.3) is an elegant utilization of the diffusion process. This stepwise formulation dramatically improves sample efficiency, achieving competitive math performance in only 20 training steps (Table 3), which addresses a major pain point in current LLM RL pipelines.

### Weaknesses
1. **Dependence on a Biased Likelihood Approximator:** While `wd1` avoids ratios, it still relies on `d1`'s highly biased single-step likelihood approximation (`log πθ(x0|q) ≈ log πθ(x0|x1, q')`) for the log-likelihood term in Eq. 10. The authors acknowledge this in Appendix D, but the paper lacks an ablation comparing `d1`'s fast approximation against more accurate (e.g., ELBO/DCE) or less biased estimators within the `wd1` framework, leaving the true sensitivity to likelihood bias unquantified.
2. **Geometric Mixture Sampling Overhead:** `wd1` theoretically samples completions from a geometric mixture of `π_old` and `π_ref` (Algorithm 1, line 4). Appendix B.3 explicitly states that exact sampling requires multiple forward passes, which introduces compute overhead. Although a practical workaround is mentioned (sampling from `π_old` and shifting the advantage), the actual throughput impact of this workaround versus pure on-policy sampling is not empirically reported, making the exact net efficiency gains slightly opaque.
3. **Format-Heavy Reward Functions:** The reward definitions in Appendix B.2 allocate substantial weight to XML structure, strict formatting, and integer validation alongside the correctness reward. While common in current RL reasoning setups, this makes it difficult to disentangle whether `wd1`'s gains stem from better policy optimization or from the advantage distribution being heavily shaped by dense format penalties. This limits claims about reasoning capability improvements on cleaner, pure-verifier setups.
4. **Limited Analysis of `wd1++` Sampling Dynamics:** The stepwise extension expands the sample pool but lacks analysis on how step selection impacts gradients. Uniform sampling over denoising steps (`l ∈ Unif{1,...,L}`) as in Eq. 10 may not be optimal, as early steps contain high noise and late steps are nearly solved. The paper does not ablate step-sampling distributions or analyze gradient variance across different diffusion timesteps, which is critical for understanding the stability of `wd1++`.

### Novelty & Significance
**Novelty:** High. Applying weighted regression/advantage-weighted objectives (akin to AWR) specifically to *masked diffusion LLMs* to bypass intractable policy ratios is a novel and timely contribution. The stepwise sample reuse (`wd1++`) and the formal connection between RL policy updates and energy-guided discrete diffusion sampling provide fresh theoretical perspectives.
**Clarity:** Good. The mathematical derivations are generally sound and well-structured, though notation is dense in places (e.g., switching between `w[+]/w[-]` and combined weight `λ`). The algorithm pseudocode and theoretical remarks are accessible to readers familiar with RL and diffusion modeling.
**Reproducibility:** Strong. The authors provide a public code repository, detailed hyperparameter tables (Table 5, 6), dataset splits, training cost estimation methodology, and explicit reward definitions. The reproducibility statement meets ICLR standards.
**Significance:** High. Efficiently scaling RL to dLLMs is a critical open problem for realizing the inference speed advantages of diffusion language models. `wd1` offers a computationally cheaper, more stable alternative that could lower the barrier for post-training dLLMs, making it highly relevant to the ICLR community interested in LLM alignment, optimization efficiency, and novel generative architectures.

### Suggestions for Improvement
1. **Ablate Likelihood Approximation Quality:** Evaluate `wd1` using different likelihood estimators (e.g., single-step vs. multi-step ELBO/DCE) to empirically measure how approximation bias impacts training stability and final performance, isolating the benefit of the ratio-free formulation from the approximation method.
2. **Quantify Sampling Efficiency:** Explicitly report the NFEs and wall-clock time for exact geometric mixture sampling versus the practical advantage-shifting workaround. This will provide transparent, quantitative evidence of the true compute savings claimed in Table 2.
3. **Test with Format-Stripped/Verifier Rewards:** Run a subset of experiments using only answer correctness (or standard math verifiers) to demonstrate that performance gains are robust and not artifacts of over-optimizing dense format rewards. Include analysis of how advantage distributions change under cleaner rewards.
4. **Deepen `wd1++` Analysis:** Ablate the step-sampling distribution in `wd1++` (e.g., uniform vs. timestep-weighted vs. confidence-based) and report gradient norms or variance per denoising step. This will clarify the dynamics driving the rapid convergence and guide optimal timestep selection for future work.

# Spark Finder Review
## How to Improve This Paper

### Missing Experiments (top 3-5 only)
1. Reproduce the `d1` baseline **without the SFT stage** to isolate the algorithmic contribution of `wd1`. Without this, the reported performance gap may simply reflect the baseline's dependence on SFT rather than the efficacy of the ratio-free objective, invalidating the core comparative claims.
2. Run `wd1` with a lower-bias likelihood estimator (e.g., multi-step ELBO) alongside the `d1` approximation to test robustness. If `wd1` only excels with the biased `d1` approximator, the results likely stem from accidental bias cancellation rather than theoretical soundness, undermining the method's general reliability.
3. Report GSM8K and MATH500 results across at least three random seeds with error bars. SOTA claims based on single-run improvements over concurrent methods with only 20 training steps lack the statistical rigor required by ICLR, as minor deltas in math reasoning are highly seed-sensitive.

### Deeper Analysis Needed (top 3-5 only)
1. Analyze the validity of computing group-relative advantages in `wd1++` across pooled intermediate denoising steps. Pooling completions with varying mask ratios and noise levels into a single advantage distribution introduces temporal misalignment and likely biases credit assignment, as intermediate outputs are structurally incomparable to final outputs.
2. Provide a stability analysis of the off-policy updates as the number of inner gradient steps ($\mu$) increases. Since `wd1` samples from a geometric mixture and performs multiple updates without explicit policy ratio clipping, you must demonstrate that the effective sample size does not collapse or that gradients do not become dominated by outliers after policy drift.
3. Quantify the true compute cost of sampling from the mixture policy $\pi_{\text{old}}^{\text{ref}}$. Mixture sampling for diffusion models typically requires specialized or repeated evaluations; if the sampling overhead is ignored and only likelihood NFEs are counted, the efficiency claims in Table 2 are incomplete and potentially misleading.

### Visualizations & Case Studies
1. Plot the effective sample size (ESS) and weight entropy of `w_+` and `w_-` over training steps to verify stability. This reveals whether the exponential weighting actually maintains a balanced contribution from the group or degenerates into weight collapse where gradients are driven by one or two samples.
2. Qualitatively showcase completion trajectories on complex Sudoku or multi-step math problems, highlighting instances where `wd1` succeeds vs. fails compared to `d1`. This exposes whether accuracy gains come from improved logical reasoning or simple reward hacking (e.g., optimizing format tags at the expense of solution validity).
3. Visualize the reward contribution per diffusion timestep in `wd1++` to determine if intermediate steps provide meaningful signal. If the objective is overwhelmingly dominated by the final step, the stepwise extension adds complexity without functional benefit, suggesting the method is not truly leveraging the denoising trajectory.

### Obvious Next Steps
1. Include a sensitivity analysis for the weight scale parameter $\psi$ across different reward distributions. Exponential weighting is highly sensitive to reward magnitude; the paper should have shown how $\psi$ interacts with sparse verifier rewards versus dense format rewards to prove the method is robust across reward regimes.
2. Benchmark `wd1` against simpler ratio-free on-policy baselines (e.g., REINFORCE with baseline or RLOO) using the same likelihood approximation. This is necessary to prove that the reverse-KL weighted objective offers specific advantages over standard policy gradients in the diffusion RL setting.
3. Ablate the sampling strategy for `wd1++` (e.g., uniform vs. timestep-weighted sampling of intermediate completions). The current uniform assumption ignores the changing difficulty and information density across the denoising process, and showing how alternative weighting affects performance is essential to validate the stepwise formulation.

# Final Consolidated Review
## Summary
This paper introduces `wd1`, a ratio-free reinforcement learning algorithm for diffusion-based large language models (dLLMs) that circumvents the high variance and computational overhead of importance sampling by reformulating the policy optimization objective as an advantage-weighted log-likelihood. Theoretically grounded in reverse-KL regularization, the method is shown to be equivalent to energy-guided discrete diffusion training combined with negative sample unlearning. An extension, `wd1++`, repurposes intermediate denoising trajectories to dramatically boost sample efficiency. Empirically, `wd1` achieves substantial reasoning accuracy improvements over diffusion-GRPO baselines like `d1` without requiring supervised fine-tuning, while significantly reducing training FLOPs, wall-clock time, and rollouts.

## Strengths
- **Principled elimination of policy ratio estimation:** The paper directly targets the intractable likelihood problem in dLLMs. By deriving the objective from the closed-form optimal policy of reverse-KL constrained optimization, `wd1` avoids the exponential amplification of approximation errors inherent in ratio-based methods (Appendix A.1 rigorously contrasts linear vs. exponential error scaling).
- **Rich theoretical interpretation:** The derivation successfully bridges RL optimization and diffusion theory. Lemma 1 and Theorem 1 provide a compelling reinterpretation of the weighted objective as energy-guided discrete diffusion sampling, while Remark 2 frames the negative weight penalty as principled data unlearning, offering valuable mechanistic insight.
- **Demonstrated training efficiency and pipeline simplification:** `wd1` removes the need for an SFT warm-up stage and reduces per-step forward passes by bypassing old/reference policy likelihood evaluations. Table 2 and Appendix B.5 transparently break down FLOPs, NFEs, and runtime, and `wd1++` effectively repurposes otherwise discarded intermediate denoising steps to achieve state-of-the-art math scores in only 20 training steps.
- **Validated design choices:** The ablation studies clearly isolate critical components: Table 4 and Eq. 8–9 confirm that the negative penalty term ($w^{[-]}$) is essential to prevent likelihood inflation of low-advantage samples, and Figure 2/Table 9 empirically justify the balanced weighting scheme.

## Weaknesses
- **Inconsistent sampling protocol and unquantified mixture overhead:** Algorithm 1, line 4 specifies sampling completions from $\pi_{\text{old}}$, while Section 3.2 and the theoretical derivation require sampling from the geometric mixture $\pi_{\text{oldref}}$. Section B.3 acknowledges that exact mixture sampling for diffusion models requires either multiple forward passes or a heuristic advantage shift. However, the actual NFE/wall-clock impact of implementing this in practice is not transparently reported, obscuring the true net efficiency gains compared to pure on-policy sampling.
- **Lack of statistical robustness reporting:** RL training with off-policy updates and advantage estimation is highly sensitive to random seeds, rollout batch composition, and temperature. The main results (Tables 1–3) report single-run deterministic values without error bars, variance across seeds, or statistical significance testing. Given the high variance inherent in this setting and the modest absolute deltas on GSM8K/MATH500 in some configurations, multi-seed validation is essential to confirm the robustness of the claimed improvements. *(Note: The paper already compares against `diffu-GRPO` without SFT in Table 1, so the claim that the no-SFT baseline was omitted is factually incorrect.)*
- **Questionable advantage pooling in `wd1++`:** The stepwise extension pools intermediate (partially masked, higher noise) and final (fully unmasked) completions into a single group to compute normalized advantages. These states differ significantly in information density and difficulty, making direct advantage comparison theoretically fragile. The paper does not analyze how this temporal misalignment affects gradient bias or whether credit assignment is systematically distorted toward later/earlier timesteps.
- **Off-policy update stability without explicit regularization:** `wd1` performs multiple inner gradient steps ($\mu = 8$ or $12$) without policy ratio clipping. While normalized exponential weights mitigate extreme updates, there is no theoretical guarantee or empirical analysis of how policy drift accumulates across the $\mu$ steps, or whether the effective sample size (ESS) degrades to a point where gradients become dominated by a few outliers.

## Nice-to-Haves
- Evaluate `wd1` with a lower-bias likelihood estimator (e.g., multi-step ELBO/DCE) alongside the `d1` single-step approximation to verify that performance gains are driven by the objective structure rather than interaction with a specific biased approximator.
- Report ESS and weight entropy trajectories during training to visualize whether the exponential weighting scheme maintains balanced gradient contributions across rollout batches.
- Disentangle the sample efficiency gains of `wd1++`: quantify how much of the speedup stems from the weighted loss itself versus the implicit trajectory-level data augmentation provided by stepwise sampling.

## Novel Insights
The paper successfully reframes a core optimization bottleneck in diffusion LLM fine-tuning through the lens of energy-guided sampling and negative data unlearning. By deriving a closed-form optimal policy from reverse-KL regularization and translating it into a practical weighted log-likelihood loss, `wd1` bridges theoretical RL optimization with the structural constraints of discrete diffusion. The insight that explicit negative advantage penalties naturally correspond to unlearning low-probability regions offers a theoretically grounded, computationally efficient alternative to heuristic rejection sampling or explicit clipping, suggesting a promising direction for stable, off-policy alignment in non-autoregressive models.

## Suggestions
- Reconcile the sampling contradiction between Algorithm 1 ($\pi_{\text{old}}$) and the main text/appendix ($\pi_{\text{oldref}}$), and explicitly report the wall-clock time and NFEs of the implemented mixture sampling strategy versus standard on-policy sampling.
- Run the primary benchmarks across at least 3 different random seeds and report mean $\pm$ standard deviation/error bars to establish statistical reliability and rule out seed-specific artifacts.
- Provide a theoretical justification or empirical ablation for the advantage normalization strategy in `wd1++`, or investigate timestep-weighted sampling/credit assignment to ensure intermediate steps contribute meaningful, unbiased gradients.
