# Harsh Critic Review
## Section-by-Section Critical Review

### Title & Abstract
- Does the title accurately reflect the contribution? Yes. "Weighted Policy Optimization for Reasoning in Diffusion Language Models" precisely captures the algorithmic core (`wd1`), the target model family (dLLMs), and the downstream application (reasoning).
- Does the abstract clearly state the problem, method, and key results? Yes. It concisely identifies the intractability of dLLM likelihoods and the variance in policy ratios, proposes a ratio-free weighted log-likelihood objective, states the theoretical interpretation (energy-guided diffusion + unlearning), and reports empirical gains over `d1` and `wd1++` results.
- Are any claims in the abstract unsupported by the paper? The claim of a "+59% improvement in accuracy" refers to absolute percentage points (76.4 - 17.6 ≈ 58.8 on Sudoku 256), which is clear in Table 1 but could be momentarily misread as relative improvement. The claim of "state-of-the-art math performance" is accurate within the dLLM RL setting shown in Table 3, but should be explicitly scoped to dLLM-based methods to avoid implying parity with top-tier autoregressive RL models (which often exceed 80%+ on MATH500 with massive compute). All claims are otherwise well-supported.

### Introduction & Motivation
- Is the problem well-motivated? Is the gap in prior work clearly identified? Yes. The shift to masked diffusion LLMs introduces non-trivial likelihood intractability. The paper correctly identifies that ratio-based RL objectives (like GRPO/PPO) exponentially amplify even small likelihood approximation errors, leading to high variance or systematic bias. This is a timely and well-defined gap for the ICLR community.
- Are the contributions clearly stated and accurate? The four contributions are accurate and map directly to later sections: (1) ratio-free WLL objective, (2) connection to energy-guided diffusion/unlearning, (3) empirical gains over `d1` without SFT, (4) `wd1++` extension leveraging intermediate steps.
- Does the introduction over-claim or under-sell? The motivation is balanced. One minor over-statement is the implication that reverse-KL regularization inherently guarantees monotonic improvement without discussing the standard step-size/trust-region assumptions required for the bound to hold (addressed later in Appendix Theorem 2, but worth a brief mention in Sec 1). Otherwise, it accurately sets expectations.

### Method / Approach
- Is the method clearly described and reproducible? Algorithm 1 is unambiguous, and Appendix B.4 provides necessary hyperparameters. The likelihood approximation adopts the efficient $t=1$ single-sample estimator from `d1`, and the loss (Eq 8-9) is straightforward to implement. Reproducibility is high.
- Are key assumptions stated and justified? Two assumptions need explicit acknowledgment:
  1. The derivation in Sec 3.1 assumes sampling from a geometric mixture $\pi_{\text{old}}^{\lambda}\pi_{\text{ref}}^{\beta}$. However, Sec 5 states that $\beta=0$ is used in practice, collapsing the sampling distribution to $\pi_{\text{old}}$. While empirically justified, the paper should explicitly state that the practical implementation deviates from the theoretical mixture sampling assumption, and clarify how this affects the importance weighting in Eq 6.
  2. Sec 3.1 assumes the optimal policy has a closed-form exponential weighting derived from soft Q-learning / maximum entropy RL (citing Peng et al., 2019; Rafailov et al., 2023). This assumes $\pi_\theta$ is sufficiently expressive to match the target distribution, a standard but important caveat.
- Are there logical gaps in the derivation or reasoning? The transition from Eq 4 to Eq 6 is standard for soft RL objectives. However, Eq 10 (`wd1++` objective) lacks a clear explanation of how rewards are assigned to intermediate denoising steps. Since verifiable rewards (e.g., math correctness) can only be computed on fully denoised sequences, it is unclear whether $R(q, x_{0|l})$ for $l < L$ reuses the final sequence's reward, or if an intermediate heuristic reward is used. If final rewards are broadcast across steps, computing group-relative advantages over a mixed pool of partially and fully denoised sequences may introduce distributional mismatch in the baseline estimation.
- Are there edge cases or failure modes not discussed? The exponential weighting in Eq 9 is sensitive to flat reward distributions (vanishing weights). The Limitations section acknowledges identical rewards, but the impact of high variance in advantage estimates across the $G \times L$ pool in `wd1++` should be discussed. Figure 4 shows $\psi=10$ degrades performance, confirming sensitivity.
- For theoretical claims: are proofs correct and complete? Appendix A.2 (Theorem 2) correctly applies $D_{TV}^2 \leq D_{KL}$ to show monotonic improvement under reverse-KL. Appendix A.3 correctly extends continuous energy-guided sampling theory to discrete masked diffusion, leveraging prior concrete score matching results. The proofs are largely correct, though Eq 61's jump from score matching to DCE relies on cited equivalences rather than explicit derivation, which is acceptable given space constraints.

### Experiments & Results
- Do the experiments actually test the paper's claims? Yes. Tables 1-3 directly test accuracy gains, computational efficiency, and the effectiveness of leveraging intermediate steps. Ablations in Sec 5.2 and Appendix C.2 isolate the impact of $w^{[-]}$, SFT removal, and weight balancing.
- Are baselines appropriate and fairly compared? Baselines (`diffu-GRPO`, `d1`, SDPO, TCR, MDPO) are the current standard for dLLM RL. One discrepancy needs addressing: `wd1++` and `wd1` use LoRA (Appendix B.4, Table 6), while MDPO and SDPO are labeled `(full)`. The paper should discuss whether parameter efficiency might limit the comparison on GSM8K (where `wd1++` is 82.7% vs MDPO's 83.4%), or acknowledge this as a factor. Otherwise, the comparison is fair.
- Are there missing ablations that would materially change conclusions? An ablation comparing likelihood approximators (e.g., ELBO with multiple $t$ samples vs. the $d1$ single-sample estimator) within `wd1` would strengthen the claim that the ratio-free objective is robust to approximation error. Currently, this is argued theoretically but not empirically validated.
- Are error bars / statistical significance reported? No. ICLR increasingly expects variance reporting for RL experiments due to high sensitivity to seeds and advantage estimation. Reporting standard deviations over multiple seeds or confidence intervals would significantly bolster credibility.
- Do the results support the claims made, or are they cherry-picked? Results appear comprehensive. The massive Sudoku/Countdown gains are genuine, while math gains are more modest but consistent with concurrent work. No obvious cherry-picking is present.
- Are datasets and evaluation metrics appropriate? Standard reasoning benchmarks (GSM8K, MATH500, Sudoku, Countdown) with pass@1 and zero-shot evaluation. Reward functions (Sec B.2) combine format and correctness verifiers, which is standard practice. Metrics are appropriate.

### Writing & Clarity
- Are there sections that are confusing or poorly explained? Sec 4 is theoretically dense. The connection between intermediate energy guidance (Lemma 1) and the practical `wd1` loss is conceptually sound but could benefit from a brief intuitive bridge before diving into concrete score matching. As noted, Sec 3.3 (`wd1++`) lacks clarity on intermediate reward propagation.
- Are figures and tables clear and informative? Yes. Table 1 cleanly shows per-length performance. Table 2 effectively communicates computational savings. Figure 4's ablation on $\psi$ and weight combinations is highly informative. The parser artifacts do not impede understanding of the logical flow.

### Limitations & Broader Impact
- Do the authors acknowledge the key limitations? Yes. They note the failure mode under uniform rewards, the restriction to text-only reasoning, and the bias introduced by the $d1$-style likelihood approximation.
- Are there fundamental limitations they missed? The discrepancy between the theoretical mixture sampling assumption and the empirical $\beta=0$ setting is not discussed as a limitation. Additionally, the assumption that intermediate denoising steps share the same advantage signal as final completions is heuristic and its theoretical grounding remains unexplored.
- Are there failure modes or negative societal impacts not discussed? The paper lacks a broader impact section. Given the compute-intensive nature of RL training, `wd1`'s efficiency reduces carbon/compute barriers, which is positive. However, like all RL reasoning methods, it risks amplifying spurious reward-hacking behaviors or brittle verification shortcuts, which warrants a brief mention.

### Overall Assessment
This paper makes a timely and practically valuable contribution to the emerging field of RL for diffusion language models. By reformulating policy optimization as a ratio-free weighted log-likelihood objective, it directly addresses the high variance and computational overhead that plague existing diffusion-GRPO methods. The theoretical framing around energy-guided diffusion and negative sample unlearning is intellectually stimulating, and the empirical results—particularly the dramatic gains on structural reasoning tasks and the stepwise `wd1++` extension—demonstrate clear practical advantages. However, the method would benefit from clarifying the intermediate reward assignment in `wd1++`, explicitly reconciling the theoretical geometric mixture assumption with the practical $\beta=0$ implementation, and adding statistical variance estimates to meet ICLR's rigor standards. Despite these points, the contribution is solid, well-motivated, and offers a scalable pathway for dLLM alignment.

# Neutral Reviewer
## Balanced Review

### Summary
This paper introduces `wd1`, a ratio-free reinforcement learning algorithm for diffusion-based LLMs (dLLMs) that reformulates the RL objective as a weighted log-likelihood, explicitly balancing positive and negative advantage-weighted completions. By eliminating the need to compute policy ratios from intractable likelihood approximations, the method reduces variance and computational overhead, with theoretical connections to energy-guided discrete diffusion and data unlearning. Empirical results on LLaDA-8B show `wd1` and its stepwise extension `wd1++` achieve substantial accuracy gains on reasoning benchmarks while requiring significantly fewer rollouts and training compute compared to diffusion-GRPO baselines.

### Strengths
1. **Clear Problem Motivation & Targeted Solution:** The paper accurately identifies a core bottleneck in applying GRPO-style RL to dLLMs: likelihood intractability forces ratio approximations that compound error and increase FLOPs/NFEs (Sec 2.3, Fig 1). `wd1` directly circumvents this by deriving a ratio-free weighted log-likelihood objective from reverse-KL regularized optimization (Sec 3.1, Eq 7-8).
2. **Strong Theoretical Grounding:** The authors formally prove that the proposed objective is equivalent to training an energy-guided discrete diffusion model while unlearning low-advantage samples (Sec 4, Lemmas 1 & 2, Thm 1, Remarks 1-2). The monotonic improvement guarantee for reverse-KL regularization is also established (Thm 2, App A.2), providing solid analytical justification.
3. **Empirical Efficiency & Performance Gains:** `wd1` eliminates the need for supervised fine-tuning (SFT) and reduces per-step training time, FLOPs, and NFEs by avoiding old/reference policy likelihood evaluations (Table 2). The `wd1++` variant achieves competitive math reasoning scores with 10× fewer rollouts than concurrent baselines (Table 3, left & right), demonstrating strong sample efficiency.
4. **Comprehensive Ablation Studies:** The paper thoughtfully validates key design choices. Removing the negative weight (`w^-`) or skewing the combined weight (`cw`) severely degrades performance or causes training collapse, confirming the necessity of balanced positive/negative reinforcement (Sec 5.2, Table 4, Table 9, Fig 2).

### Weaknesses
1. **Reliance on Biased Likelihood Approximation:** While ratio-free, `wd1` still depends on approximating $\log \pi_\theta$ using the `_d_1` single-step method ($t=1$, masked prompt). The authors acknowledge this introduces bias (Sec D, Limitations), but do not rigorously analyze or empirically test how this bias propagates through the weighted objective across different approximators (e.g., ELBO vs. `_d_1`).
2. **Unquantified Overhead of Geometric Mixture Sampling:** Sampling from $\pi_{old}^\lambda \pi_{ref}^\beta$ (Alg 1, line 4-5) requires multiple forward passes or cached logits, which partially offsets the claimed compute savings (App B.3). The paper lacks a concrete FLOPs/runtime breakdown comparing mixture sampling overhead against ratio computation to fully substantiate the efficiency claim.
3. **Benchmark Scope & Reward Shaping Concerns:** Major accuracy gains ($+58.8\%$) are concentrated on 4x4 Sudoku and a simplified 3-number Countdown subset, which may not reflect complex reasoning. Additionally, the custom reward functions heavily weight formatting/structure (e.g., $+0.125$ per XML tag, strict answer formatting in App B.2), making it unclear how much improvement stems from genuine reasoning capability versus reward hacking/format compliance.
4. **Limited Baseline Comparison & Robustness Signals:** The main experiments omit recently cited ratio-free/score-based diffusion RL methods (SPG, SEPO, `d2`), limiting contextualization. Furthermore, training dynamics show high seed sensitivity, with abrupt reward drops under a different random seed (Fig 4 right), suggesting potential fragility that isn't addressed with variance metrics or multiple runs.

### Novelty & Significance
**Novelty:** High within the dLLM subfield. The translation of advantage-weighted regression to a ratio-free diffusion RL objective, coupled with the explicit positive/negative weight decomposition and its formal equivalence to energy-guided diffusion scoring, is conceptually fresh and well-motivated.  
**Clarity:** Strong. The mathematical derivations are logically sequenced, the algorithm is clearly pseudocoded (Alg 1), and figures/tables effectively support claims. Minor density in the diffusion theory section (Sec 4) is mitigated by clear propositions.  
**Reproducibility:** Good but could be tighter. The code repository is linked, datasets and reward structures are detailed in appendices, and hyperparameters are tabulated (Table 5-6). However, the noted seed sensitivity and lack of confidence intervals/standard deviations across runs leave some uncertainty about result stability.  
**Significance:** Meaningful. As dLLMs gain traction for inference efficiency, practical RL fine-tuning methods that reduce variance and compute are highly valuable. The 10× rollout reduction in `wd1++` and elimination of SFT offer a compelling pathway for scalable diffusion LLM alignment, though impact remains somewhat domain-specific.

### Suggestions for Improvement
1. **Provide a Compute Breakdown for Mixture Sampling:** Quantify the exact forward/backward pass overhead of sampling from the geometric mixture vs. computing ratios in `_d_1`. A simple table or equation showing net FLOPs/time savings per global step would solidify the efficiency claim.
2. **Add Ablation on Likelihood Approximators:** Systematically evaluate `_wd1` using the `_d_1` single-step approximator vs. a multi-sample ELBO estimator. Report performance, variance, and training stability to demonstrate how robust the weighted objective is to approximation bias.
3. **Expand Baselines & Report Robustness Metrics:** Include empirical comparisons to concurrent ratio-free methods (SPG, SEPO, `d2`) on at least one shared benchmark. Additionally, report mean $\pm$ std over 3+ random seeds for all main results and clarify why the seed in Fig 4 (right) causes training instability.
4. **Decouple Reasoning Gains from Formatting Rewards:** Conduct an experiment using a purely correctness-based verifier reward on GSM8K/MATH500 (or report these separately) to show that performance improvements generalize beyond format compliance, addressing potential reward-shaping confounds.

# Spark Finder Review
## How to Improve This Paper

### Missing Experiments (top 3-5 only)
1. Add compute-matched learning curves (accuracy vs. total FLOPs or wall-time) for `wd1`/`wd1++` vs. `d1`/`MDPO`, because claiming efficiency via "fewer steps/rollouts" without controlling for total compute budget fails to prove true algorithmic sample efficiency.
2. Include evaluation on out-of-distribution reasoning benchmarks (e.g., OlympiadBench, AIME) alongside MATH500/GSM8K, because high scores on standard splits cannot rule out data contamination or verifier reward overfitting, which are critical validity checks for ICLR.
3. Provide results using alternative likelihood estimators (e.g., ELBO with multiple $t$ samples) instead of only the biased $t=1$ approximation, to demonstrate that performance gains stem from the ratio-free objective itself rather than a fortuitous cancellation of errors with a specific biased estimator.
4. Add a controlled experiment measuring performance under strict compute caps (e.g., fixed budget of 1000 rollouts per method), because without equal-compute ablation, the "10× fewer rollouts" claim cannot be disentangled from potential implementation advantages or hyperparameter tuning differences.

### Deeper Analysis Needed (top 3-5 only)
1. Add empirical measurement of gradient variance and weight entropy across training steps, because the theoretical worst-case bound in Appendix A.1 does not prove that normalized exponential weights actually stabilize training dynamics in practice compared to linear GRPO weighting.
2. Analyze the approximation error introduced by computing $\log \pi_{old}^{ref}$ via logit-space interpolation ($\lambda \log \pi_{old} + \beta \log \pi_{ref}$), because this inexact sampling proxy violates the exact geometric mixture assumption required for the monotonic improvement guarantees in Theorem 2.
3. Report sequence diversity metrics such as pairwise completion KL or distinct n-gram counts over training, since aggressive exponential weighting in ratio-free objectives is prone to mode collapse, and showing preserved diversity is necessary to confirm the method learns robust reasoning rather than memorizing high-reward templates.
4. Quantify the impact of the "random mask prompt" strategy ($p_{mask}=0.15$) on likelihood approximation stability, because introducing stochasticity into the context during RL updates can act as an unstated regularizer that confounding the reported gains of the proposed objective.

### Visualizations & Case Studies
1. Include side-by-side qualitative examples of Sudoku/Countdown completions showing base model failures corrected by `wd1`, specifically highlighting steps where the negative weight $w^{[-]}$ actively suppressed detrimental tokens to prove the unlearning mechanism functions as claimed.
2. Visualize the evolving distribution of advantages and corresponding weights $w^{[+]}$ / $w^{[-]}$ across diffusion steps, because histograms revealing heavy-tailed weights would indicate effective sample rejection, whereas uniform weights would suggest the method degenerates into standard SFT.
3. Provide a detailed error categorization on the 55.8% of MATH500 cases where `wd1++` fails, distinguishing between logic errors, format violations, and calculation mistakes, to demonstrate whether the method fundamentally improves reasoning or primarily optimizes towards the verifier's formatting rewards.

### Obvious Next Steps
1. Define and validate how advantage estimation in `wd1++` handles intermediate denoising steps, because applying a global sequence reward to every step $l$ in Equation 10 ignores the temporal dynamics of diffusion and requires either per-step advantage calculation or a value function to maintain theoretical validity.
2. Conduct experiments with $\beta > 0$ to activate the reference policy regularization, because the current implementation sets $\beta=0$ which removes the geometric mixture sampling component central to the paper's derivations (Proposition 2, Theorem 2), leaving the core theoretical claims unverified by the empirical results.
3. Perform an analysis comparing the reward landscape and initial gradient norms of SFT-initialized vs. base models, because simply stating SFT is unnecessary lacks mechanistic insight; showing that $w^{[-]}$ prevents early policy collapse without warm-up would explain *why* the pipeline works and strengthen the method's contribution.

# Final Consolidated Review
## Summary
This paper introduces `wd1`, a ratio-free reinforcement learning algorithm for masked diffusion language models (dLLMs) that reformulates policy optimization as an advantage-weighted log-likelihood objective. By explicitly balancing positive sample reinforcement with negative sample unlearning, the method bypasses the high-variance, error-compounding likelihood ratios required by diffusion-GRPO. A stepwise extension (`wd1++`) leverages intermediate denoising completions for credit assignment. The approach reports substantial accuracy gains on structural reasoning benchmarks and lower per-step computational overhead compared to existing diffusion RL methods.

## Strengths
- **Directly tackles a fundamental flaw in diffusion RL:** The paper correctly identifies that computing importance-sampling ratios from intractable dLLM likelihoods exponentially amplifies approximation error. The shift to a ratio-free weighted log-likelihood (Sec 3) is a mathematically principled alternative that inherently stabilizes the optimization signal.
- **Strong validation of the negative unlearning component:** Ablations rigorously demonstrate that removing $w^{[-]}$ or skewing the positive/negative weight balance severely degrades or collapses training (Table 4, Table 9). This empirically confirms that explicit negative sample penalization is critical for preventing probability drift in diffusion policies.
- **Clear reduction in per-step computational burden:** By eliminating forward passes for old/reference policy ratio computation and removing the mandatory SFT warm-up, `wd1` achieves measurable drops in NFEs, FLOPs, and wall-clock time per global step (Table 2), offering a practically viable pipeline for dLLM alignment.

## Weaknesses
- **Severe theory-practice mismatch undermines core derivations:** The theoretical guarantees for monotonic improvement (Theorem 2) and the closed-form exponential policy (Proposition 2) strictly assume sampling from a geometric mixture $\pi_{\text{old}}^\lambda \pi_{\text{ref}}^\beta$. However, the implementation sets $\beta=0$ for all main experiments, collapsing sampling to $\pi_{\text{old}}$ entirely. This bypasses the central theoretical assumption without justification or empirical analysis of how the deviation impacts objective optimality or off-policy correction.
- **Unjustified heuristic in `wd1++` credit assignment:** The stepwise objective assigns a single, terminal sequence-level reward to all intermediate denoising steps ($l < L$). The paper provides no value function, reward-to-go mechanism, or distributional correction to handle partially denoised, semantically incomplete sequences. Broadcasting terminal correctness rewards across the denoising trajectory introduces severe baseline mismatch, making the theoretical extension in Eq 10 empirically and analytically fragile.
- **Unsubstantiated efficiency and sample-efficiency claims:** The paper reports lower per-step costs but lacks compute-matched learning curves (accuracy vs. total FLOPs or wall-time). Crucially, `wd1` uses LoRA while primary baselines (MDPO, SDPO) employ full parameter fine-tuning (Table 3). Claims of "10× fewer rollouts" achieving superior performance are confounded by parameter efficiency, potential hyperparameter tuning advantages, and missing strict equal-compute ablations. Algorithmic sample efficiency remains unproven.
- **Evaluation confounds and lack of statistical rigor:** The largest reported gains (+43–59%) occur on simplified subsets (4×4 Sudoku, 3-number Countdown) where custom rewards heavily penalize formatting and XML structure over logical reasoning (Appendix B.2). On standard math benchmarks, gains are marginal. Furthermore, all main results are single-seed with no error bars, despite explicit evidence of high variance and seed sensitivity (Fig 4, right). This makes it impossible to distinguish genuine reasoning improvements from format-compliance overfitting or stochastic training artifacts.

## Nice-to-Haves
- Sequence diversity analysis (e.g., distinct n-grams or pairwise completion KL) to rule out mode collapse, as exponential advantage weighting can concentrate probability mass onto narrow, high-reward templates.
- Qualitative case studies explicitly showing how $w^{[-]}$ suppresses detrimental tokens during denoising, to ground the "unlearning" interpretation in observable generation behavior.

## Novel Insights
The paper’s core conceptual contribution—reframing dLLM fine-tuning as advantage-weighted log-likelihood maximization rather than ratio-based importance sampling—is both theoretically elegant and practically necessary given the intractability of diffusion likelihoods. By formally linking the negative-weighted loss term to energy-guided discrete diffusion sampling and data unlearning, the authors provide a unified lens through which reverse-KL regularized RL and diffusion score matching can be understood. The explicit decomposition and balancing of positive and negative weights offers a robust mechanism for actively steering denoising trajectories away from low-advantage regions, effectively solving the probability mass drift and variance explosion that plague standard diffusion-GRPO variants.

## Potentially Missed Related Work
- **SPG (Wang et al., 2025a) & SEPO (Zekri & Boullé, 2025)** — Both develop ratio-free or score-based diffusion RL objectives but are only cited without empirical comparison. Including at least one would better contextualize `wd1`'s relative advantages and clarify whether performance gains stem from the weighted formulation or other implementation choices.

## Suggestions
- **Formalize and stabilize `wd1++` credit assignment:** Implement a per-step advantage estimation mechanism (e.g., learned value baselines or stepwise TD returns) or rigorously ablate the impact of terminal-reward broadcasting under controlled distributional shift to justify Eq 10.
- **Isolate reasoning gains from formatting compliance:** Re-evaluate on GSM8K/MATH500 using a strict, format-agnostic correctness verifier, and report results disaggregated by reward component. This is critical to prove that `wd1` improves mathematical/logical reasoning rather than structural reward hacking.
- **Validate under matched compute and parameters:** Run direct comparisons using identical fine-tuning paradigms (LoRA vs. full FT) and report compute-normalized learning curves. Multi-seed evaluations (≥3 runs) with confidence intervals are mandatory for RL submissions to establish statistical validity and rule out seed-driven variance.
