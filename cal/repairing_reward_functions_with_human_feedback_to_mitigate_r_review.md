# Harsh Critic Review
## Section-by-Section Critical Review

### Title & Abstract
- **Title accuracy:** The title accurately reflects the core contribution: repairing a proxy reward function using preference feedback to mitigate reward hacking.
- **Abstract clarity:** The abstract clearly states the problem (proxy reward misalignment vs. costly RLHF from scratch), the method (iterative additive correction with targeted exploration and a new preference-learning objective), and the key results (regret bounds matching prior work up to constants, and empirical outperformance with fewer preferences).
- **Unsupported claims:** The claim of "substantially fewer preferences" is qualitatively supported by Figure 2, but the abstract does not quantify the magnitude of the sample efficiency gain. This is minor and acceptable for an abstract. The phrase "up to constants" in the theoretical claim is standard and accurate per Section 5.

### Introduction & Motivation
- **Problem motivation & gap:** The motivation is strong and well-grounded in the RLHF literature. The authors correctly identify that manual reward function iteration is slow and expertise-dependent, while learning from scratch is sample-intensive. The gap between these two is clearly articulated.
- **Contributions:** The three contributions (method, regret analysis, empirical evaluation) are explicit and accurately reflect the paper's contents.
- **Over/Under-claim:** The introduction heavily frames PBRR as automating a process that typically relies on "RL expertise that many domain experts do not have." However, the method's core exploration strategy fundamentally relies on a reference policy $\pi_{\text{ref}}$. While Appendix G.8 shows a randomly initialized $\pi_{\text{ref}}$ can work, the introduction does not preemptively address the cost or availability of a reference policy, creating a slight tension between the "automate domain expert workflow" narrative and the algorithm's actual requirements. This does not invalidate the contribution but should be acknowledged earlier to set accurate expectations.

### Method / Approach
- **Clarity & reproducibility:** Algorithm 1 and the decomposition $\hat{r}_t = \hat{r}_{\text{proxy}} + g_t$ are clearly described. The preference-learning objective (Eq. 3) is well-motivated and reproducible given the appendix details.
- **Key assumptions & justification:** The method assumes the proxy reward function is "aligned or overly optimistic" (Section 4, Footnote 1). This justifies the $L_-$ regularization term, which prioritizes *decreasing* reward on misranked trajectories rather than increasing it. The assumption is justified for many reward-hacking scenarios (e.g., loop-collecting points), but its robustness is a critical design choice. The authors note that decaying $\lambda_{1,2}$ mitigates violations, but the mechanism by which the model escapes a structural bias toward reward *reduction* when the proxy is actually *pessimistic* is not theoretically derived.
- **Logical gaps & edge cases:** 
  - **Exploration limitation ($C_1=0$):** Algorithm 1 includes a fallback to max-divergence policy pairs (Lines 6-10) controlled by $C_1$, but the authors state $C_1=0$ in all experiments. Appendix I.1 (MDP 2) explicitly demonstrates that when $C_1=0$, PBRR can converge to a suboptimal policy if $\pi_{\text{ref}}$ and the current $\pi_{\hat{r}_t}^*$ do not cover the optimal trajectory. While honestly acknowledged, this is a fundamental limitation of the empirical implementation that restricts the method to environments where inter-policy preferences between the proxy-optimized policy and reference policy are sufficiently informative.
  - **Theoretical vs. Empirical Preference Model:** The regret analysis (Section 5 & Appendix K) assumes preferences are noiselessly determined by *regret* under the ground-truth reward (Eq. 42), which is standard for theoretical alignment proofs. However, the experiments simulate preferences using the *sum of discounted rewards* (Appendix E.5, justified in Appendix A). Regret and trajectory sum rankings are not equivalent, especially when horizons differ or terminal states are absorbing. This creates a disconnect: the theory guarantees do not strictly apply to the empirical preference generation process.
- **Proof correctness:** The regret analysis heavily adapts Pacchiano et al. (2023). As stated in Appendix J, the core lemmas hold because they don't depend on the specific policy selection, only on the condition that selected policies remain in the confidence/undominated set. The derivation appears correct within its established assumptions, though it introduces an additional constant $C_1$ that reflects the relaxed exploration constraint.

### Experiments & Results
- **Testing claims:** The experiments directly test data efficiency, stability, and performance against strong baselines across four diverse environments. The results consistently support the claim that PBRR requires fewer preferences and achieves higher stability.
- **Baselines:** Comparisons to Online-RLHF, RRM, and state-constrained methods are appropriate. State-Constrained-PPO is a baseline that uses no additional preference data, serving as a lower bound on what's possible without feedback; this is fair but should be contextualized. The inclusion of RRM (concurrent work) is timely and properly differentiated.
- **Missing ablations / Statistical rigor:** 
  - The paper reports standard error over 3 random seeds in main figures. While Appendix G.9 extends to 10 seeds for one environment and demonstrates statistical significance after updates, ICLR standards increasingly expect rigorous statistical testing (e.g., significance tests, confidence intervals) across all main results to rule out seed-dependent variance, especially given the noted instability of baselines.
  - An ablation varying the decay schedule of $\lambda_{1,2}$ would strengthen the understanding of how the method recovers in pessimistic proxy settings (e.g., Glucose Monitoring). The current decay rule ($\lambda^{(i)} = 10/|D_+|$, Eq referenced in Appendix E.6) is heuristic.
- **Results support claims:** The performance gains are robust across seeds and environments. The stability plots (shaded regions in Fig 2) strongly support the claim that baselines suffer from oscillatory reward learning, while PBRR's constrained updates prevent catastrophic reward model collapse.
- **Datasets & Metrics:** Using simulated environments from Pan et al. (2022) is appropriate for reward-hacking benchmarks. However, preferences are simulated from the ground-truth reward via a Boltzmann distribution. This sidesteps well-known issues in human preference modeling (e.g., positional bias, consistency limits, non-rationality). While the authors justify this in Appendix A to isolate reward repair from preference model misspecification, it limits the direct applicability of the "RLHF" framing to real human-in-the-loop settings.

### Writing & Clarity
- **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-structured and clearly written. The separation of methodology, theory, and experiments aids readability.
- **Figures/Tables:** Figures 2 and 3 are informative but use scaled/clipped returns $[-1, 1]$ for visual clarity. While the clipping is justified (Appendix G.1) and unscaled results are provided in Appendix G.7, the main text should explicitly state when a method performs *worse* than the reference policy (clipped to $-1$) rather than just "stable at baseline." The qualitative analysis in Appendix H is exceptionally clear and highly valuable for understanding failure modes.
- **Theory-Practice Bridge:** As noted in Method, the shift from regret-based preferences (theory) to reward-sum-based preferences (experiments) is mentioned in Appendix A but not explicitly bridged in the main text. A brief discussion in Section 5 or 6 acknowledging this gap and explaining why the empirical results still align with theoretical intuition would improve cohesion.

### Limitations & Broader Impact
- **Acknowledged limitations:** The authors acknowledge the pessimistic proxy case (Appendix G.6), the $C_1=0$ exploration bottleneck (Appendix I.1), and the preference simulation model (Appendix A). This is commendable.
- **Missed limitations:** 
  - **Compute Overhead:** PBRR requires iterative retraining of both the reward correction model and an RL policy (PPO) after each preference batch. The compute cost and wall-clock efficiency compared to single-shot RLHF baselines are not discussed. In practical RLHF pipelines, policy retraining often dominates the cost, which could offset the sample efficiency gains from fewer preferences.
  - **Real Human Bias:** The paper frames the problem around human feedback but assumes Boltzmann noise. It does not discuss how systematic human biases (e.g., focusing on salient but unimportant trajectory segments, or inconsistent judgments under cognitive load) would interact with the $L_+$ and $L_-$ regularizers. If humans mislabel preferences that "agree" with the proxy, $L_+$ could incorrectly penalize necessary corrections.
- **Broader Impact/Failure modes:** Applications in policy design (pandemics, taxes) and clinical decision-making are high-stakes. The paper briefly notes these domains but does not discuss the failure mode where a suboptimal but "stable" repaired reward function (as seen in MDP 2) could be deployed with false confidence. Given the method's stability is a key selling point, a warning about over-trusting a stable but locally optimal reward model would be responsible.

### Overall Assessment
This is a strong, well-motivated paper that addresses a practical and increasingly relevant gap in alignment: how to leverage imperfect human-specified reward functions rather than discarding them or rebuilding from scratch. The proposed preference-learning objective (Eq. 3) is conceptually clean, effectively mitigating the instability common in iterative reward learning, and the empirical results across four challenging environments are convincing. The theoretical analysis, while largely adapting prior work correctly, is slightly weakened by a disconnect between the regret-based preference assumption in the proofs and the reward-sum simulation used empirically. Additionally, the reliance on $C_1=0$ in practice introduces a known exploration failure mode (Appendix I.1) that is honestly reported but limits theoretical guarantees to specific initial conditions. With minor clarifications bridging theory and practice, explicit discussion of compute trade-offs, and more rigorous statistical reporting across all seeds, this work would represent a solid contribution to the ICLR community. The core insight—that constraining reward corrections based on proxy-policy agreement significantly outperforms standard fine-tuning when repairing misspecified objectives—is valuable and likely to inspire follow-up work in efficient alignment.

# Neutral Reviewer
## Balanced Review

### Summary
The paper introduces Preference-Based Reward Repair (PBRR), an iterative alignment framework that corrects a misspecified human proxy reward function by learning an additive transition-dependent correction from trajectory preferences. PBRR combines a targeted exploration strategy contrasting the current policy with a reference policy against a novel asymmetric loss function that focuses regularization on transitions where the proxy incorrectly assigns high reward. The authors provide theoretical regret guarantees matching prior state-of-the-art bounds in linear/tabular settings and demonstrate empirically that PBRR achieves higher stability and data efficiency than standard RLHF and residual reward baselines across multiple reward-hacking benchmarks.

### Strengths
1. **Well-motivated and practical learning objective:** Equation (3) introduces a clever asymmetric regularization scheme ($L_+$ and $L_-$) that explicitly leverages the prior expectation that human proxy rewards are often "overly optimistic." Ablations in Figure 3 and Appendix G.4 confirm that without these terms, the standard preference loss leads to unstable updates and degraded performance.
2. **Strong empirical evaluation across diverse domains:** The experiments cover high-dimensional continuous control and discrete safety benchmarks (Pandemic, Glucose, Traffic, AI Safety Gridworld) where proxy rewards deliberately induce severe reward hacking. PBRR consistently outperforms strong baselines (Online-RLHF, RRM, state-constrained PPO) in both jump-start performance and final returns, while exhibiting markedly better training stability (Section 6.3, Appendix G).
3. **Solid theoretical grounding:** The regret analysis (Section 5, Appendix J) successfully adapts and matches the $\tilde{O}(\sqrt{T})$ cumulative regret bounds of Pacchiano et al. (2023) up to constants, demonstrating that the targeted exploration strategy does not theoretically sacrifice sample complexity. The asymptotic guarantee that the repaired policy outperforms $\pi_{\text{ref}}$ (Theorem K.1) further strengthens the method's theoretical foundation.
4. **Thorough ablation and implementation details:** The paper meticulously isolates the contributions of the loss function vs. exploration strategy, tests robustness to pessimistic proxies (Appendix G.6), evaluates randomly initialized reference policies (Appendix G.8), and provides full hyperparameter searches and environment configurations (Appendices B, E), supporting strong reproducibility.

### Weaknesses
1. **Theory-practice gap in exploration and function approximation:** The regret guarantees (Theorems 5.1 & 5.2) rely on linear reward parameterization and a tabular setting with a fallback mechanism ($C_1 > 0$) for uncertainty-maximizing exploration. However, Section 6 explicitly sets $C_1=0$ for all experiments and uses neural network reward models in continuous, non-linear MDPs. The paper acknowledges this but does not theoretically bridge why the empirical setup retains efficiency without the uncertainty fallback, leaving a gap between theoretical claims and practical implementation.
2. **Fragility of the "optimistic proxy" assumption in the loss design:** The $L_-$ regularization is motivated by the assumption that proxies are overly optimistic. While Appendix G.6 shows PBRR still works when this is violated, it requires more preferences, and the heuristic decay schedule for $\lambda_1, \lambda_2$ (normalized by $|D_t^+|$ in Appendix E.6) lacks formal justification for how it adapts to pessimistic or highly misaligned proxies. The method's performance under strong proxy pessimism remains theoretically and practically underexplored.
3. **Dependence on a meaningful reference policy:** PBRR's exploration core relies on contrasting trajectories from the optimized proxy against $\pi_{\text{ref}}$. While Appendix G.8 shows a random network can substitute, the paper assumes trajectories from $\pi_{\text{ref}}$ provide sufficient state-action coverage to identify mismatches. In non-ergodic or highly constrained MDPs (e.g., Glucose monitoring where poor treatments lead to absorbing states), a naive $\pi_{\text{ref}}$ may fail to visit critical corrective regions, yet the paper does not formally analyze coverage requirements or how to detect insufficient $\pi_{\text{ref}}$ coverage online.
4. **Limited statistical rigor:** Main results are averaged over 3 random seeds. While Table 6 extends to 10 seeds for one environment, the broader evaluation lacks confidence intervals or hypothesis testing across seeds. Given ICLR's emphasis on statistical robustness, the performance gaps, while visually clear, would benefit from stronger statistical validation across multiple environments.

### Novelty & Significance
**Novelty:** Moderate-High. The idea of repairing proxy rewards with an additive term is emerging (e.g., concurrent work by Cao et al., 2025), but PBRR's key novelty lies in the synergy between reference-policy-targeted exploration and the asymmetric preference loss. The formulation cleanly separates when to leave the proxy unchanged versus when to aggressively penalize misaligned high-reward transitions, which is a distinct contribution over standard cross-entropy preference learning.
**Significance:** High. Reward misspecification and the data inefficiency of RLHF are central bottlenecks for deploying RL in safety-critical domains. PBRR offers a practically viable middle ground: leveraging a human's initial (flawed) intuition while automatically correcting it with minimal preference feedback. The combination of clear theoretical bounds and rigorous benchmarking across diverse policy-design tasks aligns strongly with ICLR's focus on algorithmic advances with real-world relevance.
**Clarity:** Excellent. The paper is well-structured, with intuitive motivation, clear notation, and a comprehensive appendix that anticipates reader questions.
**Reproducibility:** Strong. Code is provided, hyperparameters are explicitly tabulated, environment setups are fully detailed, and training procedures (including PPO iterations and reward model re-initialization) are transparently documented.

### Suggestions for Improvement
1. **Explicitly discuss the theory-to-practice translation:** Add a dedicated subsection in Section 6 or Appendix analyzing how the regret bounds behave under $C_1=0$ and non-linear reward approximators. Empirically, does the policy-ref divergence naturally approximate the uncertainty-maximizing pair in these environments? A brief diagnostic would strengthen the link between theory and experiments.
2. **Formalize or empirically optimize the $\lambda$ decay schedule:** The current schedule ($\lambda_t \propto 1/|D_t^+|$) is intuitive but heuristic. Include an ablation over alternative decay strategies (e.g., fixed $\lambda$, linear decay, or decay based on preference consistency) to show robustness and provide practical guidance for tuning.
3. **Analyze reference policy coverage requirements:** Provide a qualitative or quantitative metric for $\pi_{\text{ref}}$ coverage (e.g., state-visitation overlap with $\pi_{\hat{r}_t}^*$) and discuss how to verify it online. If coverage is poor, does adding a small amount of uniform exploration or entropy regularization to the data collection step help? Brief experiments or theoretical bounds on coverage thresholds would be valuable.
4. **Strengthen statistical reporting:** Extend the 10-seed evaluation to at least one additional environment, or report bootstrapped 95% confidence intervals over the existing 3 seeds for key metrics (final return, sample efficiency). This will make the empirical claims more robust and ICLR-ready.

# Final Consolidated Review
## Summary
This paper introduces Preference-Based Reward Repair (PBRR), an iterative framework that aligns a misspecified human proxy reward function by learning an additive, transition-level correction from trajectory preferences. PBRR combines a targeted exploration strategy that contrasts the current policy against a reference policy with a novel asymmetric preference loss that regularizes updates based on proxy agreement. The method achieves strong empirical stability and data efficiency across diverse reward-hacking benchmarks, while providing theoretical cumulative regret bounds that match prior RLHF methods in tabular/linear settings.

## Strengths
- **Novel, stability-preserving learning objective:** The asymmetric loss (Eq. 3) explicitly leverages the structural prior that human proxies are often over-optimistic, penalizing corrections only where the proxy misranks high-reward transitions while preserving aligned ones. Ablations (Fig. 3, App. G.4) demonstrate this design is critical for preventing the catastrophic reward model collapse and policy oscillation endemic to standard iterative preference learning.
- **Strong empirical efficiency across challenging benchmarks:** PBRR consistently outperforms robust baselines (Online-RLHF, RRM, state-constrained PPO) in both jump-start and final performance across four high-dimensional reward-hacking environments. The method requires substantially fewer preference queries while exhibiting markedly smoother learning curves, directly addressing a core bottleneck in practical RLHF pipelines.
- **Rigorous theoretical grounding and reproducibility:** The regret analysis correctly adapts prior work to show $\tilde{O}(\sqrt{T})$ bounds up to constants, and the asymptotic guarantee that $\pi_{\hat{r}}^*$ outperforms $\pi_{\text{ref}}$ provides a solid foundation. The paper is exceptionally thorough in its appendices, covering pessimistic proxy settings, randomized reference policies, hyperparameter sweeps, and detailed environment configurations, ensuring high reproducibility.

## Weaknesses
- **Theory-practice gap in exploration and function approximation:** The regret guarantees (Thm. 5.1/5.2) depend on linear reward parameterization and an exploration fallback ($C_1 > 0$) that actively queries uncertainty-maximizing policy pairs. All empirical results set $C_1=0$ due to intractability and use neural network approximators in continuous, non-linear MDPs. While the authors acknowledge this, the paper does not analyze or empirically quantify why the $(\pi_{\text{ref}}, \pi_{\hat{r}_t}^*)$ pair selection retains sample efficiency without the theoretical fallback, nor does it characterize how often the inter-policy divergence satisfies the uncertainty-maximization condition in practice.
- **Unanalyzed dependence on reference policy coverage:** The core exploration mechanism relies on trajectory contrasts between $\pi_{\text{ref}}$ and $\pi_{\hat{r}_t}^*$. Although Appendix G.8 shows randomly initialized reference policies can work, the paper lacks any formal or empirical analysis of the state-action coverage requirements for $\pi_{\text{ref}}$. In non-ergodic or absorbing-state environments (e.g., Glucose Monitoring), insufficient coverage could prevent the algorithm from encountering critical transitions needed to correct the proxy, yet no diagnostic metric or theoretical bound on coverage sufficiency is provided.
- **Limited statistical reporting:** Main empirical claims rely on averages over 3 random seeds with standard error shading. While Appendix G.9 validates significance with 10 seeds in one environment, ICLR standards increasingly expect robust statistical validation across multiple environments. Without 95% confidence intervals or hypothesis testing for the high-dimensional settings, it remains difficult to confidently rule out seed-dependent variance given the pronounced instability of the compared baselines.

## Nice-to-Haves
- Analyze the heuristic $\lambda_{1,2}$ decay schedule ($\propto 1/|D_t^+|$, App. E.6) with ablations on fixed, linear, or consistency-based decay to demonstrate robustness, particularly when proxy optimism is heavily violated.
- Report wall-clock or compute overhead relative to single-shot baselines, as iterative PPO retraining and reward correction updates could offset sample efficiency gains in resource-constrained pipelines.
- Empirically validate the theoretical regime by enabling $C_1 > 0$ in linear/tabular settings to confirm that the regret constants and uncertainty-maximizing fallback behave as proven.
- Assess robustness to more realistic human preference models (e.g., non-Boltzmann noise, positional biases, or segment-level credit assignment) to strengthen the real-world RLHF framing beyond ground-truth reward simulation.

## Novel Insights
PBRR's conceptual advance lies in reframing reward alignment not as learning from scratch, but as a constrained repair process that treats initial human specifications as malleable scaffolds rather than fixed constraints. By explicitly partitioning preference data into trajectories where the proxy already aligns with human judgment and those where it misranks high-reward signals, the method asymmetrically regularizes corrections to prioritize penalizing over-optimistic incentives. This targeted, stability-preserving mechanism effectively sidesteps the reward model collapse that plagues standard iterative RLHF, suggesting a promising middle ground that leverages imperfect human intuition while requiring minimal preference feedback to achieve robust alignment.

## Suggestions
- Add a brief discussion or empirical diagnostic in Section 6 explaining how the $(\pi_{\text{ref}}, \pi_{\hat{r}_t}^*)$ pairing approximates the theoretical uncertainty-maximizing condition when $C_1=0$. Quantifying policy divergence, state-visitation overlap, or feature-space distance across iterations would help justify the empirical sample complexity and bridge the identified theory-practice gap.
- Strengthen statistical reporting by providing 95% confidence intervals or non-parametric hypothesis testing across $\geq 5$ seeds for at least two additional environments (e.g., Traffic Control and AI Safety Gridworld) to bolster confidence in the stability and efficiency claims.
