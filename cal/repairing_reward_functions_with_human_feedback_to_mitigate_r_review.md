# Harsh Critic Review
## Section-by-Section Critical Review

### Title & Abstract
- **Title accuracy:** The title accurately captures the core contribution: repairing proxy reward functions via preferences to address reward hacking.
- **Abstract clarity:** The abstract clearly states the problem (proxy misspecification leads to reward hacking; RLHF is sample-inefficient), the method (PBRR: iterative additive correction via targeted exploration and a structured preference loss), and the results (sublinear regret bounds matching prior theory; empirical gains over baselines on reward-hacking benchmarks).
- **Claim support:** The abstract claims PBRR requires "substantially fewer preferences." This is supported empirically in Figure 2. However, the claim implicitly assumes simulated preferences match real human feedback efficiency. The abstract could benefit from a brief qualifier that experiments rely on simulated ground-truth preferences, which is a standard but non-trivial assumption in RLHF papers targeting ICLR. Otherwise, claims are well-grounded.

### Introduction & Motivation
- **Problem motivation & gap:** The motivation is compelling. The gap is well-identified: manual reward engineering is fragile, while standard RLHF learns from scratch and ignores available domain knowledge (the proxy).
- **Contributions:** Clearly stated. The threefold contribution list is accurate.
- **Over-claiming/Under-selling:** The introduction conjectures that "humans typically provide reward functions that are aligned or overly optimistic" and builds the loss function around this. While a reasonable design hypothesis, this is presented somewhat strongly without empirical backing on how actual human designers specify rewards. ICLR reviewers will likely probe whether PBRR's advantage is tightly coupled to this optimism assumption, especially since the Glucose environment violates it. Framing this more explicitly as a *design assumption* rather than an observed human tendency would be more precise. Additionally, the introduction mentions "corrections on only a few transitions may suffice," but the experiments collect large batches of preferences per iteration (e.g., $k^2$ pairs where $k \in \{19,39,79\}$). The discrepancy between "few transitions" conceptually and the actual preference query count warrants a sentence or two of clarification.

### Method / Approach
- **Clarity & Reproducibility:** The method is generally clear. Algorithm 1 provides a concrete implementation. However, a critical reproducibility gap exists regarding how the trajectory-level preference loss backpropagates to the transition-level correction term $g(s,a,s')$. Section 4 defines $\hat{r}_{t+1} = \hat{r}_{proxy} + g_t$, and Eq. 3 uses $g(\tau)^2$. If $g(\tau) \propto \sum_t g(s_t,a_t,s'_t)$, then standard cross-entropy loss will distribute gradients uniformly across all transitions in the trajectory. This exacerbates the credit assignment issue explicitly acknowledged in Appendix A. The authors should clarify how gradients are assigned to individual $(s,a,s')$ tuples (e.g., via Monte Carlo return weighting, or if $g$ is trained with a temporal decomposition).
- **Assumptions:** The optimism assumption is stated and justified, with decay schedules for $\lambda_1, \lambda_2$ to handle violations (Appendix E.6). This is reasonable.
- **Logical gaps / Theory-Practice mismatch:** The most significant concern is the disconnect between the theoretical analysis and the empirical setup. Theorem 5.1's regret bound explicitly relies on the exploration strategy that selects policy pairs maximizing divergence within the non-dominated set (Lines 7-10, Algorithm 1, controlled by $C_1$). However, Section 6 states: *"we set $C_1 = 0$, which implies... PBRR always uses the reference policy and the policy that optimizes for the corrected proxy reward function."* This means the *theoretically justified exploration strategy is never used in the experiments*. The empirical success relies entirely on $\pi_{ref}$ providing sufficient contrast. If $\pi_{ref}$ has poor support overlap with the optimal policy, the method could theoretically stall. The authors should explicitly state that the theoretical bounds apply to the algorithm *with* $C_1 > 0$, and provide a stronger theoretical or empirical justification for why the $C_1=0$ heuristic suffices in practice.
- **Edge cases:** The method assumes preferences are elicited over full trajectories $H$. If the proxy reward induces a catastrophic failure state early in the trajectory, the full-trajectory preference label will be dominated by early rewards, making it hard for $g$ to learn nuanced corrections for later states. This is a known issue in trajectory-level RLHF, but its interaction with the $L_+$/$L_-$ regularization is not analyzed.

### Experiments & Results
- **Claim testing:** The experiments strongly test the core claim that repairing a proxy is more sample-efficient than learning from scratch. Figures 2 and 3 demonstrate clear gains.
- **Baselines:** Baselines are appropriate and cover learning ab initio (Online-RLHF), residual correction (RRM), and KL/state-constraint methods. The comparison to Cao et al. (2025) is fair, though the switch from SAC (Cao et al.) to PPO (this work) could introduce confounding factors regarding sample efficiency. A brief justification that PPO hyperparameters were tuned equivalently for all methods (as detailed in Appendix E) mitigates this, but a controlled ablation isolating RL algorithm vs. reward learning strategy would be ideal.
- **Ablations:** Section 6.4 successfully isolates the contributions of the exploration strategy and the new loss. The ablation showing that standard cross-entropy (Eq. 1) causes instability is particularly convincing.
- **Statistical significance:** Main results use 3 random seeds. While standard error is plotted, ICLR increasingly expects 5+ seeds or formal statistical tests (e.g., paired t-tests or bootstrap confidence intervals) for claims of "consistent outperformance" across multiple domains. Table 6 shows 10 seeds for one environment, which is excellent, but extending this rigor to all environments would strengthen the paper.
- **Metrics & Datasets:** The environments (Pan et al., 2022) are standard reward-hacking benchmarks. Using simulated preferences via Boltzmann distribution from $r$ is standard but limits real-world claims. The paper relies on this simulation throughout. A sensitivity analysis to label noise (beyond the inherent softmax noise) or human simulation biases (e.g., trajectory length bias) would significantly bolster the paper's readiness for a top-tier conference.

### Writing & Clarity
- **Clarity of contribution:** The paper is well-structured. The narrative flows logically from problem to method to theory to experiments.
- **Figures/Tables:** Figures 2 and 3 are informative. The scaling/clipping methodology in Appendix G.1 is transparent. Table 1 neatly summarizes environments.
- **Ambiguities:** The notation in Eq. 3 is slightly obscured by parser artifacts, but more importantly, the meaning of $g(\tau)^2$ and the indicator functions $1_{\{\tau_1 \succ \tau_2\}}$ needs clearer mathematical definition in the main text. Does $g(\tau)$ denote the cumulative correction? The explanation following Eq. 3 describes the intent well, but a precise equation showing how trajectory scores are aggregated from transition-level outputs would remove ambiguity.
- **Appendix A Discussion:** The appendix discussion on full trajectory vs. segment preferences is intellectually honest but undermines the empirical claims slightly. The authors state: *"this credit assignment issue results in the noise we observe in our results in Section 6."* For ICLR, it would be stronger to either (a) implement a trajectory-to-transition credit assignment baseline to show PBRR's loss is robust despite this noise, or (b) explicitly quantify the noise floor relative to the performance gains to prove the gains are not artifacts of the preference simulation choice.

### Limitations & Broader Impact
- **Acknowledged limitations:** The authors correctly identify the reliance on the optimism assumption, the credit assignment challenges of trajectory-level preferences, the computational intractability of computing non-dominated sets in continuous spaces (justifying $C_1=0$), and the use of simulated preferences.
- **Missed limitations:**
  1. **Non-additive misspecification:** PBRR assumes the correction $g$ can capture the misalignment additively. If the proxy reward is structurally wrong (e.g., missing a crucial state variable or interaction term), an additive correction may require a complex, high-dimensional $g$ that negates the sample-efficiency benefits. This structural limitation is not discussed.
  2. **Human Bias Propagation:** The broader impact section is absent. Automating reward repair could inadvertently amplify human biases present in the preference labels. If preferences reflect systemic biases, PBRR's targeted optimization might learn a reward function that robustly encodes these biases, potentially making misalignment harder to detect than in standard RLHF.
  3. **Reference Policy Dependence:** While Appendix G.8 shows a randomly initialized policy can work, the theoretical guarantee (Theorem K.1) assumes $\pi_{ref}$ is safe and provides meaningful contrast. In high-dimensional control, a random policy may have trivial support, making it unclear how $\pi_{ref}$ drives effective repair beyond acting as a noise baseline. The mechanism by which a useless $\pi_{ref}$ still yields gains deserves deeper analysis.

### Overall Assessment
This paper proposes PBRR, a method for efficiently repairing proxy reward functions using preference feedback. The core intuition—that proxy rewards often suffer from over-optimism and that targeted corrections guided by a reference policy can restore alignment with fewer preferences—is both practically relevant and theoretically grounded. The empirical results on established reward-hacking benchmarks are strong, and the ablations convincingly demonstrate the necessity of the novel preference loss. However, for ICLR standards, there are notable gaps: (1) a disconnect between the strategic exploration assumed in the regret bounds ($C_1 > 0$) and the fixed exploration used empirically ($C_1 = 0$), which needs explicit reconciliation; (2) the credit assignment challenge of mapping trajectory preferences to transition-level corrections is acknowledged but left unresolved, potentially introducing noise that limits the method's ceiling; and (3) the reliance on 3 random seeds and simulated preferences weakens the robustness claims. Addressing these points, particularly by clarifying the exploration mismatch, providing a clearer gradient attribution scheme for Eq. 3, and expanding statistical validation, would substantially strengthen the submission. The contribution is solid and aligns well with ICLR's focus on efficient, aligned learning, and with these clarifications, it stands as a valuable addition to the literature.

# Neutral Reviewer
## Balanced Review

### Summary
The paper introduces Preference-Based Reward Repair (PBRR), an iterative framework that aligns a human-specified proxy reward function to a true objective by learning an additive, transition-dependent correction term from trajectory preferences. PBRR combines a reference-policy-guided exploration strategy with a novel preference-learning loss that selectively penalizes overestimated rewards and preserves correct proxy rankings. Across sequential decision-making benchmarks featuring reward hacking, the method demonstrates superior sample efficiency and stability compared to standard RLHF and existing proxy-repair baselines, with theoretical cumulative regret guarantees that match prior RLHF bounds up to constants in linear/tabular settings.

### Strengths
1. **Well-motivated and effective loss design:** The three-term objective (Eq. 3) explicitly regularizes corrections toward zero on trajectory pairs where the proxy agrees with preferences ($L_+$) and prioritizes negative corrections on misclassified pairs ($L_-$). The ablation in Figure 3 and Appendix G.4 demonstrates that both regularization terms are necessary for stability and final performance, particularly in preventing unbounded reward inflation.
2. **Rigorous empirical evaluation on challenging benchmarks:** PBRR is evaluated on high-dimensional environments specifically designed to induce reward hacking (Pan et al., 2022). Figure 2 shows consistent outperformance over strong baselines (Online-RLHF, RRM, State-Constrained-PPO) in both early-stage jump-start performance and final return, while Appendix G.3/H provide detailed qualitative analysis of why baselines fail (e.g., instrumental vs. terminal goal confusion in the AI Safety Gridworld).
3. **Solid theoretical grounding with practical algorithmic fallback:** Theorem 5.1/5.2 and the detailed proofs in Appendix J establish $\tilde{O}(\sqrt{Td})$ cumulative regret for both known and unknown dynamics in linear MDPs. The algorithm's fallback mechanism (lines 6-11 of Algorithm 1) to uncertainty-maximizing policy pairs ensures the theoretical conditions are met when the reference and proxy-optimal policies lack sufficient divergence.
4. **High reproducibility and transparency:** The paper provides exhaustive implementation details in the appendices, including architecture specifications (Appendix E.1), PPO hyperparameters (Appendix E.4), preference elicitation procedures (Appendix E.5), and explicit notes on simulated preference labeling. A code replication package is promised, and all environmental configurations align with publicly available benchmarks.

### Weaknesses
1. **Theory-practice gap in representation assumptions:** The regret analysis relies on linear trajectory embeddings (Assumption 5.1) and tabular settings, while the empirical results use deep neural networks in continuous, high-dimensional state spaces (Sections 6.1, E.1). The paper acknowledges this in Section 6 but does not discuss how the regularization structure or exploration strategy theoretically extends to non-linear function approximation or representation learning.
2. **Unvalidated robustness to realistic human feedback:** Preferences are simulated using Boltzmann-distributed labels from the ground-truth reward function (Appendix E.5). Real human annotators exhibit cognitive biases, inconsistency, and varying expertise levels. The paper does not test PBRR under noisy, contradictory, or non-Bradley-Terry preference distributions, which limits conclusions about real-world deployment viability.
3. **Reference policy coverage dependency:** While Appendix G.8 shows PBRR works with a randomly initialized $\pi_{ref}$, the exploration strategy implicitly assumes $\pi_{ref}$ visits states that contrast meaningfully with $\pi_{\hat{r}_t}$. In sparse-reward or highly non-ergodic environments, a low-coverage $\pi_{ref}$ could severely bottleneck corrective signal generation. The minimum coverage requirements for $\pi_{ref}$ are not quantified or stress-tested.
4. **Incremental novelty relative to concurrent work:** Cao et al. (2025) independently propose learning residual reward corrections from preferences on robotics tasks. While PBRR's targeted loss and reference-policy exploration are distinct, the core paradigm (additive reward repair from preferences) is highly overlapping. The novelty is therefore moderate rather than transformative.

### Novelty & Significance
**Novelty:** Moderate to High. The additive reward repair paradigm overlaps with concurrent work, but PBRR introduces a distinct preference-learning objective that leverages proxy optimism assumptions and a targeted exploration scheme contrasting the proxy-optimal policy with a reference policy. The algorithmic fallback to uncertainty-maximizing pairs bridges the practical and theoretical components effectively.

**Clarity:** High. The paper is well-structured, with clear problem motivation, mathematically precise formulations, and thorough experimental breakdowns. The connection between the optimism assumption, the loss design, and the empirical baselines is logically cohesive. Minor parser artifacts do not obscure the technical narrative.

**Reproducibility:** High. Detailed hyperparameters, environment configurations, ablation protocols, and a commitment to code release strongly support reproducibility. The simulated preference protocol is explicitly documented, allowing exact replication of the experimental pipeline.

**Significance:** High for sequential decision-making alignment. By automating the tedious, expertise-heavy manual reward debugging loop, PBRR makes RL more accessible to domain experts (e.g., in healthcare, energy, or policy design). The demonstrated sample efficiency and stability advantages over learning rewards from scratch address a major bottleneck in practical RLHF pipelines.

### Suggestions for Improvement
1. **Address the representation generalization gap:** Add a discussion or supplementary experiment on how the $L_+$/$L_-$ regularization interacts with deep representation learning. Consider analyzing gradient behavior under non-linear function approximation or testing a kernel/NTK-based theoretical extension to bridge the tabular and deep RL settings.
2. **Evaluate under realistic human feedback conditions:** Supplement the simulated preference experiments with at least one setting that injects synthetic annotator noise, inconsistency, or violates the Bradley-Terry assumption (e.g., using a non-symmetric or context-dependent preference model). Report how $\lambda_1, \lambda_2$ decay and the loss structure respond to noisy labels.
3. **Quantify reference policy requirements:** Provide a formal or empirical characterization of the minimum state-action coverage or divergence between $\pi_{ref}$ and $\pi_{\hat{r}_t}$ required for successful repair. Test PBRR on environments where $\pi_{ref}$ is deliberately constrained to a narrow subspace to establish failure boundaries and propose adaptive $\pi_{ref}$ expansion heuristics.
4. **Report computational overhead:** Iteratively retraining the reward model and fine-tuning PPO at each preference batch can be expensive. Include wall-clock time, FLOP counts, or iteration latency compared to baselines to clarify PBRR's practical trade-offs in compute-constrained settings.
5. **Expand discussion of pessimistic/negative proxies:** While Appendix G.6/G.10 shows PBRR can work with pessimistic proxies, it requires more preferences and lacks formal guarantees. Propose a simple adaptive mechanism (e.g., monitoring preference flip-rates or sign of $g$ updates) to dynamically switch or relax the optimism-weighting in $L_-$, improving robustness to arbitrary proxy misspecification.

# Final Consolidated Review
## Summary
This paper introduces Preference-Based Reward Repair (PBRR), an iterative framework that aligns a human-specified proxy reward function with a ground-truth objective by learning an additive, transition-dependent correction term from trajectory preferences. PBRR combines a reference-policy-guided exploration strategy with a novel three-term preference loss that selectively regularizes corrections to target over-optimistic proxy rewards while preserving already-aligned transitions. The method demonstrates strong sample efficiency and training stability on established reward-hacking benchmarks and provides cumulative regret bounds for linear MDPs with strategic exploration.

## Strengths
- **Mechanically sound and empirically validated loss design:** The proposed objective (Eq. 3) explicitly leverages an optimism assumption to regularize the correction term toward zero when the proxy agrees with preferences, and prioritizes negative adjustments on conflicts. Ablations (Fig. 3, Appendix G.4) conclusively show this structure prevents the reward inflation and policy oscillation that plague standard cross-entropy RLHF baselines.
- **Strong empirical performance on challenging alignment benchmarks:** Across four high-dimensional, non-ergodic environments designed to induce severe reward hacking, PBRR consistently achieves higher ground-truth returns with fewer preference queries and greater stability than learning from scratch (Online-RLHF) or concurrent residual reward modeling approaches.

## Weaknesses
- **Critical fragility to policy optimization budget:** Appendix G.2 reveals that the repaired reward function's performance deteriorates significantly when the policy is trained with substantially more RL steps than used during the repair process. This suggests the learned correction $g$ may not genuinely align the underlying objective but rather acts as a brittle, optimization-budget-dependent patch. Without a formal robustness guarantee or an automated stopping criterion, deploying the repaired reward in practical RL pipelines is unreliable and risks re-inducing misaligned behavior under extended training.
- **Severe theory-practice disconnect in exploration strategy:** The cumulative regret bounds (Theorems 5.1 & 5.2) explicitly depend on an active exploration mechanism that queries policy pairs maximizing divergence within a non-dominated set ($C_1 > 0$). However, Section 6 explicitly states that $C_1=0$ for all experiments due to computational intractability in high-dimensional spaces. Consequently, the theoretical guarantees do not apply to the empirically evaluated algorithm, which relies entirely on a fixed reference policy. This leaves the claimed sample efficiency without formal justification in the evaluated regime.
- **Unsupported leap from tabular/linear theory to deep non-linear RL:** The theoretical analysis strictly assumes linear trajectory embeddings and tabular/known dynamics. The empirical evaluation operates in continuous, high-dimensional spaces with deep neural networks, directly violating every core assumption of the regret bounds. The paper provides no discussion or empirical analysis on how the $L_+$/ $L_-$ regularizers or the reference-policy exploration heuristic interact with non-linear function approximation, representation collapse, or gradient scaling, leaving a substantial gap between the theoretical contributions and the practical claims.

## Nice-to-Haves
- Sensitivity analysis for the $\lambda$ decay schedule (currently hardcoded as $10/|D^+|$ in Appendix E.6) to verify that regularization does not collapse prematurely or over-penalize in early iterations.
- Evaluation under more realistic preference noise models (e.g., inconsistent raters, trajectory-length bias, or non-Bradley-Terry judgments) to assess robustness beyond simulated ground-truth labels.
- Visualization of the learned correction term $g(s,a,s')$ projected onto task-relevant dimensions to verify that repairs selectively target hacking transitions rather than applying unstructured global reward shifts.

## Novel Insights
The paper identifies a highly practical structural assumption in reward design: human-specified proxies often fail not due to global misalignment, but due to localized over-optimism that RL agents exploit. By explicitly contrasting the proxy-optimal policy against a reference policy and applying an asymmetric loss that penalizes corrections only when conflicts arise and discourages modifications to already-correct transitions, PBRR avoids the unbounded reward escalation typical of preference learning. This targeted repair paradigm effectively decouples alignment from learning a reward function ab initio, offering a more data-efficient pathway for domains where domain experts can specify a reasonable but fragile baseline reward.

## Potentially Missed Related Work
- Recent active preference optimization methods that leverage implicit Q-learning or conservative exploration for query efficiency without reference policies (e.g., XPO / Xie et al., 2024, which is cited but not directly baselined). Directly comparing against these would help isolate whether PBRR's gains stem primarily from the novel loss structure or from contrasting against a fixed behavioral baseline.

## Suggestions
- Address the optimization-budget fragility (Appendix G.2) by either incorporating an explicit regularization term that bounds reward curvature, or empirically characterizing the safe RL step budget and providing guidelines for stable policy retraining with the repaired reward.
- Clarify that the theoretical guarantees are strictly limited to the linear/tabular regime with active exploration, and provide an empirical bridge (e.g., coverage lower bounds for $\pi_{ref}$, gradient norm analysis, or a simplified non-linear extension of the regret analysis) to justify why the $C_1=0$ heuristic succeeds in deep RL despite lacking theoretical support.
