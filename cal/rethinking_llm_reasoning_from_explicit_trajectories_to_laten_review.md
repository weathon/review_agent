# Harsh Critic Review
## Section-by-Section Critical Review

### Title & Abstract
- The title accurately captures the core idea: shifting from explicit textual trajectories to latent representations for LLM reasoning.
- The abstract clearly states the problem (overthinking/inference cost), method (Latent Reasoning Tuning with an auxiliary network), and key results (outperforms efficient reasoning baselines and Qwen3 non-thinking mode).
- The claim that LRT "surpasses the state-of-the-art Qwen3 hybrid reasoning framework" in the abstract requires careful reading. The main text (Table 2, 5) shows gains primarily in `pass@4`, while `pass@1` gains are marginal or slightly negative on some datasets. The abstract should be tempered to accurately reflect that the advantage is most pronounced under multi-sampling, not necessarily in deterministic single-pass settings.

### Introduction & Motivation
- The problem of "overthinking" and inference cost is well-motivated and timely. The review of RL-based length compression and skip-thinking prompts is concise.
- The gap is identified: current methods still decode tokens or rely on brittle prompt hacks. However, the introduction positions LRT as fundamentally "reimagining reasoning computation" without adequately discussing its strong methodological similarity to continuous/soft prompt tuning and conditional prefix tuning, which have explored similar latent conditioning paradigms for years. The novelty is somewhat undersold in positioning and risks being perceived as an incremental architectural variant rather than a paradigm shift.
- Contributions are clearly stated but partially overlap. Contribution 2 (fragmented trajectories work) is an empirical finding, not a methodological contribution, and its validity hinges on the experimental rigor of Section 2. Contribution 3 overstates the margin of improvement in some cases (gains are often 1-4% over strong baselines). The introduction would benefit from a more calibrated statement of expected gains and a clearer delineation between latent conditioning and existing prompt-tuning literature.

### Method / Approach
- The method is described in Algorithm 1, Eq. 1-3, and Appendix C. It is largely reproducible, but several technical details require clarification:
- **Architecture & Parameterization (Appendix C, Eq. 5)**: The latent representation is generated via $z = f_{out}(G_\phi(f_{in}(H_X) \odot [\hat{r}_1 \dots \hat{r}_t]))$. This is mathematically equivalent to a conditional soft-prompt or continuous prompt prepended to the model's hidden states, where $[\hat{r}_1 \dots \hat{r}_t]$ acts as a learnable template and $G_\phi$ is a bidirectional encoder conditioned on input context. The paper should explicitly acknowledge this relationship and clarify why an encoder-based soft prompt inherently captures "reasoning logic" better than standard prompt tuning.
- **Reinforcement Learning Gradient Flow (Section 3.2, Algo 1 Line 21)**: The update uses $\nabla_\phi \mathcal{L}_{GRPO}$. Since $P_\theta$ is frozen, gradients for $\phi$ must flow through the continuous latent vector $z$ into the frozen base model's attention/feedforward layers during backpropagation, or rely on a score-function estimator (REINFORCE). Standard GRPO/PPO operates on discrete token generations and uses policy gradients on the sampling distribution. The paper does not specify whether Stage 2 treats $P_\theta$ as a differentiable reward function (backprop through embeddings/weights) or applies a proper REINFORCE/GRPO gradient estimator. This omission is critical for reproducibility and theoretical soundness.
- **Assumptions & Edge Cases**: The method assumes the frozen reasoning model $P_\theta$ can seamlessly integrate arbitrary continuous vectors into its residual stream without distributional shift. No discussion is provided on how $z$ interacts with positional embeddings or layer norms. If $G_\phi$ outputs vectors that fall outside the training distribution of $P_\theta$'s hidden space, performance could degrade sharply. A brief analysis of the distribution of $z$ vs. standard text embeddings would strengthen the method.
- **Failure Modes**: What happens when the input query requires step-by-step verification that cannot be compressed into $t=256$ continuous dimensions? The method lacks a mechanism to dynamically adjust latent length based on query complexity (e.g., short for GSM8K, long for AMC), which is a known strength of explicit CoT.

### Experiments & Results
- **Baseline Fairness**: Comparisons against NoThinking, ShorterBetter, and LC-R1 are reasonable, but a critical baseline is missing: **standard soft-prompt tuning / prefix tuning** trained with the exact same SFT+RL protocol and parameter budget. Without it, it is impossible to determine whether gains stem from the "reasoning network" architecture or simply from the two-stage SFT+RL optimization recipe applied to a trainable continuous sequence.
- **Claim Validation**: Results in Tables 1, 2, and 5 generally support the efficiency/accuracy trade-off claims. However, the improvements over baselines are often modest (1-5% on average). On Qwen3-4B/1.7B, `pass@1` gains are mixed, and `pass@4` improvements suggest the latent space encourages diversity rather than higher-fidelity reasoning per path. The authors should clarify whether the goal is sample efficiency or solution diversity.
- **Ablations**: Table 3 (token count) and Table 4 (SFT+RL) are useful. However, an ablation removing $G_\phi$ and using only learnable vectors $[\hat{r}_1 \dots \hat{r}_t]$ (pure prompt tuning) is strictly necessary to isolate the value of the auxiliary encoder.
- **Efficiency Metrics (Appendix D.3, Table 7)**: The claim that LRT achieves lower latency than non-thinking (11.79s vs 14.62s) is plausible if answer generation is shorter, but the "effective throughput" calculation ($51.31 \to 73.02$ tokens/sec) is methodologically misleading. Latent tokens are generated in parallel via a single forward pass and are not decoded autoregressively. Counting them as "generated tokens" inflates the throughput metric in a way that is not comparable to text generation standards. The paper should report FLOPs or wall-clock time per query alongside strict token-throughput, and clearly separate latent generation from autoregressive decoding.
- **Statistical Significance**: Appendix D.5 provides standard deviations, which is good practice. Gains generally exceed $\sigma$, but on LSAT/GPQA, error bars overlap with baselines in some configurations. The authors should run paired statistical tests (e.g., McNemar’s or bootstrap) to formally confirm significance, especially on out-of-domain tasks where sample sizes are smaller.

### Writing & Clarity
- The prose is generally clear and logically structured. However, crucial architectural details (e.g., how $z$ is concatenated to $H_X$, positional handling, exact tensor shapes) are deferred to Appendix C, disrupting the flow in Section 3. Readers must flip to the appendix to understand the core mechanism.
- Figure 2 is severely garbled due to parser artifacts, making the quantitative claims in Section 2 difficult to verify directly from the manuscript. The text claims ~90%+ accuracy at 50% skip, which is counterintuitive and warrants either a reconstructed figure or explicit tabular results in the main text.
- The distinction between "latent reasoning" and "continuous soft prompting" is not sufficiently clarified. The terminology ("reasoning network", "latent trajectory") is evocative but risks conflating architectural form with functional reasoning capability. A clearer operational definition would improve comprehension.

### Limitations & Broader Impact
- The paper lacks a dedicated Limitations section in the main text, which is now standard at ICLR. The authors should explicitly discuss: (1) the dependence on the base model's ability to absorb continuous prefixes, (2) the fixed latent length lacking adaptive test-time compute scaling, (3) potential degradation on tasks requiring explicit step-by-step auditability (e.g., math education, safety-critical domains), and (4) memory overhead from loading both $P_\theta$ and $G_\phi$ simultaneously (Table 7 shows peak memory 6.5GB vs 3.9GB for non-thinking).
- **Societal/Safety Impact**: Not discussed. Replacing explicit reasoning with latent vectors inherently reduces interpretability and auditability. In high-stakes applications, this loss of traceability is a significant trade-off that should be acknowledged, alongside potential mitigation strategies (e.g., latent-to-text decoding probes).

### Overall Assessment
This paper addresses a highly relevant ICLR topic: mitigating the inference cost of slow-thinking reasoning models. The core idea—replacing autoregressive reasoning with a parallel latent conditioning network—is empirically validated and demonstrates consistent, albeit modest, improvements over existing compression and skip-thinking baselines. However, the contribution stands behind critical unresolved concerns. First, the architectural novelty is under-specified and closely parallels established continuous/prompt tuning methods; without a direct comparison to soft-prompt baselines trained with identical SFT+RL protocols, it is unclear whether gains stem from the auxiliary encoder or simply the two-stage optimization recipe. Second, the efficiency claims, while directionally sound, utilize a non-standard effective throughput metric and obscure the gradient mechanics of the RL stage. Third, the empirical analysis of "fragmentary trajectories" (Section 2) is conceptually weak, as random token deletion tests corruption robustness rather than reasoning sufficiency. To meet ICLR’s acceptance bar, the authors should explicitly position the method against prompt tuning, add ablations isolating the encoder’s contribution, clarify the RL gradient flow, and provide a transparent efficiency analysis using FLOPs/wall-clock time. With these revisions, the work would represent a solid, reproducible contribution to efficient reasoning.

# Neutral Reviewer
## Balanced Review

### Summary
This paper addresses the inference inefficiency and "overthinking" problem in reasoning LLMs by proposing Latent Reasoning Tuning (LRT), a framework that replaces costly autoregressive trajectory generation with compact, fixed-length latent vectors produced by a lightweight auxiliary network. By conditioning a frozen base LLM on these latent representations through a two-stage SFT and RL pipeline, LRT enables efficient answer generation while maintaining strong performance. Empirical evaluations across multiple model scales and benchmarks demonstrate that LRT consistently outperforms prompt-based and RL-compression baselines under strict token budgets, achieving lower latency and competitive accuracy.

### Strengths
1. **Strong Motivation Empirically Grounded:** The fragmental trajectory analysis (Section 2, Figure 2) convincingly demonstrates that reasoning LLMs maintain high accuracy even with 30-50% token/step dropout, providing a solid empirical foundation for bypassing explicit trajectory generation.
2. **Rigorous and Comprehensive Experimental Setup:** The evaluation covers multiple model sizes (1.5B to 8B), diverse domains (math, logic, science), and strictly enforced token budgets. The inclusion of latency, throughput, peak memory, and statistical variance (Appendix D.3, D.5) provides a holistic view of efficiency gains beyond just accuracy.
3. **Practical and Modular Architectural Design:** Freezing the base LLM parameters while training only an auxiliary reasoning network is highly practical for deployment. This design preserves original model capabilities, enables seamless mode switching (latent vs. explicit), and drastically reduces training compute compared to full-model fine-tuning or RLVR baselines.
4. **Clear Two-Stage Training Pipeline:** The combination of SFT for behavioral alignment and GRPO for reward-aligned exploration (Algorithm 1) is well-justified. The ablation in Table 4 confirms that RL significantly boosts out-of-domain generalization and complex reasoning performance over SFT alone.

### Weaknesses
1. **Ambiguity in Latent Vector Injection Mechanism:** While the paper states that latent representations are generated and condition the LLM, the exact integration into the transformer forward pass is underspecified in the main text. It is unclear how $z$ interacts with the LLM's positional embeddings, attention mask, and KV-cache. For instance, are the latent vectors treated as virtual tokens concatenated to the input sequence? Without this detail, the method's compatibility with standard LLM inference frameworks is ambiguous.
2. **Incomplete Compute Accounting in Efficiency Claims:** Table 7 reports latency and throughput gains, but the computational cost of the auxiliary 0.6B network forward pass is not fully isolated. Generating 256 latent vectors requires processing them through a transformer block, which incurs non-trivial FLOPs and memory. A direct FLOP comparison or a breakdown of time spent in $G_\phi$ vs. LLM decoding would strengthen the efficiency claims.
3. **Limited Differentiation from Prior Latent Reasoning Work:** The paper cites recent latent CoT methods (e.g., Hao et al., 2024; Geiping et al., 2025) but primarily differentiates based on modular design and fixed-length outputs. A direct experimental comparison or a more rigorous analysis of how LRT's "one-shot latent projection" compares to iterative latent refinement approaches would better position the novelty.
4. **Potential Optimization Instability in Frozen-Backbone RL:** Updating only $\phi$ while passing gradients through a frozen, highly non-linear $P_\theta$ (as implied in the GRPO loss) may suffer from vanishing/exploding gradients or poor signal-to-noise ratios. The paper does not discuss training dynamics, gradient scaling, or potential instability during the RL stage.

### Novelty & Significance
**Novelty:** Moderate to High. While latent reasoning and efficient CoT are active areas, LRT's specific formulation—using a parallel auxiliary network to generate fixed-length soft tokens that condition a frozen reasoning LLM via SFT+RL—is a clean, well-executed contribution. It diverges from iterative latent refinement and token-skipping methods by offering a strictly non-intrusive, budget-controlled alternative. **Clarity:** High. The writing is generally clear, logically structured, and well-motivated. Minor typographical errors do not detract from comprehension. **Reproducibility:** Good but requires minor supplementation. Hyperparameters, hardware, and dataset sources are provided (Appendix B). However, the exact tensor shapes for latent injection, position ID handling, and the precise implementation of the Hadamard broadcasting with learnable vectors (Appendix Eq. 5) need explicit code-level or algorithmic clarification for full reproducibility. **Significance:** High. The work directly tackles the deployment bottleneck of slow-thinking models. The modular, parameter-efficient design aligns well with industry needs for latency-constrained reasoning, making it highly relevant to the ICLR community.

### Suggestions for Improvement
1. **Explicitly Detail the Latent Injection Mechanism:** Clarify in the main text or Algorithm 1 how $z$ is integrated into the LLM's input sequence. Specify whether $z$ acts as virtual tokens appended to $H_X$, how relative/absolute positional encodings are handled for these latents, and how the attention mask is configured during the base model's forward pass.
2. **Provide FLOPs and Memory-Activation Breakdown:** Supplement Table 7 with estimated FLOPs for $G_\phi$ inference versus autoregressive decoding steps. Include a breakdown showing the exact time/memory contribution of the auxiliary network to ensure the efficiency gains are fully transparent.
3. **Add Direct Comparison with Iterative Latent Methods:** Briefly compare LRT against Coconut or similar latent-reasoning baselines in terms of latency/accuracy on a shared benchmark, or add a discussion subsection contrasting the training stability and expressivity of one-shot latent projection versus iterative hidden-state refinement.
4. **Elaborate on RL Training Dynamics:** Include a brief analysis or plot in the appendix showing reward/loss curves during the GRPO stage. Discuss how gradient flow through the frozen LLM is managed (e.g., layer normalization detachment, scaling factors, or KL penalty impact on $\phi$) to reassure readers about optimization stability.

# Spark Finder Review
## How to Improve This Paper

### Missing Experiments (top 3-5 only)
1. Report FLOP/TFLOP counts per query alongside wall-clock latency. The auxiliary 0.6B network adds compute that unoptimized HuggingFace timing hides; without FLOP accounting, the claim that LRT reduces computational cost is unverified.
2. Add a direct-answer SFT baseline trained on identical $(X,Y)$ pairs without an auxiliary network. Since the SFT objective discards $R$ entirely, you must prove performance gains come from learned latent reasoning structure rather than simply bypassing trajectory generation.
3. Conduct a joint sweep of reasoning network scale (e.g., 100M vs 2B) and latent token length. The current results only test one network size, making it impossible to determine whether the 256-token performance peak reflects architectural capacity limits or optimization artifacts.

### Deeper Analysis Needed (top 3-5 only)
1. Train linear probes to detect intermediate logical steps, self-verification tokens, and backtracking behaviors within $z$. Without this, the claim that latent vectors encapsulate "reasoning logic" rather than domain-specific answer priors remains speculative.
2. Quantify the KL divergence between $P(Y|[X,R])$ and $P(Y|[X,z])$ across benchmark difficulties. Proving that $z$ approximates the explicit reasoning distribution, rather than learning a direct $X \to Y$ shortcut, is essential to validate the method's theoretical framing.
3. Dissect failure modes on the hardest problem subsets (e.g., MATH-500 Level 5, competition-level proofs) to determine if errors stem from insufficient latent bandwidth or the inability to perform iterative verification/backtracking.

### Visualizations & Case Studies
1. Apply gradient occlusion to individual latent tokens to show whether specific indices map to discrete reasoning sub-steps or if the vector acts as an undifferentiated compressed prompt.
2. Provide side-by-side attention maps or decoding traces for problems where explicit reasoning succeeds but LRT fails, isolating exactly which logical dependencies or verification loops the latent representation collapsed.
3. Plot t-SNE/UMAP of latent vectors colored by solution graph stages (e.g., setup, derivation, verification) instead of just domain labels, revealing whether internal logical progression is preserved in the continuous space.

### Obvious Next Steps
1. Replace or augment the pure NLL SFT loss with KL divergence distillation against the explicit trajectory-conditioned distribution to force the network to emulate stepwise logic rather than just maximize final answer likelihood.
2. Re-benchmark latency and throughput using production-ready engines (vLLM, TensorRT) rather than a slow reference stack to demonstrate that the efficiency gains survive optimized KV-cache management and speculative decoding.
3. Implement a lightweight input-complexity router that dynamically switches between latent and explicit reasoning, proving the framework enables true hybrid routing rather than static token-budget forcing.

# Final Consolidated Review
## Summary
This paper proposes Latent Reasoning Tuning (LRT), a framework that replaces autoregressive reasoning trajectory generation with a fixed-length latent vector produced by a lightweight auxiliary network. The latent vector conditions a frozen base reasoning LLM, optimized via a two-stage SFT and GRPO pipeline. The method aims to reduce inference latency and token costs associated with "overthinking" while matching or exceeding the accuracy of explicit slow-thinking and compressed-reasoning baselines.

## Strengths
- **Practical, non-intrusive architectural design:** By freezing the base reasoning LLM and training only an auxiliary network (`G_φ`), LRT avoids costly full-model fine-tuning and preserves the original model's explicit reasoning capabilities for mode switching. This parameter-efficient design is clearly articulated and practically valuable for latency-constrained deployment.
- **Comprehensive empirical evaluation:** The authors rigorously evaluate across multiple model scales (1.5B–8B), diverse mathematical and logical benchmarks, and strict token budgets. The inclusion of wall-clock latency, throughput, memory profiling, and standard deviation analysis (Appendix D) demonstrates a thorough commitment to measuring efficiency beyond single-point accuracy.

## Weaknesses
- **Critical baseline omissions obscure the source of improvements:** The paper lacks comparisons to two essential controls: (1) direct $X \to Y$ supervised fine-tuning without an auxiliary network, and (2) standard soft-prompt/prefix tuning trained with the identical SFT+RL protocol and parameter budget. Without these, it is impossible to determine whether gains stem from the proposed "reasoning network" architecture or simply from the benefits of continuous prompt conditioning and the two-stage optimization recipe itself.
- **Undefined gradient mechanics in the RL stage:** Algorithm 1 specifies updating $\phi$ via a GRPO objective while $P_\theta$ remains frozen. Standard GRPO/PPO operates on stochastic token-level policies and relies on REINFORCE-style score-function estimators. The paper generates $z$ deterministically via $G_\phi(H_X)$ and does not clarify how policy gradients flow back through a frozen $P_\theta$ or whether a reparameterization trick, surrogate gradient estimator, or backpropagation through detached layers is used. This omission severely impacts reproducibility and theoretical soundness.
- **Misaligned motivational analysis:** Section 2 argues that high accuracy under 30–50% random token/step deletion proves reasoning trajectory redundancy. However, random deletion primarily measures the base model's robustness to input corruption and noise, not logical redundancy or step necessity. This conflation weakens the empirical foundation for bypassing explicit stepwise generation, especially for tasks requiring verification or backtracking where specific steps are structurally critical.
- **Inflated efficiency metrics and unaddressed interpretability trade-off:** Table 7's "effective throughput" metric treats parallel-generated latent vectors as equivalent to autoregressively decoded tokens, inflating speedup claims in a way that breaks comparability with standard text generation benchmarks. Furthermore, replacing transparent reasoning chains with opaque latent vectors fundamentally sacrifices auditability. The paper does not acknowledge this as a limitation or discuss its implications for safety-critical or educational domains.

## Nice-to-Haves
- Provide FLOP/TFLOP accounting per query to isolate the compute cost of the $G_\phi$ forward pass from the autoregressive answer decoding, ensuring latency reductions aren't masked by hardware inefficiencies.
- Clarify how positional embeddings and attention masks are configured for the injected latent sequence, as this determines compatibility with standard transformer inference stacks.
- Include a gradient occlusion or attention visualization to verify whether specific latent indices map to logical sub-steps or if the vector collapses into an undifferentiated domain-specific prior.

## Novel Insights
The paper inadvertently highlights a crucial tension in modern LLM research: the functional equivalence between "latent reasoning" and structured continuous prompting. Despite framing the auxiliary network as a reasoning module, the architecture and training objective ($X \to z \to Y$) effectively reduce to a learnable, input-conditioned soft prompt optimized for answer likelihood rather than trajectory reconstruction. This suggests that for many math/logic tasks under strict token budgets, performance gains may stem less from compressed logical inference and more from the model's ability to bypass verbose linguistic overhead and directly access answer-relevant representation manifolds. Recognizing this shifts the focus from "reasoning in latent space" to optimizing continuous conditioning interfaces for frozen reasoning experts.

## Potentially Missed Related Work
- Li & Liang (2021), *Prefix-Tuning: Optimizing Continuous Prompts for Generation* / Lester et al. (2021), *The Power of Scale for Parameter-Efficient Prompt Tuning* — Essential to formally position LRT against the established soft-prompt literature it architecturally mirrors.
- Chen et al. (2025), *Reasoning Beyond Language: A Comprehensive Survey on Latent Chain-of-Thought Reasoning* — Provides broader context on continuous latent reasoning paradigms and iterative vs. one-shot compression strategies, which would strengthen the positioning in Section 5.2.

## Suggestions
1. **Add missing baselines and ablations:** Train direct $X \to Y$ SFT and standard soft-prompt models under identical token budgets and RL protocols. This will rigorously isolate whether the auxiliary encoder provides marginal value beyond continuous conditioning.
2. **Clarify RL gradient computation:** Explicitly document the mathematical derivation of $\nabla_\phi \mathcal{L}_{GRPO}$ for deterministic $z$, specify whether score-function estimators, reparameterization, or gradient checkpointing through $P_\theta$ is used, and report RL training loss/reward curves to validate optimization stability.
3. **Decouple efficiency claims and discuss trade-offs:** Replace "effective throughput" with FLOP-normalized latency and wall-clock time per successful solution. Add a dedicated Limitations subsection addressing interpretability loss, fixed latent bandwidth constraints on complex problems, and memory overhead from loading $P_\theta + G_\phi$ simultaneously.
