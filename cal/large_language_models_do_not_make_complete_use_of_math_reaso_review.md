# Harsh Critic Review
## Section-by-Section Critical Review

### Title & Abstract
The title accurately captures the empirical core but the phrasing "do not make complete use" implies a fundamental capacity or training inefficiency that the paper only partially substantiates. The abstract claims the findings hold "even with popular test-time scaling techniques" and presents predictive multiplicity as the primary explanation. While the phenomenon is interesting, the abstract overstates the mechanistic certainty of the findings without acknowledging that the observed behavior may largely stem from well-known optimization path-dependence rather than a novel data-utilization failure. The abstract should more precisely frame the contribution as an empirical characterization of predictive multiplicity in math-reasoning fine-tuning, rather than a definitive statement on data inefficiency.

### Introduction & Background
The problem is well-motivated given the data-scarcity concerns in LLM training, and the scope is appropriately bounded to math reasoning, SFT, and RL. However, the introduction frames the phenomenon as a newly understudied failure mode without sufficiently engaging with established literature on loss landscape connectivity, mode collapse, and the Rashomon effect in deep learning. Prior works (e.g., Neyshabur et al., 2017; Fort et al., 2020 on flat vs. sharp minima; recent studies on optimizer-dependent convergence in LLMs) already document how non-convex optimization yields diverse solutions with similar risk. The paper needs to clearly delineate how its findings on *nested* training scaling and math-specific strategy multiplicity differ from these broader optimization phenomena. Without this positioning, the claimed novelty is diluted.

### Section 3: Standard Scaling Law Analyses & Test-Time Scaling
The nested subset experiment (Fig. 1, Fig. 2) is the paper's strongest empirical design. Observing that previously correct test samples flip to incorrect even when the model re-sees the original training data during later steps is genuinely interesting. However, several critical details are missing:
1. **Absolute Performance Context:** The paper reports percentage flips but omits baseline accuracies for each step. A 15% flip rate means very different things if test accuracy starts at 40% vs. 80%. Providing the baseline trajectory alongside the intersection/union metrics (Fig. 3) is essential for calibrating the severity of the phenomenon.
2. **Test-Time Scaling Interpretation (Sec 3.1):** The claim that majority voting "does not resolve the problem" mischaracterizes what self-consistency is designed to do. Majority voting reduces *inference-time* variance by sampling from a fixed policy. If the training process fundamentally shifts the model's learned parameters (i.e., changes the underlying policy or representation), majority voting should not be expected to recover lost correctness. The experiment inadvertently conflates training-induced distributional shifts with inference stochasticity. To properly test this, the authors should evaluate whether ensembling models from different training steps (rather than sampling from one model) recovers performance, which would directly speak to the "Union" metric in Fig. 3.
3. **Metric Robustness:** The "Newly Incorrectly Answered" metric relies on greedy decoding. While greedy decoding is standard, math reasoning often benefits from constrained decoding or structured extraction. The choice of greedy decoding may artificially inflate flip rates due to token-level tie-breaking. A brief discussion on decoding strategy sensitivity is warranted.

### Section 4: Fixed-Set Analysis & Theoretical Framing
This section presents the core argument but contains a significant logical and theoretical gap.

1. **Fixed-Set Analysis (4.1):** Training on the full dataset across 3 seeds and observing low intersection of correctly answered samples (Fig. 6) effectively demonstrates predictive multiplicity. However, 3 seeds is statistically thin for estimating intersection stability in high-dimensional LLM parameter spaces. ICLR standards typically expect ≥5-10 seeds or bootstrap sampling to confidently claim a stable low-intersection regime. Additionally, reporting the variance of the intersection across seeds would strengthen the claim.

2. **Theoretical Framework & Independence Assumption (4.2):** This is the most problematic part of the paper. The authors introduce Rashomon sets (Def 1) and combinatorially count permissible models (Settings 1 & 2). The critical flaw lies in the explicit assumption stated in the text: *"Note that we assume that per-sample strategies are independent of each other in that a change in strategy for one sample does not impact a change in strategy for another sample."* 
   This assumption is fundamentally at odds with how neural networks learn. Weight-sharing and gradient updates inherently couple sample representations; learning a strategy for one math problem necessarily affects the feature space for others. The combinatorial bounds (Equations following Settings 1 & 2) are mathematically sound *under the independence assumption*, but they do not describe the actual dynamics of transformer training. Consequently, the theoretical section does not explain the empirical results; it merely counts possibilities in an unrealistic discrete space. The authors should either drop the combinatorial framing and instead analyze loss landscape geometry, representation similarity (e.g., CKA or linear probe alignment across seeds), or explicitly label the combinatorics as a simplified upper-bound toy model with heavy caveats.

3. **Ablation Contradiction (Fig. 7):** The paper shows that fixing sample order and removing LoRA dropout collapses multiplicity, yielding near-perfect intersection. The authors acknowledge this but frame it as proof of "high predictive multiplicity." In reality, this strongly suggests the observed multiplicity is primarily driven by *optimization stochasticity* (data shuffling order + dropout noise) rather than a fundamental inability to "use" the data. If removing standard training randomness yields consistent functions, the issue is not data inefficiency but path-dependent convergence in a highly non-convex, symmetric loss landscape. The paper needs to reconcile this: does fixing order/dropout *actually* improve the final test accuracy (i.e., does it find a globally better solution, or just a more deterministic local minimum)? If accuracy remains the same, the "incomplete use" claim weakens significantly.

### Experimental Details & Reproducibility
The hyperparameter reporting is generally adequate, but several omissions hinder reproducibility and robustness claims:
- **RL Experiments (Sec 3.2.2):** The Qwen2.5-0.5B ZeroRL experiments use only **1 seed**. Given the high variance inherent in RL fine-tuning, drawing conclusions about predictive multiplicity and data scaling from a single RL run is insufficient. At minimum, 3 seeds are required to claim consistency.
- **Batch Size & Optimizer:** The learning rates, epochs, and PEFT methods are listed, but batch size, gradient accumulation steps, and optimizer specifics (AdamW betas, weight decay specifics per group) are missing. These heavily influence trajectory stochasticity and convergence basins.
- **Appendix A (Capacity & Full SFT):** The appendices state that full fine-tuning and larger models still show the phenomenon, which is a strong point. However, Fig. 8 and Fig. 9 are presented without statistical aggregation or baseline accuracy context, making them difficult to interpret rigorously.

### Limitations & Broader Impact
The paper lacks a dedicated limitations section. Key limitations that should be explicitly addressed include:
- The scope is limited to math word problems (GSM8K, MAWPS, MATH) and specific model sizes (0.5B-8B). It is unclear if the phenomenon scales to 70B+ models or extends to non-math reasoning tasks like code generation or logical deduction.
- The definition of "strategy" (Sec 4.2) relies on extracting "sequence of mathematical operations," which is heuristic and fragile. LLMs do not execute symbolic operations; they generate probabilistic token sequences. The robustness of this extraction mechanism to paraphrasing or alternative reasoning styles is not discussed.
- The practical implication of "incomplete data use" is not fully explored. If multiplicity stems from optimizer noise, techniques like loss-averaging, parameter-space ensembling, or deterministic training might mitigate it. The paper identifies the symptom but does not propose actionable remedies.

---

### Overall Assessment
This paper raises a thought-provoking empirical observation: scaling fine-tuning data for math reasoning induces non-trivial forgetting patterns, and models trained on identical data converge to highly divergent predictive functions with similar aggregate accuracy. The nested-subset experiment and the fixed-set intersection analysis provide genuine insights into training dynamics that could inform future data curation and optimization strategies. 

However, several critical issues currently limit the paper's readiness for ICLR acceptance. First, the theoretical framing in Section 4.2 relies on an independence assumption that is fundamentally incompatible with shared-parameter neural network training, rendering the combinatorial analysis disconnected from the empirical reality. Second, the claim that models "do not make complete use of data" is not fully supported by the ablations; fixing sample order and LoRA dropout largely eliminates multiplicity, suggesting the phenomenon is driven by standard optimization stochasticity rather than a structural data-utilization failure. Third, experimental rigor needs strengthening: RL evaluations lack sufficient seeds, test-time scaling claims conflate inference variance with training-induced distribution shifts, and baseline accuracies are omitted from key scaling curves. 

If the authors can: (1) reframe the theory around loss landscape geometry or representation similarity rather than independent sample strategies, (2) add sufficient seeds for RL and report baseline trajectories alongside flip rates, (3) clarify whether deterministic training yields better absolute performance or just consistent convergence, and (4) properly contextualize findings against existing work on optimization path-dependence, this could be a strong ICLR contribution. As currently written, the empirical core is promising but the mechanistic claims and theoretical framing require substantial revision to meet ICLR's standards for rigor and novelty.

# Neutral Reviewer
## Balanced Review

### Summary
The paper investigates whether large language models fully utilize mathematical reasoning training data, discovering that scaling training datasets causes 10-15% of previously correct test samples to become incorrectly answered despite overall accuracy gains. Through nested-subset training, fixed-set seed variation, and combinatorial modeling, the authors link this phenomenon to high predictive multiplicity (the Rashomon effect), where different seeds lead to models learning distinct solution strategies. The work concludes that optimization stochasticity, particularly from sample ordering and dropout, prevents models from converging on a unified, data-complete function.

### Strengths
1. **Rigorous per-sample tracking methodology:** Instead of relying solely on aggregate accuracy curves, the authors track sample-level correctness across nested training subsets. This design clearly isolates the "newly incorrectly answered" phenomenon (Fig. 1, 2, 4) and demonstrates that marginal net improvements mask substantial functional instability.
2. **Comprehensive empirical scope:** The evaluation spans multiple model families/sizes (Llama3, Gemma1B/4B/12B, Qwen2.5-0.5B), datasets of varying difficulty (MAWPS, GSM8K, MATH), and training paradigms (SFT with PEFT/LoRA/Full fine-tuning and ZeroRL with GRPO). The inclusion of test-time scaling (majority voting) and ablations on capacity and PEFT (Appendix A.1, A.2) strengthens the claim that the phenomenon is not an artifact of a single setup.
3. **Clear mechanistic link to training stochasticity:** The fixed-set analysis (Fig. 6) and subsequent ablation showing that fixing sample order and removing LoRA dropout drastically increase the intersection of correct samples (Fig. 7) provide a direct, empirically grounded explanation for why models learn divergent functions. This cleanly connects observed sample-level interference to well-documented optimization dynamics.

### Weaknesses
1. **Underdeveloped theoretical grounding:** While the paper defines the Rashomon set and provides combinatorial estimates for permissible models under $\epsilon$-budgets, the framework assumes independence across samples and fixed correct/incorrect splits. This serves as an intuitive illustration but lacks rigorous connection to loss landscape geometry, Hessian spectra, or SGD dynamics, making it difficult to claim a causal theoretical explanation per ICLR's standards.
2. **Insufficient validation for reinforcement learning:** The predictive multiplicity claim is a core contribution, yet the ZeroRL experiments are conducted on only a single random seed (Sec 3.2.2). Without multi-seed RL runs, it remains unclear whether the same functional divergence and per-sample instability generalize to reward-driven optimization, which typically exhibits fundamentally different loss landscapes than SFT.
3. **Limited practical pathways or competitive baselines:** The paper effectively identifies a failure mode but offers no constructive mitigation beyond removing training variance (fixing order, disabling dropout). It also omits comparison to established methods designed to maximize data utilization or preserve past knowledge during scaling, such as experience replay, data curriculum scheduling, or explicit ensemble distillation, leaving the "so what?" question for practitioners largely unanswered.

### Novelty & Significance
The paper's novelty is **moderate**. While predictive multiplicity and the Rashomon effect are well-documented in classical ML and overparameterized networks, reframing them to explain per-sample interference during data scaling in LLM math fine-tuning provides a timely and valuable perspective. The **significance** is **high** for the current ICLR community, as it directly challenges implicit assumptions about linear data scaling and synthetic data utility in RLVR pipelines. In terms of **clarity**, the narrative is well-structured, with logical progression from empirical observation to theoretical framing and ablation, though some definitions (e.g., strategy extraction) lack implementation details. **Reproducibility** is reasonable for SFT (clear hyperparameters, PEFT setup, multi-seed reporting), but weakens for RL due to the single-seed run and minimal detail on the GRPO reward formulation. Overall, the work meets ICLR's bar for empirical rigor but would benefit from deeper theoretical anchoring and broader RL validation to be considered strongly influential.

### Suggestions for Improvement
1. **Expand RL validation:** Run the ZeroRL/GRPO experiments across at least 3-5 random seeds to empirically verify predictive multiplicity in reinforcement learning. Report intersection sizes and strategy variance to determine if reward shaping mitigates or exacerbates functional divergence.
2. **Strengthen the theoretical or measurement framework:** Either (a) ground the Rashomon analysis in optimization theory by analyzing gradient alignment, loss surface connectivity, or parameter/activation space divergence (e.g., using linear mode connectivity or CKA similarity between models), or (b) clearly frame the combinatorial section as a conceptual toy model rather than a formal proof.
3. **Detail strategy extraction and add baselines:** Explicitly describe how mathematical operation sequences are parsed from reasoning traces (regex, LLM extraction, AST parsing?) and release the code. Additionally, compare the "Final Model" accuracy against a simple ensemble of the diverse-seed models or a model trained with continual replay to demonstrate how much performance is left on the table and how it might be recovered.
4. **Clarify the role of decoding and temperature:** Since the paper claims test-time scaling does not resolve the issue, discuss whether the "newly incorrect" samples stem from genuine functional forgetting or from narrowing the output distribution such that alternative (but valid) reasoning paths are suppressed. Including a small experiment tracking reasoning path diversity vs. accuracy would strengthen this claim.

# Spark Finder Review
## How to Improve This Paper

### Missing Experiments (top 3-5 only)
1. RL evaluation relies on a single seed. Predictive multiplicity is inherently stochastic; claiming high multiplicity for GRPO/ZeroRL with n=1 is statistically invalid and fails ICLR reproducibility standards. Run at least 3-5 independent RL seeds with variance bands to prove this isn't a single-run artifact.
2. No ensembling or weight-averaging baseline. If the core issue is multiplicity, simple ensembling should theoretically bridge the large gap between the "Union" upper bound and the "Final" single-model accuracy. Omitting this directly undermines the title's claim that models fail to make complete use of data, rather than just lacking the right architecture to pool learned functions.
3. Unclear subset construction protocol for scaling experiments. The paper claims nested subsets cause previously correct answers to flip, but doesn't specify if subsets are randomly sampled, stratified by difficulty, or de-duplicated. Poorly stratified random sampling introduces distribution shifts that artificially inflate the "newly incorrect" count independent of multiplicity.
4. Incomplete coverage of PEFT vs. full fine-tuning dynamics. The main SFT results use LoRA/LoftQ while RL uses full weights, but the paper claims LoRA dropout drives multiplicity. Run full-parameter SFT and remove-dropout SFT to isolate whether PEFT artifacts, rather than fundamental model limitations, are causing the observed phenomenon.

### Deeper Analysis Needed (top 3-5 only)
1. The independence assumption in the theoretical framework is violated by math reasoning. The combinatorial Rashomon set bounds assume strategy choices per question are independent, which is false given shared sub-skills (e.g., algebra, arithmetic rules). Quantify cross-sample strategy dependencies to prove the theoretical explosion is grounded in reality, not just a flawed proxy.
2. Mechanism of "newly incorrect" flips is unverified. The claims rely on automated answer extraction without analyzing whether models genuinely learn wrong heuristics, suffer from catastrophic forgetting, or simply change output formatting that breaks regex matchers. Trace-level analysis is required to distinguish true reasoning failure from evaluation artifacts.
3. Causality between multiplicity and performance degradation is assumed, not proven. Observing that models differ across seeds does not prove that multiplicity causes the scaling inefficiency; both could stem from an under-specified loss landscape. Show that forcing convergence (e.g., via consistency loss or deterministic training) actually reduces the newly incorrect rate to establish causality.

### Visualizations & Case Studies
1. Full reasoning trace comparison of a "newly incorrect" sample. Show a specific test question correctly solved at 20% data and incorrectly solved at 100% data, highlighting the exact reasoning step where the strategy diverged or degraded. This directly reveals if the model learns a fundamentally different, incorrect rule or just commits an isolated arithmetic error.
2. Pairwise intersection heatmap across seeds and data steps. Replace one-dimensional bar reports with a matrix showing per-seed agreement on test set predictions. This would visually confirm whether the "low intersection" represents uniform random diversity or structured clustering of learned heuristics, which drastically changes the interpretation of multiplicity.
3. Error bar overlays on scaling and union-vs-final curves. All current plots show single-point estimates, obscuring whether the observed gaps are robust or within random optimization variance. Adding confidence intervals is essential to convince reviewers that the "newly incorrect" rate is a systematic property, not stochastic noise.

### Obvious Next Steps
1. Test consistency-regularizing objectives to mitigate multiplicity. If high Rashomon set variance is the root cause, adding trajectory-level consistency losses or enforcing diverse path sampling during training should theoretically reduce answer flipping. The paper should at least prototype this to prove the analysis is actionable, not just diagnostic.
2. Evaluate curriculum or difficulty-based data selection. If adding random data causes performance churn, investigate whether scaling data sequentially by difficulty or strategy coverage stabilizes the learned function. This directly addresses the paper's central question of how to make models effectively use large math datasets.

# Final Consolidated Review
## Summary
This paper introduces a per-sample tracking methodology to analyze how LLM performance scales with math reasoning data. It uncovers that expanding training datasets causes 10–15% of previously correct test samples to flip to incorrect, despite net accuracy gains. Through fixed-set analysis across random seeds, the authors attribute this to high predictive multiplicity—the Rashomon effect—where models converge to diverse functions with similar empirical risk, and demonstrate experimentally that this multiplicity is largely driven by optimization stochasticity such as sample ordering and LoRA dropout.

## Strengths
- **Rigorous per-sample tracking design:** The nested-subset training protocol and the "Union vs. Final Model" metric (Fig. 3) move beyond aggregate accuracy curves to empirically isolate functional instability. This clearly demonstrates that marginal net gains mask substantial sample-level interference.
- **Comprehensive empirical validation:** The phenomenon is shown to persist across multiple model scales (0.5B to 12B), training paradigms (SFT with PEFT/LoRA/Full fine-tuning and ZeroRL), datasets of varying difficulty, and test-time scaling via majority voting. The breadth of evaluation strongly supports the claim that the effect is not an artifact of a single setup.
- **Direct mechanistic ablation:** The ablation fixing sample order and removing LoRA dropout (Sec. 4.2, Fig. 7) cleanly links the observed multiplicity to standard training stochasticity. This provides a grounded, reproducible explanation for why models learn divergent functions on identical data.

## Weaknesses
- **Theoretical independence assumption disconnects from neural dynamics:** Section 4.2 introduces a combinatorial framing to bound the size of the Rashomon set but explicitly assumes *"per-sample strategies are independent of each other in that a change in strategy for one sample does not impact a change in strategy for another sample."* This assumption is fundamentally incompatible with shared-parameter transformer training, where gradients and representations couple all samples. Consequently, the theoretical bounds describe an unrealistic discrete toy model rather than the actual continuous, high-dimensional optimization landscape, weakening the causal explanation of the empirical results.
- **Framing conflates optimization path-dependence with "incomplete data use":** The ablation in Section 4.2 shows that removing sample-order randomness and LoRA dropout collapses multiplicity and forces models to learn the same function, while maintaining similar accuracy. This strongly suggests the phenomenon is standard path-dependent convergence in a flat, symmetric loss landscape (many good minima), not a structural inability to "use" the data. The title and claims overstate a fundamental failure, whereas the evidence points to a well-known property of non-convex optimization: deterministic training *can* make complete use of the data.
- **Single-seed validation for Reinforcement Learning:** The core claim is that predictive multiplicity extends to RL fine-tuning, yet the ZeroRL/GRPO experiments are conducted on only one random seed (Sec. 3.2.2). Given the high variance intrinsic to reward-driven optimization, a single run cannot substantiate generalized claims of functional divergence or multiplicity for RLVR pipelines.
- **Fragile strategy extraction heuristic:** The paper quantifies strategy multiplicity by extracting the "sequence of mathematical operations" from reasoning traces (Definition 3). LLMs generate probabilistic token sequences, not deterministic symbolic code; this heuristic parsing is highly sensitive to formatting, verbalization of steps, and minor paraphrasing. The reported average of 3.15 incorrect strategies per sample may conflate stylistic variation with genuine reasoning divergence, undermining the robustness of the strategy-space argument.

## Nice-to-Haves
- **Ensemble baseline:** A simple ensemble or weight-averaging of the diverse-seed models would illustrate how much performance is left on the table and directly bridge the large gap between the "Union" upper bound and "Final" single-model accuracy.
- **Variance reporting:** Adding error bars or variance bands across seeds for the scaling curves and intersection metrics would clarify the stability of the "newly incorrect" flip rates.
- **Clarify subset construction:** Briefly specify whether nested subsets are randomly sampled, stratified by difficulty, or de-duplicated, to rule out distribution shift as a confounder for flip rates.

## Novel Insights
The paper provides a valuable granular lens on data scaling: marginal improvements in aggregate accuracy can hide significant "functional churn," where the model essentially relearns different subsets of the test distribution. The fixed-set intersection metric is a practical diagnostic tool for researchers to determine whether adding more data yields diminishing returns due to solution diversity rather than capacity bottlenecks. This reframes the question of data efficiency from "how much data?" to "how consistent is the model's convergence on this data?"

## Suggestions
1. **Expand RL validation:** Run ZeroRL experiments across at least 3–5 independent seeds with variance reporting. This is essential to confirm that predictive multiplicity is a systematic property of reward-driven fine-tuning and not a single-run artifact.
2. **Reframe or replace the theoretical section:** Either (a) ground the Rashomon analysis in optimization-relevant metrics by measuring representation similarity (e.g., linear CKA, activation space alignment) or loss landscape connectivity across seeds, or (b) explicitly label the combinatorial framework as a conceptual upper-bound illustration with clear caveats regarding the independence assumption.
3. **Address the framing discrepancy:** The conclusion and title should be qualified to reflect that the "incomplete use" is driven by optimization stochasticity. Clarify whether deterministic training (fixed order, no dropout) yields higher absolute accuracy or merely a more consistent local minimum, which would distinguish between "better data usage" and "consistent convergence."
4. **Release strategy extraction details and code:** Provide the exact regex/parsing logic or extraction code used for mathematical operation sequences to ensure the strategy diversity counts are reproducible and robust to verbalization changes.
