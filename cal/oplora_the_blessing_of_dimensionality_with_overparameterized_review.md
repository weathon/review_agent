# Harsh Critic Review
## Section-by-Section Critical Review

### Title & Abstract
- **Accuracy:** The title accurately captures the core idea: using overparameterized MLPs to predict LoRA weights at train-time, then discarding them.
- **Clarity:** The abstract clearly states the problem (LoRA's ill-conditioned optimization landscape and inflexible/slow custom optimizers), the method (train-time MLP hypernetwork, discarded post-training), and key results (consistent gains, large CMMD improvements, faster wall-clock than LoRA-Pro/ScaledAdamW).
- **Claims:** The claim of "zero extra cost at inference" is accurate since the MLP is discarded. The "no risk of overfitting due to increased representational capacity" is also correct, as the rank constraint strictly bounds the final adapter's capacity. All abstract claims are substantiated in later sections. No unsupported over-claims detected.

### Introduction & Motivation
- **Motivation & Gap:** Well-motivated. The authors correctly identify that LoRA's parameter efficiency comes at the cost of a distorted, ill-conditioned loss landscape (Eq. 3), and that existing fixes (gradient alignment optimizers like LoRA-Pro/ScaledAdamW) are rigid and computationally heavy. The gap for an architecture-agnostic, train-time-only reparameterization is clearly established.
- **Contributions:** Clearly listed and match the paper's content. The claim that this represents a "blessing of dimensionality" for optimization is appropriately scoped.
- **Claim Calibration:** The introduction does not over-sell. It carefully distinguishes OP-LoRA from simply increasing rank (which hurts inference/memory) and from hypernetworks that share parameters across layers. The framing aligns well with ICLR's interest in optimization dynamics and efficient fine-tuning paradigms.

### Method / Approach
- **Description & Reproducibility:** Equation 4 clearly defines the MLP formulation. The training pipeline is straightforward: generate $A,B$ via MLP, compute forward pass with frozen weights + $BA$, backprop, update MLP. Code is referenced in Appendix C. Reproducible in principle.
- **Assumptions & Justification:** 
  - The method assumes a 2-layer MLP with a single learned input vector $z$ per adapter. The justification for not sharing $z$/weights across layers (Sec 4.3 vs HyperLoader) is empirically sound.
  - **Major Caveat in Derivation:** Section 3.2 derives the "trainable learning rate" ($\|h\|^2$) and "adaptive line search" terms, but explicitly states: *"for clarity of derivation, we treat $h$ as a free parameter vector; this corresponds to only updating the bias in the first layer of the MLP."* This is a significant simplification. The actual training dynamics update $W_1, W_2, z,$ and all biases simultaneously. While Appendix A.1 extends the analysis to the full ReLU case, the core theoretical claims about implicit acceleration and dynamic step scaling in the main text rest on a highly constrained linear sub-problem. The authors must clarify whether the "adaptive line search" property rigorously transfers to the full non-conv MLP parameterization, or if it should be framed as an intuitive analogy rather than a proven guarantee.
  - The Hessian conditioning analysis (Eq. 2-3, Appendix A.2) correctly shows standard LoRA's condition number bound. OP-LoRA effectively changes the optimization space from $(A,B)$ to $(W_1, W_2, z, c)$. The paper argues this yields a smoother landscape, but does not formally bound the new Hessian. Given ICLR's emphasis on theoretical soundness, a brief discussion or empirical verification of the actual Hessian spectrum under OP-LoRA would strengthen the method section.
- **Edge Cases/Failures:** No discussion of what happens if the MLP converges to a degenerate output (e.g., rank collapse in $A$ or $B$ despite theoretical expressivity). Hypernetworks can sometimes struggle with weight collapse; a brief ablation on the empirical rank distribution of generated $A,B$ post-training would address this.

### Experiments & Results
- **Claim Testing:** The experiments directly test the claims: robustness to LR (Sec 3.3, Fig 2), performance across modalities (SD-XL, VL-BART, LLaMA-7B), and computational cost (Sec 4.4).
- **Baselines:** Strong and relevant. Compares against standard LoRA, DoRA, PiSSA, OLoRA (implicit in related work), LoRA-GA, LoRA-Pro, ScaledAdamW, DeepLoRA, and HyperLoader. This comprehensively covers optimization-aware and initialization-aware PEFT baselines.
- **Ablations & Missing Pieces:**
  - MLP width is ablated (Fig 4), showing sensitivity on VQA but flatness on LLM tasks. Good.
  - Missing multi-seed statistical reporting in main tables. Table B.4 shows substantial variance reduction for OP-DoRA (±6.7 → ±1.6), but Tables 1, 2, and 3 report single runs or point estimates. For ICLR, reporting mean ± std over ≥3 seeds on the LLM and VQA benchmarks is expected, especially given the high variance of DoRA shown in the appendix.
  - No ablation on MLP depth (all experiments use 2 layers) or alternative activation functions. While 2 layers likely suffice given Arora et al. (2018) foundations, confirming that depth >0 is the active ingredient would be valuable.
- **Results Support Claims?** Yes, but with nuance. Gains on image generation are substantial (~15 CMMD points). Gains on LLM commonsense tasks are modest (~0.8-1.2% absolute). The efficiency claim (4h vs 4.5h vs 56h, Table 4) is well-supported. However, the ~57% memory overhead (44GB → 69GB VRAM, Table 4) is non-trivial and could prevent scaling to 13B/70B models on single GPUs without gradient checkpointing or CPU offloading. This trade-off deserves explicit discussion.
- **Metrics/Datasets:** Appropriate. CMMD is a modern, reliable choice for SD-XL evaluation. CommonSense and VQA benchmarks are standard. Protocols follow established baselines (Liu et al., 2024).

### Writing & Clarity
- **Structure & Flow:** Logical progression from problem → theory → toy validation → large-scale experiments. Appendix is well-organized.
- **Confusing Sections:** The transition in Sec 3.2 from the full MLP formulation to $v = W_2 h$ is abrupt. The simplification to "only updating the bias in the first layer" is stated but risks misleading readers into thinking this is the actual training dynamics. Rewriting this subsection to explicitly frame the linear/bias-only case as a *mechanistic toy model for intuition*, followed immediately by a statement of how the full network differs and why the acceleration effect persists empirically, would greatly improve clarity.
- **Figures/Tables:** Figure 2 (loss surfaces) and Figure 4 (MLP width ablation) are informative. Table 3 is dense but readable. Qualitative samples (Fig 3, 8-25) are helpful for SD-XL, though the text extraction artifacts make them hard to verify here; assuming they render correctly in the PDF, they adequately support the CMMD claims.

### Limitations & Broader Impact
- **Acknowledged Limitations:** The authors correctly identify the train-time parameter scaling (~32x increase in trainable params for width 32) and resulting memory overhead. They note that wall-time overhead is modest compared to ScaledAdamW/LoRA-Pro.
- **Missed Limitations:**
  1. **Memory Scaling on Larger Models:** The 69GB peak memory for 7B is manageable on an H100, but scaling to 70B+ or 8x multi-lora serving setups will hit VRAM ceilings quickly without additional engineering (activation recomputation, LoRA weight caching strategies during forward).
  2. **Theoretical-Practical Gap:** The derivation's reliance on frozen first-layer dynamics vs actual end-to-end training is acknowledged but not bridged theoretically. Empirical evidence shows it works, but the "why" at scale remains partly heuristic.
  3. **Interaction with Advanced PEFT Variants:** Only DoRA is extended. It's unclear if OP-LoRA would compound, conflict with, or render redundant techniques like LoRA+ (adaptive scaling) or VeRA (vector-based scaling). A brief discussion or single-combined experiment would help position the method in the rapidly evolving PEFT landscape.
- **Societal Impact:** Standard boilerplate, acceptable for a pure optimization/method paper. No immediate dual-use concerns.

### Overall Assessment
This paper presents a novel, conceptually elegant approach to improving LoRA's optimization dynamics through train-time overparameterization. The method is easy to implement, requires zero inference overhead, and demonstrates strong empirical consistency across vision, language, and multimodal tasks. The most significant strengths are the substantial gains in generation quality (SD-XL) and the remarkable reduction in optimization variance compared to DoRA. However, the work has notable weaknesses for ICLR's high bar: (1) the core theoretical derivation of the "adaptive line search" and "trainable learning rate" mechanisms explicitly relies on a simplified model (frozen first-layer weights) that does not reflect actual training dynamics, creating a theory-practice gap; (2) main tables lack multi-seed statistical reporting, which is standard for modern LLM/VQA evaluations and necessary to confirm that the modest 0.8-1.2% gains are statistically significant; and (3) the substantial train-time memory overhead (+57% VRAM) is dismissed as "manageable" without discussing how it scales to modern 13B-70B models or concurrent multi-LoRA serving. Addressing these points—particularly by tightening the theoretical framing, adding seed variance to key tables, and discussing scaling constraints—would elevate the paper to a strong accept. As is, it represents a solid, practically useful contribution with clear mechanistic intuition, but requires more rigorous empirical validation and theoretical alignment before meeting the highest ICLR standards.

# Neutral Reviewer
## Balanced Review

### Summary
The paper introduces OP-LoRA, a train-time-only overparameterization technique that replaces direct optimization of LoRA matrices with a small MLP that predicts the adapter weights, discarding the MLP entirely after training. Motivated by the ill-conditioned loss landscapes and learning-rate sensitivity inherent to standard LoRA, the authors provide a theoretical analysis demonstrating an implicit acceleration mechanism through dynamic step-size scaling and adaptive line search. Extensive experiments across image generation (SD-XL), vision-language modeling (VL-BART), and LLM fine-tuning (LLaMA-7B) demonstrate consistent performance gains, reduced hyperparameter sensitivity, and significantly lower training wall-time compared to complex gradient-alignment optimizers, all while preserving zero inference overhead.

### Strengths
1. **Highly Practical and Zero-Inference-Cost Design**: OP-LoRA requires only minimal code modifications, generalizes trivially to existing LoRA variants (DoRA, VeRA), and incurs absolutely no additional storage or inference latency since the generating MLP is discarded post-training.
2. **Robust Empirical Validation Across Domains**: The method is evaluated on image generation (Table 1), visual QA (Table 2), and LLM commonsense reasoning (Table 3), showing consistent improvements (1-6% on text/VQA, up to 15 CMMD points on SD-XL) over strong baselines, including recent state-of-the-art optimizers.
3. **Clear Theoretical and Empirical Link to Optimization Dynamics**: The derivation in Section 3.2 effectively identifies the "trainable learning rate" ($\|h\|^2$) and "adaptive line search" terms. This is empirically grounded by the MNIST case study (Figure 2) and gradient norm analysis (Table 5), which convincingly demonstrate OP-LoRA's reduced sensitivity to learning rate selection and improved traversal of high-curvature directions.
4. **Strong Reproducibility Package**: The authors provide full hyperparameter tables (Appendix C), explicit initialization strategies, hardware specifications, and supplementary code, making the pipeline highly reproducible and immediately deployable within standard PEFT frameworks.

### Weaknesses
1. **Theoretical Assumptions Disconnect from Real-World Optimizers**: The optimization analysis (Section 3.2, Appendix A.1) relies on small learning rates, first-order Taylor approximations, and ignores the $\eta^2$ cross-term. Crucially, all large-scale experiments use AdamW, which already implements per-parameter adaptive learning rates and momentum. The paper does not analyze how Adam's moment estimates interact with OP-LoRA's implicit step-size modulation, leaving it unclear how much of the gain stems from the proposed mechanism versus Adam's existing preconditioning.
2. **Unsubstantiated/Unfair Computational Baseline for LoRA-Pro**: Table 4 reports LoRA-Pro at 56 hours vs. OP-LoRA's 4 hours. This ~14x disparity strongly suggests an unoptimized or naive implementation of LoRA-Pro rather than inherent algorithmic complexity. Without specifying whether the official code was used or detailing the exact bottleneck, this efficiency claim is misleading and undermines a fair comparison.
3. **Glossed Over Train-Time Memory Overhead**: GPU memory increases from 44 GB to 69 GB (Table 4), a ~57% jump that could easily force practitioners to reduce batch size or adapter rank on mid-tier hardware. The paper lacks practical discussion or mitigations (e.g., activation checkpointing, gradient accumulation adjustments) for this memory wall, which is a major constraint for the PEFT community.
4. **Missing Heuristic for MLP Width Selection**: Figure 4 reveals an inverted-U performance curve relative to MLP width, yet optimal widths vary drastically across tasks (4 for VL-BART vs. 32 for LLaMA vs. 768 for LLaVA). The paper provides no principled guideline, scaling rule, or automated search strategy for choosing this hyperparameter, limiting out-of-the-box usability.

### Novelty & Significance
- **Novelty (Moderate)**: While weight-generating hypernetworks and train-time overparameterization are established concepts, restricting the auxiliary network to the optimization phase to explicitly reshape LoRA's loss landscape is a novel and pragmatic contribution. The connection to implicit acceleration via product-parameterization is well-motivated but builds heavily on prior theoretical work (Arora et al., 2018).
- **Clarity (High)**: The manuscript is exceptionally well-structured. Mathematical derivations are transparent, intuitive explanations bridge theory and practice, and the narrative flow from small-scale diagnostics to large-scale benchmarks is logical and easy to follow.
- **Reproducibility (High)**: Code availability, exhaustive hyperparameter tables, clear dataset/model specifications, and hardware details meet or exceed ICLR standards. The drag-and-drop replacement claim for existing PEFT libraries further ensures downstream reproducibility.
- **Significance (High)**: Given LoRA's ubiquity, a drop-in method that reliably reduces learning-rate sensitivity, accelerates convergence, and matches complex custom optimizers with lower wall-time is highly impactful. The zero-inference-cost constraint makes it particularly valuable for deployment-constrained environments.

### Suggestions for Improvement
1. **Isolate the Mechanism from AdamW**: Run controlled experiments using SGD (with and without momentum) to demonstrate that OP-LoRA's acceleration holds independently of Adam's adaptive updates. Additionally, provide a brief analysis or simulation showing how AdamW's per-parameter scaling interacts with the $\|h\|^2$ trainable learning rate.
2. **Clarify and Fairly Benchmark LoRA-Pro Runtime**: Explicitly state whether the official LoRA-Pro implementation or a custom version was used. Provide a breakdown of where the time is spent (e.g., gradient computation, matrix projection, optimizer steps) and, if possible, re-evaluate with an optimized LoRA-Pro pipeline to establish a fair efficiency comparison.
3. **Provide Memory-Optimization Guidelines**: Address the 50%+ VRAM increase practically. Demonstrate or discuss how techniques like MLP activation checkpointing, mixed-precision training for the hypernetwork, or gradient freezing affect stability and throughput. A brief "memory-constrained best practices" subsection would greatly aid adoption.
4. **Introduce an MLP Width Selection Rule**: Add a scaling heuristic based on LoRA rank $r$ or layer dimension $d$ (e.g., $k \approx c \cdot \log(r)$ or $k \approx d/64$). A small supplementary ablation across 3-4 architectures establishing a reliable default or search strategy would significantly improve practitioner usability.
5. **Complement CMMD with Perceptual/Alignment Metrics for Vision**: While CMMD effectively measures distributional distance, adding standard generation evaluators like ImageReward, PickScore, or CLIP-Text alignment would strengthen the claim that OP-LoRA yields qualitatively better, more faithful generations, particularly for subjective tasks like style transfer.

# Spark Finder Review
## How to Improve This Paper

### Missing Experiments (top 3-5 only)
1. Multi-seed runs with variance/error bars for Tables 1-3. Without statistical significance testing across multiple seeds, the 1-3% accuracy gains and CMMD score fluctuations are indistinguishable from random noise, directly undermining claims of consistent superiority.
2. Full learning-rate sensitivity sweep on actual large-scale tasks (SDXL and LLaMA-7B) rather than only Rotated MNIST. The core thesis relies on OP-LoRA's robustness to suboptimal step sizes, but this critical property remains unproven at the target model scales.
3. Direct comparison against moderately rank-upscaled LoRA (e.g., r=64 vs. OP-LoRA r=32) matched for training memory. The paper compares to an extreme rank (r=466) but ignores the standard practitioner approach of moderate rank scaling, leaving unclear if gains stem from the MLP architecture or simply an expanded search space.
4. Memory and throughput scaling analysis on 13B/70B models with multi-GPU configurations. Training memory jumps from 44GB to 69GB (+57%), which severely limits batch sizes or requires tensor parallelism for larger models; claiming the overhead is "manageable" without multi-GPU validation is insufficient.

### Deeper Analysis Needed (top 3-5 only)
1. Empirical measurement of the proposed "trainable learning rate" and "adaptive line search" mechanisms during live SDXL/LLaMA fine-tuning. The theoretical derivation relies on simplified weight updates and isolated ReLU masks, and Appendix B validates this only on toy data; real-world gradient alignment must be proven.
2. Assessment of catastrophic forgetting and base model retention (e.g., MMLU or base prompt generation fidelity). Over-parameterizing the training trajectory may increase interference with frozen weights; without retention metrics, the claim that OP-LoRA minimizes catastrophic forgetting relative to capacity gains is unverified.
3. Train vs. validation divergence analysis to quantify generalization. The paper asserts zero added inference capacity but does not show if train-time overparameterization leads to overfitting sharper minima or genuinely better generalization valleys compared to standard LoRA.

### Visualizations & Case Studies
1. Plot effective step sizes, gradient norms, and loss trajectories for OP-LoRA versus standard LoRA across SDXL and LLaMA training epochs. This would visually confirm whether the MLP actually enables dynamic learning rate adaptation in practice or if standard Adam's internal momentum already handles the landscape equivalently.
2. Singular value distribution or effective rank plots of the final converged $\Delta W$ matrices for OP-LoRA compared to standard LoRA. Visualizing this exposes whether the method discovers meaningfully different low-rank structures or merely optimizes the same ill-conditioned subspace more aggressively.
3. Failure case analysis showing inputs where OP-LoRA produces degenerate outputs or fails to converge faster than standard LoRA. Highlighting when the "blessing of dimensionality" breaks down is essential for establishing the method's boundaries and practical reliability.

### Obvious Next Steps
1. Standardized hyperparameter search for all baselines alongside OP-LoRA. Presenting single default learning rates without demonstrating that LoRA-GA, ScaledAdamW, and DoRA were optimally tuned for each task weakens the comparison and risks unfair benchmarking.
2. Explicit architectural ablation of the MLP prediction strategy (single global MLP vs. per-layer MLPs vs. grouped heads). Parameter counts and overfitting behavior depend entirely on sharing schemes; clarifying and abating this design choice is required to validate the reported efficiency and scalability claims.
3. Comprehensive ablation on MLP width sensitivity and initialization protocols across all tasks. While Appendix Figure 4 shows an inverted U-shape for one task, commonsense results appear flat; a systematic sensitivity analysis is needed to prove the method is robust enough for practitioners without extensive re-tuning.

# Final Consolidated Review
## Summary
OP-LoRA introduces a train-time-only MLP hypernetwork to predict Low-Rank Adaptation (LoRA) weights, which is discarded after optimization to ensure zero inference overhead. The method aims to resolve the ill-conditionered loss landscapes and extreme learning-rate sensitivity of standard LoRA by implicitly inducing dynamic step-scaling and subspace-biased updates. The authors provide theoretical motivation, a small-scale diagnostic study, and empirical evaluations across image generation, vision-language QA, and LLM commonsense reasoning, reporting consistent gains over LoRA variants and faster wall-clock training than gradient-alignment optimizers.

## Strengths
- **Zero-inference-cost design with high practical utility:** By strictly bounding final capacity to the low-rank constraint and discarding the hypernetwork post-training, OP-LoRA delivers improved training dynamics without inflating deployment storage or latency. The drag-and-drop integration with standard PEFT libraries is highly practical.
- **Strong empirical gains, particularly in generative modeling:** The method achieves substantial improvements on SD-XL fine-tuning (~15 CMMD points lower distributional distance) alongside consistent 1–4% accuracy gains across VQA and LLM reasoning benchmarks, demonstrating cross-modal validity.
- **Clear mechanistic hypothesis with supporting diagnostics:** The theoretical framing connecting overparameterized product-parameterization to implicit acceleration is well-motivated. The Rotated MNIST and synthetic matrix factorization case studies effectively visualize reduced learning-rate sensitivity, faster convergence in ill-conditioned landscapes, and adaptive gradient step modulation.

## Weaknesses
- **Core theoretical-practice disconnect regarding optimizer interaction — why it matters:** The derivation of the "trainable learning rate" ($\|h\|^2$) and "adaptive line search" mechanisms explicitly relies on a simplified linear/bias-only update model, dropping $\mathcal{O}(\eta^2)$ terms and ignoring the actual joint gradient flows through both MLP layers. Crucially, all large-scale experiments use AdamW, which already implements per-parameter adaptive learning rates, momentum, and variance scaling. The paper fails to theoretically or empirically disentangle whether the observed gains stem from OP-LoRA’s proposed implicit mechanism or simply from AdamW’s existing preconditioning interacting with a larger optimization manifold. This obscures the actual source of acceleration and weakens the methodological contribution for an ICLR audience expecting rigorous optimization analysis.
- **Central robustness claim is unvalidated at target scales — why it matters:** The paper’s primary motivation is mitigating LoRA’s notorious learning-rate sensitivity caused by Hessian distortion. However, the LR robustness sweeps and trajectory visualizations (Figure 2) are exclusively conducted on a toy Rotated MNIST classifier. No multi-LR evaluations are provided for SD-XL, VL-BART, or LLaMA-7B, where the curvature, loss topology, and optimal hyperparameters are fundamentally different. Without scale-level validation, the core thesis that OP-LoRA reliably stabilizes large-scale PEFT remains an unproven hypothesis rather than an established result.
- **Lack of statistical rigor and questionable efficiency baselines — why it matters:** Main result tables report single-run point estimates without multi-seed variance. Given Appendix B.4 reveals high baseline instability for methods like DoRA (±6.7% standard deviation), the reported 0.8–1.2% absolute improvements on LLM tasks are statistically indistinguishable from random seed fluctuations. Additionally, the claimed 14× wall-time advantage over LoRA-Pro (56h vs 4h) strongly suggests an unoptimized or naive baseline implementation rather than inherent algorithmic inefficiency. Finally, the ~57% VRAM increase (44GB → 69GB) is dismissed as "manageable" without discussing feasibility for 13B/70B models or multi-LoRA serving, severely limiting the claimed accessibility for resource-constrained practitioners.

## Nice-to-Haves
- Provide a principled heuristic or scaling rule for selecting MLP hidden width, as performance exhibits an inverted-U relationship on VQA but remains flat on commonsense tasks, complicating out-of-the-box deployment.
- Include base-model retention or catastrophic forgetting metrics (e.g., pre-trained MMLU or zero-shot generation fidelity) to empirically confirm that train-time overparameterization avoids shifting the adapted weights into regions that degrade underlying capabilities.
- Visualize the effective rank or singular value distributions of the final converged $\Delta W$ matrices to show whether OP-LoRA discovers structurally different low-rank subspaces compared to standard LoRA.

## Novel Insights
OP-LoRA successfully reframes train-time overparameterization not as a pathway to expanded model capacity, but as a dynamic optimization landscape reshaper. By demonstrating that a discarded auxiliary network can implicitly emulate learning-rate annealing and gradient-aligned subspace searches, the work bridges the gap between mathematically rigid, computationally heavy gradient-projection optimizers and flexible, architecture-agnostic reparameterizations. This suggests that strategically decoupling optimization pathways from inference capacity is a viable, underexplored paradigm for efficient large-model adaptation.

## Potentially Missed Related Work
- **Muon / K-FAC-inspired PEFT preconditioning:** Recent first-order quasi-Newton and orthogonal update methods for LLM fine-tuning share the goal of navigating ill-conditioned PEFT landscapes without second-order costs. Comparing OP-LoRA’s implicit dynamics to explicit preconditioners could clarify if the gains stem from curvature-aware updates.
- **Low-rank implicit bias & spectral analysis in hypernetworks:** Works analyzing the spectral convergence of hypernetwork-generated parameters (e.g., in implicit neural representations or weight prediction tasks) may offer complementary theoretical bounds on the expressivity and stability of discarded generating networks.

## Suggestions
- Run multi-seed learning-rate sweeps on LLaMA-7B and SD-XL to statistically validate the claimed robustness and ensure that reported improvements are significant beyond optimizer/stochastic noise.
- Include a direct, training-memory-matched comparison against moderately rank-scaled LoRA (e.g., standard LoRA with $r$ scaled to match OP-LoRA’s gradient checkpointing memory footprint) to conclusively isolate architectural benefits from simple search-space expansion.
- Provide explicit runtime breakdowns for the LoRA-Pro baseline and re-evaluate using optimized implementations or published SOTA runtimes to ensure the efficiency claims are not artifacts of unoptimized reference code.
- Briefly discuss and benchmark practical mitigations for the train-time memory overhead, such as activation checkpointing for the MLP, mixed-precision training of the hypernetwork, or layer-wise forward caching, to improve adoption on sub-80GB hardware setups.
