# Harsh Critic Review
## Section-by-Section Critical Review

### Title & Abstract
- The title accurately describes the proposed method, though it slightly mischaracterizes the contribution as "interpretability" broadly; the paper focuses specifically on global feature weighting/selection via a post-hoc layer.
- The abstract clearly outlines the problem (interpreting deployed models), the method (post-hoc FSL on frozen tabular networks), and key results (comparable predictive power, improved clustering/visual metrics, reduced stability). 
- The claim that the method yields "superior performance on visual and clustering-based interpretability... as measured by weighted t-SNE and the silhouette score" is partially supported by Table 6, but conflating geometric cluster separability in a 2D projection with actual model interpretability or faithfulness of weights is a conceptual overreach. The abstract does not mention that the method requires access to ground-truth labels during fine-tuning (if supervised), which significantly changes how readers should understand a "post-hoc" method.

### Introduction & Motivation
- The problem is well-motivated: the need for non-invasive interpretability tools for high-stakes, already-deployed models is a realistic and important use case. The gap in prior work (original FSL requires joint training from scratch) is correctly identified.
- Contributions are implied but not explicitly delineated. The introduction states the proposal of a "post-hoc variant" that "preserves FSL’s effectiveness," but it does not clearly list the specific technical or empirical contributions (e.g., training protocol, stability trade-offs, evaluation on HDLSS data), making it harder for reviewers to quickly extract novelty.
- The introduction contains a mild over-claim: it states the method can "potentially improve predictive performance" by adding the FSL post-hoc. Since the backbone is frozen, any change in predictive performance stems solely from input scaling, which may actually break internal normalization (e.g., BatchNorm) assumptions in the frozen weights. The paper later shows the baseline often outperforms both FSL and post-hoc FSL (Table 5), undercutting the predictive improvement claim.

### Method / Approach
- The method is not fully reproducible in its current form. Section 3.2 outlines a 4-step procedure, but critically omits the definition of the loss function $\ell$, the optimization algorithm (Adam, SGD, etc.), learning rate schedule, number of epochs, batch size, early stopping criteria, and the regularization strength $\lambda$ (Equation 1 lacks the actual formula value, though it references $W^{[FSL]}_{t-1}$ and normalization). Without these, reproducing the fine-tuning is impossible.
- A major logical/conceptual gap exists regarding the training objective. If the loss uses ground-truth labels (supervised cross-entropy), the method is not a post-hoc explanation of the frozen black-box model; it is a supervised input adapter learning to map $w \odot x$ to labels. If the loss uses the frozen model's original predictions (unsupervised/self-distillation), this is not stated. The distinction fundamentally impacts whether the learned weights reflect the original model's reliance or a new supervised signal.
- Edge cases and failure modes are under-discussed. Scaling inputs by learned weights $\vec{w}$ alters the input distribution seen by the frozen network. If the frozen model uses Batch Normalization or activation functions sensitive to input scale (e.g., ReLU thresholds, saturation points), the fixed internal statistics/parameters will process scaled inputs incorrectly. The paper does not analyze or rule out this distribution shift effect, yet Table 5 shows the post-hoc FSL consistently underperform the baseline in predictive metrics, suggesting this issue may be actively harming the model during evaluation.

### Experiments & Results
- The experiments test the claims but lack necessary depth in ablation and training details. While baselines (IG, SHAP, DeepLIFT, etc.) are appropriate, the comparison mixes local attribution methods (which produce per-sample scores) with a global weighting method. Averaging local scores is common, but the paper should clarify how this aggregation was performed to ensure fair comparison.
- Missing ablations materially affect confidence. An ablation on initialization (why $1.0$ instead of $1/n$?), activation function choice (ReLU vs Sigmoid for weights), and regularizer strength is needed to justify design choices in Section 3.3. Additionally, varying the number of fine-tuning epochs would clarify if performance/instability stems from under-training or over-fitting to the training fold.
- Results appear mixed rather than clearly cherry-picked, but the evaluation metric reliance is weak. The primary evidence for "superior interpretability" rests on weighted t-SNE silhouette scores (Table 2, Table 6). Silhouette scores on 2D non-linear projections are highly sensitive to t-SNE hyperparameters (perplexity, learning rate) and do not measure attribute correctness, faithfulness to the model's decision boundary, or stability of selection beyond geometry. Standard interpretability metrics (Sanity Checks, Faithfulness/Sufficiency/AUC-Faithfulness, Insertion/Deletion curves) are absent.
- Dataset usage raises statistical concerns. Applying neural networks to microarray datasets with $n=48$ (Liver) and $n=151$ (Breast), then freezing and interpreting them, is highly prone to extreme overfitting and unstable gradients. The variance reported (e.g., $\pm 0.00$, $\pm 0.004$) seems surprisingly low for such small HDLSS regimes; clarification on cross-validation splits, seed control, and model initialization is required to ensure these results are not artifacts of lucky initialization or data leakage. Statistical significance tests are mentioned ("marked in orange") but exact p-values and test assumptions are not reported, reducing transparency.

### Writing & Clarity
- The method description in Section 3.2 is concise to the point of ambiguity. Readers are left guessing the loss function, optimizer, and stopping criteria. Section 3.1 claims the architecture is "computationally efficient" and references superiority over AFS/RF/ReliefF from the original FSL paper; this comparative claim is out of place for a *post-hoc* method paper and distracts from the actual contribution.
- Figures are described in text, but the narrative relies heavily on "visual separability" claims (e.g., "The reason behind the highest silhouette score is visually discernible") without rigorous justification beyond the silhouette number itself. Tables are structured logically, but the presentation of stability metrics (Table 1, Table 4) includes repeated values like "0 . ± 0 ." which, while noted as possible parser issues, makes it impossible to verify if standard deviations were truly zero (perfect stability) or if formatting hid the variance. The authors must verify the underlying data is correctly represented.
- The distinction between FSL and post-hoc FSL could be sharper. The paper frequently discusses FSL results alongside post-hoc FSL, but the comparison sometimes conflates embedded training benefits with post-hoc adaptation, making it harder to isolate the gain of the proposed method.

### Limitations & Broader Impact
- The authors acknowledge key limitations: poor performance on multi-class problems (global weights cannot capture class-specific saliency), applicability restricted to tabular data, and instability in rankings. These are honest and accurate based on the results.
- Fundamental limitations are missed. The potential violation of internal frozen layer assumptions (BatchNorm/activation shift) is a critical technical limitation not discussed. The reliance on labeled data for fine-tuning (if supervised) contradicts the "post-hoc black-box interpreter" framing; if unsupervised, the loss definition must be clarified. Additionally, the use of neural networks on extremely small biological datasets is limited; the instability observed in Breast dataset results may stem from the backbone model itself rather than the FSL, which should be discussed.
- Failure modes are partially covered (multi-class failure). No societal harms or negative impacts are discussed, though in high-stakes healthcare interpretation (microarray data), unstable weights or misleading importance scores could lead to incorrect biological hypotheses if deployed by practitioners. A brief caution regarding this risk would align with ICLR ethics expectations.

### Overall Assessment
This paper tackles a relevant problem—enabling feature relevance analysis for deployed neural networks without retraining—by adapting the embedded Feature Selection Layer to a post-hoc paradigm. The idea is intuitive and has practical appeal. However, the manuscript currently falls short of ICLR's bar for methodological rigor and evaluation strength due to critical ambiguities. The training procedure lacks essential details (loss function, optimizer, hyperparameters), leaving it unclear whether the method is a supervised input adapter or a truly post-hoc attribution technique. Furthermore, the primary empirical evidence relies on weighted t-SNE silhouette scores, which are weak proxies for interpretability, faithfulness, or correctness of feature weights. The paper also overlooks the risk of input distribution shift breaking frozen internal layers, and applies neural nets to extremely small datasets where stability guarantees are elusive. The contribution stands as a promising direction, but to warrant acceptance, the authors must clarify the training objective, provide complete reproducibility details, include standard faithfulness/robustness evaluations alongside geometric clustering metrics, and rigorously address the frozen architecture constraints.

# Neutral Reviewer
## Balanced Review

### Summary
This paper introduces a post-hoc adaptation of the Feature Selection Layer (FSL) that attaches a single trainable weight per input feature to frozen pre-trained neural networks, enabling non-invasive feature attribution without modifying the base model. The method is evaluated across synthetic and real-world high-dimensional tabular datasets against established gradient-based and perturbation attribution techniques using predictive, feature selection, visual clustering, and stability metrics. Results indicate that the post-hoc FSL maintains baseline predictive performance and improves visual cluster separability, though it demonstrates lower attribution stability and struggles with multi-class classification compared to joint training or alternative post-hoc methods.

### Strengths
1. **Clear, computationally efficient pipeline:** Section 3 outlines a straightforward mechanism where the FSL is trained post-hoc with frozen backbone parameters, L1 regularization, and ReLU activation. This design allows easy deployment on already-trained models without costly retraining, directly addressing the practical constraint highlighted in the introduction.
2. **Comprehensive empirical benchmarking:** The evaluation spans controlled synthetic datasets (XOR, SynthA) and challenging HDLSS real-world datasets (Liver, Breast microarray), with direct comparisons to Integrated Gradients, DeepLIFT, Gradient SHAP, Feature Ablation, and Noise Tunnel. The inclusion of multiple complementary metrics (PIFS/PSFI, silhouette, Jaccard/Spearman/Pearson) provides a multi-faceted view of performance.
3. **Transparent reporting of limitations:** The authors explicitly acknowledge in Section 6.1 and Table 4/9 that stability metrics degrade compared to baselines and that the method struggles with multi-class problems. This candid assessment strengthens academic integrity and clarifies the method's operational boundaries.

### Weaknesses
1. **Overreliance on geometric proxies for interpretability:** The paper heavily weights weighted t-SNE and silhouette scores (Section 4.2.2, Tables 2 & 6) as primary evidence of success. In the interpretability community, these measure low-dimensional cluster geometry, not whether attributions faithfully reflect model reliance. Standard ICLR-expected metrics like faithfulness/comprehensiveness (e.g., deletion/insertion AUC, ROAR, or perturbation-based correlation) are absent.
2. **Stability deficit undermines high-stakes applicability:** The introduction emphasizes healthcare and high-stakes domains, yet Tables 1, 4, and 9 consistently show post-hoc FSL underperforming baselines in Spearman and Pearson stability. The paper frames this as a minor "trade-off," but in safety-critical or regulatory settings, unstable feature rankings across data folds or runs are a significant flaw that warrants deeper analysis or mitigation.
3. **Incremental novelty and lack of theoretical grounding:** Adapting an existing embedded weight layer to a post-hoc fine-tuning regime is primarily an engineering adaptation. The paper lacks theoretical justification for why post-hoc optimization converges to meaningful attributions rather than local optima, and does not analyze the relationship between the learned FSL weights and underlying gradients or Shapley values.
4. **Narrow dataset and model diversity:** Real-world evaluation is dominated by microarray datasets (Table 7), and architectural testing is limited to standard MLPs plus a single brief TabPFN experiment. This restricts claims of broad generalizability across tabular domains and model families.

### Novelty & Significance
**Novelty** is low-to-moderate: the core contribution is a practical adaptation of an existing layer rather than a new algorithmic or theoretical insight. **Clarity** is high; the architecture, training procedure, and experimental protocol are well-defined and easy to follow. **Reproducibility** is strong; the methodology is explicit, datasets and code repositories are cited, and the fine-tuning setup requires standard deep learning libraries. **Significance** is moderate for applied practitioners needing lightweight post-hoc tabular interpretability, but below ICLR's typical threshold due to the reliance on non-standard interpretability metrics, documented instability, and absence of theoretical analysis. The work would serve better as a solid baseline or applied workshop paper than a flagship conference contribution in its current form.

### Suggestions for Improvement
1. Replace or supplement t-SNE/silhouette with standard attribution faithfulness metrics (e.g., insertion/deletion curves, ROAR, AUPC, or sensitivity to irrelevant feature masking) to rigorously prove that learned weights align with actual model behavior.
2. Investigate and mitigate the stability deficit: analyze optimization dynamics (e.g., learning rate sensitivity, loss landscape conditioning), and test simple stabilizers such as exponential moving average ensembling, weight smoothing across CV folds, or auxiliary consistency losses.
3. Broaden empirical validation: evaluate on diverse tabular benchmarks beyond microarray (e.g., OpenML-CC18, AUC/ROC-heavy datasets), test across multiple architectures (gradient-boosted tree surrogates, Transformers, CNNs on structured grids), and include a systematic ablation on regularization strength, initialization scheme, and activation choice.
4. Add theoretical or empirical linkage to established attribution theory: compare post-hoc FSL weight convergence to normalized gradient magnitudes, or provide a convergence analysis explaining under what conditions the frozen-backbone fine-tuning yields globally meaningful vs. locally optimal attributions.

# Spark Finder Review
## How to Improve This Paper

### Missing Experiments (top 3-5 only)
1. Add standard faithfulness/perturbation experiments (e.g., MoRF or deletion-insertion curves) because t-SNE and silhouette scores are unsupervised geometric proxies that cannot verify whether the learned weights actually drive the frozen model’s predictions.
2. Evaluate post-hoc FSL across multiple pre-trained architectures (e.g., varying depths, activations, or ensembles) to substantiate the core claim that the method is broadly architecture-agnostic rather than coincidentally effective on a single unspecified baseline.
3. Run ablations on learning rate, regularization strength ($\lambda$), and training epochs, as post-hoc convergence is highly sensitive to optimization settings and their omission leaves the reported instability and dataset-specific performance drops irreproducible.

### Deeper Analysis Needed (top 3-5 only)
1. Provide gradient flow analysis during post-hoc training to explain how meaningful global attributions emerge from a frozen network, ensuring the weights converge to faithful signal rather than optimization artifacts that artificially improve geometric separation.
2. Diagnose the root cause of consistently lower Spearman/Pearson stability scores to determine whether instability stems from local minima in the post-hoc loss landscape, noisy high-dimensional inputs, or the specific L1 penalty formulation.
3. Quantify the degradation mechanism in multi-class settings by contrasting global FSL weights against class-conditional baselines, rigorously establishing when a single global weight vector is mathematically insufficient for capturing decision boundaries.

### Visualizations & Case Studies
1. Plot learned weight distributions alongside domain-grounded feature annotations (e.g., known biomarkers in microarray data, discriminative word frequencies in spam) to verify the method prioritizes known causal features rather than amplifying spurious dataset correlations.
2. Include training trajectory plots showing weight magnitudes over epochs to demonstrate whether the post-hoc layer robustly converges or exhibits high variance, directly visualizing the stability trade-offs admitted in the results.
3. Present ablation-based counterexamples where top-weighted features are zeroed out in the frozen model, showing the exact drop in predictive accuracy to prove the weights are functionally faithful rather than merely convenient for 2D projection.

### Obvious Next Steps
1. Implement and benchmark a class-conditioned post-hoc FSL variant (e.g., parallel weight layers per class or logit-weighted aggregation) to resolve the multi-class limitation instead of deferring it to future work.
2. Compare computational cost (FLOPs, memory, wall-clock time) against gradient-based and perturbation baselines, as iterative post-hoc fine-tuning may negate the claimed "lightweight" advantage critical for practical deployment.
3. Replace reliance on weighted t-SNE/silhouette as primary evidence with standardized interpretability benchmarks (e.g., faithfulness AUC, parameter-randomization sanity checks) to meet ICLR's rigorous expectations for attribution methodology.

# Final Consolidated Review
## Summary
This paper introduces a post-hoc adaptation of the Feature Selection Layer (FSL) that attaches a single trainable weight per input feature to a frozen pre-trained neural network, enabling non-invasive global feature attribution for tabular data. Evaluated on synthetic and high-dimensional real-world datasets against established gradient-based and perturbation baselines, the method maintains baseline predictive performance and improves geometric cluster separation, but exhibits notable attribution instability and degrades on multi-class tasks.

## Strengths
- **Practical, non-invasive architecture:** By freezing the backbone and training only a lightweight linear scaling layer, the method directly addresses the real-world constraint of interpreting already-deployed models without expensive retraining or architectural modification.
- **Transparent empirical reporting across challenging regimes:** The evaluation spans controlled synthetic data and HDLSS biological datasets, and the authors openly report stability degradation and multi-class failure modes, which clarifies the operational boundaries of the approach.

## Weaknesses
- **Critical ambiguity in the training objective undermines the interpretability claim:** Section 3.2 never specifies the loss function $\ell$, optimizer, learning rate, or early stopping criteria. More importantly, it is entirely unclear whether fine-tuning uses ground-truth labels (supervised) or the frozen model's original predictions (self-distillation). If supervised, the method learns a new input adapter rather than explaining the frozen network's intrinsic reliance, fundamentally breaking the "post-hoc explanation" framing expected at ICLR.
- **Overreliance on geometric clustering metrics as proxies for attribution faithfulness:** The paper's primary evidence for interpretability rests on weighted t-SNE and silhouette scores. These measure low-dimensional, unsupervised geometry, not whether the learned weights actually drive the model's decisions. The absence of standard faithfulness and sensitivity evaluations (e.g., deletion/insertion curves, ROAR, MoRF, or parameter-randomization sanity checks) leaves the core claim of enhanced interpretability empirically unverified.
- **Severe attribution instability without mechanistic analysis:** Stability metrics (Spearman, Pearson, Jaccard) consistently trail established post-hoc baselines across folds and datasets (Tables 1, 4, 9). While acknowledged as a limitation, this deficit is severely downplayed given the paper's motivation in high-stakes healthcare applications, where inconsistent feature rankings across minor data splits are a critical safety flaw rather than a minor trade-off.
- **Unaddressed input distribution shift breaking frozen architectures:** Multiplying input features by learned weights $\vec{w}$ fundamentally alters the input distribution fed to the frozen network. If the backbone employs Batch Normalization or uses activation functions with scale-sensitive thresholds, the fixed internal statistics will misprocess the weighted inputs. The paper never analyzes, controls for, or mitigates this distribution shift, yet the baseline often outperforms the FSL variants on predictive metrics (Table 5), suggesting this mismatch may actively corrupt both predictions and derived attributions.

## Nice-to-Haves
- Explicitly report the complete fine-tuning hyperparameters (optimizer, LR schedule, batch size, $\lambda$ scheduling, epochs) and provide the open-source training script to guarantee reproducibility.
- Clarify how local baseline attributions (e.g., Integrated Gradients, SHAP) were aggregated into global weight vectors for fair comparison, detailing any normalization or averaging strategy.
- Report computational complexity (wall-clock time, FLOPs, memory overhead) against iterative perturbation and gradient-based methods to concretely substantiate the "lightweight" deployment claim.

## Novel Insights
The paper inadvertently highlights a critical disconnect in tabular interpretability: the divergence between geometric feature separability and true model reliance. While post-hoc FSL successfully scales inputs to tighten cluster boundaries in non-linear projections, this geometric improvement does not reliably translate to stable, globally consistent feature rankings. This suggests that fine-tuning a linear scaling probe on a frozen landscape may optimize for surface-level alignment with the data manifold rather than capturing the frozen model's actual decision logic—a behavior that systematically degrades as class counts and dimensionality increase, exposing a fundamental limitation of single-vector global attribution methods in complex domains.

## Potentially Missed Related Work
- *Adebayo et al. (NeurIPS 2018) "Sanity Checks for Saliency Maps" — highly relevant for establishing baseline faithfulness checks to verify whether the learned weights respond to model parameters or merely to data distribution.*
- *Petsiuk et al. (BMVC 2018) & Hooker et al. (NeurIPS 2019) "Robustness/ROAR" frameworks — provide standardized perturbation-based faithfulness metrics directly applicable to tabular feature scalers, offering a rigorous alternative to t-SNE geometry.*

## Suggestions
1. Explicitly define the loss function and optimization pipeline. If the method is supervised, reframe the contribution as a lightweight input adapter for deployed models rather than a model-agnostic explainer; if unsupervised/distillation-based, detail how the frozen model's logits drive the gradient flow.
2. Replace or supplement weighted t-SNE/silhouette evaluations with standardized faithfulness and sensitivity benchmarks (e.g., insertion/deletion AUC, ROAR, feature masking experiments) to rigorously prove that learned weights correspond to actual model reliance rather than geometric coincidence.
3. Audit the frozen backbone for Batch Normalization or scale-sensitive components. Either retrain the baseline without BN, apply appropriate feature rescaling post-hoc, or empirically demonstrate that attribution stability and predictive performance are invariant to input distribution shifts.
4. Diagnose the optimization dynamics behind the observed instability: visualize weight magnitude trajectories over epochs, test consistency regularization or ensemble averaging during fine-tuning, and formally contrast global FSL weights against class-conditional baselines to establish the mathematical boundaries of single-vector attribution in multi-class settings.
