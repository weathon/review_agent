# Harsh Critic Review
## Section-by-Section Critical Review

### Title & Abstract
- **Title Accuracy:** The title accurately reflects the proposed method and its scope (post-hoc adaptation for tabular data).
- **Abstract Clarity:** The abstract clearly outlines the problem (interpretability of frozen models), the method (post-hoc FSL), and the evaluation setup. However, it conflates several distinct claims. 
- **Unsupported Claims:** The statement that post-hoc FSL "successfully identified relevant features across the different datasets" is partially contradicted by the paper's own results on SynthA (Section 5.1), where it achieves lower PIFS/PSFI (0.90 vs FSL's 1.0) and substantially worse stability (Table 4). The claim of "distinct advantages over other state-of-the-art methods" is heavily caveated by the paper; these advantages are primarily limited to silhouette scores on t-SNE projections, which are not standard or robust proxies for attribution quality. The abstract should be tempered to acknowledge the notable stability degradation and dataset-dependent performance drops.

### Introduction & Motivation
- **Motivation & Gap:** The motivation for post-hoc interpretability in high-stakes domains is well-established and the paper correctly identifies that embedded FSL requires joint training from scratch, making it incompatible with pre-trained models. This is a valid gap.
- **Contributions:** Clearly listed: architectural adaptation, training procedure, and empirical evaluation across synthetic/real datasets. 
- **Over/Under-claiming:** The introduction appropriately scopes the work to tabular data and does not overclaim theoretical novelty. However, it frames post-hoc feature weighting as a largely solved problem gap-wise, when in reality, fine-tuning lightweight adapters for interpretability is a known pattern (e.g., learnable input masks, LINSPECT-style adapters). The introduction should more explicitly position this diagonal scaling layer against related "adapter-based" or "feature mask" attribution literature.

### Method / Approach
- **Clarity & Reproducibility:** The architecture is straightforward. However, Section 3.2 (Step 2) states that "the final output is used to compute the loss function of the whole model" without specifying the loss target. Is the training objective minimizing $\mathcal{L}(f_{\text{pre}}(x'), y)$ against ground truth labels, or is it a distillation loss like $\mathcal{L}(f_{\text{pre}}(x'), f_{\text{pre}}(x))$ to match the original model's outputs? The abstract implies matching the original model's outputs, but the experimental section reports predictive metrics against $y$. This is a critical ambiguity: training on $y$ transforms this into a fine-tuned adapter, not purely a post-hoc explainer.
- **Assumptions & Justification:** The method assumes that scaling input features via a diagonal weight matrix and frozen parameters will meaningfully reveal feature importance without causing catastrophic input distribution shift. This is not justified. The frozen model was trained on the distribution $P(X)$; applying learned weights $w \odot X$ shifts the input distribution to $P(w \odot X)$. The paper does not discuss how the frozen layers handle this covariate shift, nor does it analyze whether the frozen representations remain calibrated or stable under such transformations.
- **Logical Gaps & Edge Cases:** Section 2.2 notes that the original FSL uses a *modified* L1 regularization to capture feature interactions (Eq 1), but Section 3.3 states the post-hoc version adopts the *standard* L1. The rationale for this design change is missing. Standard L1 applied post-hoc cannot capture the intended inter-feature penalty from the original work, fundamentally changing the optimization dynamics. Furthermore, ReLU on weights forces non-negativity, which ignores potentially suppressive feature effects (antagonistic interactions), a known limitation in attribution that is not discussed.

### Experiments & Results
- **Testing Claims:** Experiments cover predictive performance, synthetic ground-truth matching, visual separability, and stability. However, the reliance on visual metrics as primary evidence of interpretability is problematic.
- **Baselines:** Standard gradient-based and perturbation baselines are included. However, Kernel SHAP is excluded from the main comparison tables (only briefly mentioned in Section 5.1 for TabPFN) despite being a standard reference. Missing comparisons to other input-masking or feature-selection baselines (e.g., LIME, SHAP variants, or recent tabular attribution methods) weakens the claim of "state-of-the-art" distinct advantages.
- **Missing Ablations:** There are no ablation studies for the FSL initialization ($1.0$ vs $1/n$), the choice of ReLU activation vs. sigmoid/tanh, or the L1 regularization coefficient $\lambda$. Given the paper argues $1.0$ initialization improves training dynamics (Section 3.3), empirical validation of this claim is necessary.
- **Statistical Significance & Error Bars:** Standard deviations are reported, and Kruskal-Wallis/Dunn tests are mentioned. However, the main claims of superiority (silhouette scores) often show marginal absolute differences (e.g., Table 6: Post-hoc FSL 0.138 vs FSL 0.133 on Spam). While marked as statistically significant due to low variance, these tiny improvements are practically negligible and heavily dependent on t-SNE hyperparameters (not reported).
- **Dataset & Metric Appropriateness:** The heavy reliance on weighted t-SNE and silhouette coefficients as the primary measure of feature attribution quality is a significant weakness. t-SNE is highly sensitive to perplexity, learning rates, and random initialization. High silhouette in a 2D projection does not equate to accurate feature identification; it can simply reflect downweighting noise enough to create artificial low-dimensional separation, regardless of biological or semantic correctness. On SynthA, where ground truth is known, post-hoc FSL's Pearson stability (0.770) lags far behind IG/DeepLIFT/G-SHAP (>0.970). This contradicts the abstract's implication of consistent superior performance and raises reliability concerns for the method's weighting scheme.

### Writing & Clarity
- **Confusing Sections:** The training objective definition in Section 3.2 is ambiguous (loss target unspecified). Equation 3 defines the forward pass $\vec{y} = f_{\text{pre}}(\vec{x}')$, but the loss $\ell$ is never explicitly tied to $\vec{y}_{\text{true}}$ or a teacher model's outputs. This must be formalized with a clear loss equation.
- **Figures & Tables:** The t-SNE visualizations are frequently cited as evidence of "meaningful feature attributions," but t-SNE hyperparameters (perplexity, iterations, early exaggeration, random seed) are never specified. Since weighted t-SNE is central to the paper's argument (Section 4.2.2), omitting these parameters renders the visual analysis irreproducible and its quantitative metric (silhouette score) fragile. The tables are structurally clear, but the statistical significance markers (orange text referenced in captions) are not visible in standard parsing; the text should explicitly state which comparisons are significant rather than relying on color-coding.

### Limitations & Broader Impact
- **Acknowledged Limitations:** The authors correctly identify poor multi-class performance and tabular-specific design. They also acknowledge stability metrics (Jaccard/Spearman/Pearson) are lower.
- **Missed Limitations:** 
  1. **Distribution Shift:** As noted in the Method section, the paper fails to address the covariate shift introduced by $w \odot X$ on a frozen network, which could invalidate the attribution if the frozen layers extrapolate poorly.
  2. **Negative Feature Effects:** The ReLU constraint forces all weights to be positive, preventing the model from learning that certain features are actively detrimental to a prediction, limiting attribution fidelity.
  3. **Metric Fragility:** The reliance on silhouette scores on t-SNE as a proxy for interpretability is not robustly validated. In high-stakes healthcare (Section 1), using an unstable or artifact-driven metric to claim "enhanced interpretability" carries real risk.
- **Societal Impact:** The paper mentions high-stakes healthcare but does not discuss how the observed instability in feature rankings (Table 9, Spearman 0.312 for Post-hoc FSL) could lead to inconsistent clinical explanations across different training runs. If doctors are shown varying feature weights for the same patient sample, trust in the system collapses. This ethical implication of the method's variance should be explicitly addressed.

### Overall Assessment
This paper presents a straightforward post-hoc adaptation of the Feature Selection Layer for tabular data by freezing a pre-trained model and optimizing a diagonal weight layer to scale inputs. While the motivation for post-hoc attribution in deployed systems is clear, the contribution is incremental and faces significant empirical and methodological concerns that fall short of the ICLR acceptance bar. The most critical issues are: (1) ambiguity in the training objective (fine-tuning on labels vs. distilling model outputs), (2) unaddressed input distribution shift when scaling inputs to frozen layers, and (3) over-reliance on t-SNE silhouette scores as a primary proxy for attribution quality without validating robustness to visualization hyperparameters or correlating with stronger attribution benchmarks. Experimental results show notable instability (Table 4, 9) and performance drops on synthetic data where ground truth is known, undermining claims of distinct advantages. To reach ICLR standards, the authors must clarify the mathematical formulation, conduct ablations on hyperparameters/initialization, replace or heavily supplement t-SNE metrics with standard attribution benchmarks (e.g., faithfulness/perturbation curves, ground-truth correlation on synthetic), and thoroughly analyze the stability implications for high-stakes use cases.

# Neutral Reviewer
## Balanced Review

### Summary
This paper proposes a post-hoc adaptation of the Feature Selection Layer (FSL), enabling feature relevance attribution for pre-trained, frozen neural networks on tabular data. By optimizing only the linear scaling weights of the FSL while keeping the base model's parameters fixed, the method aims to identify salient features without requiring architectural changes or full model retraining. The authors evaluate the approach against the original embedded FSL and several gradient/perturbation-based baselines across synthetic and high-dimensional real-world datasets, reporting on predictive metrics, visual cluster separation, and attribution stability.

### Strengths
1. **Practical Motivation & Lightweight Design:** The method directly addresses a realistic deployment constraint: interpreting already-trained or "black-box" models without access to training data or the ability to modify the backbone. The post-hoc FSL is computationally efficient and requires training only `d` parameters (where `d` is feature dimension), making it highly scalable for tabular data (Sections 3.1-3.2).
2. **Comprehensive Empirical Scope:** The evaluation spans controlled synthetic datasets (XOR, SynthA) with known ground truth, challenging high-dimensional/low-sample-size microbiome data, and a modern tabular foundation model (TabPFN). This demonstrates thoughtful dataset curation for stress-testing the method across different difficulty regimes (Section 4.1).
3. **Transparent Reporting & Reproducibility Commitment:** The authors openly report where their method underperforms, notably on stability metrics and multi-class tasks, and clearly state limitations (Section 6.1). The reproducibility statement provides links to code, dependencies, and dataset sources, aligning with open-science expectations.

### Weaknesses
1. **Incremental Methodological Novelty:** Repurposing an embedded linear scaling layer into a post-hoc fine-tuning probe is conceptually similar to standard linear probing or weight masking. The paper lacks theoretical analysis explaining *why* optimizing this layer yields faithful attributions for a highly non-linear frozen backbone, raising questions about whether the weights capture true feature importance or merely surface-level input-output correlations.
2. **Reliance on Non-Standard Interpretability Metrics:** The primary evidence for interpretability relies heavily on weighted t-SNE and silhouette scores, which measure geometric cluster separation in a 2D projection rather than faithfulness to the model's decision function. Standard interpretability metrics (e.g., insertion/deletion AUC, faithfulness tests, sparsification curves, or correlation with ground-truth attributions) are absent, making it difficult to validate claims of superior interpretability against SHAP/IG (Sections 4.2.2, 5).
3. **Consistently Inferior Stability & Multi-Class Limitations:** Across Tables 4 and 9, post-hoc FSL consistently exhibits lower Jaccard, Spearman, and Pearson stability scores compared to established post-hoc methods. Furthermore, the method struggles on the 6-class Breast dataset due to its global weight formulation, explicitly failing to capture class-local feature relevance (Sections 5.2, 6.1). For ICLR, poor stability and multi-class generalizability are significant concerns for any attribution method.
4. **Unclear Baseline Implementation & Evaluation Mixing:** The paper compares post-hoc attribution accuracy with predictive metrics (Accuracy/F1) that conflate attribution quality with model capacity. It is also unclear whether baseline methods (IG, SHAP, etc.) had their hyperparameters (e.g., baseline references, noise levels, number of Monte Carlo samples) rigorously tuned per dataset, which could skew comparative results.

### Novelty & Significance
**Novelty:** Low-to-moderate. The contribution is an engineering adaptation of an existing embedded layer rather than a new algorithmic or theoretical framework. The post-hoc training procedure is straightforward fine-tuning of a diagonal weight matrix. **Clarity:** Good. The architecture, training steps, and experimental pipeline are well-documented and easy to follow. **Reproducibility:** High. Code, dataset links, and detailed metric formulations are provided, with explicit instructions for replication. **Significance:** Niche but practically useful for specific tabular deployment scenarios where model weights are frozen. However, without stronger theoretical justification or demonstration of superiority on established interpretability benchmarks, the broader impact for the deep learning/interpretability community at ICLR is limited. The work is better suited for an applied ML or domain-specific venue unless substantially strengthened methodologically and empirically.

### Suggestions for Improvement
1. **Adopt Standard Interpretability Benchmarks:** Replace or supplement t-SNE/silhouette analysis with established attribution metrics such as Faithfulness/Insertion-Deletion AUC, ROAR, or correlation with ground-truth weights on synthetic data. This will provide direct evidence of attribution quality and align the evaluation with ICLR/NeurIPS interpretability standards.
2. **Provide Theoretical/Analytical Grounding:** Analyze the optimization dynamics of the frozen-backbone setup. Is the post-hoc FSL learning a linear Taylor approximation of the model's input-output mapping? Include a discussion or ablation on conditions under which the method succeeds/fails (e.g., when the frozen model relies on high-order feature interactions that a linear layer cannot capture).
3. **Address the Multi-Class Limitation Empirically:** Propose and evaluate a straightforward extension, such as training separate class-conditional FSL probes (one per output node) or using a softmax-weighted aggregation of class-specific attributions. Compare this against the current global-weight formulation to demonstrate improved multi-class stability and interpretability.
4. **Clarify Baseline Tuning & Separate Predictive from Attribution Evaluation:** Explicitly document hyperparameter selection for all post-hoc baselines (e.g., number of steps, reference distributions). Separate predictive metrics (which measure model performance) from attribution metrics (which measure explanation quality), and clarify if post-hoc FSL weights can be used for actual downstream feature selection without degrading the frozen model's accuracy.

# Spark Finder Review
## How to Improve This Paper

### Missing Experiments (top 3-5 only)
1. Add direct comparisons against Input×Gradient or Gradient Magnitude attribution. Since post-hoc FSL optimizes diagonal scaling weights via backpropagation through a frozen network, its update signals are mathematically proportional to first-order gradient attributions; without this baseline, the claim of superior interpretability over existing gradient methods is unsupported.
2. Evaluate true feature selection utility by training a fresh classifier on only the top-k features selected by post-hoc FSL on real-world datasets. The paper currently relies on PIFS/PSFI only for synthetic data; without demonstrating predictive performance using the selected subset on real data, the core claim that the layer performs actual feature selection, rather than generating continuous attributions, remains unproven.
3. Conduct a sensitivity analysis sweeping the L1 regularization parameter ($\lambda$) and learning rate to characterize the stability-performance trade-off. Post-hoc FSL consistently yields lower Jaccard and Spearman stability than baselines across all tables; experiments varying $\lambda$ are needed to show whether stability can be meaningfully improved without collapsing silhouette scores or predictive metrics.

### Deeper Analysis Needed (top 3-5 only)
1. Provide a mathematical derivation linking the post-hoc FSL optimization objective to iterative gradient accumulation or feature masking frameworks. If the method reduces to regularized iterative Gradient×Input, the paper must explicitly analyze what novel signal the optimization loop extracts and how convergence differs from single-pass gradient methods to preserve the novelty claim.
2. Supplement weighted t-SNE and silhouette scores with established interpretability faithfulness metrics such as Insertion/Deletion AUC or fidelity drop. Silhouette scores measure global cluster separability in feature space, not faithfulness to the frozen model's decision boundary, meaning high scores could merely reflect variance reduction rather than accurate attribution of the model's internal logic.
3. Analyze weight variance and attribution conflict in multi-class settings to explain the performance degradation on the Breast dataset. The paper notes the method fails to capture class-specific relationships, but a quantitative breakdown of weight disagreement across classes or folds is required to determine if the global weighting scheme is fundamentally ill-suited for complex multi-class distributions.

### Visualizations & Case Studies
1. Plot the empirical distribution of learned weights (e.g., histograms or violin plots) across cross-validation folds for real-world datasets. Visual proof of clear bimodality, distinguishing between pruned features (weights near zero) and kept features (significant positive weights), is essential to validate that the layer performs discrete feature selection rather than uniform smooth scaling.
2. Include a domain-grounded case study mapping top-ranked features to known ground truth variables (e.g., verified biomarkers for microarray data or definitive spam indicators). Without demonstrating that high weights align with scientifically validated critical features, silhouette improvements alone cannot convince practitioners that the method captures meaningful signal.
3. Provide side-by-side attribution comparisons for specific samples where post-hoc FSL heavily upweights features that SHAP or DeepLIFT correctly downweight. Exposing these specific failure modes clarifies systematic errors, such as over-attributing importance to correlated but non-causal noise, which aggregate visual metrics like t-SNE obscure.

### Obvious Next Steps
1. Benchmark computational efficiency (FLOPs and wall-clock time) against single-pass post-hoc baselines. Since post-hoc FSL requires iterative gradient descent over multiple epochs to converge, it is likely orders of magnitude more expensive than SHAP or IG; quantifying this overhead is mandatory to assess whether the interpretability gains justify the inference-time cost for deployed systems.
2. Validate the method on externally sourced, pre-trained architectures (e.g., TabNet, public tabular benchmarks, or tree-boosting proxies) rather than only author-trained models. Testing solely on models trained by the same pipeline introduces risks of data leakage or favorable hyperparameter alignment; evaluation on truly frozen, off-the-shelf models is needed to prove robust post-hoc applicability.
3. Provide complete training configurations, including hidden layer sizes, activation choices, optimization settings for the frozen models, and exact cross-validation split indices. ICLR requires rigorous reproducibility; the absence of these details makes it impossible to verify that improvements stem from the post-hoc FSL rather than baseline misconfiguration or split variability.

# Final Consolidated Review
## Summary
This paper adapts the embedded Feature Selection Layer (FSL) into a post-hoc attribution method for frozen tabular neural networks. By inserting a trainable diagonal scaling layer between the input and a pre-trained model, the approach learns feature relevance weights via optimization while keeping the backbone parameters fixed. The method is evaluated on synthetic and high-dimensional real-world datasets, comparing predictive performance, geometric cluster separability, and attribution stability against embedded FSL and standard gradient/perturbation baselines.

## Strengths
- **Addresses a practical deployment constraint with a lightweight design:** The post-hoc FSL directly targets a realistic scenario where interpreting a deployed, frozen model is required without architectural modification or full retraining. Optimizing exactly `d` parameters makes it computationally efficient and trivially integrable with existing tabular pipelines.
- **Thoughtful evaluation across stress-test regimes:** The authors evaluate the method on controlled synthetic data with known ground truth, extremely high-dimensional/low-sample microarray data, and a modern foundation model (TabPFN). This demonstrates systematic dataset curation aimed at probing the method's limits rather than just cherry-picking favorable benchmarks.
- **Transparent reporting and geometric isolation of signal:** Despite acknowledging limitations, the paper honestly reports where the method underperforms (e.g., stability metrics, multi-class tasks). On high-dimensional real-world datasets, the learned weights consistently improve weighted t-SNE silhouette scores over gradient and perturbation baselines, suggesting the method effectively downweights noise to produce cleaner low-dimensional feature manifolds.

## Weaknesses
- **Primary interpretability claims rely on non-standard, geometric metrics rather than faithfulness to the model:** The paper uses weighted t-SNE silhouette scores as the central evidence for superior interpretability. This metric measures 2D cluster compactness, not faithfulness to the frozen model's actual decision function or predictive reliance. Standard attribution benchmarks (e.g., Insertion/Deletion AUC, fidelity drop under feature masking, or correlation with ground-truth feature importance) are entirely absent. Without these, it is unclear whether the learned weights accurately reflect the model's reasoning or merely produce a smoother input variance structure that benefits projection algorithms.
- **Consistently inferior attribution stability with insufficient analysis or mitigation:** Across synthetic and real-world datasets, post-hoc FSL exhibits substantially lower Spearman, Jaccard, and Pearson stability compared to established post-hoc methods (e.g., Tables 4 and 9). The authors acknowledge this in Section 6.1 but do not investigate whether the observed variance stems from optimization sensitivity, insufficient regularization, or data-specific noise. For a method positioned for high-stakes domains, unstable feature rankings across minor data perturbations severely undermine practical reliability.
- **Ambiguous training objective and unvalidated design choices:** The abstract states weights are fine-tuned "based on the original model’s learned outputs," implying a self-distillation or teacher-student objective. However, the experimental results report standard classification metrics against ground-truth labels $y$, suggesting supervised fine-tuning. If trained on $y$, the layer functions as a lightweight supervised adapter rather than a pure post-hoc explainer of the frozen model's intrinsic behavior. Additionally, key architectural decisions (initializing weights to $1.0$ instead of $1/n$, switching from the original FSL's modified L1 to standard L1, and enforcing ReLU non-negativity) lack empirical ablation or justification, leaving their impact on convergence and attribution fidelity opaque.

## Nice-to-Haves
- Compare against Input$\times$Gradient or gradient magnitude baselines, as the optimization dynamics of the diagonal scaling layer may mathematically approximate iterative gradient accumulation. Clarifying this relationship would sharpen the novelty claim.
- Evaluate computational overhead (training epochs, wall-clock time, FLOPs) relative to single-pass attribution methods. Iterative gradient descent will likely be costlier than SHAP/IG; quantifying this is essential for deployment feasibility.
- Provide weight distribution visualizations (e.g., histograms or violin plots across CV folds) and a domain-grounded case study mapping top weights to verified biomarkers or known spam indicators to validate real-world semantic alignment.
- Report t-SNE hyperparameters and explicitly document baseline attribution settings (reference inputs, number of Monte Carlo steps, perturbation magnitudes) to ensure full reproducibility of the geometric fidelity claims.

## Novel Insights
The paper reveals a practical tension in tabular interpretability: optimizing a simple diagonal scaling layer against a frozen backbone reliably improves geometric cluster separability in high-dimensional regimes, yet consistently suffers from attribution instability compared to single-pass gradient/perturbation methods. This divergence suggests that the post-hoc FSL's strength may lie less in exact feature recovery or strict faithfulness to the original model's decision boundary, and more in learning a stable, sparsified input manifold that enhances visualization and downstream feature filtering. The results implicitly highlight how regularization-driven weight optimization and iterative signal accumulation differ from axiomatic attribution, pointing to a promising but underexplored middle ground between supervised feature selection and model-agnostic explanation.

## Suggestions
- Explicitly formalize the loss function in Section 3.2, specifying whether optimization targets ground-truth labels $\mathcal{L}(f_{\text{pre}}(\vec{x}'), y)$ or distills the frozen model's outputs $\mathcal{L}(f_{\text{pre}}(\vec{x}'), f_{\text{pre}}(\vec{x}))$. Adjust the methodological narrative and positioning accordingly to avoid conflating supervised adapter fine-tuning with post-hoc attribution.
- Introduce at least one standard faithfulness or perturbation-based metric (e.g., Insertion/Deletion AUC, ROAR, or sparsification curves) alongside silhouette scores. This will directly validate whether high-weight features are genuinely relied upon by the frozen model for predictions.
- Conduct ablation studies on the L1 regularization coefficient $\lambda$, weight initialization scheme ($1.0$ vs $1/n$), and activation constraint (ReLU vs unconstrained), demonstrating how each choice impacts the observed stability-performance trade-off and whether stability can be meaningfully recovered without sacrificing geometric separability.
