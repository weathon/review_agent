# Harsh Critic Review
## Section-by-Section Critical Review

### Title & Abstract
- **Accuracy of title:** The title accurately reflects the core contribution: a grounding-focused dataset curated from human demonstrations and resulting models. It aligns with the paper's scope.
- **Clarity of abstract:** The abstract clearly states the problem (lack of high-quality desktop grounding data), the method (GROUNDCUA dataset construction + GROUNDNEXT SFT/RL pipeline), and key results (SOTA on 5 benchmarks with ~1/10th the data, RL gains, strong agentic performance with o3 planner).
- **Unsupported claims:** The claim that RL post-training "further improves performance" is technically true but slightly overstates the magnitude relative to SFT (e.g., 3B Avg moves from 66.4 to 68.4 in Table 3). The abstract also emphasizes training efficiency, but does not clarify that the 700K instruction-tuning pairs required significant human curation and 72B-parameter MLLM prompting to synthesize. This isn't unsupported, but the abstract frames the contribution purely around data *quantity* vs. quality, leaving cost implicit. A minor clarifying sentence would strengthen alignment.

### Introduction & Motivation
- **Problem motivation & gap identification:** Well-motivated. The introduction correctly identifies desktop grounding as a critical bottleneck for CUAs and clearly articulates the gap in prior datasets: over-reliance on automated DOM/accessibility extraction (missing dense iconography) or synthetic generation (lacking real-world UI complexity). The comparison to desktop-specific challenges (high density, small bounding boxes, diverse applications) is precise and grounded in literature.
- **Contributions:** Clearly stated and accurate. The three contributions (dataset, model family, analysis/open release) map directly to Sections 3–5 and the Appendix.
- **Over/under-claiming:** The introduction does not significantly over-claim. The thesis that "high-quality, densely grounded supervision and targeted instruction design can substitute for raw scale" is directly tested empirically. However, the intro lightly glosses over the computational and human labor required to achieve this "quality" compared to fully automated pipelines. Acknowledging this trade-off earlier would provide a more complete framing for ICLR reviewers.

### Method / Approach
- **Clarity & reproducibility:** The data pipeline (app selection → human demos → keyframe extraction → dense annotation → MLLM instruction generation) is logically laid out. Training details (Qwen2.5-VL base, SFT hyperparameters, RLOO RL setup, batch sizes) are provided in Section 4 and Appendix C. The methodology is highly reproducible; the authors commit to releasing data, weights, and code.
- **Key assumptions:** The method assumes that human demonstration keyframes capture realistic state distributions better than breadth/depth search, and that a 72B MLLM can reliably generate grounded instructions given dense visual/textual context (supported by the 4% human error rate in App B.6). The assumption that a normalized distance reward can effectively guide RL for coordinate prediction is empirically validated, but the discrete binning introduces quantization effects not fully analyzed theoretically.
- **Logical gaps/reasoning:** The reward formulation (Section 4.1) relies on $D_{ref}$ adapting based on whether $\hat{p} \in B$. The parser garbled the equation slightly, but the textual explanation clarifies it. A minor logical gap: the reward penalizes misses based on normalized distance to the *closest point* in $B$, but does not account for element salience or hierarchical UI importance (e.g., missing a primary "Submit" button vs. a decorative divider). The authors justify their simplified scheme empirically, but this design choice could bias the model toward geometric precision over functional correctness.
- **Edge cases/failure modes:** The paper addresses near-misses and spatial reasoning limits (Section E). However, it does not discuss how the pipeline handles overlapping/occluded bounding boxes, highly dynamic UIs (pop-ups, animations), or multi-monitor setups, which are common in desktop environments. The static keyframe approach inherently struggles with temporal continuity, which should be noted as a methodological constraint beyond the general limitations section.
- **Theoretical claims:** No formal theoretical claims are made. The empirical derivations (reward design, instruction type impact) are sound and well-ablated.

### Experiments & Results
- **Testing claims:** Experiments directly test the core claims. The dataset quality thesis is validated via controlled 100k sub-sampling across datasets (Figure 3) and Table 17 RL ablation. The SFT/RL scaling claims are supported by Tables 2, 3, and 18. Cross-domain generalization is tested on SSv2 and MMBench-GUI. The experimental design aligns tightly with the narrative.
- **Baselines:** Comprehensive and fair. SFT-only and RL-tuned baselines across 3B/7B scales are included. Comparisons use established benchmarks. One concern is the agentic evaluation (Table 4): pairing GROUNDNEXT with OpenAI o3 as a planner isolates grounding well, but comparing against models like OpenCUA-72B or Claude-4.5-Sonnet conflates planner capability with execution capability. The paper notes JEDI-7B uses o3, which helps, but a clearer discussion of the attribution of error to the planner vs. the grounding model would strengthen this comparison.
- **Missing ablations:** Strong ablations on instruction types, reward granularity, group size, and RLOO vs. GRPO are present. A notable missing ablation is the *cost-efficiency* trade-off: comparing human-curated GROUNDCUA instructions against purely heuristic/automated instructions at equal scale would more sharply isolate the value of human-in-the-loop curation.
- **Error bars / statistical significance:** Not reported. Given the deterministic nature of VLM evaluation on static benchmarks, this is common, but ICLR standards increasingly favor multi-seed runs or variance reporting to rule out lucky splits, especially for the 10k RL fine-tuning step.
- **Cherry-picking / result support:** Results appear consistent across desktop, mobile, web, and agentic splits. No obvious cherry-picking. The modest RL gains (Section 5.2) are transparently reported and honestly analyzed.
- **Datasets & metrics:** Benchmarks (ScreenSpotPro, OSWorld-G, UI-Vision, SSv2, MMBench-GUI) are state-of-the-art and appropriate. Accuracy within bounding box is standard. Metrics and evaluation protocols are clearly defined.

### Writing & Clarity
- **Confusing sections:** Section 4.1's reward equation contains parser-induced formatting issues, but the accompanying text sufficiently explains the piecewise discrete reward. The instruction generation pipeline (Section 3) is clear, though exact prompt templates are relegated to the appendix; this is acceptable but could be streamlined.
- **Figures & tables:** Tables 2–4 effectively summarize SOTAs and agentic performance. Table 11-15 provide granular breakdowns but are dense; summarizing key trends in the main text would improve readability. Figure 3 is heavily garbled by the extractor, but the caption and text describe it adequately. Overall, visualizations and tables are informative and well-chosen.

### Limitations & Broader Impact
- **Acknowledged limitations:** Section F correctly identifies key constraints: bias toward common open-source apps, static keyframes missing dynamics/temporal elements, human annotation cost/consistency, and lack of end-to-end task completion testing. The ethics statement covers PII, licensing, and annotator compensation appropriately.
- **Missed fundamental limitations:** The paper acknowledges modest RL gains but attributes them primarily to a "strong SFT ceiling." A deeper limitation worth discussing is that *grounding is fundamentally a perceptual/localization task rather than a reasoning-heavy policy*, which mathematically limits the effectiveness of RL compared to algorithmic or reasoning tasks. The reward signal saturates quickly once the model learns basic spatial priors, making extensive RL fine-tuning inherently inefficient for this specific sub-problem. This insight would contextualize the RL findings more fundamentally.
- **Failure modes & societal impact:** The paper notes potential misuse in the ethics statement but does not deeply analyze failure cascades in agentic settings (e.g., grounding a "Delete" command instead of "Save" due to spatial similarity). Given the real-world deployment focus, a brief discussion on safety-critical grounding failures and mitigation (e.g., action confirmation layers in CUAs) would strengthen the broader impact section.

### Overall Assessment
This is a strong, well-executed paper that directly addresses a critical bottleneck in computer-use agent research: high-quality desktop grounding data. The introduction of GROUNDCUA, with its dense, human-verified annotations across 87 applications, represents a meaningful contribution to the community and is likely to become a standard benchmark for desktop GUI grounding. The GROUNDNEXT models are trained efficiently and achieve state-of-the-art results with substantially less data than prior work, convincingly supporting the paper's core thesis. ICLR reviewers will appreciate the thorough ablation studies, transparent reporting of modest RL gains, and commitment to open-sourcing all artifacts. The main areas requiring clarification or deeper analysis are: (1) disentangling planner capability vs. grounding performance in the agentic evaluation, (2) a more fundamental discussion on why RL plateaus for perceptual grounding tasks (beyond just "strong SFT ceilings"), and (3) addressing how static keyframe annotations limit applicability to dynamic/temporal desktop workflows. These are addressable and do not undermine the paper's core contributions. The work meets ICLR's bar for novelty, empirical rigor, and community impact.

# Neutral Reviewer
## Balanced Review

### Summary
The paper introduces **GROUNDCUA**, a large-scale desktop UI grounding dataset featuring over 3.56M human-verified annotations across 56K screenshots and 87 applications, alongside the **GROUNDNEXT** family of 3B and 7B vision-language models. By leveraging a curated 700K instruction-tuning set followed by lightweight RL post-training with a discrete distance-based reward, the models achieve state-of-the-art grounding accuracy across five benchmarks. The work empirically demonstrates that high-quality, densely annotated human data can substantially outperform prior methods trained on orders of magnitude more data, offering an efficient pathway for reliable computer-use agents.

### Strengths
1. **High-Quality, Dense Dataset Construction:** GROUNDCUA addresses a clear gap in desktop UI data by replacing automated accessibility-tree parsing and synthetic generation with expert human annotations. The density (avg. 64 elements/screenshot vs. ~7-12 in prior datasets) and small average element area (0.13%) directly tackle the fine-grained localization bottleneck in complex desktop interfaces. (Section 3, Table 1)
2. **Strong Data Efficiency & Empirical Performance:** The authors convincingly show that 700K carefully curated SFT samples outperform models trained on multi-million-scale corpora (e.g., JEDI's 9M). Both 3B and 7B variants set new state-of-the-art averages on ScreenSpot-Pro, OSWorld-G, MMBench-GUI, and UI-Vision, and the 3B model competes with 7B+ and proprietary models in multi-step agentic tasks. (Tables 2 & 3, Section 5.3)
3. **Rigorous Training & Ablation Analysis:** The paper provides a transparent two-stage pipeline (SFT + RLOO) and thoroughly dissects the contribution of each stage. The analysis correctly identifies a "strong SFT ceiling" that explains diminishing RL returns, supported by controlled experiments comparing GROUNDCUA against other datasets for RL fine-tuning. This intellectual honesty and empirical depth align well with top-tier conference standards. (Section 5.2, Figure 3, Appendix D.6)
4. **Clear Path to Reproducibility & Open Science:** The authors commit to releasing the full dataset, model checkpoints, training code, and detailed annotation guidelines. Focusing on permissive open-source applications further removes legal barriers for downstream research. (Reproducibility & Ethics Statements, Section 3)

### Weaknesses
1. **Modest Absolute RL Gains:** While the RL stage provides consistent improvements (+2.0–2.7 Avg), the gains are relatively small. The authors attribute this to a strong SFT baseline, but this makes the RL component feel more like an optional polish step than a core methodological contribution. The reward function, though carefully designed, is not rigorously compared against modern LLM-judge or trajectory-based reward schemes beyond a brief mention. (Section 5.2, Section 4.1)
2. **LLM-Dependent Instruction Generation:** The instruction-tuning data relies on prompting Qwen2.5-VL-72B with constrained metadata. While a 4% human error rate is reported for the final instructions, the heavy reliance on a single large VLM for functional and spatial prompt generation introduces potential stylistic bias and limits the dataset's independence from model capability priors at the time of creation. (Appendix B, B.6)
3. **Uneven Cross-Domain Generalization:** Despite strong desktop performance, the model lags behind specialized baselines on web splits (e.g., SSv2 Web) and shows notable failure cases on mobile aspect ratios. The analysis acknowledges this distribution shift but does not provide a concrete ablation showing how even a small fraction of web/mobile data would affect grounding. (Section 5.3, Appendix E, Table 2)
4. **Isolated Grounding Evaluation in Agentic Setting:** The agentic evaluation uses OpenAI `o3` as a fixed planner, meaning GROUNDNEXT is only tested as a grounding executor. This isolates the grounding bottleneck but does not demonstrate how the model behaves under real-time feedback loops, compounding errors, or when paired with smaller, open-source planners. (Section 5.3, Appendix F)

### Novelty & Significance
**Novelty:** Medium-High. The primary novelty lies in the dataset's scale, annotation density, and expert-driven curation pipeline, which contrasts sharply with the synthetic or automated scraping approaches dominating recent literature. The training pipeline itself follows established SFT+RL paradigms, but the empirical finding that targeted, high-quality supervision can substitute for massive scale in UI grounding is a novel and well-validated contribution.
**Significance:** High. Grounding remains a critical failure point for autonomous computer use. By demonstrating that <1/10th the training data yields superior performance, this work challenges the prevailing "scale-at-all-costs" assumption in GUI agents and provides the community with open, legally clean resources that will serve as strong baselines for future research. The paper meets ICLR's expectations for empirical rigor, clear methodology, and practical impact on multimodal/embodied AI.

### Suggestions for Improvement
1. **Reframe the RL Contribution:** Explicitly position the RL stage as a "targeted refinement mechanism" rather than a primary innovation. Include a brief discussion or citation on scaling laws for RL in discrete grounding tasks, and clarify whether further RL steps (e.g., online sampling, harder negatives, or judge-based rewards) could break the observed ceiling.
2. **Address Instruction Generation Bias:** Add a small ablation or qualitative analysis comparing model performance when trained on human-written vs. LLM-generated functional/spatial instructions. Even a 10k-sample comparison would strengthen confidence in the instruction pipeline's independence.
3. **Deepen Cross-Domain & Spatial Failure Analysis:** Quantify the performance drop on web/mobile by category (e.g., dynamic vs. static layouts, non-rectangular touch targets). For spatial reasoning, analyze whether increasing the spatial instruction ratio from 15% to 30% or introducing relative coordinate tokenization mitigates the ~20% UI-Vision spatial gap.
4. **Clarify Baseline Comparisons:** Table 2 notes that "Only top-performing models are presented." To fully satisfy reviewer scrutiny, either provide a supplementary table with all compared baselines or explicitly state which omitted models were evaluated and under what conditions, ensuring transparency in SOTA claims.

# Spark Finder Review
## How to Improve This Paper

### Missing Experiments (top 3-5 only)
1. Add a strict quantitative contamination analysis measuring exact screenshot, application, and instruction-embedding overlap between GROUNDCUA and all five benchmarks. Without it, the SOTA claims may reflect data leakage rather than superior grounding capability, which is a primary ICLR rejection criterion.
2. Report actual training compute (GPU-hours, FLOPs) and wall-clock time, plus per-step inference latency, to substantiate the "efficient training" and "resource-constrained" claims. Dataset size reduction does not automatically translate to computational or deployment efficiency.
3. Run RL with rejection-sampled hard negatives and scale beyond 10K samples (e.g., 50K/100K) using the same discrete reward. The current <2% marginal gain undermines the claim that RL meaningfully refines grounding without complex reward engineering; a proper hard-case ablation is needed to prove utility.

### Deeper Analysis Needed (top 3-5 only)
1. Provide inter-annotator agreement metrics (e.g., Fleiss’ Kappa, double-blind QA overlap rates) and pre/post error-correction statistics. Without quantitative IAA, the "high-quality human-verified" claim is subjective and cannot support the core quality-over-quantity argument.
2. Isolate grounding failures from planner errors in the OSWorld-Verified results by logging exact action traces. Relying on a black-box proprietary planner (o3) conflates reasoning capability with grounding accuracy, making the agentic performance claim unverifiable.
3. Quantify the distribution shift between desktop and mobile/web splits (aspect ratios, icon density, resolution scaling) and correlate it with specific error rates. Noting a web performance drop without mechanistic analysis leaves the "cross-platform generalization" claim incomplete and speculative.

### Visualizations & Case Studies
1. Provide a side-by-side failure gallery showing identical or semantically similar UI elements where GROUNDNEXT succeeds on desktop but fails on mobile/web, with explicit overlay of predicted vs. ground-truth coordinates. This will reveal whether the method truly generalizes or memorizes desktop layouts.
2. Plot the rollout reward distribution across RL training steps alongside normalized distance histograms for correct vs. incorrect predictions. Without visualizing how the discrete reward shapes the policy's probability mass over the bounding box, the reward design claim remains unverified.
3. Show concrete examples of MLLM-generated instructions that were rated incorrect in the 4% human audit, and map them to actual benchmark errors. This will expose whether instruction ambiguity or annotation noise actively degrades grounding, directly testing the data pipeline's reliability.

### Obvious Next Steps
1. Include an agentic evaluation using a fixed, reproducible open-source planner (e.g., base Qwen2.5-VL-72B or a standard ReAct loop) to decouple grounding performance from LLM reasoning. ICLR requires fully reproducible multi-step evaluations without proprietary API dependencies.
2. Extend the SFT scaling curve to at least 1.5M–2M samples to test whether the reported quality-over-quantity advantage holds or saturates at scale. A 100K→700K curve is insufficient to claim that high-quality data systematically outperforms 9M-sample corpora.
3. Provide a standardized efficiency comparison table reporting training GPU-hours, data preprocessing time, and inference throughput against JEDI, UGround, and UI-TARS. Without direct compute and latency metrics, claims of practical efficiency remain qualitative and unpublishable at ICLR.

# Final Consolidated Review
## Summary
This paper introduces GROUNDCUA, a large-scale desktop GUI grounding dataset comprising 56K screenshots and 3.56M human-annotated UI elements across 87 applications, alongside the GROUNDNEXT 3B/7B vision-language models. Trained via supervised fine-tuning on 700K curated instructions followed by lightweight RL post-training, the models achieve state-of-the-art performance on five grounding benchmarks. The work argues that high-quality, densely annotated human data can outperform prior methods trained on orders of magnitude larger datasets, offering an efficient pathway for reliable computer-use agents.

## Strengths
- **High-density, expert-curated dataset construction:** GROUNDCUA replaces noisy automated accessibility-tree parsing and synthetic generation with dense human annotations, averaging 64 elements per screenshot (vs. ~7–12 in prior datasets). This directly targets the fine-grained localization bottleneck in complex, high-resolution desktop interfaces. (Sec 3, Table 1)
- **Strong empirical evidence for data efficiency:** Rigorous subsampling experiments demonstrate that 700K carefully curated instruction pairs outperform models trained on multi-million-sample corpora (e.g., JEDI, OS-Atlas, UGround) across ScreenSpot-Pro, OSWorld-G, and UI-Vision. This convincingly challenges the prevailing “scale-at-all-costs” assumption in GUI agent training. (Sec 5.1, Table 2)
- **Transparent ablation and honest reporting:** The paper provides a clear dissection of SFT vs. RL contributions, reward granularity ablations, and instruction-type impact. The transparent acknowledgment of marginal RL gains and the “strong SFT ceiling” hypothesis demonstrates empirical rigor and aligns with top-tier reproducibility standards. (Sec 5.2, App D.6)

## Weaknesses
- **Unquantified dataset contamination and annotation rigor** — The paper claims SOTA improvements driven by “high-quality” data but provides zero quantitative train-test overlap analysis (e.g., image, instruction, or application embedding similarity). Given the highly repetitive nature of desktop UIs, performance gains may partially reflect data leakage rather than superior grounding capability. Furthermore, annotation quality lacks objective inter-annotator agreement metrics (e.g., mean IoU or Fleiss’ Kappa) for the 3.56M bounding boxes; the reported 4% error rate applies only to LLM-generated text instructions, leaving the core spatial supervision unvalidated statistically. Without these, the foundational premise of the dataset is empirically unsupported.
- **Opaque efficiency claims and hidden computational costs** — The paper heavily emphasizes “efficient training” and practical utility but omits training GPU-hours, FLOPs, wall-clock time, and inference latency. The instruction generation pipeline relies on expensive Qwen2.5-VL-72B prompting and extensive human labor for keyframe extraction. Without explicit compute and throughput comparisons against baselines like JEDI or UI-TARS, claims of data and computational efficiency are qualitative and unverifiable. ICLR requires quantitative efficiency validation to substantiate practical deployment claims.
- **Conflated agentic evaluation and marginal RL methodological contribution** — The multi-step agentic results (Table 4) rely entirely on OpenAI o3 as a planner, conflating LLM reasoning capabilities with grounding execution accuracy. Without an open-source, fixed planner baseline, it is impossible to isolate the grounder’s actual contribution to task completion. Additionally, the RL stage yields only ~2-point absolute gains across benchmarks. While honestly reported, the lack of hard-negative sampling, larger RL scales, or comparative judge-based rewards renders the RL component an unconvincing methodological pillar rather than a targeted refinement mechanism.

## Nice-to-Haves
- Quantify cross-platform distribution shifts (aspect ratio, resolution, icon density) and correlate them with specific error categories to strengthen the generalization analysis beyond qualitative observations.
- Extend the SFT scaling curve beyond 700K samples (e.g., 1.5M–2M) to verify whether the quality-over-quantity advantage continues linearly or saturates, directly addressing comparisons to 9M-scale corpora.
- Provide side-by-side failure visualizations contrasting successful desktop grounding against mobile/web failures to diagnose whether cross-domain drops stem from spatial reasoning limits or vision encoder architectural biases.

## Novel Insights
The paper effectively demonstrates that for perceptual coordinate prediction tasks in dense desktop environments, high-quality, spatially precise supervision yields stronger empirical returns than scaling raw data volume or applying complex RL reward engineering. The observation that a strong SFT baseline creates a performance “ceiling” that diminishes marginal RL returns is a valuable contribution to the GUI agent literature. It suggests that RL should be strategically framed as targeted error refinement for hard cases rather than a primary performance driver for static spatial grounding, a finding that aligns with broader trends in geometric vision-language fine-tuning.

- Screen2AX (Muryn et al., 2025) and related dynamic UI state generation works — relevant for understanding how temporal/interactive UI representations could complement static keyframe annotations.
- Recent RLVR scaling studies for vision-language spatial reasoning — relevant for contextualizing geometric distance rewards against process-supervision or trajectory-based spatial objectives.

## Suggestions
1. **Conduct strict contamination analysis:** Measure exact image, application, and instruction-embedding overlap between GROUNDCUA and all five evaluation benchmarks. Report leakage rates to validate that SOTA claims reflect genuine grounding capability rather than distributional overlap.
2. **Quantify spatial annotation quality:** Report mean Intersection-over-Union (IoU) or Cohen’s/Fleiss’ Kappa for bounding box annotations across multiple annotators. Decouple this from the LLM text generation audit to substantiate the “human-verified” dataset claim.
3. **Isolate grounding in agentic testing and report compute:** Replace or supplement the proprietary o3 planner with a fixed, reproducible open-source planner (e.g., base Qwen2.5-VL or a standard ReAct loop) to disentangle reasoning from execution. Additionally, publish a standardized efficiency table reporting training GPU-hours, data preprocessing time, and per-step inference latency against primary baselines.
4. **Reframe RL as targeted refinement:** Explicitly position the RL component as an optional polish step for hard negatives rather than a core methodological innovation. Include a brief discussion on why geometric rewards saturate quickly for spatial regression, grounding the findings in the mathematical properties of the task rather than solely dataset scale.
