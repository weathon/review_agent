# Harsh Critic Review
## Section-by-Section Critical Review

### Title & Abstract
- The title accurately reflects the paper's focus on identifying and rectifying misinformation in MASs.
- The abstract clearly states the problem (MAS vulnerability to misinformation injection), the proposed dataset (MISINFOTASK), the method (ARGUS), and key quantitative results.
- **Concern:** There is a numerical inconsistency between the Abstract and the Introduction. The Abstract claims an average reduction in Misinformation Toxicity (MT) of ~28.17%, whereas the Introduction claims a reduction of ~38.24%. Reviewers need to know which metric calculation method (e.g., average of per-model reductions vs. reduction of overall averages) is canonical. Additionally, the Abstract claims a ~10.33% improvement in Task Success Rate (TSR), but Table 1 shows this improvement varies significantly by model and attack vector (e.g., ~1.3% to ~20.9%). The abstract should reflect the variability or clarify how the grand average is computed to avoid over-generalization.

### Introduction & Motivation
- The problem is well-motivated. The distinction between overtly malicious/jailbreak content and covert misinformation is relevant, and the claim that existing datasets lack complex, multi-step tasks provides a reasonable gap.
- Contributions are clearly stated: MISINFOTASK dataset and ARGUS defense framework.
- **Concern:** The introduction slightly over-claims the distinctiveness of "misinformation" versus standard false premises or hallucinatory content in the LLM security context. Operationally, defending against a "factually incorrect but semantically benign" statement is functionally similar to factual verification/hallucination correction. The paper needs to clarify why existing fact-checking or self-correction mechanisms fail specifically in *inter-agent propagation* scenarios, beyond just the added complexity. The motivation would be stronger if the introduction explicitly articulated the unique challenges of misinformation *propagation* (e.g., consensus formation, multi-hop belief updates) that make single-agent defenses inadequate.

### Method / Approach
- The two-stage design (Adaptive Localization + Goal-Aware Rectification) is logically structured and training-free, which is a practical advantage.
- **Concern 1 (Adaptive Localization Blind Spot):** Section 4.1.2 states that `a_cor` infers the misinformation goal *only* from the messages on the edges it monitors. If misinformation initially propagates through a channel *not* selected in the Top-k for round `r`, the corrective agent will not see it and thus cannot infer the goal for the next round's scoring. This creates a detection blind spot where low-topology or low-frequency edges can serve as undetected propagation paths, undermining the "adaptive" claim. The authors should discuss how the system recovers if misinformation is missed in early rounds.
- **Concern 2 (Intervention Mechanism):** Section 4.2 describes "Heuristic Persuasive Reconstruction," and Algorithm 1/Figure 13 show `a_cor` outputting a revised message. However, the paper does not specify the system-level integration architecture. Does `a_cor` intercept and replace the message in the recipient's buffer before processing? Does it append a correction? This detail is critical for reproducibility and understanding potential race conditions or message overwrite conflicts.
- **Concern 3 (Heuristic Scoring):** Equation 9 uses a weighted sum of topology, frequency, and relevance. The choice of weights (`α=0.2`, `β=0.2`, `γ=0.6`) is fixed. While ablated in Table 3, the method lacks a principled justification for these values (e.g., sensitivity analysis or dynamic weighting based on attack phase).
- **Concern 4 (Edge Cases/Failure Modes):** The method assumes `a_cor` has sufficient parametric knowledge to correct misinformation. If the misinformation concerns domain-specific knowledge *outside* the corrective agent's training distribution, `a_cor` may hallucinate a correction or incorrectly flag a novel true statement as false. This false-positive risk is not discussed.

### Experiments & Results
- Experiments test multiple models, attack types, and topologies, demonstrating ARGUS generalizes well across setups.
- **Concern 1 (Baseline Compute Mismatch):** The primary baselines are `Self-Check` (internal prompting) and `G-Safeguard` (GNN pruning). ARGUS introduces a dedicated external corrective agent that consumes additional tokens and latency. The performance gains could stem from *increased compute/inference budget* rather than the adaptive localization methodology. A compute-matched baseline (e.g., a dedicated fact-checking agent assigned to random or all edges with the same token budget as ARGUS) is necessary to isolate the contribution of the proposed localization and goal-aware reasoning mechanisms.
- **Concern 2 (Metric Robustness & Statistical Significance):** Both MT and TSR are evaluated entirely using an LLM-as-a-judge. There is no human validation, inter-annotator agreement, or correlation analysis presented. LLM judges are known to be sensitive to phrasing, recency, and prompt structure. Furthermore, Figure 2 mentions outcomes from "three independent experimental trials." Three runs are insufficient for robust claims in high-variance LLM evaluations. Standard deviations or confidence intervals are missing from Table 1, making it difficult to assess whether the improvements are statistically significant.
- **Concern 3 (Dataset Scale & Attack Diversity):** MISINFOTASK contains 108 tasks. While carefully curated, this scale is small for benchmarking generalization. There is a risk of overfitting to the dataset's specific misinformation styles. Additionally, the prompt in Section B.2 describes injecting misinformation into a single compromised agent. It is unclear if the 4-8 prepared arguments per task are injected simultaneously or sampled. Clarification on injection density and selection randomness is needed for reproducibility.
- **Concern 4 (Ablation Scope):** Table 3 only ablates weights for Prompt Injection. The impact of weights on Tool Injection and RAG Poisoning (where message content/frequency dynamics differ) should be reported to confirm hyperparameter robustness.

### Writing & Clarity
- The overall structure is logical, and figures (Fig 1, 3, 5) effectively visualize the pipeline and results.
- **Concern:** Section 4.2 uses marketing-heavy terminology such as "Internal Knowledge Resonance" and "Heuristic Persuasive Reconstruction" rather than precise technical descriptions. "Internal Knowledge Resonance" appears to be standard parametric retrieval/verification via CoT, and "Persuasive Reconstruction" is LLM-based rewriting. Replacing these terms with standard NLP/Agent terminology would improve technical clarity.
- Algorithm 1 contains minor notation mismatches with the text (e.g., variables like `S_m` and `Ge'r` vs. text descriptions like `Score_rel` and `g'_mis`), which slightly impedes mapping the algorithm to Equations 5-7.

### Limitations & Broader Impact
- The authors acknowledge computational overhead and the limitation to static knowledge, which is appropriate.
- **Concern 1 (Scalability):** The method relies on Edge Betweenness Centrality (Eq 2), which typically requires `O(VE)` or worse computation. Recomputing or updating this alongside LLM embedding similarity for all messages every round may not scale to large, dynamic MAS topologies with hundreds of agents. This limitation should be explicitly discussed in Section 7.
- **Concern 2 (Adversarial Evasion):** The threat model assumes a static injection point. An adaptive attacker aware of the defense could minimize the edge betweenness or message frequency of the compromised channel to evade detection by the Top-k selection. The paper does not discuss the framework's robustness against such strategic evasion.
- **Concern 3 (Societal Impact):** The ethics statement is generic. A more concrete discussion on dual-use (e.g., the dataset and prompt templates could be reverse-engineered to craft better misinformation attacks against other systems) or the risks of deploying automated "persuasive rectification" in critical decision-making systems (where automated rewriting could introduce liability or censorship issues) would strengthen this section.

### Overall Assessment
This paper addresses a timely and relevant problem in MAS security, proposing a novel dataset (MISINFOTASK) and a training-free defense framework (ARGUS) that leverages adaptive localization and goal-aware reasoning. The empirical results demonstrate promising improvements in task success and toxicity reduction across multiple backbones and attack vectors. However, for ICLR standards, the contribution currently faces significant methodological and experimental gaps. The adaptive localization mechanism has a potential blind spot regarding unmonitored edges, and the lack of system-level details on intervention integration hinders reproducibility. Experimentally, the reliance on LLM-only evaluation without human validation, the small dataset size, and the absence of a compute-matched baseline make it difficult to attribute performance gains solely to the proposed methodology versus increased inference budget. Additionally, the reported metrics contain minor inconsistencies, and key limitations regarding scalability (betweenness cost) and false-positive risks of automated rectification are underdiscussed. With rigorous ablation against compute-equivalent baselines, expanded statistical reporting, human evaluation of metrics, and clearer technical exposition, this work has the potential to make a solid contribution to the community.

# Neutral Reviewer
## Balanced Review

### Summary
This paper addresses the vulnerability of LLM-based Multi-Agent Systems (MASs) to covert misinformation injection by introducing `MISINFOTASK`, a dataset of 108 complex, real-world tasks designed for red-teaming, and `ARGUS`, a two-stage, training-free defense framework. `ARGUS` dynamically identifies high-risk communication channels using topological centrality and semantic goal-matching, then deploys a corrective agent that leverages internal knowledge and chain-of-thought reasoning to rectify misleading messages. Experimental results across multiple LLMs, attack vectors, and network topologies demonstrate that ARGUS significantly reduces misinformation toxicity (~28%) and improves task success rates (~10%) compared to baseline defenses.

### Strengths
1. **Well-Motivated Problem & Dataset Gap:** The paper correctly identifies that prior MAS security research disproportionately focuses on overt jailbreaks or malicious commands, neglecting covert, plausibly-deniable misinformation that disrupts reasoning. `MISINFOTASK` directly addresses this by providing complex tasks with explicit misinformation arguments and ground truths across five reasoning categories (Sec 3.1, App C).
2. **Practical & Training-Free Architecture:** `ARGUS` avoids the data inefficiency and domain-shift risks of training-based defenders. The two-stage design (Adaptive Localization + Goal-Aware Rectification) is modular and leverages existing LLM capabilities (CoT, internal knowledge resonance) in a resource-conscious manner, making it highly deployable in real-world MAS pipelines.
3. **Comprehensive Empirical Validation:** The evaluation rigorously tests three distinct injection vectors (Prompt, RAG, Tool), four diverse LLM backbones, five topological structures, hybrid attacks, and agent scaling (Sec 5, App D.1-D.2). The consistent performance gains across these varied conditions strongly support the framework's robustness and generalization.
4. **Transparent Ablation & Cost Analysis:** The authors provide clear ablation studies for both architectural components and hyperparameters (Table 2, Table 3), and explicitly quantify the API overhead introduced by the defense module (Table 8, App D.4), demonstrating responsible experimental design aligned with ICLR's reproducibility expectations.

### Weaknesses
1. **Evaluation Metric Limitations:** The core metric, Misinformation Toxicity (MT), relies entirely on a single LLM judge (GPT-4o) outputting a 0-10 score (Eq. 1). The paper does not report human validation, inter-annotator agreement, or judge calibration, raising concerns about potential circularity or bias in assessing a defense method that also relies on LLM reasoning. Additionally, the threshold $\theta_m$ used for TSR calculation is mentioned but never explicitly defined or justified in the main text.
2. **Ad Hoc Hyperparameter Weighting:** The adaptive localization scoring combines topological, frequency, and semantic relevance scores with fixed weights ($\alpha=0.2, \beta=0.2, \gamma=0.6$). While Table 3 shows ablation results, there is no sensitivity analysis, validation procedure, or theoretical justification for these values, nor discussion of how they might adapt to different tasks or topologies without manual tuning.
3. **Baseline Comparison Gap:** The comparison is limited to `Self-Check` (a basic prompting technique) and `G-Safeguard` (a GNN-based method requiring training on prior logs). The paper omits comparison to other modern, training-free MAS defense strategies, robust routing protocols, or consensus/debate mechanisms, which weakens the claim of state-of-the-art performance.
4. **Unaddressed Failure Modes & Knowledge Boundaries:** The rectification stage assumes the corrective agent's parameterized knowledge aligns with ground truth. The authors note this in limitations (Sec 7) but do not experimentally analyze cases where the LLM's knowledge is outdated, ambiguous, or conflicts with domain-specific task constraints, leaving the false-positive/false-negative behavior of `ARGUS` under-explored.

### Novelty & Significance
The novelty of this work is **moderate but practically valuable**. Conceptually, `ARGUS` synthesizes established graph-theoretic channel monitoring with LLM-driven goal inference and persuasive text rewriting, rather than proposing a fundamentally new learning paradigm or theoretical bound. However, its contribution is highly significant for the ICLR community's growing focus on trustworthy, scalable multi-agent systems. By shifting the security lens from overt adversarial attacks to insidious misinformation propagation, and providing a reproducible, training-free mitigation framework alongside a well-constructed benchmark, the paper offers actionable insights for MAS architects. It meets ICLR's bar for empirical rigor and system-level relevance, though it would benefit from deeper theoretical grounding or more competitive baselines to elevate its impact.

### Suggestions for Improvement
1. **Validate the MT Metric:** Augment the LLM-judge scoring with a human evaluation study on a representative subset of tasks. Report correlation scores or Cohen's Kappa to establish reliability, and explicitly state the $\theta_m$ threshold used for TSR, along with an ablation on its sensitivity.
2. **Justify or Automate Hyperparameters:** Provide a grid-search or Bayesian optimization analysis for $\alpha, \beta, \gamma, k,$ and $\theta_{sim}$ across different topologies. Alternatively, propose a lightweight self-tuning mechanism (e.g., dynamic weight adjustment based on round-over-round MT trends) to reduce manual dependency.
3. **Strengthen Baselines:** Include at least one or two recent training-free baselines, such as multi-agent debate/consensus filtering, or prompt-based information sanitization pipelines, to contextualize `ARGUS`'s gains more fairly against methods that don't require prior training data.
4. **Analyze Failure Cases & False Positives:** Add a qualitative section or appendix table detailing instances where `ARGUS` failed to detect misinformation (false negatives) or incorrectly modified accurate, nuanced messages (false positives). Discuss how to mitigate these scenarios, particularly in time-sensitive or highly specialized domains where LLM internal knowledge may lag.

## How to Improve This Paper

### Missing Experiments (top 3-5 only)
1. Add comparisons against recent multi-agent consensus/debate and external fact-checking baselines because Self-Check and G-Safeguard are outdated for misinformation, making ARGUS's claimed superiority unverified against actual state-of-the-art defenses.
2. Report false positive and over-rectification rates to quantify how often benign or accurate messages are incorrectly altered because without it, the MT reduction could simply stem from information suppression, artificially inflating TSR.
3. Provide statistical significance (confidence intervals across multiple independent runs) and evaluate MT with at least two distinct LLM judges because single-run, single-judge scoring is highly vulnerable to stochastic variance and prompt bias, fundamentally undermining result credibility.

### Deeper Analysis Needed (top 3-5 only)
1. Conduct a systematic failure-mode analysis that isolates whether errors stem from flawed topological localization, semantic threshold misalignment, or weak CoT rectification because aggregate MT/TSR masks which component actually breaks down.
2. Analyze resilience against adaptive or multi-point attackers who change injection timing, targets, or use misinformation that partially aligns with LLM pretraining because the static single-point threat model severely overestimates real-world robustness.
3. Quantify the informational depth, completeness, and nuance of MAS solutions under ARGUS because aggressive content filtering can preserve binary task success while silently degrading output quality and reasoning fidelity.

### Visualizations & Case Studies
1. Provide a round-by-round propagation graph for a specific task instance that maps the exact trajectory of a misinformation claim, marks ARGUS's interception points, and shows how rectified messages alter downstream agent decisions to visually prove adaptive localization works.
2. Include side-by-side tables showing the raw intercepted message, ARGUS's internal CoT verification trace, and the final persuasive rewrite for both high-confidence successes and failures to expose whether the rectification mechanism actually reasons correctly or just rewrites superficially.

### Obvious Next Steps
1. Report per-round latency, token consumption, and throughput scaling as a function of the monitoring budget $k$ and agent count because ICLR requires efficiency validation, and unprofiled training-free defenses can easily render MAS operationally intractable.
2. Supplement automated LLM-judge scoring with expert human ratings on a representative task subset because security claims grounded entirely in model-generated "toxicity" and "success" scores lack the human grounding required for publication acceptance.

# Final Consolidated Review
## Summary
This paper investigates the vulnerability of LLM-based Multi-Agent Systems (MASs) to covert misinformation injection. The authors introduce MISINFOTASK, a dataset of 108 complex, realistic tasks designed for red-teaming, and propose ARGUS, a two-stage, training-free defense framework. ARGUS dynamically identifies high-risk communication channels using topological and semantic scoring, then deploys a corrective agent that leverages goal-aware reasoning to intercept and rectify misleading messages.

## Strengths
- **Addresses a critical security gap:** The paper shifts focus from overt jailbreak attacks to covert misinformation, which poses a distinct threat to MAS belief states and collaborative execution. MISINFOTASK directly fills this niche with realistic, multi-step tasks spanning five reasoning categories and carefully curated arguments.
- **Practical, training-free architecture:** ARGUS avoids domain-shift and data inefficiency risks of training-based defenders. By combining graph-theoretic channel monitoring with LLM-driven goal inference and persuasive rewriting, it offers a modular, deployable solution that generalizes across different MAS topologies without requiring retraining.
- **Comprehensive empirical validation:** The evaluation rigorously tests three injection vectors, four diverse LLM backbones, five topological structures, and hybrid attacks. The consistent performance gains across these varied conditions strongly support the framework's robustness, and the inclusion of explicit API cost analysis demonstrates responsible experimental design aligned with reproducibility expectations.

## Weaknesses
- **Unattributed compute gains and lack of information preservation analysis** — ARGUS introduces an external corrective agent that consumes additional tokens and latency compared to the `Self-Check` and `G-Safeguard` baselines. Without a compute-matched baseline (e.g., a random or full-coverage monitor with an equivalent token budget), it is difficult to isolate whether performance gains stem from the adaptive localization methodology or simply from increased inference compute. Furthermore, the paper reports reduced Misinformation Toxicity (MT) but omits false-positive or over-rectification rates. There is a concrete risk that MT reductions partially stem from aggressive information suppression rather than precise rectification, which could degrade the informational depth and reasoning fidelity of final task outputs.
- **Evaluation fragility and reporting inconsistencies** — The primary evaluation relies entirely on a single LLM-as-a-judge with no reported variance, confidence intervals, or human validation across the stated three independent trials. In security evaluations, this makes it impossible to assess statistical significance or rule out judge-specific prompt bias. Additionally, there is a direct numerical discrepancy between the abstract (~28.17% MT reduction) and the introduction (~38.24% MT reduction), which undermines reporting precision and requires immediate correction.
- **Architectural blind spots and undefined knowledge boundaries** — The adaptive localization mechanism infers misinformation goals exclusively from messages on currently monitored edges. If misinformation initially propagates through unmonitored channels in early rounds, the system fails to capture the attacker's goal, creating a persistent detection blind spot that the paper does not address. The paper also assumes the corrective agent's parametric knowledge is sufficient to rectify all claims; it does not analyze failure modes where the LLM encounters domain-specific or novel true statements that conflict with its training distribution, leaving the framework's false-negative and hallucination-correction behaviors under-explored.

## Nice-to-Haves
- **Dynamic hyperparameter weighting:** The current fixed weights for edge scoring ($\alpha=0.2, \beta=0.2, \gamma=0.6$) could benefit from a sensitivity analysis across varying task complexities or a lightweight self-tuning mechanism.
- **Technical clarity in messaging:** Replacing marketing-heavy terminology (e.g., "Internal Knowledge Resonance") with standard verification terms would improve technical precision. Explicitly detailing how the corrective agent interfaces with the MAS message buffer (e.g., interception vs. appending) would enhance reproducibility.
- **Expanded ablation scope:** Reporting the impact of hyperparameter ablations across Tool Injection and RAG Poisoning scenarios would confirm whether the weighting scheme generalizes beyond Prompt Injection.

## Novel Insights
The research demonstrates that misinformation in multi-agent systems propagates most effectively through structurally central communication pathways, and that defending against it requires tracking the *intent* of the information rather than just its surface semantics. By coupling topological centrality with semantic goal inference, ARGUS reveals that targeted interception of high-impact channels is significantly more efficient than blanket fact-checking. Furthermore, the goal-aware rectification strategy shows that framing corrections to align with the inferred misleading narrative is more effective at neutralizing misinformation in MAS contexts than standard factual rebuttals, highlighting the importance of persuasive coherence in automated defense.

## Suggestions
- Implement and report results for a compute-matched baseline (e.g., assigning a corrective agent to randomly selected edges with an identical token budget to ARGUS) to rigorously isolate the contribution of the adaptive localization mechanism from raw compute increases.
- Correct the numerical inconsistency between the abstract and introduction, and revise the evaluation methodology to report variance/standard deviations across multiple runs, ideally supplemented by a secondary judge or human subset verification.
- Add a qualitative or quantitative analysis of false positives/over-rectification, explicitly measuring whether ARGUS inadvertently alters accurate, nuanced statements or suppresses beneficial information while reducing toxicity.
- Clarify the exact message handling mechanism (interception vs. replacement) within the MAS communication flow, and discuss the system's behavior when the corrective agent's internal knowledge conflicts with domain-specific ground truth.
