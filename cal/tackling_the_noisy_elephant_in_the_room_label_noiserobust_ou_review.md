# Harsh Critic Review
## Section-by-Section Critical Review

### Title & Abstract
- **Title accuracy:** The title accurately reflects the core contribution: combining loss correction with low-rank decomposition to improve OOD detection under label noise.
- **Abstract clarity:** The abstract clearly identifies the problem (OOD degradation under noisy labels), the proposed method (feature cleansing via joint loss correction and low-rank/sparse constraints), and the key outcome (outperformance on synthetic and real-world noisy benchmarks).
- **Claim verification:** The abstract states the framework "cleanses" feature embeddings and introduces a metric quantifying "OOD-ness." These claims are substantiated by the method section and empirical results. The phrase "principled solution" is acceptable given the formulation, though the theoretical grounding of the low-rank assumption on deep features needs careful scrutiny (see Method section).

### Introduction & Motivation
- **Motivation and Gap:** The motivation is well-grounded in the observation that label noise corrupts latent feature geometry (Fig. 1), directly harming distance-based OOD scores. The gap is clearly articulated: classification-oriented noise-robust methods optimize predictive probabilities, which does not guarantee well-structured feature spaces for OOD detection.
- **Contributions:** The contributions are explicitly listed and align with the paper's content. The claim of being "first to offer a principled solution" should be tempered slightly to acknowledge prior work on contrastive or representation learning under noisy labels that might indirectly impact OOD, even if not explicitly designed for it.
- **Claims:** The introduction does not over-claim. It appropriately frames the severity of the problem and sets realistic expectations for the proposed framework.

### Method / Approach
- **Reproducibility & Gradient Flow:** A critical methodological gap exists regarding backpropagation. The framework applies a batch-wise power-iteration (PI) SVD approximation (Alg. 2) followed by QR orthonormalization to compute $H_{ID}$ and $\mathcal{L}_{\text{sparse}}$. Gradients must flow from $\mathcal{L}_{\text{sparse}}$ through the iterative approximation and orthonormalization back to the backbone parameters $\theta$ in the CE loss. Differentiating through SVD/QR is numerically sensitive, especially when singular values are close (common in deep features). Alg. 1 mentions standard `SGDOptimizer` but omits how gradient stability is ensured. Are custom gradient formulas used? Is a straight-through estimator employed? This must be clarified for reproducibility.
- **Low-Rank Assumption:** The assumption that penultimate layer features $H$ admit a rank-$K$ decomposition (where $K$ is the number of classes) is strong. Modern CNN backbones produce high-dimensional embeddings that capture complex intra-class variations often not confined to a strict $K$-dimensional subspace. Forcing $H \approx QQ^\top H$ via Eq. 4 may oversimplify the feature manifold. The authors should justify why rank $K$ is sufficient or discuss the impact of feature dimensionality vs. intrinsic rank.
- **Batch-wise Instability:** Alg. 1 computes $Q$ per mini-batch. This means the target subspace $H_{ID} = QQ^\top H$ and the regularizer $\mathcal{L}_{\text{sparse}}$ change stochastically every iteration, even for the same sample if paired with different batch-mates. This batch-level volatility can destabilize training or lead to a moving target that hinders consistent feature alignment. A mechanism to stabilize $Q$ across epochs or use a running estimate would strengthen the approach but is currently absent.
- **Selection Strategy Justification:** Eq. 5 selects the top $(1-p)\%$ training samples with the highest "OOD-ness" score $o(x_n)$ to construct the reference set for $k$NN/Mahalanobis. Intuitively, $o(x_n)$ measures deviation from the low-rank projection, which likely captures noisy or ambiguous samples. Standard OOD detectors typically benefit from clean, prototypical reference points. Relying on high-deviation points as anchors is counter-intuitive unless they serve to tightly bound the ID manifold. The authors must provide stronger theoretical or empirical justification for why retaining "hard" or high-residual samples improves density estimation for OOD.

### Experiments & Results
- **Evaluation Completeness:** The empirical evaluation is comprehensive, covering synthetic symmetric noise and three real-world human-annotated noisy datasets. The comparison against both OOD-specific baselines and noise-robust classification methods is appropriate.
- **Statistical Rigor:** The results in Tables 1 and 2 report point estimates without standard deviations or error bars across multiple random seeds. Given ICLR's emphasis on statistical significance and robustness, especially under stochastic training with mini-batch decomposition, reporting variance is essential. Were results averaged over seeds? How was $τ$ selected on the validation set across seeds?
- **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** Table 7 shows NOODLE achieves optimal performance at $\lambda = 10^{-4}$, with degradation at higher $\lambda$. This suggests the low-rank regularizer should be very weak, implying the loss correction term drives most of the robustness, while the low-rank component plays a minor shaping role. This sensitivity warrants discussion: does the method collapse if $\lambda$ is too large? Conversely, if $\lambda$ is negligible, is the low-rank mechanism truly necessary?
- **Rank Selection $K$:** Table 8 ablates $K$ for CIFAR-100N, showing $K=20$ performs best despite $K_{\text{classes}}=100$. This contradicts the method description that $K$ is selected according to the number of classes. Choosing $K=20$ for 100 classes implies a significant mismatch with the stated prior. Furthermore, hyperparameter tuning on $K$ requires an OOD-aware validation set, which leaks OOD performance signals. The authors must address how $K$ should be chosen in practice without OOD validation leakage.
- **ID vs OOD Trade-off:** While supplementary tables report ID accuracy, the main text lacks explicit discussion on the ID/OOD trade-off. Forcing features toward a low-rank subspace may improve OOD separation but could compromise discriminative power for fine-grained ID classes. The results show NOODLE maintains competitive ID accuracy, but an explicit analysis in the main text would strengthen the claim.

### Writing & Clarity
- **Clarity of Scoring:** The paper distinguishes between the internal "OOD-ness" score $o(x_n)$ and the final OOD detection score $s(x)$ in Section 3. However, this distinction could be sharper. $o(x_n)$ is a training-time metric for filtering reference examples, while $s(x)$ is the test-time $k$NN/Mahalanobis distance. Clarifying that $o(x_n)$ is *not* the final OOD detector would prevent reader confusion.
- **Algorithm Description:** The transition between Eq. 4 and the joint loss $\mathcal{L}_F$ is clear. However, the relationship between the loss correction module (Eq. 3) and the low-rank module needs elaboration on their interaction. Does the loss correction affect the computation of $H$, which then feeds the low-rank block? Yes, but a unified computational graph diagram (or clear text description) of the forward/backward pass would greatly improve clarity regarding how gradients from both terms update the shared backbone.

### Limitations & Broader Impact
- **Acknowledged Limitations:** The authors note the computational overhead of PI and the sensitivity to $\lambda$.
- **Missing Limitations:**
    - **Feature Collapse Risk:** The method assumes ID features can be compressed to rank $K$. For datasets with high intra-class diversity fine-grained categories, or continuous attributes, forcing low-rank structures could cause feature collapse, destroying OOD separability. This scope limitation is not discussed.
    - **Noise Type Assumption:** Synthetic noise is symmetric. Real-world noise is often instance-dependent and structured. The low-rank decomposition assumes deviations are "sparse." If label noise creates systematic feature clusters (e.g., misclassifying all cats as dogs), these flips may not be sparse outliers, potentially causing the model to learn erroneous subspaces. The method's robustness to correlated or instance-dependent noise should be discussed.
- **Broader Impact:** The introduction mentions safety-critical applications, but the paper lacks a dedicated broader impact section. Given OOD detection's role in deployment safety, briefly discussing how robust OOD under noise prevents false confidence in high-stakes domains (medicine, autonomous systems) and potential risks if the low-rank assumption fails would align with ICLR expectations.

### Overall Assessment
This paper addresses a timely and important problem: the degradation of OOD detection under label noise. The core insight—that classification-level noise robustness is insufficient without feature-space correction—is well-motivated and supported by empirical evidence across multiple benchmarks. The proposed NOODLE framework consistently improves FPR95 and AUROC, particularly under severe noise regimes. However, significant methodological clarifications are required for ICLR acceptance. The reproducibility of gradient computation through batch-wise PI/QR decomposition must be detailed, and the stability of batch-stochastic subspace targets needs addressing. Additionally, the counter-intuitive strategy of selecting high-residual samples for the $k$NN reference set, the sensitivity of $\lambda \to 0$, and the mismatch between class count and optimal rank $K$ require stronger theoretical or empirical justification. If the authors can clarify the optimization dynamics, address batch instability, and rigorously discuss the scope of the low-rank assumption, this work would make a solid contribution to robust representation learning and OOD detection. As it stands, the empirical gains are promising, but the methodological depth and clarity need reinforcement to fully meet the ICLR bar.

# Neutral Reviewer
## Balanced Review

### Summary
This paper addresses the underexplored challenge of out-of-distribution (OOD) detection when training data contains severe label noise. The authors propose NOODLE, a framework that integrates robust loss correction with batch-wise low-rank and sparse decomposition to explicitly "cleanse" latent feature representations, separating invariant ID structures from noise-corrupted residuals. By quantifying these residuals into an OOD-ness score and using it to construct a robust reference set for distance-based scoring, the method demonstrates consistent improvements over existing baselines across synthetic and real-world noisy benchmarks.

### Strengths
1. **Well-motivated conceptual gap:** The paper effectively argues that existing label-noise-robust methods primarily target prediction probability calibration rather than feature space geometry, which is critical for distance-based OOD detection (Sec. 1, Fig. 1). Framing OOD robustness as an explicit feature-cleansing problem is a strong, practical insight.
2. **Comprehensive empirical evaluation:** Experiments span multiple synthetic noise rates (10%–50%), real-world noisy datasets (CIFAR-10N, CIFAR-100N, Animal-10N), and diverse OOD benchmarks. Extensive ablations across loss correction strategies (CM, SCE), distance metrics (kNN, Mahalanobis), and key hyperparameters ($\lambda$, $p$, $K$) provide strong evidence for each component’s contribution (Figs. 3–5, Tables 1–2, Sup. Tables 7–10).
3. **Practical and efficient design:** Replacing exact SVD with a power-iteration (PI) approximation for batch-wise low-rank decomposition keeps computational overhead manageable. The modular architecture allows swapping in different robust losses or distance metrics, making the framework adaptable to various deployment constraints (Remark 1, Alg. 2).

### Weaknesses
1. **Lack of statistical validation:** All main results (Tables 1 & 2) report single-point FPR95/AUROC values without standard deviations or multiple random seeds. Given the high variance associated with noisy label training and stochastic optimization, ICLR reviewers expect results averaged over $\geq 3$ seeds with confidence intervals to substantiate claims of consistent superiority.
2. **Baseline training protocol ambiguity:** The supplementary notes that post-hoc OOD baselines share a single CE-trained encoder, and that CIDER/SSD+ used an adapted DenseNet-101 "without full fine-tuning." It is unclear whether noise-robust classification baselines (e.g., SCE, CM, VolMinNet) were trained exclusively for accuracy before feature extraction, or if they were tuned for OOD. Unequal training budgets or suboptimal baseline configurations could confound the reported gains.
3. **Notation inconsistencies and selection threshold analysis:** Following Equation 4, the text contains a clear typo: "the low-rank component $H_{OOD}$ focuses on learning the cleanest possible version..." should read $H_{ID}$. Furthermore, the quantile-based selection strategy ($p$) assumes the sparse residual accurately ranks sample difficulty/noise, but the paper does not analyze potential misclassification of hard-but-clean ID samples as "OOD-like," nor does it justify the choice of $p$ beyond a grid search.

### Novelty & Significance
**Novelty** is incremental but meaningful. Low-rank decomposition and robust loss correction are established techniques; however, explicitly coupling them within an end-to-end OOD training loop to purify distance metrics is a solid algorithmic contribution. The derived OOD-ness scoring mechanism is a pragmatic extension rather than a theoretical breakthrough. **Clarity** is generally good, with well-structured sections and clear algorithmic descriptions, though minor notation typos and ambiguous baseline protocols detract from readability. **Reproducibility** is strong in principle: pseudo-code, hyperparameter ranges, architecture, and optimizer details are provided, and batch-wise PI implementation is explained. However, the lack of seed reporting limits exact result replication. **Significance** is moderate-to-high, as robust OOD detection under realistic label noise directly addresses a deployment bottleneck in safety-critical AI. The method’s simplicity and consistent empirical gains make it a valuable addition to the robust OOD literature, meeting a practical need at ICLR.

### Suggestions for Improvement
1. **Report statistical variance across seeds:** Re-evaluate Tables 1 and 2 over at least three random seeds and report mean ± standard deviation. This is essential to demonstrate that NOODLE’s improvements are robust to initialization and noise sampling variability.
2. **Standardize and clearly document baseline protocols:** Add a table explicitly listing the training loss, epochs, optimizer, and whether validation tuning was applied for every baseline. Ensure noise-robust baselines are trained under identical computational budgets and architectural settings. If CIDER/SSD+ were not fully fine-tuned, either provide their fine-tuned results or acknowledge the limitation transparently.
3. **Correct notation and analyze the OOD-ness selector:** Fix the $H_{ID}$/$H_{OOD}$ reference post-Equation 4. Add an ablation or visualization showing the alignment between the proposed $o(x_n)$ scores and ground-truth noisy/clean labels (e.g., ROC curve for noise detection). This would validate whether the selection mechanism reliably isolates noisy/harmful samples versus merely capturing ambiguous but valid ID examples.
4. **Discuss inference overhead and scalability:** While training complexity is analyzed, test-time latency depends on storing and querying the reference set $\mathcal{S}$ for kNN. Provide a brief discussion on memory footprint, query time as a function of $(1-p)$, and potential approximate nearest neighbor replacements for large-scale deployment.

# Spark Finder Review
## How to Improve This Paper

### Missing Experiments (top 3-5 only)
1. **Evaluation on larger benchmarks and modern architectures:** Replace or augment CIFAR/DenseNet-101 experiments with ResNet/ViT backbones on larger-scale datasets (e.g., ImageNet-1K or WebVision), because without this, claims of scalable, generalizable robustness are unconvincing.
2. **Strict tuning protocol validation:** Explicitly report whether the detection threshold $\tau$ and selection quantile $p$ are tuned using clean or noisy validation labels; if clean labels are required, add experiments showing performance when these are tuned using noisy-only proxies or self-supervised estimates to prove practical viability.
3. **Asymmetric and pair-flip noise settings:** Add experiments using class-dependent and asymmetric noise flips, because real-world annotation errors are rarely symmetric and relying solely on uniform noise hides potential failure modes of the decomposition module.

### Deeper Analysis Needed (top 3-5 only)
1. **Quantitative validation of the cleansing hypothesis:** Provide metrics (e.g., mutual information with ground-truth labels or precision of $o(\mathbf{x})$ against known noise flags) proving $H_{ID}$ actually recovers clean semantics and $H_{OOD}$ isolates noise, because without this, the low-rank/sparse separation remains an unverified heuristic rather than demonstrated feature correction.
2. **Isolated contribution of the decomposition module:** Report results for state-of-the-art loss correction alone versus loss correction + decomposition, because the paper's core novelty hinges on whether the signal processing step provides meaningful marginal gains beyond modern robust training objectives.
3. **Rank parameter sensitivity analysis:** Evaluate performance across a wider or mismatched $K$ range (not just fixed to class counts), because high label noise or overlapping semantics likely distort the true intrinsic rank; without this analysis, the method's stability in uncontrolled settings is questionable.

### Visualizations & Case Studies
1. **Selection step precision-recall against true labels:** Plot the distribution of $o(\mathbf{x})$ scores stratified by ground-truth clean vs. noisy samples, because this directly reveals whether the score actually quantifies "OOD-ness/noisiness" or merely filters out low-confidence examples irrespective of label correctness.
2. **Batch singular value spectrum evolution:** Visualize the singular value decay of feature matrices across epochs for clean, high-noise, and OOD-contaminated batches, because this empirically validates or refutes the foundational low-rank + sparse assumption underlying the entire framework.
3. **Failure cases on near-OOD benchmarks:** Provide case studies using fine-grained or semantically overlapping OOD datasets (e.g., CIFAR-100 subsets), because visualizing where the low-rank projection fails to maintain class boundaries exposes the realistic limits of ID-OOD separability under noise.

### Obvious Next Steps
1. **Full computational and memory profiling:** Report wall-clock training time, peak VRAM, and OOD inference latency relative to standard KNN/Energy baselines, because the batch-wise SVD approximations and dense matrix operations introduce overhead that must be quantified to assess deployment feasibility.
2. **Automated heuristic selection for hyperparameters:** Propose or evaluate data-driven rules for $K$ (e.g., eigen-gap detection) and $p$ (e.g., estimated corruption rate), because manual grid searches contradict the claim of a principled, automated robust framework.
3. **Open-set noise integration testing:** Evaluate how NOODLE performs when the training set contains actual distributional outliers mixed with noisy labels, because modeling "OOD-ness" in training features naturally implies the method should handle open-world recognition, not just label flip correction.

# Final Consolidated Review
## Summary
This paper addresses the degradation of distance-based out-of-distribution (OOD) detection under training label noise by proposing NOODLE, a feature-cleansing framework that couples robust loss correction with batch-wise low-rank and sparse decomposition. By treating noise-corrupted feature deviations as sparse perturbations to a low-rank in-distribution (ID) manifold, the method derives an OOD-ness scoring function to construct a refined reference set for kNN/Mahalanobis detection. Extensive experiments across synthetic and real-world noisy datasets demonstrate consistent FPR95 and AUROC improvements over both OOD-specific and noise-robust classification baselines.

## Strengths
- **Clear conceptual bridge between robust learning and OOD geometry:** The paper correctly identifies that classification-oriented label noise methods optimize predictive probabilities, not feature space structure. Framing OOD robustness as an explicit geometric cleansing problem (separating low-rank ID structure from sparse residuals) is well-motivated and directly targets the failure mode shown in Fig. 1.
- **Comprehensive and modular empirical validation:** Evaluation spans multiple noise rates, three human-annotated noisy benchmarks, and diverse OOD test sets. The modular design allows transparent ablations across loss correction strategies (CM, SCE) and distance metrics, with consistent gains supporting the core hypothesis.
- **Computationally mindful approximation:** Replacing exact nuclear-norm optimization with a power-iteration (PI) based low-rank approximation keeps training overhead tractable, and the explicit complexity analysis in Remark 1 demonstrates awareness of practical deployment constraints.

## Weaknesses
- **Baseline adaptation protocol introduces potential unfair comparison:** The supplementary explicitly notes that CIDER and SSD+ are evaluated using a DenseNet-101 adapter "without full fine-tuning" due to architecture mismatch. Suboptimal baseline configurations in a comparative OOD study can artificially inflate the relative gains of the proposed method. Without fully fine-tuned baselines or a clear bounding of this limitation, the true magnitude of NOODLE's improvement remains ambiguous.
- **Rank-$K$ heuristic contradicts empirical tuning:** The method section suggests selecting rank $K$ "according to the number of classes," yet Table 8 demonstrates $K=20$ is optimal for CIFAR-100 (100 classes). Relying on OOD-aware validation to tune $K$ or the selection quantile $p$ leaks distribution-shift signals into training, violating the closed-world assumption of practical OOD deployment. The paper lacks a principled, OOD-agnostic heuristic for setting $K$.
- **Unverified feature-cleansing hypothesis:** The core claim is that low-rank projection recovers semantic ID structure while the sparse component isolates noise/OOD information. However, no quantitative analysis validates this separation (e.g., correlation of $o(\mathbf{x})$ scores with ground-truth noise labels, or mutual information between $H_{ID}$ and clean semantics). Without this, the improved performance could stem from implicit regularization or feature smoothing rather than explicit noise/OOD disentangling.
- **Unspecified gradient stability through iterative decomposition:** Algorithm 2 applies power iteration and QR orthonormalization per mini-batch. Differentiating through iterative subspace projection is numerically sensitive to near-colliding singular values and can introduce gradient instabilities. The paper does not detail the autodiff implementation (e.g., custom gradient hooks, numerical stabilization tricks) or discuss how batch-stochastic subspace targets might cause training oscillations, leaving a reproducibility gap.

## Nice-to-Haves
- **Statistical variance reporting:** While single-run evaluation is common in large-scale image OOD benchmarks, reporting results averaged over $\geq 3$ seeds with standard deviations would strengthen claims under high-noise stochasticity.
- **Asymmetric and instance-dependent noise testing:** Real-world annotation errors are rarely purely symmetric. Evaluating performance under class-pair flips or instance-dependent corruption would better establish robustness boundaries.
- **Inference-time scaling analysis:** Providing memory footprint estimates for the reference set $\mathcal{S}$ and kNN query latency, along with a discussion of approximate nearest-neighbor replacements, would improve deployment practicality claims.

## Novel Insights
The paper successfully reframes robust OOD detection from a *probability calibration* problem to an *explicit geometric purification* problem. By leveraging signal processing priors (low-rank + sparse decomposition) on mini-batch feature matrices, it reveals that the most informative reference points for distance-based OOD scoring are not necessarily the cleanest prototypes, but rather the boundary-defining samples captured by the sparse residuals. This inversion—retaining high-residual embeddings to sharpen density estimation—offers a fresh perspective on how label noise structurally corrupts feature manifolds and how targeted regularization can restore ID-OOD separability without explicit clean labels.

## Suggestions
1. **Standardize baseline training or explicitly bound claims:** Re-evaluate the adapted CIDER/SSD+ baselines under identical fine-tuning budgets, or add a dedicated ablation showing that their performance ceiling under fair training does not negate NOODLE's gains. Clearly document the training protocol for all baselines in the main text.
2. **Provide a data-driven heuristic for rank $K$:** Replace the class-count heuristic with an eigen-gap threshold, cumulative explained variance criterion, or a self-supervised estimator. Demonstrate that this heuristic yields stable performance without requiring OOD validation leakage.
3. **Quantitatively validate the cleansing mechanism:** Leverage the known clean/noisy labels in CIFAR-10N/CIFAR-100N to plot the distribution of $o(\mathbf{x})$ scores stratified by ground-truth noise status, and compute alignment metrics (e.g., precision/recall for noise identification). This will empirically ground the low-rank/sparse separation claim.
4. **Clarify autodiff and stability details for PI/QR:** Briefly specify the framework-level implementation for differentiating through Algorithm 2, note any numerical safeguards used, and discuss empirical observations regarding training stability or batch-to-batch subspace variance.
