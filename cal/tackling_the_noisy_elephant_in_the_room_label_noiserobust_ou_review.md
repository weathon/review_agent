# Harsh Critic Review
## Section-by-Section Critical Review

### Title & Abstract
- **Does the title accurately reflect the contribution?** Yes. The title captures the core problem (label noise in OOD detection) and the two main technical mechanisms (loss correction + low-rank decomposition).
- **Does the abstract clearly state the problem, method, and key results?** Yes. It cleanly outlines the degradation of OOD under noise, introduces the end-to-end cleansing strategy, describes the OOD-ness scoring, and claims empirical superiority.
- **Are any claims in the abstract unsupported by the paper?** The claim `"we derive a novel metric that quantifies the 'OOD-ness' content"` is slightly overstated. Equation for $o(\mathbf{x}_n)$ is simply the $\ell_2$ residual from a low-rank projection, not derived from first principles or formal OOD theory. It functions as a heuristic proxy rather than a theoretically grounded metric. Additionally, `"first principled solution"` is a strong claim; while the empirical gap is acknowledged, recent concurrent work on feature regularization under noise may challenge the "first principled" framing without careful positioning.

### Introduction & Motivation
- **Is the problem well-motivated? Is the gap in prior work clearly identified?** Yes. The mismatch between classification-focused noise-robust training and distance-based OOD detection requirements is clearly articulated and well-supported by Fig. 1 and prior empirical findings (Humblot-Renaux et al., 2024).
- **Are the contributions clearly stated and accurate?** The three contributions are distinct and accurately reflect the paper's contents: (1) framework, (2) scoring/selection, (3) empirical validation.
- **Does the introduction over-claim or under-sell?** Slight over-claiming in framing the method as highly "novel" without sufficiently distinguishing it from the rich literature of Robust PCA and feature distillation/regularization. However, the synthesis for OOD detection under noise is a valid and timely extension.

### Method / Approach
- **Is the method clearly described and reproducible?** No, there is a critical omission regarding gradient computation. The framework minimizes $\mathcal{L}_F = \mathcal{L}_{mod\_CE} + \lambda \|\mathbf{H}_{OOD}\|_{2,1}$ where $\mathbf{H}_{OOD}$ depends on $\mathbf{Q}$ from Power Iteration + QR. These are iterative subspace methods that are generally **non-differentiable** in standard autodiff frameworks unless specific implicit differentiation or straight-through tricks are applied. The paper does not explain how gradients flow through $( \mathbf{Q}\mathbf{Q}^\top )\mathbf{H}$ and $\mathbf{H} - (\mathbf{Q}\mathbf{Q}^\top)\mathbf{H}$ during training. Without this, the method is not reproducible.
- **Are key assumptions stated and justified?** The core assumption is that clean latent features exhibit a low-rank structure (rank $\approx K$). While common in representation learning literature, this is empirically assumed here without citation or theoretical justification for modern deep networks, especially under label correction. Furthermore, applying batch-level Power Iteration assumes the *minibatch* top-$K$ subspace approximates the *global dataset* subspace, which is known to be unstable at small batch sizes or high noise rates. This limitation is unaddressed.
- **Are there logical gaps in the derivation or reasoning?** Yes. **Equation 5 is highly problematic.** The selection set is defined as $S = \{ n \mid o(\mathbf{x}_n) \geq \alpha_p \}$, which retains the top $(1-p)\%$ samples with the *highest* OOD-ness scores (i.e., the most deviant/noisy training samples). Using the *most* OOD-like or noisy samples as reference points for a $k$NN or Mahalanobis detector intuitively degrades ID-OOD separability, yet the paper states this yields a "robust detector." The authors must clarify if this is a typo (should be $\leq \alpha_p$ to keep the cleanest samples) or provide a strong geometric/intuitive justification for why highly deviant boundary samples improve OOD scoring.
- **Are there edge cases or failure modes not discussed?** Yes. If noise is high enough to corrupt the top-$K$ singular directions, $\mathbf{Q}$ will capture noise rather than class structure, making $\mathbf{H}_{ID}$ misleading. The method lacks a mechanism to validate or correct the estimated subspace during training.

### Experiments & Results
- **Do the experiments actually test the paper's claims?** Yes. The synthetic and real-noise settings directly probe robustness to label corruption, and the comparison against both OOD and noise-robust baselines is well-aligned with the introduction.
- **Are baselines appropriate and fairly compared?** Strong baseline selection. Using DenseNet-101 for all methods ensures fairness. The inclusion of transition-matrix (CM, VolMinNet), robust loss (SCE, GCE), and sample selection (DivideMix, Co-teaching) methods is appropriate.
- **Are there missing ablations that would materially change conclusions?** Yes. 
  1. **Batch size stability:** Since PI operates on batches, results at $B=16$ vs $B=128$ could vary significantly. No ablation on batch size or $\mathbf{Q}$ update frequency.
  2. **ID Accuracy trade-off:** Improving OOD detection can sometimes stem from feature collapse or underfitting. ID accuracy is relegated to the supplement (Tables 3-6) without discussion in the main text. The main text should explicitly report ID accuracy to ensure OOD gains aren't due to degraded classification.
  3. **Gradient implementation detail:** As noted above, how backprop is handled changes the optimization dynamics entirely.
- **Are error bars / statistical significance reported?** **No.** All tables (Table 1, 2) present point estimates. ICLR standards strongly encourage reporting mean $\pm$ std over multiple seeds (typically 3-5) or explicitly stating deterministic training. Without variance metrics, it is unclear if the 1-4% FPR95 improvements (e.g., 27.34% vs 31.35% at 50% noise) are statistically significant or seed-dependent.
- **Do the results support the claims made, or are they cherry-picked?** The results appear consistent across datasets and noise levels, not cherry-picked. Tables 1 & 2 show NOODLE generally leads or is highly competitive. However, the magnitude of improvement varies; on CIFAR-100N, the gains are larger, while on low-synthetic-noise they are more modest. This is acceptable but warrants discussion.

### Writing & Clarity
- **Are there sections that are confusing or poorly explained?** 
  1. The reference sample selection strategy (around Eq. 5) is confusing. The rationale for prioritizing high-residual training samples for the reference set is absent and contradicts standard intuition in distance-based OOD detection.
  2. The integration of the PI-based decomposition into the training loop (Algorithm 1, Step 10) simply states `Compute $\nabla \mathcal{L}_F(\dots)$`. The non-trivial step of handling non-differentiable orthogonalization (QR) and rank truncation during gradient descent is glossed over.
- **Are figures and tables clear and informative?** Figures 1-3 are clear and effectively illustrate feature corruption vs. cleansing and the behavior of $o(\mathbf{x}_n)$. Tables are dense but follow standard conventions. Figure 3b showing FPR reduction with selection strategy is useful, but again, without clarifying *which* samples are selected (high vs low score), the interpretation is muddled.

### Limitations & Broader Impact
- **Do the authors acknowledge the key limitations?** The paper briefly notes the computational overhead of PI is moderate (Remark 1) but does not discuss the fundamental limitations: sensitivity to rank $K$ estimation, batch-size dependency of the subspace approximation, and the unresolved question of why high-deviation samples are used for reference.
- **Are there fundamental limitations they missed?** 
  1. **Instance-dependent noise:** Real-world noise is rarely symmetric. While CIFAR-10N contains human noise, the method's reliance on low-rank assumptions may struggle with semantic shifts within classes (e.g., ambiguous boundary images that are correctly labeled but naturally high-variance).
  2. **Test-time adaptation:** The method constructs a fixed reference set $S$ post-training. If the test OOD distribution shifts significantly, a fixed $S$ may become suboptimal compared to adaptive methods like Dice or ReAct.
- **Are there failure modes or negative societal impacts not discussed?** No specific broader impact analysis is provided. In safety-critical domains, if the low-rank projection inadvertently projects out rare but valid ID subgroups (which might appear as "sparse" outliers due to representation bias), the method could disproportionately flag them as OOD. This distributional fairness issue is worth a sentence in limitations.

### Overall Assessment
This paper addresses a highly relevant and under-explored intersection of label noise and OOD detection, demonstrating that standard noise-robust classifiers do not inherently learn OOD-robust features. The proposed synthesis of loss correction, batch-wise low-rank decomposition, and distance-based scoring is conceptually sound and yields strong empirical performance across multiple benchmarks. However, for ICLR's high bar on technical soundness and reproducibility, the manuscript requires addressing three critical concerns: (1) explicitly detailing how gradients are computed through the iterative Power Iteration/QR steps, (2) clarifying and justifying the counterintuitive selection of high-residual training samples for the OOD reference set in Equation 5, and (3) reporting statistical significance (multiple seeds, error bars) and discussing ID accuracy trade-offs in the main text. The method is promising and well-motivated, but the current lack of algorithmic clarity regarding the optimization landscape and the ambiguity in the selection strategy prevent a stronger endorsement. With rigorous clarification on these points and added statistical rigor, the contribution would be a solid fit for the conference.

# Neutral Reviewer
## Balanced Review

### Summary
This paper addresses the degradation of out-of-distribution (OOD) detection performance when models are trained on label-noisy data. The authors propose NOODLE, a framework that cleanses latent feature representations by combining established loss correction strategies with batch-wise low-rank plus sparse decomposition, deriving an "OOD-ness" residual score to select reference embeddings for distance-based detectors. Extensive experiments across synthetic and real-world noisy benchmarks demonstrate that the proposed method consistently outperforms both standard OOD detection and noise-robust learning baselines in FPR95 and AUROC metrics.

### Strengths
1. **Clear Problem Diagnosis & Motivation:** The paper effectively identifies a critical objective misalignment: existing label-noise methods correct predictive probabilities but do not rectify feature geometry, which is essential for distance-based OOD detection. This gap is well-supported by UMAP visualizations (Fig. 1) showing severe feature space distortion under noise, motivating the need for explicit feature cleansing.
2. **Practical & Computationally Efficient Design:** Instead of relying on exact nuclear norm minimization (which requires costly full SVDs), the authors employ a power-iteration (PI) based approximation for batch-wise low-rank decomposition. Remark 1 provides a clear complexity analysis ($O(T(DBK) + DK^2)$), demonstrating that the overhead is moderate and feasible for standard deep learning training loops.
3. **Comprehensive Empirical Validation:** The evaluation is rigorous, covering synthetic symmetric noise (10%-50%), multiple real-world noisy datasets (CIFAR-10N/100N, Animal-10N), and six diverse OOD test sets. Tables 1 and 2 show consistent, substantial improvements. The ablation studies (Figs. 4, 5; Appendix Tables 3-10) thoroughly investigate different loss correction backbones, distance metrics, and hyperparameters $\lambda$ and $p$.

### Weaknesses
1. **Conceptual Inconsistency in Reference Set Construction:** The "OOD-ness" score $o(x_n) = \|h(x_n) - h_{ID}(x_n)\|_2$ measures deviation from the learned low-rank subspace, with higher values indicating noisy/ambiguous samples. However, Eq. 5 defines $S = \{n \mid o(x_n) \ge \alpha_p\}$, meaning the framework explicitly selects samples with the *highest* residual scores to build the ID reference library for $k$NN/Mahalanobis. If these are the most deviated/noisy samples, using them as reference centers should theoretically degrade ID-OOD separation. The paper does not adequately explain why selecting high-residual samples improves performance compared to selecting clean, low-residual ones.
2. **Incremental Novelty & Limited Theoretical Grounding:** The core components (transition matrix/robust loss correction, low-rank + $\ell_{2,1}$ sparse decomposition akin to Robust PCA, and feature-caching distance scores) are well-established. While their synthesis is useful, the methodological novelty is moderate. The paper lacks formal theoretical analysis explaining why low-rank structure perfectly captures ID semantics under arbitrary noise, or bounds on how the residual score correlates with true OOD detection error.
3. **Hyperparameter Sensitivity & Practical Tuning:** Appendix Table 7 reveals extreme sensitivity to $\lambda$. On the "worst" noise case, FPR95 nearly doubles from 27.94% ($\lambda=0.0001$) to 43.07% ($\lambda=0.001$). The paper treats $\lambda$ as a simple grid-search parameter without discussing how it should scale with dataset dimensionality, noise rate, or batch size, limiting out-of-the-box robustness.
4. **Baseline Fairness & Notation Clarity:** The appendix acknowledges that CIDER and SSD+ baselines were adapted to DenseNet-101 "without additional fine-tuning" and "may require additional fine-tuning to fully realize their potential." This caveat weakens the strength of the comparative claims. Additionally, naming the sparse residual matrix $H_{\text{OOD}}$ is misleading, as these features originate from training samples, not test-time OOD data. Algorithm 1 also contains a redundant nested `while stopping criterion` loop.

### Novelty & Significance
**Novelty:** Moderate. The work is a principled combination of robust subspace decomposition and loss correction tailored for OOD detection, rather than a fundamentally new architectural or algorithmic primitive. The application mirrors prior robust PCA approaches used for anomaly detection, though adapting it dynamically during training with a specific sample selection criterion for OOD scoring is a reasonable contribution. **Clarity:** Generally strong, but compromised by the ambiguous justification for selecting high-residual samples and minor notational/algorithmic inconsistencies. **Reproducibility:** Adequate. The paper provides clear pseudo-code, explicit hyperparameter search spaces, architecture details, and multiple ablation tables. Missing exact random seeds, compute specifications, and training runtimes, which is standard but could be improved. **Significance:** High for applied ML and safety-critical deployments. Given the ubiquity of label noise and the growing demand for reliable OOD detection, providing a working pipeline that demonstrably fixes feature geometry (rather than just logits) addresses a real, underexplored gap. The empirical gains are substantial and practically relevant.

### Suggestions for Improvement
1. **Clarify & Justify the Sample Selection Mechanism:** Rigorously explain why Eq. 5 selects high-residual samples. If the intent is to exclude them, the inequality/quantile definition must be inverted. If selecting them is intentional (e.g., they lie near the decision boundary and improve boundary-aware distance metrics), provide empirical or theoretical justification, and visualize the selected set $S$ via UMAP to confirm it does not contain spurious noise.
2. **Strengthen Theoretical & Analytical Foundation:** Include a brief analysis linking the low-rank residual to feature Lipschitz smoothness or cite/extend robust PCA recovery guarantees to show under what noise conditions the cleaned features $H_{\text{ID}}$ provably approximate the clean feature manifold. Even empirical analysis correlating $o(x_n)$ with estimated noise probability would add rigor.
3. **Ensure Fair & Fully-Tuned Comparisons:** Re-evaluate training-based OOD baselines (CIDER, SSD+, SNN) under the same DenseNet-101 architecture with appropriate hyperparameter adjustment for noisy settings. If full re-tuning is infeasible, explicitly discuss the known architecture mismatch in the main text and quantify its expected impact.
4. **Improve Notation & Algorithmic Clarity:** Rename $H_{\text{OOD}}$ to $H_{\text{res}}$ or $H_{\text{noise}}$ throughout to prevent confusion with actual out-of-distribution test samples. Fix the duplicate `while` loop in Algorithm 1 and Algorithm 2 to reflect standard mini-batch training epochs/iterations.
5. **Provide Practical Guidance on $\lambda$:** Discuss the sensitivity observed in Table 7. Suggest a heuristic for choosing $\lambda$ relative to the noise rate or batch statistics (e.g., adaptive regularization based on running estimates of residual variance) to make NOODLE more robust to the practitioner's hyperparameter budget.

# Spark Finder Review
## How to Improve This Paper

### Missing Experiments (top 3-5 only)
1. Add a controlled experiment with fully synthetic noise and synthetic OOD distributions to isolate the effect of the reference set selection strategy ($p$) from dataset-specific covariate shifts. Without it, it's impossible to know if the gains are from principled noise cleansing or accidental alignment with specific benchmark shifts.
2. Compare against a strong, minimal baseline: train with a robust loss (e.g., CM), compute clean class centroids from the learned features, and use centroid-based distances for OOD scoring. This is necessary to prove that the low-rank/sparse decomposition and dynamic reference selection are actually required over standard prototype-based OOD.
3. Report detailed training time, GPU memory footprint, and inference latency compared to standard OOD baselines. The batch-wise power iteration, QR factorization, and reference set management introduce non-trivial overhead that must be quantified to claim practical viability.

### Deeper Analysis Needed (top 3-5 only)
1. Provide a rigorous explanation for why constructing the reference set $S$ from the samples with the *highest* OOD-ness scores (i.e., the noisiest/most ambiguous inputs) improves detection. Standard kNN theory suggests using clean, high-confidence samples; without a mechanistic justification, this appears to be an empirical hack that risks overfitting to label noise patterns.
2. Validate the foundational assumption that clean ID features truly reside in a $K$-dimensional low-rank subspace. Report the singular value spectrum of $H$ across training epochs to show whether the model naturally learns this structure or if the PI regularizer artificially forces it, potentially discarding valid high-variance semantic information.
3. Analyze gradient flow and numerical stability through the power-iteration and QR decomposition steps during SGD updates. These operations are non-trivial to differentiate automatically, and without a proof of stable gradients or implicit differentiation, the claim of a true "end-to-end" learning criterion is mathematically unverified.

### Visualizations & Case Studies
1. Plot the empirical distribution of the learned "OOD-ness" scores $o(x_n)$ for verified clean vs. verified noisy training samples. This would directly show whether the score actually separates mislabeled examples or just captures inherent class intra-variance.
2. Visualize the spatial geometry of the selected reference set $S$ in UMAP space alongside the full training set and true ID/OOD test data. This will expose whether $S$ successfully captures class boundaries or merely clusters around hard outliers and corrupted features.
3. Present a systematic failure case analysis for OOD datasets where NOODLE underperforms (e.g., Places365 at 30% noise in Table 1). Show the specific feature overlaps or label confusion patterns that break the low-rank decomposition to clarify the method's hard boundaries.

### Obvious Next Steps
1. Derive and implement explicit backpropagation rules or an implicit differentiation scheme for the PI+QR module, then report its convergence stability and impact on final test metrics. ICLR will reject the current black-box auto-diff assumption without this theoretical grounding.
2. Conduct cross-validated hyperparameter tuning for $\lambda$ and $p$ with statistical significance testing across all real-noise datasets. Reporting a single "best" configuration without variance estimates or sensitivity analysis undermines the claim of robust, generalizable performance.
3. Release the exact implementation of the batch-wise low-rank decomposition as open-source code alongside training/inference scripts. The computational complexity claims cannot be independently verified or adopted by the community without functional, reproducible code that matches the mathematical formulation.

# Final Consolidated Review
## Summary
This paper proposes NOODLE, a framework for label-noise-robust out-of-distribution (OOD) detection that integrates probabilistic loss correction with batch-wise low-rank plus sparse decomposition of latent features. By isolating a residual component from the learned low-rank subspace, the method derives an "OOD-ness" score to construct a reference feature set for distance-based OOD detectors, reporting consistent empirical gains across synthetic and real-world noisy benchmarks.

## Strengths
- **Clear Problem Formulation & Motivation:** The paper correctly identifies a critical objective misalignment in prior work: existing label-noise methods optimize predictive probabilities but neglect the geometric structure of latent features, which is the foundation of modern distance-based OOD detectors. UMAP visualizations (Fig. 1) clearly demonstrate feature space corruption under noise, strongly motivating the need for explicit feature cleansing.
- **Comprehensive Empirical Evaluation:** The experimental pipeline is rigorous, systematically testing across varying synthetic noise rates (10%–50%), multiple human-annotated noisy datasets (CIFAR-10N/100N, Animal-10N), and six distinct OOD benchmarks. The ablation studies across different loss correction backbones and distance metrics thoroughly validate the modular design of the framework.
- **Computationally Efficient Approximation:** The substitution of exact nuclear-norm minimization with a power-iteration (PI) based low-rank decomposition is highly practical. The explicit $O(T(DBK) + DK^2)$ complexity analysis demonstrates that the overhead is moderate and viable for standard training loops without prohibitive computational cost.

## Weaknesses
- **Counterintuitive and Unjustified Reference Set Selection Strategy:** Equation 5 explicitly constructs the OOD reference library by selecting training samples with the *highest* OOD-ness residuals ($o(\mathbf{x}_n) \geq \alpha_p$). In distance-based detection, reference points should represent high-density, class-representative regions to accurately model the ID manifold. Selecting the most deviant/noisy training examples contradicts established geometric intuition for $k$NN/Mahalanobis scoring. The paper provides no theoretical derivation, boundary-aware geometric justification, or empirical comparison against low-residual (clean) selection, making this core mechanism appear as an ad-hoc heuristic rather than a principled design choice.
- **Undefined Gradient Mechanics & Optimization Rigor:** The training objective requires backpropagation through iterative power-iteration and QR decomposition steps applied to batch-wise feature matrices. These operations involve non-linear projections and iterative orthogonalization that are notoriously unstable under standard automatic differentiation. The manuscript completely omits discussion of gradient flow, implicit differentiation, or numerical stabilization techniques for the decomposition module, leaving the claimed "end-to-end" optimization mathematically unverified and difficult to reproduce reliably.
- **Unvalidated Structural Assumptions & Extreme Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The framework assumes latent features naturally form a strict $K$-dimensional low-rank subspace, but provides no singular value spectrum analysis to validate this premise for modern deep networks under label correction. More critically, performance is highly sensitive to the regularization weight $\lambda$; Table 7 in the appendix shows average FPR95 nearly doubling from 27.94% to 43.07% when $\lambda$ shifts from $10^{-4}$ to $10^{-3}$. The paper offers no heuristic or adaptive strategy for tuning $\lambda$, which severely limits practical robustness and out-of-the-box applicability.
- **Lack of Statistical Reporting Standards:** All primary results (Tables 1 & 2) are reported as single-seed point estimates without standard deviations or confidence intervals. Given the marginal absolute improvements over strong baselines in several settings (often 1–4% FPR95), it is impossible to distinguish genuine robustness gains from stochastic seed variance. This falls short of current ICLR reproducibility standards and weakens confidence in the claimed superiority of the method.

## Nice-to-Haves
- Provide a spectral analysis (singular value distribution) of the latent feature matrices before and after decomposition to empirically validate the low-rank assumption and show how rank-$K$ projection interacts with increasing noise rates.
- Compare the proposed framework against a minimal baseline that simply combines robust loss correction with class-centroid distance scoring, to isolate whether the dynamic low-rank/sparse decomposition and residual selection provide meaningful gains over prototypical approaches.
- Include a brief discussion of inference-time latency and memory overhead caused by maintaining and querying the reference set $S$, alongside training runtime comparisons to standard OOD baselines.

## Novel Insights
The work's central contribution lies in diagnosing that classification-focused noise robustness does not naturally align with the feature geometry required for distance-based OOD detection. By dynamically decomposing representations into a structured low-rank "clean" subspace and a sparse residual component during training, the authors provide a constructive mechanism to explicitly enforce ID compactness while isolating anomalous or mislabeled variations. This bridges classical robust subspace learning with modern representation-based OOD scoring, though the novelty remains primarily integrative rather than theoretical, as it does not establish formal guarantees on how the residual metric correlates with true distributional shift under arbitrary noise processes.

## Potentially Missed Related Work
- Feature regularization techniques for OOD detection (e.g., contrastive representation learning for ID compactness, or Mahalanobis distance with feature perturbation) — highly relevant for contextualizing the low-rank geometric regularization objective.
- Deep robust matrix factorization and anomaly detection literature (e.g., deep RPCA variants or subspace learning for novelty detection) — would strengthen the theoretical grounding of the $\mathbf{H}_{ID} + \mathbf{H}_{OOD}$ decomposition and clarify how it improves upon existing latent-space denoising methods.

## Suggestions
- Rigorously justify the reference set selection direction in Equation 5. If selecting high-residual samples is intentional, provide a formal geometric argument (e.g., how boundary-hard samples define a tighter decision margin) and include an ablation directly comparing high-residual vs. low-residual selection across varying noise rates.
- Explicitly detail the backward-pass implementation for the PI/QR module. Specify whether implicit differentiation, stop-gradient approximations, or custom autograd functions are used, and provide a stability analysis of the gradient norms during training to confirm numerical soundness.
- Report mean ± standard deviation over at least 3–5 random seeds for all primary tables, and include ID classification accuracy in the main text to demonstrate that FPR95 improvements are not artifacts of severe underfitting or representation collapse.
