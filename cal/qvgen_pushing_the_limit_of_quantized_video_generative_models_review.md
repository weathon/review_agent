# Harsh Critic Review
## Section-by-Section Critical Review

### Title & Abstract
- **Does the title accurately reflect the contribution?** Yes. "Pushing the Limit of Quantized Video Generative Models" accurately captures the focus on achieving high-performance, low-bit (≤4-bit) quantization for video diffusion models.
- **Does the abstract clearly state the problem, method, and key results?** The problem (video DMs' high memory/compute, failure of existing quantization at ≤4 bits) and method (QAT with auxiliary modules Φ + rank-decay strategy) are clearly stated. Key results (first FP-comparable 4-bit video DM, strong VBench gains over SOTAs) are explicitly mentioned.
- **Are any claims in the abstract unsupported by the paper?** The claim "the first to reach full-precision comparable quality under 4-bit settings" is largely supported by Table 1, where most metrics are within ~1-2% of BF16. However, metrics like `Scene Consistency` and `Dynamic Degree` show non-trivial gaps in some models. The abstract should slightly temper "full-precision comparable" to reflect that this holds for *most* but not all dimensions, or clarify it refers to the overall score trend.

### Introduction & Motivation
- **Is the problem well-motivated? Is the gap in prior work clearly identified?** Yes. The computational bottleneck of modern video DiTs is well-established. The introduction correctly identifies that PTQ and existing image-focused QAT methods collapse at W4A4/W3A3 for video models due to complex spatiotemporal dynamics and activation distributions. The gap is clearly articulated.
- **Are the contributions clearly stated and accurate?** The contributions are well-structured and accurate relative to the experiments. The claim of being the "first QAT method for video generation" appears justified given the cited literature timeline.
- **Does the introduction over-claim or under-sell?** Minor over-claiming exists in framing the theoretical analysis as a primary driver for Φ's design. The text implies a strong causal proof that minimizing `||g_t||_2` *requires* Φ, which is conceptually a heuristic motivation backed empirically. It would be more precise to frame the theory as diagnostic motivation rather than a prescriptive proof of optimality.

### Method / Approach
- **Is the method clearly described and reproducible?** Yes. Algorithm 1, Eq. 9, 11-14, and the decay schedule are mathematically precise. The initialization `W_Φ = W - Q_b(W)` (Sec. 3.1) and the iterative SVD+truncation pipeline (Sec. 3.2) are reproducible.
- **Are key assumptions stated and justified?** The method assumes that small singular values correspond to low-impact components that can be safely decayed. This is supported empirically in Fig. 4 and theoretically by standard low-rank approximation lore, but the assumption that SVD *during training* is strictly necessary (vs. using a fixed low-rank adapter that naturally decays via weight decay) is not fully justified.
- **Are there logical gaps in the derivation or reasoning?** Theorem 3.1 is a standard convex online learning regret bound. While mathematically correct, applying it to highly non-convex QAT is a stretch. Appendix C correctly provides a non-convex smoothness bound, but this is also standard gradient descent analysis. The theoretical contribution does not establish a novel convergence property for quantized models; it primarily validates that reducing gradient norm correlates with stability. The methodological leap from "gradient norm matters" to "we need an auxiliary module Φ decayed via SVD" relies heavily on empirical design choices rather than theoretical necessity.
- **Are there edge cases or failure modes not discussed?** The rank-decay strategy assumes `r` is small initially (Sec. 3.2 notes `r << d`). The paper does not discuss how QVGen behaves if `W_Φ` develops large singular values in specific layers (e.g., attention value projections or FFN up-projections known for outliers). If certain layers resist rank collapse, the fixed iteration-per-phase schedule might prune useful components prematurely or leave residual overhead.

### Experiments & Results
- **Do the experiments actually test the paper's claims?** Yes. Experiments span multiple architectures (CogVideoX, Wan), sizes (1.3B to 14B), and bit-widths (W4A4, W3A3, W6A6), thoroughly validating the core claims.
- **Are baselines appropriate and fairly compared?** Baselines (LSQ, Q-DM, EfficientDM, SVDQuant) are appropriate. However, fairness is questionable due to hyperparameter tuning. Sec. 4.1 states: *"The same settings are applied to all QAT baselines."* QAT methods have notoriously different LR sensitivities and convergence trajectories. Using identical LR/epochs/schedulers without per-method tuning may unfairly handicap baselines. Additionally, SVDQuant is compared at W4A6 or fine-grained W4A4 to make it work, which is disclosed but highlights that vanilla W4A4 PTQ fails entirely.
- **Are there missing ablations that would materially change conclusions?** A critical missing ablation is a direct comparison to a standard LoRA-style auxiliary module *without* SVD decomposition. If a simple low-rank adapter (initialized similarly or randomly) trained alongside the quantized model and linearly decayed to zero achieves comparable results, the computational complexity of repeated SVD and rank-truncation may be unnecessary. The paper compares to Sparse, Residual Q, and Linear decay, but not a baseline that uses the same LoRA structure without SVD-guided component selection.
- **Are error bars / statistical significance reported?** No. VBench scores can vary significantly across seeds, prompts, and sampling steps (Sec. D mentions 5 videos per prompt, which is lower than typical ICLR standards for generative modeling). Reporting mean±std across ≥3 seeds or employing bootstrap confidence intervals is necessary to validate the claimed margins, especially for close metrics.
- **Do the results support the claims made, or are they cherry-picked?** Results appear comprehensive and uncherry-picked. Table 1 shows QVGen winning across nearly all dimensions. The paper honestly reports degradation on `Scene Consistency` and `Dynamic Degree` where present.
- **Are datasets and evaluation metrics appropriate?** OpenVidHQ-0.4M for training and VBench/VBench-2.0 for evaluation are standard and appropriate for video generation. However, the paper does not discuss whether the QVGen models were evaluated on out-of-distribution prompts or temporal length generalization beyond the fixed 49/81 frames.

### Writing & Clarity
- **Are there sections that are confusing or poorly explained?** Section 3.2's formulation of `γ` in Eq. 13 (`concat([1], [u])`) is slightly informal but clarified by the text. The interaction between the learned `L, R` and the decaying `γ` could be clearer: since `L` and `R` are continuously updated via backprop while `γ` decays, the SVD is only used for *structural identification* of components to mask out. This iterative identify-then-decay pipeline is understandable but computationally heavy; a brief pseudocode breakdown or cost analysis of the SVD step relative to the QAT forward pass would improve clarity.
- **Are figures and tables clear and informative?** Figures 1, 3, 4, and 6 are highly informative. Tables 1-2 are dense but well-organized. Table 7's training cost breakdown is clear. No major clarity issues impede understanding of the contribution.

### Limitations & Broader Impact
- **Do the authors acknowledge the key limitations?** Yes. The conclusion notes the focus on video generation, leaves extension to NLP/multimodal for future work, and acknowledges that 3-bit quantization still incurs notable drops on large models and complex metrics.
- **Are there fundamental limitations they missed?** The reliance on knowledge distillation (KD) from a teacher model is standard but not critically discussed in the limitations. KD requires a full-precision teacher forward pass during training, which multiplies memory/compute costs. While Sec. 4.4 acknowledges ~1.01x memory overhead over naive QAT, it doesn't discuss scenarios where teacher inference is impractical (e.g., on-device fine-tuning or when the FP teacher is unavailable/downgraded). Additionally, the method only addresses linear/FFN layers; attention layers remain in FP (using FlashAttention), which limits end-to-end memory reduction and leaves a potential bottleneck unaddressed.
- **Are there failure modes or negative societal impacts not discussed?** Standard model compression carries standard deployment risks. No major negative societal impacts specific to this method are anticipated. A minor failure mode not discussed is potential collapse under extreme quantization (e.g., W2A2) where the residual error `W - Q_b(W)` might be non-smooth or bimodal, breaking the low-rank assumption of Φ.

### Overall Assessment
QVGen presents a timely, well-motivated, and empirically strong contribution to the quantization of video diffusion models. The proposed auxiliary module with a structured rank-decay schedule effectively bridges the gap between full-precision performance and ultra-low-bit (W4A4/W3A3) efficiency, achieving state-of-the-art results across multiple model families. The writing is clear, the experimental scope is broad, and the method is practically grounded. However, for ICLR acceptance, the paper requires: (1) reporting variance/error bars across multiple seeds to solidify the statistical significance of VBench gains, (2) addressing the fairness of baseline comparisons by either justifying uniform hyperparameters or including per-baseline tuned results, (3) adding an ablation against a simple LoRA-decay baseline to isolate the necessity of the SVD-guided rank-decay strategy, and (4) slightly tempering theoretical claims to accurately reflect their role as empirical motivation rather than novel convergence proofs. With these clarifications, the work represents a solid, publication-ready contribution that meaningfully advances low-bit video generative modeling.

# Neutral Reviewer
## Balanced Review

### Summary
This paper introduces QVGen, a quantization-aware training (QAT) framework tailored for ultra-low-bit (3-4 bit) video diffusion models (DMs). By theoretically linking QAT convergence to gradient norm reduction, the authors design auxiliary modules to stabilize training and a novel rank-decay strategy that progressively eliminates these modules via SVD and cosine-annealing regularization, ensuring zero inference overhead. Extensive experiments across multiple video DMs (1.3B to 14B parameters) demonstrate state-of-the-art reconstruction quality and practical deployment efficiency at low precisions.

### Strengths
1. **Strong Theoretical Motivation & Empirical Validation:** The paper correctly identifies that existing QAT methods fail on video DMs due to unstable optimization, linking this to high gradient norms (Fig. 3). Both convex (Thm. 3.1) and non-convex (App. C) analyses rigorously support minimizing $||g_t||_2$, and the empirical reduction in gradient norm directly correlates with improved VBench scores.
2. **Elegant & Effective Rank-Decay Design:** The progressive elimination of auxiliary modules via iterative SVD and rank-based regularization is well-conceived. Ablations convincingly show that decaying components associated with small singular values incurs negligible quality loss while fully removing inference overhead (Tabs. 3, 4, 6, L.1-L.4).
3. **Comprehensive, Reproducible Evaluation:** The framework is validated across 4 diverse, SOTA video DMs at multiple bit-widths, with thorough ablations, efficiency profiling, and qualitative comparisons. Detailed hyperparameters, dataset specifications, hardware setups, and the release of code/models ensure high reproducibility and strong experimental rigor suitable for ICLR.

### Weaknesses
1. **Theory-to-Practice Gap in Convergence Analysis:** While Thm. 3.1 provides a standard regret bound under convexity, the authors correctly note that deep DiTs are highly non-convex (App. C). The non-convex proof yields a standard $O(1/T)$ stationary point bound but does not deeply explain *how* the auxiliary module reshapes the highly ill-conditioned loss landscape of *quantized temporal modeling*, making the theoretical contribution somewhat incremental rather than fundamentally new.
2. **Modest Inference Speedups Despite Strong Quality Gains:** The primary contribution is quality recovery at low bits, but reported latency improvements (~1.21×–1.44× on A800) are constrained by non-fused kernels and memory-bound operations (Sec. 4.4, App. N-O). While honestly acknowledged, the practical *speed* advantage is less pronounced than the theoretical bit-reduction implies, which may limit immediate deployment impact.
3. **Training Cost of Repeated SVD:** The rank-decay mechanism requires iterative SVD across all linear layers during QAT. Although mitigated by a low initial rank ($r=32$) and reported as only ~2% training overhead (Tab. A/7), the scalability of exact SVD on 14B models across many decay phases is not deeply profiled. A more efficient approximation (e.g., randomized SVD or power iteration) is not discussed.

### Novelty & Significance
**Novelty:** High. While auxiliary modules for quantization error and progressive pruning exist in adjacent fields, their synergistic combination—grounded in gradient-norm stabilization for video DM QAT and executed via SVD-driven rank decay—is novel and well-tailored to the unique activation distributions of temporal diffusion models. **Clarity:** Excellent. The paper is logically structured, with clear progression from motivation to theory, method design, and extensive validation. Notation and equations are precise, and figures/tables effectively illustrate key behaviors. **Reproducibility:** Outstanding. Full methodological details, training recipes, hardware configurations, and open-sourced code/models are provided, meeting ICLR's high reproducibility standards. **Significance:** Substantial. Enabling 3/4-bit QAT for billion-parameter video DMs without inference overhead directly addresses a major bottleneck in deployment and scalable AI generation. The framework sets a new Pareto frontier and provides valuable insights for future ultra-low-bit generative modeling.

### Suggestions for Improvement
1. **Deepen the Theoretical Connection to Quantized Loss Landscapes:** Extend the analysis to discuss how the auxiliary module specifically mitigates activation outliers or hessian ill-conditioning induced by low-bit quantization in DiTs, bridging the gap between standard regret bounds and practical QAT behavior in video diffusion.
2. **Profile & Optimize SVD Overhead:** Provide a detailed breakdown of the SVD computation cost (frequency, memory footprint, wall-clock time per decay phase) for the largest models. Discuss or implement randomized SVD / iterative low-rank approximations to further reduce training bottlenecks and improve scalability.
3. **Enhance Deployment Efficiency Discussion:** Since video generation is heavily memory-bandwidth bound, clarify how QVGen's weight/activation quantization integrates with modern kernel-fusion pipelines (e.g., TensorRT-LLM, vLLM, or custom DiT fused kernels). Providing projected end-to-end latency with fused implementations would strengthen the practical deployment case.
4. **Elevate Video vs. Image QAT Comparison to Main Text:** The gradient norm disparity between video and image DM QAT (Appendix H) is a key motivator for the paper's video-specific focus. Moving this analysis to the main introduction or preliminaries would better contextualize the novelty and necessity of QVGen over existing image-DM quantization methods.

# Spark Finder Review
## How to Improve This Paper

### Missing Experiments (top 3-5 only)
1. **Human Evaluation Study:** VBench relies on vision-language proxies that often misalign with human perception of temporal coherence and visual fidelity. Add a side-by-side human perceptual study comparing QVGen against baselines and full-precision, otherwise the claim of "full-precision comparable quality" lacks rigorous validation.
2. **End-to-End Wall-Clock Latency:** The reported 1.2x–1.44x speedup only profiles the DiT linear projections per-step, ignoring the video VAE, text encoder, and sampling loop which dominate actual generation time. Measure and report full-pipeline latency on a target GPU to substantiate deployment claims.
3. **Overfitting vs. Generalization Test:** Training 2B–14B models on only 16K videos risks memorization. Report VBench/FVD scores on a held-out prompt distribution distinct from the training captions to prove the method generalizes video dynamics rather than overfitting to a narrow data slice.
4. **Apples-to-Apples Baseline Configuration:** SVDQuant uses per-group (g=64) quantization while QVGen uses per-channel. Re-run a strong QAT baseline (e.g., Q-DM) with identical per-group settings to verify QVGen's gains stem from $\Phi$/rank-decay rather than baseline quantization granularity disadvantages.

### Deeper Analysis Needed (top 3-5 only)
1. **Quantization-Specific Convergence Proof:** The theoretical bounds (Thm 3.1, App C) are standard smooth optimization results that do not uniquely address quantization error. Derive or empirically show how $\Phi$ directly reduces the discrete-to-continuous gradient mismatch specifically for diffusion score matching, rather than just general training instability.
2. **Differentiation from LoRA/Adapter Finetuning:** Since $\Phi$ is initialized from weight error and structured as a low-rank update, it functionally resembles LoRA. Analyze how the explicit rank-decay schedule differs from training a standard LoRA adapter and subsequently pruning/fusing it, to justify the novel schedule's complexity and necessity.
3. **Layer-Wise Sensitivity Breakdown:** Applying $\Phi$ uniformly ignores that temporal attention, spatial attention, and FFNs have vastly different quantization sensitivities. Provide layer-type gradient norm reduction and performance retention metrics to justify uniform application or demonstrate targeted efficacy.
4. **Mechanism of Rank Collapse:** The paper claims the model naturally "absorbs" errors, causing $\mathbf{W}_\Phi$ to collapse, but provides no evidence separating natural convergence from schedule-forced decay. Run an ablation that tracks singular value decay *without* the rank-decay schedule to prove the collapse is an emergent optimization property rather than an artifact of $\gamma$.

### Visualizations & Case Studies
1. **Temporal Coherence Failure Modes:** Aggregate metrics hide qualitative breakdowns. Provide frame-sequence visualizations highlighting where 3-bit vs. 4-bit QVGen fails (e.g., object flickering, texture warping, or motion stutter) to transparently expose the method's actual limits.
2. **Singular Value Evolution Heatmaps:** Replace the averaged line plots (Fig 4) with layer-specific heatmaps tracking the singular value distribution of $\mathbf{W}_\Phi$ across Attention vs. FFN blocks during decay phases. This will visually validate whether rank collapse occurs uniformly or is driven by specific architectural components.
3. **Complex Prompt Adherence Cases:** Generate and compare outputs for prompts requiring precise spatial-temporal relationships (e.g., multi-object interactions or specific camera movements) across BF16, 4-bit, and 3-bit models to qualitatively assess text-to-video alignment degradation under quantization.

### Obvious Next Steps
1. **Report Statistical Variance Over Seeds:** All main tables report single-run VBench scores, which is insufficient for diffusion models given stochastic sampling and proxy variance. Provide mean ± standard deviation over 3+ random seeds and seeds for the evaluation metrics to establish statistical reliability.
2. **Data Scale vs. Performance Curve:** Explicitly plot QVGen's VBench recovery as a function of training dataset size (e.g., 4K, 8K, 16K videos) to quantify the data efficiency of the method and identify the minimum calibration size needed for 14B models.
3. **Activation Outlier Analysis:** The paper notes activations are harder to quantize than weights (App L.6) but uses standard per-token dynamic quantization. Quantify activation outlier distributions across layers and timesteps to demonstrate whether activation quantization error remains the dominant bottleneck after applying $\Phi$.

# Final Consolidated Review
## Summary
This paper introduces QVGen, a quantization-aware training (QAT) framework tailored for ultra-low-bit (3- and 4-bit) video diffusion models. By linking QAT convergence stability to gradient norm reduction, the authors introduce trainable auxiliary modules ($\Phi$) to stabilize optimization, followed by a novel rank-decay schedule that iteratively applies SVD and rank-based regularization to progressively eliminate $\Phi$, yielding a fully quantized model with zero inference overhead. Extensive experiments across multiple state-of-the-art video DiTs (1.3B–14B parameters) demonstrate that QVGen achieves near full-precision visual quality under 4-bit quantization, significantly outperforming existing PTQ and QAT baselines.

## Strengths
- **Effective & Methodologically Sound Pipeline:** The synergistic design of gradient-norm-stabilizing auxiliary modules and an SVD-guided decay schedule is conceptually elegant and empirically validated. Results (Fig. 3, Tabs. 1–3) clearly show that $\Phi$ drastically reduces training instability, while progressive rank decay successfully bridges the training-inference gap with negligible quality loss.
- **Comprehensive & Reproducible Empirical Evaluation:** The framework is rigorously tested across four diverse, large-scale video DiTs at multiple bit-widths. Ablations systematically isolate component contributions, and the provision of open code, detailed training/hardware recipes, and transparent efficiency profiling meets high reproducibility standards expected at ICLR.
- **Correct Diagnosis of the Video QAT Bottleneck:** The paper accurately identifies that standard image-focused QAT collapses on video models due to complex spatiotemporal dynamics that amplify gradient instability (Appendix H). Grounding the method in this empirical observation, rather than blindly transplanting image-DM techniques, demonstrates strong domain awareness and practical relevance.

## Weaknesses
- **Limited Theoretical Novelty & Disconnect from Quantization Dynamics:** While Theorem 3.1 and Appendix C correctly establish that minimizing the gradient norm improves convergence, these are standard regret and smoothness bounds for gradient descent. The theory does not uniquely characterize how quantization discretization alters the diffusion score-matching objective or why $\Phi$ specifically bridges the continuous-discrete gradient mismatch. The mathematical framing functions more as diagnostic motivation than a novel convergence guarantee for quantized video models.
- **Absence of Statistical Significance & Robustness Reporting:** All main tables report single-run VBench scores with limited sampling (5 videos per prompt, fixed seeds). Given the high stochasticity of diffusion sampling and known variance in vision-language proxy metrics, the lack of mean $\pm$ std across multiple training/evaluation seeds makes it difficult to verify whether the reported margins over baselines are statistically robust or susceptible to random fluctuation.
- **Missing Direct Comparison to Standard Decaying Low-Rank Adapters:** The rank-decay mechanism relies on iterative SVD to identify and prune low-impact components. However, the paper does not ablate against a standard low-rank adapter (e.g., LoRA-style) that is simply trained alongside the quantized model and decayed to zero without SVD-driven component selection. Without this baseline, it remains unclear whether the computational overhead of the SVD-guided schedule is strictly necessary, or if simpler adapter-decay strategies could yield comparable convergence and final quality.

## Nice-to-Haves
- **End-to-End Deployment Latency Profiling:** The reported speedups (~1.2×–1.44×) isolate linear layer kernels. Providing full-pipeline wall-clock latency (incorporating text encoding, VAE decoding, and attention overhead) would better contextualize real-world deployment impact.
- **Layer-Wise Sensitivity & Uniformity Analysis:** Applying $\Phi$ uniformly across temporal attention, spatial attention, and FFN blocks is simple but likely suboptimal. A brief breakdown of gradient norm reduction and quantization sensitivity per layer type would justify the uniform design or highlight avenues for mixed-precision QAT extensions.
- **Activation Outlier Quantification Across Timesteps:** The paper correctly notes activations are harder to quantize than weights (App. L.6). Quantifying activation outlier magnitude and frequency across early/late diffusion timesteps would clarify whether dynamic per-token quantization remains the dominant bottleneck after weight quantization error is mitigated.

## Novel Insights
The core insight is that ultra-low-bit QAT for video diffusion models is fundamentally constrained by training instability driven by amplified gradient norms, not merely by static quantization discretization error. By empirically linking temporal modeling complexity to gradient explosion and demonstrating that learnable auxiliary modules can absorb this instability during training, the work shifts the paradigm from post-hoc error correction to proactive convergence stabilization. Crucially, the observation that $\Phi$ naturally collapses into a low-rank structure as the main weights gradually adapt to quantized dynamics reveals a "self-regularizing" optimization property. This enables a structured decay schedule that safely bridges the training-inference gap without permanent architectural overhead, a finding that generalizes well beyond the specific video architectures tested.

## Potentially Missed Related Work
- None identified. The paper thoroughly covers PTQ/QAT for diffusion models, video generation acceleration, and low-rank/SVD-based adaptation techniques.

## Suggestions
1. Add statistical variance reporting (mean $\pm$ std over $\ge$ 3 seeds) to all main VBench tables to establish the robustness of the performance claims and satisfy ICLR reproducibility expectations for generative benchmarks.
2. Include a direct ablation comparing the SVD-guided rank-decay schedule against a standard low-rank adapter trained alongside the quantized model and linearly/cosine-decayed to zero without component-level SVD selection. This will isolate whether the SVD step is algorithmically necessary or if the primary benefit stems simply from having a trainable error-compensating adapter.
3. Reframe Theorem 3.1 and Appendix C in the text as empirical motivators for gradient-norm regularization rather than novel convergence proofs specific to quantized diffusion. Explicitly detail how $\Phi$ empirically mitigates quantization-induced loss spikes to maintain mathematical rigor.
4. Move the critical comparison of video vs. image DM gradient norms (currently Appendix H) to the main motivation or preliminaries section. This will strongly justify the necessity of a video-specific QAT framework early in the narrative.
