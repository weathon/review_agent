# Harsh Critic Review
## Section-by-Section Critical Review

### Title & Abstract
- **Does the title accurately reflect the contribution?** Yes. It correctly signals the paper's focus on empirical scaling analysis and methodology rather than introducing a novel algorithm.
- **Does the abstract clearly state the problem, method, and key results?** Yes. The abstract clearly identifies the lack of predictive scaling laws for RL, introduces a sigmoidal compute-performance framework, outlines the large-scale ablation study (>400k GPU-hours), and summarizes the resulting **SCALERL** recipe and its predictability.
- **Are any claims in the abstract unsupported by the paper?** The claim that **SCALERL** "successfully scaling and predicting validation performance on a single RL run scaled up to 100,000 GPU-hours" is well-supported by Figure 1 and Section 5. However, the phrase "establishes a new state-of-the-art" appears in Section 1 (not the abstract) but sets expectations that are slightly misaligned with the actual results: **SCALERL** outperforms the *compared ablation baselines* in asymptotic reward (Figure 2), but the paper does not compare against contemporary public SOTA models on standard downstream benchmarks (e.g., GSM8K, AIME-25 scores relative to published leaderboards). The abstract should be calibrated to reflect that the "state-of-the-art" claim pertains to the *scaling trajectory* within the controlled experimental pool, not necessarily open benchmarks.

### Introduction & Motivation
- **Is the problem well-motivated? Is the gap in prior work clearly identified?** Highly motivated. The contrast between pre-training scaling laws and the ad-hoc nature of current LLM-RL training is timely and clearly articulated. The gap (lack of principled, predictive methodology for RL compute) is well-defined.
- **Are the contributions clearly stated and accurate?** Yes. The three principles and the predictive framework are explicitly stated. The authors accurately frame their contribution as methodological and empirical rather than algorithmic novelty.
- **Does the introduction over-claim or under-sell?** The introduction slightly over-claims in stating **SCALERL** "establishes a new state-of-the-art" (p.3, left col) without immediately clarifying the scope of that claim. It also introduces the "Bitter Lesson" framing, which implies early high-performing methods will strictly underperform at scale; this is supported in some cases (Fig 2), but the paper later clarifies that most design choices affect efficiency ($B$) rather than asymptote ($A$). The tension between "raising ceilings" and "improving efficiency" could be framed more precisely in the contribution list.

### Method / Approach
- **Is the method clearly described and reproducible?** The scaling framework (Eq 1) and fitting procedure are clearly defined. The **SCALERL** recipe is explicitly itemized with references to specific implementation choices. The appendix provides sufficient hyperparameter details for replication in principle, assuming access to comparable compute.
- **Are key assumptions stated and justified?** Two critical assumptions are stated but rely primarily on empirical convenience:
  1. *Sigmoidal over power-law*: Justified in Appendix A.4 with empirical observations on bounded metrics and early-phase fitting instability. While reasonable, the paper lacks a deeper discussion of alternative bounded saturating functions (e.g., Gompertz or inverse logarithmic forms) or theoretical grounding for why RL reward specifically follows this S-curve.
  2. *Excluding early training*: The decision to omit the first ~1.5k GPU hours (Sec 2.1, App A.7) is explicitly labeled a "heuristic chosen empirically." This risks introducing confirmation bias by fitting only the regime where the curve already behaves sigmoidally, potentially inflating the perceived predictive power.
- **Are there logical gaps in the derivation or reasoning?** Not a theoretical paper, so no derivations to critique. Empirically, the logical flow from component ablations to the combined recipe is sound.
- **Are there edge cases or failure modes not discussed?** Truncation-induced instability is thoroughly discussed (App A.16). However, the paper does not discuss how the fitting procedure behaves when training *oscillates* or *diverges* before reaching the saturation regime, which is common in uncurated RL. The framework assumes a monotonic, saturating trajectory.
- **For theoretical claims**: N/A. The work is empirical.

### Experiments & Results
- **Do the experiments actually test the paper's claims?** Yes. The systematic ablations in Section 3 and the LOO experiments in Section 4 directly test which components affect $A$ vs $B$, and whether extrapolation holds. Section 5 robustly tests cross-axis scaling.
- **Are baselines appropriate and fairly compared?** This is the weakest point. In Appendix A.17, the authors modify the DAPO and MiniMax baselines by *increasing* their batch size to 1280 and disabling dynamic sampling to avoid system inefficiencies. While practically motivated, giving selected baselines a larger batch size inherently boosts their compute efficiency and potentially their asymptotic reward, confounding the comparison with **SCALERL** (batch 768). The paper should either equalize batch sizes across all recipes or clearly ablate batch size effects on those specific baselines to prove the win is algorithmic, not infrastructural. Additionally, the GRPO baseline destabilizes at 6k hours (Sec 2.2), which is expected given its known entropy collapse issues; comparing its truncated curve to extrapolated SCALERL curves should be explicitly caveated.
- **Are there missing ablations that would materially change conclusions?** The learning rate schedule is constant. A standard practice in large-scale RL is cosine decay or step decay, which can affect final asymptote and stability. Not abling this leaves open whether the predictable scaling is tied to the specific constant LR regime.
- **Are error bars / statistical significance reported?** No, with one exception. Appendix A.5 reports a ±0.02 variance for $A$ across 3 seeds for a single run. However, all primary figures (Fig 1, 2, 4, 6, 12) show single-run trajectories without confidence intervals or error bands. Given the high stochasticity of RL rollouts and generation randomness, reporting only point estimates weakens the statistical rigor expected at ICLR.
- **Do the results support the claims made, or are they cherry-picked?** The extrapolation results strongly support predictability for stable recipes. However, the claim that "Common interventions... do not materially shift the asymptote" is somewhat contradicted by the FP32 fix (Fig 4c shows a jump from 0.52 to 0.61) and loss type (Fig 4b shows substantial gains for CISPO/GSPO). The authors clarify in Section 4 that within the fully optimized SCALERL recipe, LOO changes are smaller, but the absolute impact of these components on raw performance is indeed material.
- **Are datasets and evaluation metrics appropriate?** Polaris-53k is a suitable math-reasoning dataset. Using in-distribution validation pass rate for fitting is standard for scaling laws, but the downstream generalization claim (AIME-24, Fig 1b) would be strengthened with quantitative correlation metrics (e.g., Pearson/Spearman coefficients between val pass rate and downstream scores) rather than qualitative visual alignment.

### Writing & Clarity
- **Are there sections that are confusing or poorly explained?
  * *Compute Metric Ambiguity:* The paper consistently uses "GPU-hours" as the compute metric $C$. However, GPU-hours are hardware-dependent and conflate algorithmic efficiency with system throughput. The authors acknowledge PipelineRL gains come from system efficiency (App A.20, Fig 24), yet they are plotted on the same axis as algorithmic improvements. It would be clearer to discuss whether the scaling parameters $A, B, C_{mid}$ generalize to token counts or FLOPs.
  * *Equation/Notation Issues:* Due to the parser, some equations are garbled, but the conceptual definitions (e.g., importance ratios, advantage normalization) are standard and decipherable. The notation for asymmetric clipping (Eq 3) is slightly cluttered but standard in RL literature.
  * *LOO Visualization:* Figure 5 transforms the curve to linearize efficiency ($F(R_c) = C^B$). While mathematically valid, this transformation obscures the original sigmoidal extrapolation claim for the reader. A supplementary plot showing the raw LOO curves with extrapolation regions would improve clarity.
- **Are figures and tables clear and informative?** Generally yes. Figure 1 and 2 are compelling. Figure 5 is clever but dense. Table 1 is useful. Some figure captions (e.g., Fig 12 acronyms like "loo-plan") require the reader to hunt the appendix for definitions.

### Limitations & Broader Impact
- **Do the authors acknowledge the key limitations?** Yes. Section 7 explicitly notes: focus on math domain, preliminary results for multi-task, constant LR, single data distribution validation vs test generalization gap, and that SCALERL is not the final word.
- **Are there fundamental limitations they missed?**
  1. *Reproducibility Barrier:* The 400k GPU-hour cost, while impressive, makes the empirical findings effectively inaccessible to most researchers. The paper lacks a "low-compute" validation showing the framework's predictive accuracy on a fraction of the budget (e.g., <5k hours), which would be more actionable for the community.
  2. *Task Specificity:* The results are heavily tied to verifiable reasoning tasks with binary/sparse rewards. It is unclear if this sigmoidal framework generalizes to dense-reward settings, multi-turn agentic loops, or RLHF with human preference models.
  3. *Hardware Lock-in:* Results rely on specific GPU types (GB200) and asynchronous infra. System-level bottlenecks may shift the observed $C_{mid}$ and $B$ on different clusters.
- **Are there failure modes or negative societal impacts not discussed?** Standard for systems papers to omit, but given the focus on scaling compute, a brief mention of the environmental/cost implications of blindly scaling RL to 100k+ hours without guaranteed downstream gains would align with responsible AI practices.

### Overall Assessment
This paper presents a rigorous, large-scale empirical study that addresses a significant gap in LLM post-training: the lack of predictive scaling laws for RL. The proposal of a sigmoidal compute-performance framework and the systematic derivation of the **SCALERL** recipe are valuable contributions. The extrapolation results up to 100k GPU-hours provide compelling evidence that stable RL training can be predictable, bringing the field closer to pre-training methodology standards. However, the paper has notable weaknesses that should be addressed for a strong ICLR acceptance. Specifically, the lack of statistical error bars across main figures, potential confounding in baseline comparisons (batch size modifications and disabled dynamic sampling for DAPO/MiniMax), and the heuristic exclusion of early training data for fitting weaken the statistical and methodological rigor. Additionally, the reliance on "GPU-hours" rather than hardware-agnostic metrics and the heavy computational barrier to reproduction limit the immediate accessibility of the findings. If the authors can provide statistical robustness metrics, clarify baseline fairness, and discuss the generalizability of the fitting heuristic, this work would represent a highly impactful methodological advance for the RLHF and LLM scaling communities.

# Neutral Reviewer
## Balanced Review

### Summary
This paper introduces a systematic framework for predicting and scaling reinforcement learning compute in large language models, proposing a sigmoidal compute-performance curve to jointly model asymptotic reward ceilings and sample efficiency. Through an extensive empirical study spanning over 400,000 GPU-hours, the authors ablate key RL training design choices (loss type, precision, off-policy setup, normalization, curriculum) and consolidate them into a unified recipe, **SCALERL**. The authors demonstrate that performance can be reliably extrapolated from mid-scale runs (~16k GPU-hours) to extreme compute budgets (>100k GPU-hours) across different model sizes, batch configurations, and task mixtures.

### Strengths
1. **Unprecedented Empirical Scale & Practical Impact:** The study encompasses >400,000 GPU-hours, culminating in single runs of 100k (8B dense) and 50k (17B×16 MoE) GPU-hours (Sec 5, Fig 1). This scale directly addresses the field's current lack of predictable scaling benchmarks and provides concrete evidence that stable RL training trajectories exist at the compute levels used by frontier labs.
2. **Clear, Interpretable Predictive Framework:** The parametric sigmoidal model (Eq 1 with parameters $A$, $B$, $C_{\text{mid}}$) cleanly decouples asymptotic performance ceilings from compute efficiency. As shown in Fig 3 and Sec 2.1, this allows practitioners to forecast RL outcomes early in training and compare algorithmic scalability without waiting for convergence, a significant methodological advance for RL engineering.
3. **Rigorous Forward & Leave-One-Out (LOO) Ablations:** The paper systematically isolates the impact of each design axis (loss aggregation, advantage normalization, FP32 precision, off-policy setup, zero-variance filtering, curriculum) via forward sweeps (Sec 3) and LOO experiments at 16k GPU-hours (Sec 4, Fig 5). This design robustness confirms that individual components synergize rather than cancel each other out at scale.
4. **Actionable Stability Diagnostics:** The empirical analysis of importance sampling mismatches (Sec A.19, Figs 21-23), truncation-induced instability (Sec A.16), and the sensitivity of DAPO's clipping hyperparameter (Sec A.18.1) provides highly practical debugging guidelines that will reduce wasted compute in large-scale RL runs.

### Weaknesses
1. **Limited Algorithmic Conceptual Novelty:** **SCALERL** is a synthesis of existing components (CISPO/mini-max loss, PipelineRL, FP32 logits, prompt averaging, zero-variance filtering, interruption-based truncation) rather than a new algorithmic foundation (Sec 3-4). While the empirical validation is strong, the paper lacks theoretical analysis or novel mechanistic insights into *why* these specific choices interact to shift the asymptote $A$, positioning it closer to an engineering benchmark than a conceptual advance.
2. **Heuristic Curve-Fitting Lacks Statistical Rigor:** The fitting procedure relies on a grid search over $A \in \{0.45, 0.455, \dots, 0.8\}$ and linearly spaced $C_{\text{mid}}$ values, followed by fitting $B$ (Sec A.5). The arbitrary exclusion of the first ~1.5k GPU-hours, absence of confidence intervals, residual diagnostics, or formal goodness-of-fit metrics (e.g., $R^2$, bootstrapped error bands) weakens the statistical validity of the extrapolation claims.
3. **Narrow Task & Reward Scope:** The framework is evaluated almost exclusively on math reasoning with binary, outcome-verifiable rewards (Polaris-53k dataset, Sec A.3). It does not test dense/process rewards, learned reward models (RLHF), or stochastic agentic environments, limiting claims about the generalizability of the sigmoidal scaling laws to broader RLVR regimes.
4. **High Compute Barrier for Reproduction:** While the framework aims to make scaling predictable, validating the LOO results individually requires ~16k GPU-hours per configuration (Sec 4). The promised code release only covers curve fitting, leaving the extensive hyperparameter sweeps and system-level optimizations (PipelineRL streaming, FP32 kernel integration) inaccessible to most academic labs, which conflicts with ICLR's emphasis on reproducible, community-verifiable science.

### Novelty & Significance
The paper's significance is high for the LLM post-training and RL communities, which currently lack standardized, predictive methodologies for scaling compute beyond ad-hoc recipe sharing. By formalizing a compute-performance forecasting framework and empirically validating it at scales comparable to frontier training runs, the work establishes much-needed methodological rigor and reduces the financial risk of exploring new RL algorithms. However, its novelty is primarily empirical and compositional rather than algorithmic or theoretical. The use of sigmoidal curves to model bounded metrics is a known practice in pre-training literature, adapted here to RL without deriving new principles specific to off-policy gradient dynamics or distributional shift. For ICLR, the paper will be highly valued as a reference for empirical RL scaling and a stable baseline, but may face scrutiny for its heavy reliance on compute and lack of deeper analytical grounding.

### Suggestions for Improvement
1. **Strengthen Statistical Rigor of Scaling Fits:** Report formal confidence intervals (e.g., via non-parametric bootstrapping), include residual analysis, and compute goodness-of-fit metrics ($R^2$, AIC/BIC) for all extrapolations. Conduct a sensitivity analysis on the 1.5k GPU-hour cutoff to prove that parameter estimates remain stable across different early-exclusion windows.
2. **Expand Evaluation to Diverse Reward Regimes:** Validate the predictive framework on at least one setting with dense/process verifiers or a learned reward model (RLHF). Discuss how reward sparsity, stochasticity, and non-stationary reward distributions affect the sigmoidal parameters, particularly the asymptote $A$ and the required compute threshold $C_{\text{mid}}$.
3. **Clarify Discovery vs. Validation Pipeline & Compute Budget:** Explicitly document how the **SCALERL** components were identified (exhaustive sweep vs. literature-informed hypothesis). Provide a transparent compute breakdown showing how many GPU-hours were spent on exploratory ablations versus final validation, helping smaller labs understand the trade-offs when adapting the framework.
4. **Provide Mechanistic/Theoretical Grounding:** Elevate the work beyond empirical composition by hypothesizing or analyzing why specific choices (CISPO's truncated IS, FP32 logit precision, PipelineRL's streaming updates) collectively raise the asymptotic ceiling $A$ rather than merely improving efficiency $B$. Linking these to reduced gradient variance, tighter policy drift bounds, or stabilized importance weights would significantly strengthen the scientific contribution.

# Spark Finder Review
## How to Improve This Paper

### Missing Experiments (top 3-5 only)
1. Vary dataset size while scaling compute (e.g., 50% vs 100% vs 200% of Polaris-53k) to disentangle true algorithmic compute scaling from dataset exhaustion. Without this, the observed saturation is indistinguishable from standard epoch-limited memorization, directly undermining the "RL scaling law" claim.
2. Run formal model selection (AIC/BIC, cross-validation, or Bayesian predictive checks) comparing sigmoidal and power-law fits across multiple seeds. Heuristic residual sums and visual alignment are insufficient for ICLR to claim functional superiority.
3. Re-evaluate baseline recipes (GRPO, DAPO) with identical learning rate schedules, KL regularization, and entropy control sweeps applied to SCALERL. The baselines appear to hit lower ceilings likely due to suboptimal tuning, which artificially inflates SCALERL's asymptotic advantage.

### Deeper Analysis Needed (top 3-5 only)
1. Quantitatively reconcile the contradiction between the claim "RL Performance Ceilings are Not Universal" and the LOO results, which show nearly identical asymptotic A (±0.02) across all variants. The paper must clarify when design choices actually shift the ceiling versus merely accelerating convergence speed.
2. Provide statistical confidence intervals for extrapolated predictions, especially for asymptote A. Fitting a saturating curve with ~20–30 mid-training points is ill-conditioned; reporting only point estimates masks the true uncertainty of 50k-to-100k extrapolations.
3. Analyze the correlation strength between in-distribution validation fits and downstream generalization (AIME-24/LiveCodeBench) across compared recipes. If the framework predicts training distribution accuracy but fails to track out-of-distribution generalization, its utility for capability forecasting is limited.

### Visualizations & Case Studies
1. Plot shaded prediction intervals for the 100k GPU-hour extrapolation and cross-recipe comparisons. Marker-only extrapolations hide fitting variance and misrepresent predictive certainty, making the "predictable scaling" claim appear artificially tight.
2. Provide longitudinal plots of truncation rates, effective batch sizes, and KL/entropy drift for all baselines versus SCALERL. This exposes whether observed baseline failures are algorithmic or merely stem from uncontrolled length explosion and effective batch collapse.
3. Show a concrete failure regime where the sigmoidal fit significantly diverges from actual performance after 8k GPU hours, with analysis of the triggering pathology. Demonstrating boundary conditions and failure modes is required to trust the framework's operational range.

### Obvious Next Steps
1. Explicitly report total training epochs passed over Polaris-53k at each compute milestone and formalize how multi-epoch RL scaling differs from single-epoch pre-training scaling laws. Omitting this conflates data reuse with compute scaling, invalidating the direct analogy to Kaplan/Hoffmann paradigms.
2. Open-source the complete training infrastructure, random seeds, and exact hyperparameter configurations for all 400k GPU-hour runs, not just post-hoc curve-fitting scripts. ICLR requires full pipeline reproducibility to validate scaling claims of this magnitude.
3. Validate the framework on non-verifiable or densely rewarded tasks (e.g., open-ended instruction following, RLHF with learned reward models) to prove the sigmoidal relationship generalizes beyond deterministic math/code verification. A universal RL scaling methodology cannot be claimed from a single sparse-reward domain.

# Final Consolidated Review
## Summary
This paper presents a large-scale empirical study (>400,000 GPU-hours) that establishes a sigmoidal compute-performance framework to predict and analyze reinforcement learning (RL) training dynamics for LLMs. By systematically ablating key design choices across algorithmic, system, and curriculum axes, the authors derive the **SCALERL** recipe and demonstrate that stable training configurations follow highly predictable trajectories, enabling accurate extrapolation from mid-scale runs (~16k GPU-hours) to extreme budgets (>100,000 GPU-hours) across varying model sizes, batch configurations, and context lengths.

## Strengths
- **Empirical scale and systematic methodology:** The study rigorously isolates design axes (loss type, precision, asynchronous setup, normalization, filtering) through forward and leave-one-out (LOO) ablations at unprecedented compute budgets. This provides concrete, reproducible evidence that stable RL training saturates predictably rather than diverging chaotically at scale.
- **Actionable stability diagnostics:** The paper identifies and resolves critical failure modes in large-scale RL (e.g., FP32 logit mismatches distorting importance sampling, truncation-induced batch collapse, DAPO's clipping sensitivity). Linking engineering choices like CISPO and PipelineRL to robust, predictable scaling offers immediate practical value for frontier training pipelines.

## Weaknesses
- **Conflation of algorithmic scaling with system throughput:** The primary compute metric is wall-clock GPU-hours, which inherently mixes algorithmic sample efficiency with hardware/streaming optimizations. As acknowledged in Appendix A.20, PipelineRL gains stem largely from reduced generator-trainer idle time rather than improved gradient statistics. Without normalizing to hardware-agnostic metrics (e.g., tokens processed, effective FLOPs, or data coverage), the claim of establishing an "algorithmic RL scaling law" is materially undermined, as axis shifts like batch size or off-policy streaming confound system throughput with learning dynamics.
- **Lack of statistical rigor for extrapolation claims:** The framework relies on heuristic curve-fitting (single-run point estimates, manual early-training cutoffs) without reporting confidence intervals, residual diagnostics, or formal model selection (e.g., AIC/BIC, bootstrapped error bands). Extrapolating to 100k hours from ~20–30 mid-training evaluation points is inherently ill-conditioned; presenting tight deterministic fits without uncertainty quantification misrepresents the true predictive bounds and inflates the perceived reliability of the forecasting framework.
- **Entanglement of compute scaling with fixed data regimes and narrow reward scope:** All scaling experiments operate by running multiple epochs over a fixed 53k-prompt dataset, making it impossible to disentangle true algorithmic compute scaling from dataset saturation/epoch-limited memorization. Furthermore, restricting validation to binary, verifiable math/code rewards severely limits claims about generalizability. RLHF with learned reward models, dense/process rewards, or stochastic multi-agent environments exhibit non-stationary reward distributions and covariate shift that likely invalidate a simple sigmoidal bound, yet the paper does not establish whether the framework even conceptually applies beyond sparse, deterministic verification.

## Nice-to-Haves
- Quantify the correlation strength (e.g., Pearson/Spearman $\rho$, rank alignment) between in-distribution validation fits and downstream generalization (AIME/LiveCodeBench) across all compared recipes to empirically validate the forecasting utility beyond qualitative curve matching.
- Open-source the full training infrastructure, random seeds, and exact configurations beyond the post-hoc curve-fitting script to lower the reproduction barrier and enable community verification of the 400k-hour methodology.
- Include a dedicated low-compute validation regime (<5k GPU-hours) demonstrating that the fitting methodology remains stable and predictive when extrapolating from earlier, noisier phases typical of academic or under-resourced settings.

## Novel Insights
The paper compellingly reframes large-scale RL training from an unstable, heuristic-driven process to a structured engineering problem governed by a decoupled efficiency-ceiling trade-off. A genuinely useful observation emerges from the LOO ablations: while isolated components (e.g., FP32 precision, loss truncation, zero-variance filtering) can dramatically shift the asymptotic ceiling when applied to naive baselines, their marginal impact diminishes once combined into a stable recipe, primarily modulating compute efficiency ($B$) rather than the final ceiling ($A$). This highlights that predictable RL scaling at frontier budgets is bottlenecked not by novel algorithmic breakthroughs, but by "boring" stability engineering that prevents distribution collapse and gradient distortion, effectively demystifying the field's current reliance on ad-hoc recipe sharing and providing a concrete methodology for forecasting compute allocation.

## Potentially Missed Related Work
- Recent work on data-constrained scaling and optimal compute-data tradeoffs (e.g., Muennighoff et al., 2025) would strengthen the discussion on why multi-epoch RLVR behaves differently from pre-training and how dataset saturation might artificially cap the observed asymptote.
- Statistical diagnosis studies of RL post-training (e.g., Vattikonda et al., 2026) and online curriculum scaling (e.g., SPEED-RL) could be integrated to contextualize how the observed efficiency parameters ($B$, $C_{\text{mid}}$) might shift under dynamic data mixing or dense reward feedback.

## Suggestions
- Replace or supplement GPU-hours with hardware-agnostic metrics (tokens/FLOPs per update) in primary scaling plots to cleanly isolate algorithmic improvements from system throughputs, and explicitly decouple the $A/B/C_{\text{mid}}$ parameters from pipeline efficiency gains.
- Implement formal statistical validation for the scaling framework: report bootstrapped 95% confidence intervals for asymptote $A$ and efficiency $B$, provide goodness-of-fit metrics ($R^2$, AIC/BIC), and perform sensitivity analysis on the early-training exclusion threshold using formal residual diagnostics rather than heuristic justification.
- Conduct controlled sweeps over dataset size (e.g., 25%, 50%, 100% of Polaris) while holding compute fixed to explicitly disentangle multi-epoch data reuse/compute scaling from algorithmic learning curves.
- Re-run key baseline comparisons (DAPO, GRPO) with matched hyperparameter search budgets (clipping thresholds, LR schedules, entropy/KL controls) and identical batch sizes to conclusively verify that observed asymptote gaps reflect fundamental algorithmic ceilings rather than optimization asymmetries.
