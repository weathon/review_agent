# Harsh Critic Review
## Section-by-Section Critical Review

### Title & Abstract
- **Accuracy**: The title accurately reflects the paper's focus: empirical analysis of how RL training compute scales for LLMs and the development of a stable recipe. 
- **Clarity**: The abstract effectively outlines the problem (lack of predictive RL scaling methodology), the method (400k GPU-hours of systematic ablation, sigmoidal curve fitting, recipe consolidation), and key results (asymptotic ceilings vary by algorithm, most design choices modulate efficiency, SCALERL predicts 100k GPU-hour behavior). 
- **Unsupported Claims**: The claim that SCALERL "brings RL training closer to the predictability long achieved in pre-training" is slightly overstated. Pre-training scaling laws robustly predict both loss and downstream generalization across data/model/compute axes. Here, the authors explicitly acknowledge in §7 and Appendix A.13/A.15 that in-distribution validation fits do *not* consistently predict downstream generalization for certain knobs (e.g., batch size, generation length). The abstract should temper this predictability claim or explicitly limit it to in-distribution validation trajectories.

### Introduction & Motivation
- **Motivation & Gap**: The motivation is strong and timely. RL for reasoning has become compute-intensive, yet the community relies on fragmented, model-specific reports. The gap—lack of a predictable, algorithm-agnostic framework for evaluating RL compute efficiency vs. asymptotic ceilings—is clearly identified and highly relevant to ICLR's focus on foundational ML methodology.
- **Contributions**: Clearly stated: (1) predictive sigmoidal scaling framework, (2) large-scale ablation principles, (3) SCALERL recipe validated at 100k GPU-hours. 
- **Over/Under-claiming**: The introduction frames the work as building the "science of RL scaling." While the empirical rigor is substantial, the work remains largely phenomenological (curve-fitting) rather than mechanistic. This is not a fatal flaw, but the authors should avoid equating stable curve-fitting with a fundamental scientific theory. The three principles listed are accurate, though the tension between "RL ceilings are not universal" and "details primarily modulate compute efficiency without materially shifting the asymptote" needs tighter phrasing: the authors later clarify that only *loss type, FP32 precision, and data curriculum* significantly shift $A$, while others affect $B$. This should be explicitly reconciled in the introduction.

### Method / Approach
- **Clarity & Reproducibility**: The method is described with high operational clarity. The generator-trainer split, dataset (Polaris-53k), batch construction, hyperparameters (LR, $\epsilon$, sequence lengths), and hardware are well documented. The promised code repository and detailed Appendix (A.3, A.5) strongly support reproducibility.
- **Assumptions & Justification**: The choice of a sigmoidal fit over a power-law is empirically justified in Appendix A.4 (bounded metric, robust extrapolation from low-compute regimes). This is reasonable for RL post-training, but remains a heuristic ansatz. The decision to exclude the initial $\sim$1.5k GPU-hour regime from fitting is defended empirically (early instabilities, entropy burn-in), but the threshold feels somewhat arbitrary. A principled criterion (e.g., when gradient variance stabilizes, or when mean@16 exceeds a baseline) would strengthen the framework.
- **Logical Gaps**: The framework treats compute scaling purely as running more epochs over a fixed prompt distribution. In pre-training, compute scaling inextricably involves data scaling. Here, repeated exposure to the same prompts introduces memorization/overfitting ceilings that the sigmoidal $A$ parameter conflates with algorithmic limits. The paper does not disentangle whether higher $A$ comes from better optimization or from deeper data memorization.
- **Edge Cases/Failure Modes**: The method robustly addresses truncation-induced instability (§A.16) and numerical mismatch via FP32 logits. However, it does not extensively discuss reward model collapse, entropy exhaustion, or prompt distribution shift, which become prominent when scaling beyond fixed data distributions.

### Experiments & Results
- **Testing Claims**: The experiments directly test the predictability claim by extrapolating from 8k→16k, 16k→30k, and 50k→100k GPU-hours. The LOO ablations (§4) and cross-axis scaling (§5) effectively validate the recipe. Comparisons to GRPO, DAPO, Magistral, and MiniMax-M1 (§2, Fig 2) ground the work against current practice.
- **Baselines & Fairness**: Baselines are appropriate and fairly compared. The authors transparently adjust baseline batch sizes upward (to 1280) when 0-variance filtering drops prompts, preventing unfair sample-efficiency advantages. This is rigorous.
- **Missing Ablations**: A critical missing ablation is **data scaling vs. compute scaling**. The study increases compute via more epochs, not more unique prompts. Given that RLVR performance is heavily data-bound, isolating whether SCALERL's predictive curve holds when prompt count scales proportionally to compute would significantly strengthen the framework.
- **Statistical Significance & Error Bars**: The paper reports a $\pm 0.02$ error margin on $A$ based on three independent runs (Fig 7a), which is honest but sparse. Most figures show single fitted curves without confidence intervals on the regression itself. Given ICLR's emphasis on statistical rigor, reporting bootstrapped confidence bounds on $A$ and $B$ or running more seeds would improve confidence in the extrapolation claims.
- **Cherry-picking / Claim Alignment**: The results strongly support compute efficiency and stability claims. However, the predictability of *downstream* performance is nuanced. Figure 6c and Appendix A.13 explicitly show that in-distribution validation curves can be misleading for generalization (e.g., small batches plateau on AIME despite good validation fits). The authors acknowledge this, but it partially undermines the abstract/conclusion framing of "predictable scaling." The paper should more explicitly distinguish *in-distribution predictability* (achieved) from *out-of-distribution predictability* (partially achieved, depends on scaling knob).
- **Datasets/Metrics**: Polaris-53k for training and AIME-24/MATH-500 for downstream evaluation are standard and appropriate. The mean@16 pass rate is the correct metric for reasoning RL.

### Writing & Clarity
- **Clarity**: Overall, the paper is well-structured and readable. The progression from baseline ablations → LOO validation → large-scale extrapolation is logical.
- **Confusing Sections**: The transformation of the sigmoidal fit to isolate efficiency in Figure 5 is mathematically correct but pedagogically opaque. The text states they "re-arrange Equation (1) into $F(R_C) = C^B$... and plot log $F$ vs. log $C$," but it takes several reads to realize $A$ is fixed to the cross-run mean precisely to compare slopes. A clearer, step-by-step derivation or a direct reference to linearization of the sigmoid would improve accessibility.
- **Figures/Tables**: Figures are highly informative. Figure 2's comparison of prevalent methods is excellent. Figure 3's schematic of $A, B, C_{\text{mid}}$ aids intuition. Figure 12 (Appendix) is slightly cluttered with too many overlapping LOO curves but remains interpretable. Table 1 in the appendix usefully summarizes parameters. No major clarity impediments exist beyond the Fig 5 explanation.

### Limitations & Broader Impact
- **Acknowledged Limitations**: The authors honestly discuss the generalization gap between in-distribution validation and downstream benchmarks (§7, §A.15), the fixed domain (math), and the heuristic nature of some design choices. They also note that larger batches/generation lengths lift ceilings but reduce sample efficiency.
- **Fundamental Missed Limitations**: 
  1. **Data Saturation**: The framework assumes a fixed prompt distribution. The asymptotic parameter $A$ likely conflates algorithmic optimality with the information ceiling of Polaris-53k. Without varying dataset size, the scaling law cannot predict how $A$ shifts when novel data is introduced.
  2. **Compute Accessibility**: Training at 400k-100k GPU-hours is infeasible for academia. While the methodology enables cheaper extrapolation, the baseline recipe development still requires frontier resources. The societal/academic impact should address how this methodology lowers (or fails to lower) barriers to entry.
  3. **Alignment/Safety**: Scaling RL compute for reasoning can exacerbate reward hacking, deceptive alignment, or capability overhang. While outside the core scope, a brief acknowledgment of safety implications given the compute scale would align with ICLR's broader impact expectations.
- **Failure Modes**: High truncation rates (>10-15%) are correctly identified as instability triggers, and the paper provides mitigation (interruptions, larger models, FP32). The entropy trajectory analysis (§A.13) appropriately notes that entropy alone is not a reliable training health metric.

### Overall Assessment
This is a strong, timely contribution that addresses a critical methodological gap in RL for LLMs: the transition from ad-hoc, recipe-chasing to predictable, compute-aware algorithm design. The empirical scale (400k GPU-hours), comprehensive ablations, and release of code meet high standards for rigor and reproducibility expected at ICLR. The primary conceptual weaknesses are (1) the phenomological nature of the sigmoidal fitting framework, which conflates optimization efficiency with data memorization ceilings, and (2) the acknowledged but structurally problematic divergence between in-distribution validation predictability and out-of-distribution generalization for certain scaling knobs (notably batch size). Addressing the arbitrary nature of the 1.5k GPU-hour fitting cutoff, providing more statistical confidence on curve parameters, and explicitly separating data scaling from training compute scaling would meaningfully elevate the work. Despite these concerns, the paper delivers actionable, empirically validated insights and a stable recipe that will directly benefit practitioners scaling RL post-training. I recommend **accept** (or **strong accept** with minor revisions) as it aligns well with ICLR's emphasis on systematic empirical methodology and reproducible scaling studies.

# Neutral Reviewer
## Balanced Review

### Summary
The paper introduces a principled framework for predicting reinforcement learning (RL) performance in LLMs as compute scales, proposing a sigmoidal compute-performance curve characterized by asymptotic ceiling ($A$) and efficiency ($B$, $C_{mid}$) parameters. Through a systematic 400,000 GPU-hour ablation study across loss functions, precision handling, data filtering, and asynchronous training setups, the authors consolidate best practices into the SCALERL recipe. They demonstrate that SCALERL scales stably to 100,000 GPU-hours, with curves extrapolated from early training phases accurately predicting long-horizon performance across multiple scaling axes.

### Strengths
1. **Unprecedented empirical scale and systematic rigor:** The study spans ~400,000 GPU-hours with tightly controlled ablations across six major design axes (loss type, aggregation, advantage normalization, precision, off-policy setup, curriculum). This scale and methodological thoroughness provide high-confidence empirical grounding rarely seen in RL scaling literature (Sections 3–4, Appendix A).
2. **Clear, predictive scaling framework:** The sigmoidal parameterization ($A$, $B$, $C_{mid}$) successfully decouples asymptotic performance from compute efficiency. The framework enables low-cost extrapolation (e.g., fitting on ≤16k hours to predict 100k-hour runs) and is empirically validated, as extrapolated curves closely match extended training trajectories (Figures 1, 2, 5).
3. **Strong evidence for practical best practices:** The leave-one-out (LOO) experiments convincingly show that while individual components often yield similar asymptotes within SCALERL, they collectively improve training stability and compute efficiency (Figure 5, Appendix A.10). The consistent behavior across dense 8B, 17B×16 MoE, varied batch sizes, and multi-task settings demonstrates robust recipe transferability (Section 5).
4. **Accessible and reproducible methodology:** The paper transparently details fitting procedures, hyperparameter choices, truncation monitoring, and system setup (Appendix A.3–A.5). The promised release of code for curve-fitting and recipe implementation lowers the barrier for academic groups lacking frontier compute.

### Weaknesses
1. **Narrow task domain:** The framework is evaluated almost exclusively on verifiable math reasoning with binary rewards. The paper acknowledges this limitation but does not test whether the sigmoidal scaling or SCALERL components hold for reward-model-based RLHF, dense/process rewards, or agentic interaction loops, where reward sparsity and variance differ substantially (Section 7, Discussion).
2. **Empirical but under-theorized curve choice:** While the sigmoidal form is justified empirically over power laws (Appendix A.4), the paper lacks a mechanistic or theoretical explanation for why RL performance specifically follows this saturating trajectory. The high-compute equivalence to power laws is noted but not explored in terms of underlying optimization dynamics or capacity limits.
3. **Ad-hoc fitting procedure:** The grid search over $A$ and $C_{mid}$ followed by $B$ fitting is somewhat heuristic. The paper reports a $\pm 0.02$ error margin for $A$ based on three random seeds but does not provide confidence intervals, sensitivity to initial conditions, or statistical tests for comparing fits across recipes, which limits rigorous scientific comparison (Appendix A.5).
4. **Limited analysis of distributional shift:** Predictive scaling is measured on in-distribution validation data held out from the training prompt pool. While downstream correlations are shown (Appendix A.15), the paper does not quantify the prediction error gap between in-distribution validation fit accuracy and true out-of-distribution generalization (e.g., AIME/MATH-500), leaving open whether the framework predicts *generalization* or merely *memorization/overfitting* on the prompt distribution.

### Novelty & Significance
The paper's novelty lies primarily in its methodological contribution: establishing the first predictive, parameterized framework for RL compute scaling in LLMs, rather than proposing a new base algorithm. SCALERL itself is a principled synthesis of existing techniques, but the scientific value comes from systematically disentangling what drives compute efficiency versus asymptotic ceilings across a massive empirical sweep. This directly addresses a critical bottleneck in frontier LLM development, where RL scaling is currently treated as an expensive black box. The work is highly significant for ICLR's audience, as it provides academically accessible tools to evaluate RL designs at small scale, formalizes best practices that stabilize long-horizon training, and bridges the gap between pre-training scaling laws and post-training RL. While algorithmically incremental, the framework-level novelty and empirical rigor align well with ICLR's emphasis on foundational, reproducible ML science.

### Suggestions for Improvement
1. **Broaden domain validation:** Evaluate the scaling framework and SCALERL on at least one non-verifiable setting (e.g., RLHF with a learned reward model, instruction following with process rewards, or a lightweight agentic task) to demonstrate that the sigmoidal predictability and recipe transferability are not artifacts of binary math rewards.
2. **Formalize fitting and uncertainty quantification:** Replace or supplement the grid search with standard non-linear least squares (e.g., Levenberg-Marquardt) and report bootstrapped or Bayesian confidence intervals for $A$, $B$, and $C_{mid}$. Include prediction uncertainty bands on extrapolation plots (Figures 1, 2, 6) to clarify the statistical reliability of early-run forecasts.
3. **Quantify ID-to-OOD prediction fidelity:** Explicitly measure and report the correlation and absolute prediction error between in-distribution validation extrapolations and downstream benchmark scores (AIME, MATH-500). A brief analysis of when and why the validation proxy diverges from generalization would strengthen the practical utility of the framework.
4. **Clarify compute accounting and hardware dependency:** Specify whether "GPU hours" reflects raw wall-clock time multiplied by allocated GPUs, or accounts for generator/trainer utilization, I/O bottlenecks, and system-level overhead. Hardware-aware normalization would make the scaling curves more transferable to groups with different cluster configurations.
5. **Address curriculum risks:** Briefly discuss the long-term implications of "No-Positive-Resampling" (Appendix A.9, Fig 11b). While filtering easy prompts improves efficiency, it may implicitly alter training distribution dynamics. A short ablation or discussion on whether permanently excluding prompts affects performance on those specific prompts at evaluation would complete the empirical picture.

# Spark Finder Review
## How to Improve This Paper

### Missing Experiments (top 3-5 only)
1. Add downstream benchmark extrapolation (fit sigmoidal curves directly on AIME/LiveCodeBench) because without it, the claim that predictable scaling unlocks real-world capabilities remains an unproven correlation with in-distribution pass rate.
2. Add a sensitivity analysis of the early-compute exclusion cutoff (testing 0.5k, 2k, 3k GPU hours) because if heuristic boundary shifts drastically alter the extrapolated asymptote, the framework's scientific robustness and practical predictability collapse.
3. Add scaling runs on at least one intermediate model size (e.g., 13B or 70B dense) because a two-point comparison (8B to 17Bx16 MoE) cannot establish model-size invariance, undermining the generality of the proposed scaling law.
4. Add explicit stability baselines that artificially induce truncation or off-policy staleness to quantify the breaking point of the sigmoidal fit, because the framework's utility for *selecting* scalable methods depends on its ability to flag non-scalable regimes, not just fit stable ones.

### Deeper Analysis Needed (top 3-5 only)
1. Add bootstrap confidence intervals for fitted parameters $A$, $B$, and $C_{mid}$ across multiple training seeds because point estimates alone lack statistical rigor, making the core claim of "predictable extrapolation" unverifiable for real compute allocation.
2. Add gradient norm or effective sample size analysis to explain why certain choices (e.g., FP32 logits, CISPO loss) shift the asymptote $A$ versus efficiency $B$, because purely empirical parameter tracking fails to establish mechanistic credibility for why the recipe works.
3. Add a formal decomposition of hardware throughput versus algorithmic sample efficiency for PipelineRL because conflating system utilization with gradient convergence undermines the scientific interpretation of the compute efficiency parameter $B$ and misguides algorithm design.
4. Add a statistical test comparing the sigmoid fit against alternative bounded curves (e.g., saturating exponential, Gompertz) to rule out overfitting, because the scientific claim of a "universal RL scaling law" requires demonstrating it's not just the best fit among untested models.

### Visualizations & Case Studies
1. Add time-evolution histograms of importance sampling (IS) ratios with/without the FP32 fix at multiple checkpoints, because showing only initialization mismatches fails to prove the fix prevents off-policy divergence as generator staleness accumulates mid-training.
2. Add a concrete failure-mode trajectory showing how a method's fitted parameters ($B$ decay, $C_{mid}$ shift) degrade before catastrophic instability, because without demonstrating predictive warning signs on failing runs, the framework only validates success and lacks diagnostic utility.
3. Visualize the truncation rate dynamics alongside the validation pass rate for the unstable baselines, because directly correlating the proposed "truncation warning signal" with scaling curve deviations is needed to operationalize the framework for practitioners.

### Obvious Next Steps
1. Include a joint 2D compute-isocurve sweep across batch size and context length, because independent sweeps cannot reveal whether compute-optimal RL training lies on a sharp Pareto frontier or suffers from compounding inefficiencies.
2. Derive a parametric scaling equation for multi-task mixing ratios, because treating math and code as independent parallel curves ignores cross-task gradient interference, which is required to claim generalizable multi-domain scaling.

# Final Consolidated Review
## Summary
This paper establishes the first predictive compute-scaling framework for reinforcement learning post-training of LLMs, modeling validation pass rate versus compute using a parameterized sigmoidal curve. Through ~400,000 GPU-hours of systematic ablations across loss types, precision handling, aggregation methods, and asynchronous training setups, the authors derive three empirical principles and consolidate them into the SCALERL recipe. The framework is empirically validated by accurately extrapolating early-run trajectories to stable 100,000 GPU-hour runs across multiple scaling axes (model size, context length, batch size).

## Strengths
- **Unprecedented empirical scale with controlled ablations:** The study spans ~400,000 GPU-hours with tightly held constants across six major RL design axes, providing high-confidence empirical grounding that is rare in the RL scaling literature. The systematic isolation of asymptotic ceilings ($A$) from compute efficiency ($B, C_{\text{mid}}$) is methodologically rigorous.
- **Predictive and actionable scaling parameterization:** The sigmoidal framework successfully decouples performance ceilings from convergence speed, enabling low-cost extrapolation from ≤16k GPU-hour runs to 100k-hour trajectories. This directly addresses the community's reliance on expensive, ad-hoc trial-and-error.
- **Robust recipe synthesis and system transparency:** SCALERL consistently outperforms prevalent baselines (GRPO, DAPO, MiniMax) in both asymptote and efficiency. The authors transparently decompose system-level throughput from algorithmic sample efficiency (Appendix A.20), explicitly document fitting procedures, and acknowledge instability triggers (truncation rates), greatly enhancing reproducibility.
- **Empirical validation of the "bitter lesson" in RL:** The experiments clearly demonstrate that methods appearing superior at low compute often saturate at lower asymptotes, validating the need for parameterized early-run diagnostics over short-horizon benchmark chasing.

## Weaknesses
- **Conflation of data saturation with algorithmic performance ceilings:** The study scales compute by running more epochs over a fixed prompt distribution (Polaris-53k). The fitted asymptotic parameter $A$ inherently conflates algorithmic optimization limits with the information ceiling of the fixed dataset. Without decoupling data scaling from compute scaling, the framework cannot yet predict how $A$ shifts when novel data is introduced or when prompting distributions expand, limiting its generalizability beyond fixed-data regimes.
- **Limited out-of-distribution (OOD) predictability:** While in-distribution validation fits are highly accurate, downstream generalization (e.g., AIME, MATH-500) does not consistently track the predicted curves across all scaling axes. The paper acknowledges this divergence, but the core framing of "predictable scaling" in the abstract and introduction slightly overextends to real-world capability forecasting. Without explicit calibration or error bounds between ID validation extrapolation and OOD benchmark scores, practitioners cannot directly translate the predicted $A$ to downstream task readiness.

## Nice-to-Haves
- Reporting bootstrapped confidence intervals or uncertainty bands for the fitted parameters ($A, B, C_{\text{mid}}$) on the primary extrapolation figures would strengthen statistical reporting, though full multi-seed runs at this scale are computationally prohibitive and single-run curve fitting is common in frontier LLM studies.
- Formal statistical comparison of the sigmoidal fit against alternative bounded curves (e.g., Gompertz, saturating exponential) to rule out functional form overfitting, though the empirical justification in Appendix A.4 is pragmatically sufficient.
- Deriving a parametric scaling equation for multi-task mixing ratios to account for cross-task gradient interference, as the current parallel-curve analysis treats domains independently.
- Including an intermediate dense model size (e.g., 13B–70B) to better establish model-size invariance, as the current 8B → 17B×16 MoE jump combines architectural and parameter scale changes.

## Novel Insights
The most significant conceptual advance is the empirical decoupling of compute efficiency from asymptotic performance ceilings in RL post-training. The study reveals that most widely adopted optimizations (loss aggregation, advantage normalization, length control, adaptive filtering) primarily accelerate convergence ($B, C_{\text{mid}}$) without meaningfully raising the ultimate ceiling ($A$). Conversely, foundational choices like loss truncation (CISPO), FP32 logit precision, and batch definition dictate the hard limit of achievable performance. This inversion challenges common practice: instead of stacking marginal stability heuristics, practitioners should use early-run sigmoidal fits to identify algorithms with high $A$ potential, accepting slower initial convergence as a worthwhile trade-off for scalable returns.

## Suggestions
- Explicitly calibrate the scope of "predictable scaling" in the abstract and introduction to in-distribution validation trajectories, clearly distinguishing it from downstream generalization. Provide a brief quantitative mapping (or correlation table) between ID validation $A$ and OOD benchmark gains across scaling axes to set realistic practitioner expectations.
- Include a dedicated subsection or appendix discussing the data saturation confounder: acknowledge that $A$ currently reflects both algorithmic optimality and dataset capacity, and propose a preliminary methodology for future work to disentangle the two (e.g., jointly fitting curves across varying dataset sizes).
- Clarify the efficiency transformation in Figure 5 by adding a one-step mathematical derivation or footnote explaining that fixing $A$ to the cross-run mean isolates the slope $B$, making the log-linear plot immediately interpretable as a pure efficiency comparison.
