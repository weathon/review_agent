# Harsh Critic Review
## Section-by-Section Critical Review

### Title & Abstract
- **Does the title accurately reflect the contribution?** Mostly, but the title uses "Semantic Disentanglement Error," while Section 3.2 defines it as "Semantic Separability Error" and Section 4.1 refers to it as "Semantic Decomposition Error." This terminological drift creates ambiguity about what the core contribution actually measures.
- **Does the abstract clearly state the problem, method, and key results?** Yes, the problem (dominant components suppressing minor ones), method (SDE metric + adaptive weighting), and claimed results (consistent gains, especially under skew) are clearly stated.
- **Are any claims in the abstract unsupported by the paper?** The claim of "consistent gains" is contradicted by Table 3 (see Experiments section). Additionally, describing the method as "plug-able" implies architectural agnosticism, but the paper only integrates it into CoST, which already uses a dual-branch trend/seasonal architecture. Extending it to holistically-encoding models (like TS2Vec or TNC) is not demonstrated.

### Introduction & Motivation
- **Is the problem well-motivated? Is the gap in prior work clearly identified?** Yes. Time-series contrastive learning's tendency to prioritize high-amplitude trends at the expense of weaker periodic patterns is a practical and relevant concern. The gap in prior work (lack of explicit decomposition bias, time-domain-only contrast, isotropic collapse) is clearly articulated.
- **Are the contributions clearly stated and accurate?** The threefold contribution statement is clear. However, claiming to address "isotropic embedding collapse" conflates dimensional/uniformity collapse with *component dominance*. Isotropic collapse refers to embeddings concentrating near a single point or uniform manifold (Wang & Isola, 2020), whereas this paper identifies semantic suppression. The mapping is imprecise.
- **Does the introduction over-claim or under-sell?** It slightly over-claims by framing asymmetric perception weighting as a cure for isotropic collapse, when it is specifically a loss-rebalancing heuristic for trend/seasonal asymmetry.

### Method / Approach
- **Is the method clearly described and reproducible?** The high-level pipeline is clear, but the mathematical grounding of SDE lacks rigor. The SDE metric (Sec 3.2, Eq. 1) implicitly assumes `v(a+b) - v(b) ≈ v(a)`, which requires the encoder `v` to be linear or at least approximately additive in its latent space. Deep encoders used in contrastive learning are highly non-linear, making this additivity assumption theoretically unsound without empirical validation or architectural constraints.
- **Are key assumptions stated and justified?** The additive linearity assumption in latent space is unstated and unjustified. Additionally, the decomposition of real-world signals using a fixed LPF/HPF pipeline assumes strictly additive, stationary trend+seasonality, which rarely holds in complex multivariate series.
- **Are there logical gaps in the derivation or reasoning?** Yes. The loss weighting formula in Sec 3.4/4.4.3 (`1 + γ·∆`) can easily yield negative weights if `|Δ| > 1/γ`, flipping the gradient direction and destabilizing training. The paper does not specify clipping, normalization, or a moving average to stabilize `Δ`. How `Δ` gradients flow back through the contrastive loss is also unclear; typically, such diagnostic metrics use `stop_gradient` to avoid oscillatory optimization, but this is not discussed.
- **Are there edge cases or failure modes not discussed?** Multi-periodic signals, non-stationary shifts, and multiplicative trend-seasonality (common in real-world domains like traffic or retail) are not addressed. The method assumes cleanly separable linear components.
- **For theoretical claims:** The SDE is not a true bound or convex surrogate; it's a heuristic cosine distance. Without proving why minimizing/maximizing it correlates with better downstream forecasting, the theoretical link remains empirical.

### Experiments & Results
- **Do the experiments actually test the paper's claims?** Partially. Sec 4.2 validates SDE's sensitivity to amplitude ratios on synthetic data, which works well. Sec 4.4 tests forecasting performance, but the experimental design does not fully stress-test the "plug-and-play" claim.
- **Are baselines appropriate and fairly compared?** TS2Vec, TNC, and CoST are reasonable. However, strong recent baselines (e.g., SimMTM, PatchTST self-supervised variants) are omitted, which is expected at ICLR to contextualize the performance gains properly.
- **Are there missing ablations that would materially change conclusions?** Yes. Critical ablations are missing: (1) Impact of `γ, γ'` sensitivity, (2) Effect of the MLP fusion layer vs. simple concatenation, (3) Whether APW improves TS2Vec/TNC (to prove the "plug-able" claim), and (4) Impact of varying LPF cutoffs on `Δ` stability.
- **Are error bars / statistical significance reported?** No. ICLR standards require results averaged over multiple random seeds (typically 3-5) with variance/error bars. Table 3 reports single-run values, making it impossible to assess if gains fall within noise.
- **Do the results support the claims made, or are they cherry-picked?** Table 3 directly contradicts the claim of "consistent gains." On the Electricity dataset at horizons 168, 336, and 720, CoST+APW (MSE: 0.566, 0.677, 1.010) significantly *degrades* performance compared to vanilla TS2Vec (0.429, 0.565, 0.863) and CoST (0.425, 0.576, 0.911). The authors claim superiority but the data shows clear failure modes on highly periodic, long-horizon tasks where over-weighting seasonality likely causes overfitting or optimization instability.
- **Are datasets and evaluation metrics appropriate?** Yes, standard TS forecasting datasets and MSE/MAE are appropriate.

### Writing & Clarity
- **Are there sections that are confusing or poorly explained?** Sec 3.2 to Sec 4.3 shows inconsistent naming: "Semantic Disentanglement Error" (Title/Abstract), "Semantic Separability Error" (Sec 3.2), and "Semantic Decomposition Error" (Sec 4.1, 4.3). This must be unified. Furthermore, the transition from Sec 4.3 (SDE as direct regularizer) to Sec 4.4 (SDE used for dynamic loss weighting) is abrupt; the paper notes regularization "fails to provide constructive gradients" but doesn't explain why the same metric suddenly becomes stable and useful when multiplicative with the contrastive loss.
- **Are figures and tables clear and informative?** Table 1 effectively demonstrates Δ sensitivity. Table 3 is dense and difficult to parse at a glance; formatting improvements (bolding best/worst per horizon, clearer dataset grouping) would help, but the data itself is present. Figure 1 is conceptually clear but lacks detail on how SDE gradients are handled during training.

### Limitations & Broader Impact
- **Do the authors acknowledge the key limitations?** The conclusion hints at future work (low-pass only decomposition, MLP analysis) but does not explicitly frame these as limitations. ICLR papers typically benefit from a dedicated limitations section.
- **Are there fundamental limitations they missed?** The reliance on additive decomposition is a major limitation. Many real-world series exhibit heteroscedasticity, non-linear interactions between trend/seasonality, and structural breaks. The method's heuristic weighting may amplify noise when components are not separable. Additionally, computational overhead from repeated LPF/HPF passes and SDE computation during training is not quantified.
- **Are there failure modes or negative societal impacts not discussed?** The failure mode on Electricity (long horizons) is evident but not analyzed. Broader impact is minimal given the domain, but no discussion of how misbalanced representations could affect downstream decision-making (e.g., in energy forecasting) is present.

### Overall Assessment
This paper addresses a practically relevant issue in time-series contrastive learning: the tendency for high-amplitude components to suppress weaker semantic signals. The proposed SDE metric and asymmetric perceptual weighting (APW) offer an elegant, heuristic approach to rebalance training dynamics without heavy architectural changes. However, the paper currently falls short of ICLR's acceptance bar in two critical areas. First, the theoretical foundation of SDE relies on an unverified additive linearity assumption (`v(a+b) - v(b) ≈ v(a)`) in highly non-linear deep encoders, leaving its optimization behavior mathematically opaque. Second, the experimental validation is incomplete and partially contradictory: results are reported from single seeds without statistical variance, and the method noticeably degrades forecasting accuracy on the Electricity dataset for longer horizons, directly undermining the claim of "consistent gains." To reach acceptability, the authors must unify their terminology, rigorously justify or empirically validate the latent-space additivity assumption, implement gradient stabilization (e.g., weight clipping, moving averages, stop-gradients) for `Δ`, conduct multi-seed experiments, and honestly analyze the failure cases where APW hurts performance. Without these revisions, the contribution remains an intriguing but insufficiently validated heuristic.

# Neutral Reviewer
## Balanced Review

### Summary
This paper addresses semantic component imbalance in contrastive time-series representation learning, where dominant trends often suppress weaker periodic signals. The authors propose the Semantic Disentanglement Error (SDE), a diagnostic metric quantifying component recoverability via latent-space vector arithmetic, and integrate it into an adaptive asymmetry-aware weighting scheme that dynamically rebalances view-specific contrastive losses. Experiments demonstrate that integrating this mechanism into the CoST framework improves forecasting accuracy and reduces semantic bias across standard benchmarks.

### Strengths
1. **Clear Problem Identification & Diagnostic Tool:** The paper accurately identifies a well-documented but under-mitigated issue in time-series SSL: representation collapse toward high-energy components. The SDE metric (Sec 3.2) offers an intuitive, quantitative probe to measure trend-seasonality recoverability, providing the community with a standardizable diagnostic for evaluating component balance.
2. **Systematic Methodological Iteration:** The authors transparently document the failure of direct SDE regularization (Table 2, Sec 4.3) and provide a reasoned pivot toward asymmetric loss weighting. This honest ablation narrative (diagnostic → naive regularization → dynamic weighting) demonstrates rigorous scientific reasoning and strengthens the paper's logical flow.
3. **Consistent Empirical Improvements:** The integrated CoST+APW pipeline yields consistent MSE/MAE reductions over TS2Vec, TNC, and vanilla CoST across multiple datasets and forecasting horizons (Table 3). The results validate that explicitly penalizing semantic asymmetry during optimization translates to tangible downstream gains, particularly on datasets with pronounced periodic components like Weather.

### Weaknesses
1. **Questionable Theoretical Foundation for SDE:** The SDE metric (Sec 3.2) relies on the assumption that $v(\mathbf{a}+\mathbf{b}) - v(\mathbf{b})$ approximates $v(\mathbf{a})$, implicitly assuming linearity in the encoder's latent space. Given that $v(\cdot)$ is a non-linear neural network, this vector arithmetic property is highly unlikely to hold without justification. The paper provides no linear probing results or geometric analysis to verify this assumption, making SDE's validity theoretically ambiguous.
2. **Ambiguous Unsupervised Pipeline & Decomposition Dependency:** It is unclear how components $\mathbf{a}$ and $\mathbf{b}$ are obtained during training on real datasets to compute $\Delta$. Section 4.3 describes using LPF/HPF on raw signals, while CoST inherently uses frequency-domain encoders to derive trend/seasonal views. If ground-truth or heavy preprocessing is required to compute $\Delta$ during training, the method loses its unsupervised/self-supervised appeal. If estimated components are used, the sensitivity of $\Delta$ to decomposition noise is unanalyzed, leaving open questions about training stability.
3. **Optimization Stability & Missing Implementation Details:** The weighting formula $\mathcal{L} = (1 + \gamma\Delta)\mathcal{L}_{season} + (1 + \gamma'(-\Delta))\mathcal{L}_{trend}$ can yield negative coefficients if $|\Delta| > 1/\gamma$ (Table 1 shows $\Delta$ reaching ~1.1), which risks gradient inversion and optimization collapse. The paper does not specify how weights are clamped, normalized, or bounded. Additionally, critical reproducibility details are omitted: exact values for $\gamma, \gamma'$, the architecture of the MLP fusion layer $g_\phi$ (Sec 4.4.2), and training compute/runtime are not reported.
4. **Limited Baseline Scope & Statistical Rigor:** The empirical evaluation focuses on older SSL methods (TS2Vec, TNC, CoST) and omits recent state-of-the-art time-series forecasting/representation baselines (e.g., TimesNet, PatchTST, SimMTM). Furthermore, improvements in Table 3 are sometimes marginal (e.g., ETTh2 24h: 0.069 vs 0.079) and lack standard deviations or statistical significance testing across multiple random seeds, which is expected for ICLR submissions.

### Novelty & Significance
The paper tackles a relevant and practically important problem in time-series self-supervised learning. The conceptual contribution (SDE + dynamic loss reweighting) is moderately novel but remains incremental, building directly on CoST's dual-branch architecture and standard adaptive weighting techniques. Its significance lies in offering a straightforward, plug-and-play diagnostic and mitigation strategy. However, the reliance on unverified linearity assumptions, ambiguous unsupervised decomposition, and lack of training stability analysis limit its readiness for top-tier acceptance. ICLR typically expects stronger theoretical grounding, comprehensive reproducibility documentation, and rigorous statistical evaluation, which are currently underdeveloped.

### Suggestions for Improvement
1. **Validate the Linearity Assumption Empirically:** Add an experiment probing whether $v(\mathbf{a}+\mathbf{b}) - v(\mathbf{b})$ aligns with $v(\mathbf{a})$ in the latent space (e.g., via cosine similarity heatmaps, linear regression scores, or PCA analysis). If linearity doesn't hold, propose a non-linear SDE variant or justify why the first-order approximation is sufficient for asymmetric weighting.
2. **Clarify the Fully Unsupervised Pipeline:** Explicitly detail how $\mathbf{a}$ and $\mathbf{b}$ are derived during CoST training. If using filters, add an ablation on filter bandwidth/decay rates and quantify how decomposition error propagates to $\Delta$. Alternatively, propose a fully self-supervised $\Delta$ proxy using frequency-domain energy ratios computed directly from the model's internal spectral views, eliminating external preprocessing.
3. **Ensure Optimizer Stability & Provide Reproducibility Checklist:** Modify the weighting scheme to prevent negative coefficients (e.g., $\mathcal{L} = \text{softplus}(W_s)\mathcal{L}_{season} + \text{softplus}(W_t)\mathcal{L}_{trend}$ or weight normalization). Report exact $\gamma, \gamma'$ values, MLP layer dimensions, activation functions, learning rate schedules, and training time/FLOPs to enable exact replication.
4. **Strengthen Empirical Evaluation:** Compare against recent strong baselines (e.g., TimesNet, DLinear, PatchTST, or SimMTM) to contextualize performance gains. Run each configuration with at least 3 random seeds, report mean ± standard deviation in Table 3, and perform statistical significance testing (e.g., paired t-test) to substantiate claims of consistent improvement.

## How to Improve This Paper

### Missing Experiments (top 3-5 only)
1. **Isolate the source of gains (Weighting vs. MLP).** Add ablations testing only the Δ reweighting, only the fusion MLP, and their combination against vanilla CoST. Without this, it is unclear if improvements arise from semantic rebalancing or simply from the added capacity and implicit regularization of a new projection layer.
2. **Validate SDE correlation with real-world downstream performance.** Table 1 only validates SDE on synthetic signals; you must empirically demonstrate that higher ∆ (imbalance) correlates with worse forecasting or classification accuracy on real datasets to prove SDE measures a genuine failure mode.
3. **Include statistical rigor and forecasting protocol details.** Report mean ± standard deviation across multiple random seeds with significance tests, and explicitly state whether forecasting uses linear probing or full fine-tuning. Without statistical validation and protocol transparency, claims of "consistent gains" over baselines are unreliable.

### Deeper Analysis Needed (top 3-5 only)
1. **Analyze stability of the self-referential feedback loop.** Using a metric derived from current embeddings (∆) to weight the very losses that update those embeddings creates a closed optimization loop. Provide gradient flow analysis or justify stability mechanisms (e.g., moving averages, stop-gradients), or reviewers will suspect training oscillations or collapse.
2. **Justify the linearity assumption in contrastive space.** SDE assumes $v(\textbf{a}+\textbf{b}) - v(\textbf{b}) \approx v(\textbf{a})$, implying additive superposition in the embedding manifold. Contrastive objectives learn highly non-linear representations; you must empirically verify if cosine distances actually preserve component separability or if the metric is fundamentally misspecified.
3. **Quantify robustness to imperfect signal decomposition.** Real data rarely splits cleanly into trend/seasonality via simple low-pass filtering. Analyze how noise or filter cutoff selection in the decomposition step propagates to ∆, proving the weighting mechanism does not hallucinate imbalance due to filter artifacts.

### Visualizations & Case Studies
1. **Embedding subspace projection plots.** Visualize PCA or UMAP projections of the seasonal embeddings for CoST vs. CoST+APW, colored by true periodic strength. This directly exposes whether your method actually disentangles seasonality from trend or merely distorts the latent space.
2. **Training dynamics graph of ∆ and loss weights.** Plot ∆, weighted $\mathcal{L}_{season}$, and weighted $\mathcal{L}_{trend}$ over training epochs to show the mechanism actually stabilizes and rebalances optimization. Flat or erratic lines would indicate the weighting is ineffective or purely decorative.
3. **Failure case study on irregular signals.** Demonstrate the method's behavior on a time-series with non-stationary seasonality, changepoints, or heavy noise. Showing where SDE misestimates imbalance exposes boundary conditions and prevents overgeneralization of the "always improves" narrative.

### Obvious Next Steps
1. **Detail online ∆ computation and gradient routing.** Specify exactly when trend/seasonal splits occur during mini-batch training, whether SDE computation blocks gradients from the loss weights back to the encoder, and report compute overhead. Without this, the "plug-in" claim is unsubstantiated and the code is not reproducible.
2. **Validate claims across all downstream tasks mentioned in the Introduction.** The intro claims semantic imbalance degrades anomaly detection and classification, yet only forecasting results are provided. Adding results on standard UEA classification or anomaly detection benchmarks is required to support the claimed "universal representational robustness."
3. **Conduct a hyperparameter sensitivity sweep for $\gamma$ and $\gamma'$.** Provide contour plots or heatmaps showing forecasting MSE/MAD across $\gamma/\gamma'$ values. If gains only appear within an extremely narrow scaling range, the method is brittle and the dynamic weighting offers minimal practical utility over static loss scaling.

# Final Consolidated Review
## Summary
The paper diagnoses semantic component imbalance in contrastive time-series representation learning, where high-amplitude trends often suppress weaker periodic signals. It introduces the Semantic Disentanglement Error (SDE) to quantify component recoverability via latent-space vector arithmetic, and leverages the resulting asymmetry factor to dynamically reweight trend and seasonal contrastive losses within the CoST framework. The authors claim this modular mechanism improves representational balance and forecasting accuracy across standard benchmarks.

## Strengths
- **Targeted Problem Formulation & Diagnostic Metric:** The paper accurately isolates a tangible failure mode in time-series SSL (component suppression under distributional skew) and provides SDE as a quantitative, interpretable probe for measuring trend-seasonality recoverability.
- **Transparent Methodological Iteration:** The authors honestly document the empirical failure of direct SDE regularization (Table 2) and provide a clear rationale for pivoting to adaptive loss weighting. This diagnostic-to-optimization progression demonstrates sound scientific reasoning.
- **Architecturally Minimal Intervention:** The proposed asymmetric weighting operates at the objective level, requiring only the addition of a lightweight fusion MLP, which aligns well with the goal of preserving existing encoder designs.

## Weaknesses
- **Mathematically Unfounded Core Assumption:** SDE relies on the implicit assumption that $v(\mathbf{a}+\mathbf{b}) - v(\mathbf{b}) \approx v(\mathbf{a})$, equating to linear superposition in the latent space. Contrastive encoders (CNNs/Transformers) learn highly non-linear manifolds where this vector arithmetic rarely holds. Without empirical validation (e.g., linear probing, cosine alignment scores) or architectural constraints, the metric's optimization signal is heuristically fragile.
- **Optimization Instability & Contradictory Empirical Claims:** The linear weighting scheme $\mathcal{L} = (1+\gamma\Delta)\mathcal{L}_{\text{season}} + (1+\gamma'(-\Delta))\mathcal{L}_{\text{trend}}$ can easily yield negative coefficients since $\Delta$ reaches ~1.1 (Table 1) and $\gamma$ is unspecified. Negative weights invert gradients, risking training collapse, yet no stabilization mechanism (clipping, normalization, moving averages, or `stop_gradient`) is provided. Furthermore, results directly contradict the claimed "consistent gains": on the Electricity dataset, CoST+APW significantly degrades MSE at horizons 168, 336, and 720 compared to both CoST and TS2Vec, suggesting the mechanism over-amplifies periodic noise at longer ranges.
- **Insufficient Rigor & Unsubstantiated "Plug-and-Play" Claims:** Results are reported from single runs without error bars or statistical significance testing, making it impossible to distinguish true improvements from stochastic variance. Critical ablations are missing: the contribution of the dynamic weighting is not isolated from the added fusion MLP, and no sensitivity analysis for $\gamma/\gamma'$ is provided. Additionally, the method is only validated on CoST's inherently dual-branch architecture, failing to demonstrate the claimed extensibility to holistic encoders like TS2Vec or TNC.
- **Overextended Scope & Terminological Inconsistency:** The introduction promises gains across forecasting, anomaly detection, and classification, yet only forecasting is evaluated. The core metric is inconsistently named across the manuscript (Disentanglement, Separability, Decomposition), obscuring the precise mathematical object being optimized.

## Nice-to-Haves
- Visualize training dynamics (e.g., epoch-wise trajectories of $\Delta$, reweighted losses, and embedding subspace projections) to empirically verify that the feedback loop stabilizes rather than oscillates.
- Evaluate on standard UEA classification or anomaly detection benchmarks to substantiate the broader "representational robustness" claims.
- Explore fully self-supervised proxies for $\Delta$ (e.g., internal frequency-energy ratios) to eliminate dependence on external LPF/HPF decomposition during training.

## Novel Insights
The paper's exploration reveals a fundamental tension in self-supervised time-series learning: heuristic diagnostic metrics designed to probe representational structure rarely possess gradient-compatible landscapes for direct optimization. The transparent failure of SDE regularization highlights that cosine-distance probes in non-linear contrastive spaces are structurally misaligned with backpropagation, necessitating indirect mitigation strategies like adaptive loss weighting. This underscores a broader methodological principle for foundation model design: decoupling diagnostic probing from gradient routing is often necessary to stabilize self-referential optimization loops without distorting the learned manifold.

## Potentially Missed Related Work
- Uncertainty-weighted multi-task learning & dynamic loss balancing (e.g., Kendall et al., CVPR 2018; Liu et al., CVPR 2019) — highly relevant for contextualizing adaptive weighting schemes.
- Recent contrastive representation balancing methods in vision/NLP that address feature suppression via explicit subspace regularization or contrastive decoupling — would strengthen positioning.

## Suggestions
- **Stabilize the weighting mechanism:** Replace linear scaling with a bounded, positive function (e.g., softmax over learnable logits or softplus transformation of $\Delta$) and explicitly document gradient routing to prevent sign-flipping instability.
- **Validate the linearity assumption:** Empirically test whether $v(\mathbf{a}+\mathbf{b}) - v(\mathbf{b})$ aligns with $v(\mathbf{a})$ via latent-space probing; if alignment is weak, reformulate SDE using a kernelized similarity or non-linear reconstruction objective.
- **Strengthen experimental rigor:** Report mean ± std over ≥3 random seeds, conduct an MLP-ablation study, and include a $\gamma$ sensitivity sweep to demonstrate robustness. Clarify exact filtering pipelines used for real-world decomposition and report computational overhead.
