# Harsh Critic Review
## Section-by-Section Critical Review

### Title & Abstract
- The title accurately captures the core contribution: integrating domain knowledge and causal reasoning to adjust RL rewards.
- The abstract clearly states the problem (spurious reward signals hindering generalization), the proposed method (KARMA: knowledge-augmented state representation, causal discovery, counterfactual reward adjustment), and the key results (up to 30% performance gains, faster convergence, improved OOD generalization).
- However, the claim of "theoretical guarantees on convergence and sample efficiency" is not substantiated in the main text (detailed below). The abstract implies rigorous proofs, which creates an expectation mismatch. Additionally, the claim "mitigating the impact of misleading correlations" is stated abstractly, but the experiments do not explicitly inject spurious or corrupted reward signals to validate this specific resilience claim.

### Introduction & Motivation
- The problem is well-motivated: standard RL struggles with reward design, and recent RLVR findings (Shao et al., 2025) highlight how models can latch onto formatting or random cues rather than true task semantics.
- The gap in prior work is clearly identified: traditional reward shaping lacks causal grounding, knowledge-augmented RL is often static/correlation-based, and causal RL rarely focuses on dynamic reward signal refinement.
- The introduction slightly overclaims by heavily leaning on LLM alignment/RLVR as motivation, while the empirical work remains in classical control/simulation domains (grid, robotics, traffic). The conceptual bridge connecting LLM spurious reward phenomena to low-dimensional environment reward shaping is never established, making the motivation feel somewhat fragmented.
- Contributions are clearly enumerated, but Contribution 3 ("Theoretical Guarantees") is under-delivered in the introduction since no formal statements or proof strategies are introduced.

### Method / Approach
- The method is outlined modularly, but crucial details required for reproducibility are missing. Equation 1 defines $R'(s,a,r,s')$, but the computational forms of $R_{knowledge}$ and $R_{causal}$ are never mathematically specified.
- The description of $R_{causal}$ as being "obtained through counterfactual queries on $C$, using Pearl’s do-calculus to estimate causal effects" is a categorical mismatch. Do-calculus is a framework for *identification*, not an algorithmic procedure for reward computation. The paper does not specify the structural equation model (SEM), how counterfactuals $Y_{X=x}$ are estimated in continuous or partially observed spaces, or how the causal effect metric translates to a scalar reward bonus/penalty. This is a critical logical gap.
- Key assumptions are not stated or justified. Causal discovery typically requires faithfulness, causal sufficiency (no unmeasured confounders), and correct temporal ordering. In non-stationary RL loops where the policy changes every 1000 steps (Sec 4.4), the i.i.d. assumptions underlying the PC/FCI algorithms are violated. The paper does not discuss how distribution shift impacts causal graph identifiability over training.
- Theoretical claims in Sec 3.4 are presented only as a bulleted list ("Convergence of Causal Discovery," "Policy Invariance," "Sample Efficiency," "Convergence of KARMA-RL") without theorem statements, conditions, or proof sketches in the main text. Relying entirely on an external zip file falls short of ICLR's expectation for self-contained theoretical rigor.
- Failure modes: What if the knowledge graph contains incorrect priors or conflicts with data-driven causal edges? The method mentions "cross-validation" but provides no mechanism for conflict resolution or trust calibration between $w_K(t)$ and data signals.

### Experiments & Results
- The experiments partially test the framework's efficacy but miss a direct validation of its core claim: robustness to spurious rewards. Environments contain "interfering features," but baselines are not evaluated under explicitly corrupted or randomized reward functions, so the claimed mitigation of misleading correlations remains speculative.
- Baselines are comprehensive and well-chosen, covering standard, knowledge-augmented, and causal RL. However, it is unclear if hyperparameter tuning and compute budgets are strictly matched across methods. KARMA's ~30% gains over strong causal baselines like CDA and IPL are unusually large; without details on compute parity or tuning effort, it is difficult to rule out implementation disparities or over-tuning on specific seeds.
- Ablations (Table 2) are restricted solely to GCD-Complex. Given the paper's claims of broad applicability across robotics and traffic control, omitting ablations on RSA and TSC is a material weakness. It leaves open the possibility that performance gains are environment-specific or heavily dependent on the simplified KG in grid domains.
- Statistical significance is mentioned (t-tests with $\alpha=0.05$) but never reported. Tables 1 and 2 lack significance markers or $p$-values, making it impossible to verify if the margins between KARMA and nearby baselines (e.g., CDA in GCD) are statistically meaningful or within variance.
- The sample efficiency metric (Eq 3) depends on $J(\pi^*)$, but the method for approximating the optimal policy return is not stated. In continuous control (RSA) and traffic (TSC), $J(\pi^*)$ is typically intractable. If $J(\pi^*)$ is estimated via long-horizon expert runs or best-of-$N$ heuristic, this must be disclosed, as it directly biases SE comparisons.

### Writing & Clarity
- The paper is structurally well-organized, and the modular breakdown (KG -> Causal -> Reward) aids readability.
- Section 3.3 is the primary clarity bottleneck. The leap from "counterfactual queries on $C$" to a practical reward shaping algorithm lacks algorithmic steps, pseudocode, or mathematical formulation. Readers cannot reconstruct how $R_{causal}$ is computed from learned dynamics, nor how $w_K(t)$ and $w_C(t)$ are annealed beyond "linear annealing."
- Figures are referenced clearly and described adequately, though the actual visualizations cannot be evaluated here. The metric equations (2)-(5) are readable despite OCR artifacts.
- No major grammatical or structural confusion impedes high-level understanding, but the lack of precise mathematical definitions for the causal reward component prevents deep technical comprehension.

### Limitations & Broader Impact
- The authors appropriately acknowledge two key limitations: sensitivity to KG/causal graph quality, and scalability in high-dimensional spaces. Suggestions for uncertainty-aware discovery and neuro-symbolic extensions are reasonable future directions.
- Missed limitations: (1) Computational latency during environment stepping. Counterfactual inference via structural models is typically expensive; the paper reports training time but does not discuss if real-time reward computation imposes a bottleneck in control frequency-critical tasks. (2) Partial observability and non-stationarity, both common in real-world RL and problematic for static causal graphs. (3) The method assumes full specification of the knowledge graph; the overhead of expert knowledge engineering is not discussed.
- Broader societal impact is briefly touched on in the context of robustness and alignment, which aligns with ICLR norms given the RL safety angle. No major omissions or negative societal impacts are apparent.

### Overall Assessment
KARMA proposes a timely and conceptually relevant direction: unifying structured domain knowledge with causal discovery to dynamically shape reward signals in RL. The modular design and empirical motivation address genuine pain points in sample efficiency and spurious correlation mitigation. However, the current manuscript falls short of ICLR's acceptance bar due to significant gaps in methodological specificity and empirical rigor. The core innovation—counterfactual-based reward adjustment—is described too vaguely; do-calculus is cited without a concrete computation pipeline for $R_{causal}$, and the theoretical guarantees are relegated to unverified bullet points. Empirically, the lack of ablations on two of the three domains, unreported statistical significance, missing details on $\pi^*$ approximation, and the mismatch between the "spurious reward" motivation and the actual experimental setup weaken the evidence chain. If the authors can provide precise mathematical formulations for the causal reward, state and address non-stationarity assumptions, expand ablations and statistical reporting, and directly test robustness to corrupted reward signals, this work could be highly competitive. As submitted, it reads more as a promising framework proposal than a fully validated methodological contribution.

# Neutral Reviewer
## Balanced Review

### Summary
The paper introduces KARMA, a reinforcement learning framework that dynamically adjusts reward signals by integrating structured domain knowledge (via knowledge graph embeddings) with causal inference (via constraint-based causal discovery and counterfactual reasoning). By employing a time-varying weighting scheme that gradually transitions from knowledge-guided shaping to causally-derived reward adjustments, the method aims to filter out spurious correlations and improve sample efficiency. The authors provide convergence and efficiency claims alongside empirical results across grid navigation, robotic manipulation, and traffic control environments, demonstrating consistent performance gains and stronger robustness to distribution shifts compared to standard, knowledge-augmented, and causal RL baselines.

### Strengths
1. **Clear Problem Formulation and Modular Architecture:** The paper correctly identifies reward misspecification and spurious correlation sensitivity as core RL bottlenecks. The decomposition into three distinct, parallel modules (knowledge integration, causal structure learning, and counterfactual adjustment) creates an interpretable pipeline that cleanly separates semantic priors from data-driven causal discovery.
2. **Comprehensive Empirical Validation & Ablations:** Experiments span environments with varying causal complexity. The inclusion of diverse baselines (standard RL, knowledge-augmented, and causal RL) alongside systematic ablations (removing knowledge, causal learning, or reward adjustment; testing static vs. dynamic weights) provides strong evidence that each component contributes meaningfully to the observed gains. The evaluation of generalization, robustness to observation noise, and computational overhead is thorough.
3. **Intuitive Dynamic Weighting Mechanism:** The scheduling of $w_K(t)$ and $w_C(t)$ to favor domain knowledge early in training and causal signals later is well-motivated. It addresses the well-known cold-start problem in causal discovery (which requires sufficient data to identify dependencies) by using reliable structural priors to bootstrap exploration.

### Weaknesses
1. **Underdeveloped Theoretical Guarantees:** Section 3.4 presents critical claims (convergence, policy invariance, sample efficiency) as high-level bullet points without formal theorems, explicit assumptions, or proof sketches in the main text. Policy invariance under reward shaping strictly requires potential-based functions (Ng et al., 1999), yet it is unclear how the counterfactual causal reward $R_{causal}$ or the time-varying combined function $R'$ satisfy this condition, or if the authors intentionally break it (and under what theoretical guarantees). Referencing an appendix without visible formalism in the main text is insufficient for ICLR's theoretical rigor standards.
2. **Methodological Vagueness and Scalability Concerns:** Key implementation details are missing. Equation (1) introduces $R_{knowledge}$ and $R_{causal}$ without specifying their exact mathematical forms or how they are normalized/combined with $r$. Additionally, constraint-based causal discovery (e.g., PC algorithm) scales poorly and typically requires discrete variables or strong independence assumptions. The paper does not explain how continuous, high-dimensional state/action spaces (e.g., 7-DOF robot joints or traffic flow metrics) are discretized, nor how causal graph updates every 1000 steps remain computationally feasible given Table 3's modest training time claims.
3. **Motivation-Experiment Misalignment:** The introduction heavily motivates KARMA using recent findings from LLM alignment and RLVR (e.g., Shao et al., 2025), emphasizing spurious format/random reward signals. However, all experiments are confined to classical control/simulation tasks. The paper does not demonstrate that KARMA actually mitigates the type of deceptive or model-mismatched reward corruption highlighted in LLM alignment, nor does it test on perception-rich or high-dimensional RL benchmarks where spurious correlations are notoriously difficult.

### Novelty & Significance
Novelty is moderate to good. While knowledge-augmented RL, causal discovery for RL, and counterfactual reward shaping have been explored separately, KARMA's systematic unification with a dynamic, knowledge-to-causality transition schedule represents a thoughtful engineering synthesis. The conceptual framing advances the reward shaping literature by explicitly decoupling semantic priors from empirically learned causal dependencies. Significance hinges on addressing the theoretical gaps and scaling beyond low-to-mid-dimensional simulators; if successfully formalized and applied to high-dimensional or language-aligned tasks, it could offer a valuable toolkit for reward design. Currently, it stands as a solid incremental contribution rather than a foundational breakthrough.

### Suggestions for Improvement
1. **Formalize Theoretical Contributions:** Replace the bullet-point claims in Section 3.4 with explicit theorems stating precise assumptions (e.g., faithfulness, realizability, mixing rates of the MDP). Provide a proof or proof sketch demonstrating convergence and clarify under what conditions policy invariance holds. If invariance is intentionally relaxed, formally characterize the bias introduced by $R_{causal}$ and the dynamic weighting, and prove that it still guarantees convergence to an $\epsilon$-optimal policy.
2. **Detail Algorithmic Mechanics & Address Continuous/Causal Scalability:** Explicitly define $R_{knowledge}$ and $R_{causal}$. Explain how the PC algorithm (or its equivalent) handles continuous variables in the state space (e.g., via Fisher-z tests, mutual information estimators, or discretization thresholds). Provide pseudocode for the online reward adjustment loop, including how counterfactuals are queried efficiently from the structural equation model during RL steps.
3. **Align Evaluation with Stated Motivation & Expand Benchmarks:** Either adapt the experimental suite to directly test KARMA on environments with injected spurious reward signals or deceptive feedback (to match the RLVR motivation), or explicitly narrow the introduction's focus to control/robotics RL to avoid overstating relevance to LLM alignment. Including a high-dimensional perceptual benchmark (e.g., ProcGen with distractors or a visually rich robotics domain) would significantly strengthen the empirical case and meet ICLR's expectations for robustness testing.

# Spark Finder Review
## How to Improve This Paper

### Missing Experiments (top 3-5 only)
1. **Benchmark against Potential-Based Reward Shaping (PBRS)**: You claim dynamic reward adjustment improves convergence and performance, yet omit the standard policy-preserving shaping baseline. Without PBRS, your 5-30% gains could simply reflect dense reward padding rather than genuine causal or knowledge-driven improvements.
2. **Validate sample efficiency on a defined optimal baseline**: Your "samples to 90% optimal" metric relies on an unspecified $J(\pi^*)$, which is unknown in stochastic or partially observed control tasks. Provide a tractable value iteration lower bound or use a high-performing reference agent to make this metric verifiable; otherwise, the 50-60% speedup claim is ungrounded.
3. **Multi-environment ablations**: All component removals (Table 2) only run on GCD-Complex, yet you claim universal superiority across robotics and traffic control. Removing knowledge or causal inference in RSA/TSC may behave entirely differently, so the framework's modular contribution claims remain unverified for 2/3 of your domains.

### Deeper Analysis Needed (top 3-5 only)
1. **Policy-induced distribution shift during causal discovery**: RL data is non-stationary and policy-dependent, violating standard i.i.d. assumptions required for PC/FCI algorithms. Analyze how active policy updates change variable coverage and induce spurious edges during graph updates, or your causal model will likely learn policy artifacts, not environment structure.
2. **Identifiability of counterfactuals in continuous action spaces**: You claim to use do-calculus/SEMs to compute causal effects in Franka arm and traffic environments, but do not state how continuous interventions are modeled or identified. Without explicit structural equations and identifiability proofs for continuous controls, the counterfactual reward is mathematically under-specified.
3. **Systematic knowledge graph corruption study**: You claim robustness to imperfect knowledge, but only test observation noise, not KG errors. Quantify performance degradation as relation noise or missing entities increase to prove the framework actually disentangles bad priors from causal signals, rather than collapsing when knowledge is flawed.

### Visualizations & Case Studies
1. **Learned vs. Ground-Truth Causal Graphs**: Plot the actual discovered graph structure for GridWorld and Traffic against known environment dynamics to verify the algorithm finds true reward drivers and not superficial state correlations.
2. **Trajectory divergence under spurious signals**: Show side-by-side agent paths in GCD where standard PPO gets stuck in background-color shortcuts, while KARMA takes longer but correct paths. This directly proves the counterfactual adjustment actively suppresses spurious correlations instead of just boosting total reward.
3. **Dynamic weight and adjustment magnitude plots**: Visualize $w_K(t)$ and $w_C(t)$ over training alongside a histogram of actual reward deltas $|R' - R|$. This confirms your claimed "early knowledge to late causal" transition actually occurs in practice and quantifies when the method intervenes vs. passes through raw rewards.

### Obvious Next Steps
1. **Include full theoretical proofs in the submission**: Delegating your core convergence and policy invariance theorems to an external zip file violates ICLR's reproducibility and review standards. Include all assumptions, lemmas, and proofs in the main appendix, and explicitly state how they hold under dynamic weight updates.
2. **Provide an explicit algorithm for counterfactual reward computation**: Replace high-level references to "do-calculus" with a step-by-step procedure showing how $R_{causal}$ is approximated from mini-batch trajectories, including how confounders are identified and how the SEM is fit online during RL.
3. **Evaluate on at least one vision-based or language-aligned task**: The introduction heavily motivates the work around RLVR spurious rewards, but all experiments use low-dimensional state simulators. Adding one high-dimensional or LLM-proximal environment (e.g., LLaMA math reasoning or a visual navigation task) is necessary to validate the primary problem statement you opened the paper with.

# Final Consolidated Review
## Summary
KARMA proposes a reinforcement learning framework that dynamically refines reward signals by integrating domain knowledge (via knowledge graph embeddings) with causal inference (via constraint-based discovery and counterfactual reasoning). The method employs a time-varying weighting schedule that transitions from knowledge-guided early exploration to causality-driven late optimization, reporting gains in sample efficiency, final performance, and OOD generalization across grid navigation, robotic manipulation, and traffic control tasks.

## Strengths
- The modular architecture cleanly separates semantic priors, causal structure discovery, and reward modification, offering an interpretable pipeline for tackling reward misspecification without entangling the components.
- The dynamic scheduling mechanism ($w_K(t)$, $w_C(t)$) is intuitively sound, addressing the data-hungry cold-start problem of causal discovery by leveraging reliable structural priors to bootstrap exploration before relying on empirical causal signals.
- The empirical evaluation spans three distinct domains with a comprehensive set of baselines (standard, knowledge-augmented, and causal RL), and consistently demonstrates improved robustness to observation noise and distribution shifts compared to existing methods.

## Weaknesses
- **Algorithmic under-specification and continuous causal identifiability** — The core innovation, computing $R_{causal}$ via counterfactual queries using do-calculus, is mathematically undefined. The paper provides no explicit structural equations, normalization procedures, or computational steps for estimating counterfactual effects in continuous, multi-dimensional spaces. Citing identification theory without an actionable approximation algorithm renders the central mechanism unreproducible.
- **Theory delegated to external files and unverified policy invariance** — Section 3.4 consists solely of bullet points claiming convergence, sample efficiency, and policy invariance guarantees, with formal content relegated to an external zip archive. Crucially, the paper does not establish how non-potential-based causal shaping or time-varying weights preserve optimality, breaking standard reward shaping invariance theorems without formal justification. ICLR requires self-contained theoretical rigor, not references to supplementary archives.
- **Methodological flaws in sample efficiency and ablation scope** — The sample efficiency metric relies on an unspecified $J(\pi^*)$, which is intractable in continuous control and traffic environments. Without disclosing how optimal returns are approximated, claims of 50–60% sample speedup are ungrounded. Additionally, all ablation studies are restricted to GridWorld-Complex. Without verifying component contributions across the Franka and Traffic domains, claims of broad architectural validity remain weak.
- **Violation of i.i.d. assumptions in online causal discovery** — The framework employs the PC algorithm updated every 1,000 steps while completely ignoring policy-induced distribution shift. RL agents generate fundamentally non-stationary, feedback-driven trajectories, violating the i.i.d. assumptions required for consistent structure learning. Without addressing how active policy updates induce spurious edges or degrade graph identifiability, the learned causal model risks encoding policy artifacts rather than environment dynamics.
- **Motivation-experiment misalignment** — The introduction heavily motivates the method using LLM alignment and RLVR findings regarding spurious/format-corrupted reward signals, yet all experiments are confined to low-dimensional classical simulations. The paper never evaluates KARMA under explicitly corrupted, deceptive, or high-dimensional perceptual reward conditions, leaving its primary motivation unvalidated.

## Nice-to-Haves
- Include a Potential-Based Reward Shaping (PBRS) baseline to empirically disentangle gains from genuine causal intervention versus standard dense reward padding.
- Quantify robustness to deliberate Knowledge Graph corruption (e.g., missing edges or false relations) to validate claims that the framework tolerates imperfect priors.
- Provide visualizations of the learned causal graphs versus ground-truth environment dynamics, alongside trajectory comparisons under spurious features to transparently demonstrate counterfactual reward intervention.

## Novel Insights
The conceptual core of KARMA—using structured domain knowledge to bootstrap exploration while dynamically ceding control to data-driven causal models as trajectories accumulate—addresses a real friction point in RL. The insight that reward shaping should temporally transition from prior-driven to causality-driven feedback is a useful paradigm for sample-limited settings. However, the current treatment reduces causal inference to a modular plug-in rather than confronting the fundamental statistical challenges of learning causality from policy-active, non-stationary data streams. Bridging this conceptual gap would elevate the work from an architectural combination to a methodological advance.

## Potentially Missed Related Work
- Recent literature on causal representation learning under non-stationary policies and confounding from active exploration, which directly addresses the distribution shift issues in the PC algorithm used here.
- Scalable counterfactual inference methods for continuous state-action spaces and dynamic structural causal model learning, which would provide necessary algorithmic grounding for the claimed $R_{causal}$ computation.

## Suggestions
- Provide explicit mathematical formulations for $R_{knowledge}$ and $R_{causal}$, including normalization, counterfactual estimation in continuous spaces, and a clear algorithm/pseudocode for the reward adjustment loop. Integrate all theoretical proofs into the main manuscript appendix and explicitly state the conditions under which policy invariance is maintained or intentionally relaxed.
- Redefine the sample efficiency metric using a verifiable high-performing reference agent or tractable lower bound instead of an unspecified $J(\pi^*)$. Extend the ablation study to the RSA and TSC environments to validate the generalizability of each module.
- Align the experimental suite with the stated RLVR motivation by testing in environments with injected spurious or corrupted reward signals, or narrow the introduction's scope to classical control to avoid overstating applicability to alignment problems. Explicitly analyze and mitigate the impact of policy-induced non-stationarity on the causal graph learning stability.
