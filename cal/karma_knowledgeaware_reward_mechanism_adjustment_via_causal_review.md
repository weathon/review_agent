# Harsh Critic Review
## Section-by-Section Critical Review

### Title & Abstract
- Does the title accurately reflect the contribution? Yes, it captures the integration of knowledge, causality, and reward adjustment. However, "Causal AI" is a broad umbrella term; the title would be more precise if it reflected the actual technique used (e.g., constraint-based causal discovery and counterfactual reward shaping).
- Does the abstract clearly state the problem, method, and key results? The problem (spurious reward signals) and method (KARMA's three-module architecture) are clear. The claimed results (30% gain, faster convergence, theoretical guarantees) are stated.
- Are any claims in the abstract unsupported by the paper? The abstract claims "theoretical guarantees on convergence and sample efficiency," yet Section 3.4 only provides four high-level bullet points without formal theorem statements, bounds, or proof sketches. The abstract also mentions "causal representation learning," but the method relies on KG embeddings (TransE) and constraint-based structure learning (PC), not representation learning in the neural latent-space sense. This is a slight terminological overreach that should be aligned with the actual implementation.

### Introduction & Motivation
- Is the problem well-motivated? Yes. The fragility of correlation-based reward signals and the empirical observations around spurious feedback in RL (Sec 1.1) are well-contextualized. The proposed KC-MDP formalization (Sec 1.2) directly targets this.
- Are the contributions clearly stated and accurate? Yes, the four-fold contribution list maps cleanly to the paper's structure.
- Does the introduction over-claim or under-sell? The RLVR motivation (citing Shao et al., 2025 from a Notion blog) is timely but conceptually disconnected from the experimental setup (GridWorld, robotic control, traffic signals). Bridging LLM alignment challenges with low-dimensional continuous control requires a clearer argument about why spurious reward dynamics in one domain transfer to another. The introduction also under-specifies the mathematical gap: it states the goal of learning $f: R \times K \times C \to R'$, but does not explain how this differs in principle from existing potential-based or model-based shaping frameworks until Section 3.

### Method / Approach
- Is the method clearly described and reproducible? No, not yet. Equation 1 defines $R_{causal}$ and $R_{knowledge}$ at a conceptual level but lacks operational definitions. How is $R_{knowledge}$ quantified from KG constraints? How is $R_{causal}$ computed via counterfactual queries in high-dimensional or continuous state spaces (e.g., 7-DOF Franka arm) without specifying a structural equation model (SEM) parameterization or discretization strategy? Algorithm 1 is referenced in the appendix, but ICLR standards require core update rules and reward computation steps in the main text.
- Are key assumptions stated and justified? The use of the PC algorithm assumes causal sufficiency, causal faithfulness, and conditional independence tests reliable from finite interaction data. These are acknowledged indirectly but not justified for the sample regimes used in RL (where data is heavily policy-dependent and non-i.i.d.). The choice of FCI vs. PC is mentioned but neither is specified for continuous control tasks, which typically require functional causal models or discretization.
- Are there logical gaps in the derivation or reasoning? The dynamic weights $w_K(t)$ and $w_C(t)$ in Eq 1 make the effective reward function explicitly non-stationary. Standard RL convergence theorems (for PPO, SAC, TD3) assume stationary MDPs or bounded distribution shift. The paper claims "Convergence of KARMA-RL" in Sec 3.4 without addressing how time-varying rewards are handled theoretically or algorithmically (e.g., importance weighting, experience replay adjustments, or decaying learning rates). This is a critical logical gap.
- Are there edge cases or failure modes not discussed? If the causal graph is misspecified (e.g., an edge is wrongly directed or omitted), counterfactual queries yield biased causal rewards. The framework assumes the graph is "correct up to Markov equivalence" but does not discuss how downstream planning reacts to graph uncertainty. No uncertainty-aware reward weighting is implemented, making the method brittle under poor discovery conditions.

### Experiments & Results
- Do the experiments actually test the paper's claims? Yes, they cover performance, efficiency, and OOD/robustness. However, the evaluation of "sample efficiency" using $J(\pi^*)$ in Eq 3 is problematic: in continuous control and traffic environments, the true optimal return $J(\pi^*)$ is intractable. Using a heuristic upper bound invalidates cross-environment SE comparisons unless explicitly stated and justified.
- Are baselines appropriate and fairly compared? The baseline set spans standard RL, knowledge-augmented RL, and causal RL. However, it is unclear whether KBRL, LGRL, KGPN, CIRL, etc., were given identical KG priors and computational budgets. If prior methods use different knowledge formats or hyperparameters, the comparison may be confounded by implementation choices rather than algorithmic merit.
- Are there missing ablations that would materially change conclusions? Ablations (Table 2) are only reported on GCD-Complex. Given that RSA and TSC are high-dimensional continuous environments, the scalability and effectiveness of the knowledge/causal modules in those domains remain unverified. An ablation on continuous tasks is essential to prove that the framework generalizes beyond discrete gridworlds.
- Are error bars / statistical significance reported? Error bars are shown in Fig 5 and means/stds in tables, with t-tests claimed at $\alpha=0.05$. However, exact p-values are not provided, and no correction for multiple comparisons across three environments/five runs is mentioned. For ICLR, explicit p-values or non-parametric tests (e.g., Wilcoxon) are preferred when claiming statistically significant gains.
- Do results support claims or are they cherry-picked? Table 1 shows consistent improvements, and the 30% figure holds in GCD-Simple vs PPO, but the average gain across all tasks is closer to 15-20%. The "5-10% over causal RL baselines" (Sec 5.1) aligns with Table 1 (e.g., 92.6 vs 87.2 in TSC). The claims are broadly supported but should be calibrated toward average relative gains rather than peak improvements.
- Are datasets and evaluation metrics appropriate? Environments are well-chosen but lack specification (e.g., is RSA simulated in MuJoCo, IsaacGym, or PyBullet? Is TSC using SUMO or a simplified traffic grid?). Environmental details are critical for reproducibility and context.

### Writing & Clarity
- Are there sections confusing or poorly explained? Section 3.2 and 3.3 conflate high-level intuition with algorithmic steps. For example, "using Pearl’s do-calculus to estimate causal effects of actions, disentangled from confounders" is stated without showing the structural equations, the identification assumptions satisfied by the MDP, or how do-queries are approximated during online interaction. Readers cannot implement $R_{causal}$ from the text alone.
- Are figures and tables clear and informative? Tables 1-3 are well-structured and report standard deviations. Figure 6 (transfer/noise robustness) is conceptually clear but the exact nature of "distribution shifts" (e.g., changed goal locations vs. altered dynamics vs. modified causal structure) is not defined in the caption or text, making the generalization claims hard to verify.

### Limitations & Broader Impact
- Do the authors acknowledge the key limitations? Yes, dependency on KG quality, imperfect causal models, and high-dimensional scalability are noted in Sec 6.
- Are there fundamental limitations they missed? First, the non-stationarity induced by $w_K(t), w_C(t)$ directly conflicts with off-policy baselines (SAC, TD3) which assume a fixed environment distribution unless corrected. This implementation detail limits the fairness of the baseline comparison if not handled. Second, constraint-based causal discovery scales combinatorially with variable count; updating it every 1000 steps (Sec 4.4) in high-dimensional spaces will bottleneck training throughput, which is under-discussed compared to Table 3's modest overhead report.
- Are there failure modes or negative societal impacts not discussed? Since the paper references alignment (Sec 1.4), the authors should discuss scenarios where an incorrect causal graph amplifies reward misalignment (e.g., rewarding actions that exploit a misspecified causal link rather than correcting it). The broader impact section could explicitly address that causal reward shaping does not inherently guarantee safer behavior if the prior knowledge itself encodes biased or suboptimal constraints.

### Overall Assessment
KARMA addresses a genuinely important problem in RL: the fragility of correlation-based reward signals and the need for principled reward adjustment. The integration of knowledge graphs, causal discovery, and counterfactual reasoning into a unified shaping framework is conceptually promising and well-motivated. However, the paper currently falls short of ICLR’s acceptance bar due to insufficient technical depth and experimental rigor. The core reward adjustment mechanism (Eq 1) lacks operational definitions for $R_{knowledge}$ and $R_{causal}$, making reproduction difficult. The theoretical claims regarding convergence and sample efficiency are asserted without formal statements or proofs addressing the critical non-stationarity introduced by time-varying weights. Additionally, the empirical evaluation is heavy on discrete grid environments but lacks crucial ablations and methodological details for continuous control tasks, undermining the generality of the claims. The sample efficiency metric is ill-defined when $\pi^*$ is unknown, and baseline comparisons lack clarity on knowledge equivalence and off-policy corrections. If the authors can provide precise algorithmic formulations for the causal and knowledge reward components, formally analyze or mitigate the impact of dynamic reward non-stationarity on PPO/SAC convergence, add ablations for continuous environments, and clarify evaluation metrics and environmental setups, this work would represent a strong contribution to reliable RL. As it stands, it requires significant strengthening in mathematical rigor, algorithmic transparency, and empirical validation to meet conference standards.

# Neutral Reviewer
## Balanced Review

### Summary
This paper introduces KARMA, a reinforcement learning framework that dynamically adjusts reward signals by integrating structured domain knowledge (via knowledge graphs) and causal structure learning (via counterfactual reasoning). The method aims to mitigate spurious correlations, improve sample efficiency, and enhance robustness in sequential decision-making. Experiments across grid navigation, robotic manipulation, and traffic control demonstrate performance gains of up to 30% and faster convergence relative to standard RL, knowledge-augmented, and causal RL baselines.

### Strengths
1. **Clear Motivation & Timely Problem Formulation:** The paper directly addresses a critical pain point in RL and modern alignment research: the fragility of correlation-based reward signals. Section 1.1 effectively contextualizes the problem using recent findings on spurious rewards in RLVR and frames it within the formal KC-MDP objective.
2. **Well-Designed Modularity & Comprehensive Empirics:** KARMA is cleanly decomposed into knowledge integration, causal discovery, and counterfactual reward adjustment. The experimental design spans environments of varying complexity (GCD, RSA, TSC), uses 9 strong baselines, and provides detailed evaluations on sample efficiency, zero-shot generalization, observation noise, and computational overhead. Table 1 and Table 2 clearly demonstrate consistent empirical superiority and isolate the contribution of each component.
3. **Strong Generalization & Robustness Results:** The evaluation extends beyond average returns to distribution shift and noise robustness (Sec 5.3, Figure 6), showing KARMA retains 78–85% performance in unseen settings and >80% under 20% observation noise. This aligns with ICLR's emphasis on robust, out-of-distribution generalization rather than pure leaderboard chasing.

### Weaknesses
1. **Theoretical Guarantees Lack Formal Rigor in the Main Text:** Section 3.4 lists convergence and policy invariance claims as bullet points without stating formal theorems, precise assumptions, or mathematical bounds. For instance, policy invariance is noted to hold *if* $R_{knowledge}$ is potential-based (Ng et al., 1999), but the paper does not prove or explicitly show that the knowledge reward satisfies this condition in practice.
2. **Mathematical & Algorithmic Details of Reward Adjustment Are Under-Specified:** Equation (1) introduces $R_{knowledge}$ and $R_{causal}$, but their exact mathematical formulations are missing. The paper states $R_{causal}$ uses do-calculus to estimate causal effects, yet counterfactual computation in continuous-state/action spaces requires explicit structural equation modeling (SEM) definitions and identification assumptions (e.g., causal sufficiency, no unmeasured confounders). Furthermore, Section 3.3 describes weights as "dynamic," but Section 4.4 reveals they are merely linearly annealed from fixed initial values, which contradicts the claim of adaptive reasoning.
3. **Gap Between Motivation and Experimental Validation:** The introduction heavily cites RLHF/RLVR and spurious signals in LLM fine-tuning as motivation, yet all empirical evaluations are conducted on classical control and planning environments. The transferability of KARMA to high-dimensional, discrete-language-action spaces or noisy human feedback loops remains untested, leaving the stated motivation only partially supported by evidence.

### Novelty & Significance
- **Novelty:** The synthesis of neuro-symbolic knowledge graphs with causal discovery for dynamic reward shaping is a meaningful step beyond existing causal RL or knowledge-injected RL methods. While KG integration and PC-based causal discovery are individually established, their joint use for counterfactual reward adjustment with temporally shifting emphasis is novel in formulation.
- **Clarity:** The paper is well-structured, logically organized, and generally easy to follow. However, the lack of explicit mathematical definitions for the reward components and causal queries reduces technical clarity for reproduction.
- **Reproducibility:** The promised public code, standard hardware setup, and transparent baseline selection are positives. However, missing hyperparameter details for the causal discovery step (e.g., discretization strategy for continuous variables in RSA/TSC, exact counterfactual sampling procedure) and the static vs. dynamic weight discrepancy may hinder exact reproduction.
- **Significance:** Reward shaping and spurious signal mitigation are foundational challenges in safe and efficient RL. If KARMA's causal grounding can be reliably extended to real-world or alignment-critical domains, it offers high significance. The current empirical gains are solid but incremental over strong baselines; the true significance hinges on theoretical soundness and broader domain validation.

### Suggestions for Improvement
1. **Formalize the Theoretical Claims & Reward Functions:** Provide explicit mathematical definitions for $R_{knowledge}(s,a,s')$ and $R_{causal}(s,a,s')$ in the main text. If $R_{knowledge}$ relies on potential-based shaping, prove or empirically verify that it satisfies the potential condition. Move key convergence assumptions and bounds from the appendix to the main text or clearly reference them with theorem-style formatting to meet ICLR's theoretical standards.
2. **Align Terminology with Implementation & Clarify Causal Computation:** If the weights are linearly annealed, describe them as a "scheduled curriculum" rather than "dynamic adaptation," or implement a genuinely dynamic mechanism (e.g., learning-based gating or uncertainty-weighted mixing). Additionally, detail how the SEM is parameterized and updated for continuous environments, and explicitly state the causal assumptions (e.g., causal sufficiency, faithfulness) required for the PC algorithm to yield valid counterfactuals.
3. **Bridge the Experimental Gap to the Stated Motivation:** Add an experiment or controlled simulation that directly tests KARMA's ability to handle spurious/noisy reward feedback analogous to RLVR settings (e.g., reward flipping, synthetic human labeling noise, or format-based distractors). This would empirically validate the paper's core motivation and significantly strengthen its relevance to modern alignment research.

# Spark Finder Review
## How to Improve This Paper

### Missing Experiments (top 3-5 only)
1. **Online causal graph recovery accuracy:** Report Structural Hamming Distance between the discovered graph and ground truth across training steps to prove the causal module learns valid structures from non-i.i.d. RL rollouts rather than fitting noise.
2. **Systematic spurious correlation strength sweep:** Evaluate performance while varying the correlation coefficient between spurious features and rewards in the GridWorld; without this, you cannot claim the framework actively discauses misleading signals rather than simply adding dense reward shaping.
3. **Knowledge graph noise injection:** Experimentally degrade KG quality by injecting false triples and removing critical edges to quantify the claimed robustness to imperfect priors and identify the exact failure threshold of the method.

### Deeper Analysis Needed (top 3-5 only)
1. **Formal theorem derivation:** Replace bullet-point assertions with explicit mathematical assumptions and theorem statements for convergence under dynamic weighting to meet ICLR's theoretical rigor standards.
2. **Dynamic weight schedule sensitivity:** Ablate the linear annealing of $w_K$ and $w_C$ against fixed and non-linear schedules to prove performance gains stem from causal mechanisms rather than a heuristic curriculum learning effect.
3. **Representation vs. reward shaping isolation:** Compare KARMA against a baseline that concatenates identical KG embeddings to the state but uses raw rewards; this isolates how much gain is attributable to the counterfactual reward module versus enriched state perception, which is currently conflated.

### Visualizations & Case Studies
1. **Ground-truth vs. learned graph overlay:** Visualize the recovered causal structure against the true DAG at early and late training stages to verify that counterfactual queries operate on a valid causal model rather than unstable or incorrect edges.
2. **Reward divergence trajectory analysis:** Plot state-action trajectories where the counterfactual reward $R'$ significantly corrects a high raw reward $r$ caused by spurious features, explicitly showing when KARMA overrides signals that deceive baseline agents.
3. **Feature attribution heatmaps:** Display state visitation frequencies or attention maps highlighting focus on causally relevant variables versus spurious distractors under distribution shift, directly validating the "causal compass" mechanism intuition.

### Obvious Next Steps
1. **RLVR/LLM alignment experiment:** The introduction cites spurious rewards in LLM verification as the primary motivation but experiments ignore this domain entirely; add an RLVR or math-reasoning benchmark to bridge this critical motivation-method gap.
2. **Differentiable causal discovery scaling:** Constraint-based PC algorithms are computationally prohibitive for high-dimensional continuous control; you must demonstrate scalability or integrate differentiable DAG learning methods since standard PC will fail on dense visual or high-DOF states.
3. **Policy invariance formalization:** Explicitly derive how KARMA’s counterfactual shaping violates or satisfies Potential-Based Reward Shaping conditions to clarify when the optimal policy is mathematically guaranteed versus when the reward adjustment is intentionally altering the optimization landscape.

# Final Consolidated Review
## Summary
This paper introduces KARMA, a reinforcement learning framework that dynamically adjusts reward signals by integrating structured domain knowledge (via knowledge graphs) and causal discovery (via counterfactual reasoning). The method aims to disentangle true causal drivers from spurious correlations, claiming theoretical convergence guarantees and demonstrating up to 30% performance gains, faster convergence, and improved robustness across grid navigation, robotic control, and traffic management benchmarks.

## Strengths
- **Well-motivated, modular architecture:** The paper cleanly decomposes the reward shaping problem into knowledge integration, causal structure learning, and counterfactual reward adjustment. This modularity is directly supported by the ablation study (Table 2), which isolates each component's contribution and confirms the central role of the reward adjustment module.
- **Comprehensive empirical validation across diverse settings:** Experiments span discrete, low-dimensional continuous, and multi-agent-adjacent environments with 9 strong baselines. Results consistently show gains in cumulative reward, sample efficiency, and robustness to observation noise and distribution shifts, with error bars reported across 5 independent runs.
- **Clear alignment of design choices with intended behavior:** The use of MDP temporal constraints to guide the PC algorithm, coupled with knowledge-guided graph refinement, provides a principled mechanism for reducing the combinatorial search space of causal discovery in RL, directly addressing a known bottleneck in causal RL.

## Weaknesses
- **Under-specified reward formulations hinder reproducibility:** Equation (1) introduces $R_{knowledge}$ and $R_{causal}$ as additive shaping terms, but their precise mathematical definitions are absent from the main text. The paper states $R_{causal}$ uses "do-calculus" and "structural equation modeling," yet it does not specify how the SEM is parameterized, how counterfactual distributions are estimated in continuous spaces (e.g., the 7-DOF panda arm), or how causal sufficiency is enforced given non-i.i.d. RL rollouts. Without explicit computation steps or pseudocode, the core contribution cannot be independently reproduced.
- **Theoretical claims conflict with the implemented scheduling mechanism:** Section 3.4 claims convergence guarantees and policy invariance, but Section 4.4 reveals that the weighting terms $w_K(t)$ and $w_C(t)$ are linearly annealed. Time-varying rewards induce non-stationarity, which standard PPO/SAC convergence theorems do not cover. The paper does not formally address how the annealing schedule interacts with off-policy replay or gradient estimation, creating a gap between the asserted theoretical guarantees and the curriculum-style implementation.
- **Narrative-method gap regarding stated motivation:** The introduction heavily frames the problem around spurious feedback in RLVR/LLM alignment (citing Shao et al., 2025), positioning KARMA as a solution to unreliable verification signals. However, all experiments are restricted to classical control and planning environments. While the chosen benchmarks are standard for RL, they do not test the framework's ability to handle the specific high-dimensional, noisy feedback or discrete-language-action spaces highlighted in the motivation, weakening the empirical validation of the paper's primary problem statement.

## Nice-to-Haves
- Add an ablation injecting false triples or removing critical edges from the KG to quantify the claimed robustness to imperfect priors and identify empirical failure thresholds.
- Report the Structural Hamming Distance (SHD) or similar graph recovery metric between the discovered causal structure and any known ground truth during training to empirically validate that counterfactual queries are built on valid structures.
- Include a controlled experiment with synthetic spurious rewards (e.g., reward flipping or distractor-based formatting penalties) to directly bridge the gap between the RLVR motivation and the method's capabilities.
- Report exact p-values or apply non-parametric statistical tests across the three environments to formally confirm significance beyond visual overlap in learning curves.

## Novel Insights
The paper reveals a practical tension between theoretically grounded causal adjustment and the realities of RL training dynamics: while coupling knowledge graphs with constraint-based discovery successfully reshapes reward landscapes away from spurious correlations, the "dynamic" adaptation currently functions as a fixed curriculum (linear weight annealing) rather than a data-driven, uncertainty-aware mechanism. This suggests that the empirical gains may stem partly from structured reward scheduling rather than pure causal reasoning, highlighting an open direction for integrating learned uncertainty bounds from causal discovery directly into the reward weighting policy.

## Suggestions
- Provide explicit mathematical definitions or a clear algorithmic pseudocode in the main text for computing $R_{knowledge}(s,a,s')$ from KG constraints and $R_{causal}(s,a,s')$ via counterfactual queries, explicitly detailing how the SCM/SEM is fit and queried for continuous state-action spaces.
- Align the theoretical claims with the implementation: either formally extend the convergence analysis to handle non-stationary, scheduled reward shaping, or rephrase Section 3.4 to frame the weight mechanism as a curriculum strategy rather than an adaptive causal inference process.
- Clarify the discretization strategy, functional form assumptions, or conditional independence test used to apply the PC algorithm to the RSA and TSC environments, and explicitly state how the policy-dependent, temporally correlated nature of RL data impacts causal faithfulness and sufficiency assumptions.
