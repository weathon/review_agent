# Harsh Critic Review
## Section-by-Section Critical Review

### Title & Abstract
- The title accurately captures the core contribution (scalable experimental design for ethical testing) and aligns with the paper's focus on Bayesian experimental design (BED) and hierarchical modeling.
- The abstract clearly outlines the problem (lack of objective/subjective metrics, high evaluation cost), the method (hierarchical GPs, joint acquisition, LLM proxy), and reports quantitative results (up to 2× optimal candidates, 1.25× coverage). However, the metrics "optimal test candidates" and "coverage" are not defined in the abstract, making the reported improvements opaque without reading further.
- The claim "generating up to 2× optimal test candidates compared to baselines, withdimensional search spaces" contains a parsing artifact, but more importantly, it lacks a clear operational definition of what constitutes an "optimal" candidate in this context (e.g., does it mean highest subjective utility, most informative for the GP, or closest to a failure boundary?). Clarifying this metric upfront in both abstract and main text is necessary for the claims to land accurately.

### Introduction & Motivation
- The problem is well-motivated: ethical testing of autonomous systems is expensive, subjective, and lacks standardized metrics. The three challenges identified (measurement difficulty, evolving stakeholder preferences, sample constraints) are concrete and align with real deployment barriers.
- Contributions are clearly stated and map well to the paper structure. However, the claim that this is the *first* framework to explicitly consider both objective and subjective ethical evaluation criteria is an overstatement. Prior work in Bayesian optimization with preference exploration (e.g., Lin et al., 2022; Astudillo & Frazier, 2019) already formalizes composite objective-to-preference mappings. The novelty here lies in the specific BED formulation, hierarchical VGP structure, and the joint acquisition function, not in the conceptual coupling itself. Toning down this claim to focus on the methodological distinctions would strengthen the positioning.
- The introduction appropriately sets up the sample-constrained setting and justifies the BED paradigm without over-claiming performance guarantees.

### Method / Approach
- The hierarchical VGP (HVGP) structure is intuitively motivated and clearly separated into an objective GP ($g: x \mapsto y$) and a subjective GP ($h: y \mapsto z$). Assumptions A1–A3 are standard for BED/preference learning and are reasonably justified for fixed-policy system testing.
- **Critical gap in acquisition function derivation:** The core technical contribution is Eq. (2): $V(x) = I(g_x; y|\mathcal{D}) + \mathbb{E}_{q_\phi(y|x)}[ I(h_y; z|\mathcal{D}) + \mathbb{E}_{q_\psi(h_y)}[h_y] ]$. While the intuition (explore objective uncertainty, explore subjective uncertainty, exploit predicted utility) is reasonable, the mathematical justification for *additively combining* mutual information (an information-theoretic quantity measured in nats/bits) with an expected utility score (measured in arbitrary latent units) is missing. These terms operate on different scales and lack normalization or weighting mechanisms, making the optimization sensitive to hyperparameters and kernel length-scales. ICLR reviewers will expect either a principled scalarization, a Pareto-based formulation, or a clear discussion of how term magnitudes are balanced during maximization.
- **Computational tractability:** The inner expectation $\mathbb{E}_{q_\phi(y|x)}[\cdot]$ requires numerical integration or Monte Carlo estimation since $y$ is continuous. Coupled with the intractability of mutual information for GP classification under a probit likelihood (Appendix A.4), the practical computation of $V(x)$ is non-trivial. The paper does not detail the approximation scheme, number of MC samples, reparameterization tricks, or gradient estimators used to maximize $V(x)$. Without this, reproducibility and the claim of scalability remain unsubstantiated.
- **Edge cases:** If the objective GP and subjective GP suggest conflicting query points (e.g., highly informative $y$ regions vs. high-utility $y$ regions), Eq. (2) offers no mechanism to resolve this trade-off beyond additive summation. A discussion of failure modes under strong objective-subjective misalignment would strengthen robustness claims.

### Experiments & Results
- The experiments test the sample efficiency and preference alignment claims across two primary domains (Power Grid, Fire Rescue) plus an appendix case study. Using custom simulators is acceptable for ICLR when ground-truth systems are unavailable, provided the setup is rigorous.
- **Baseline fairness:** VS-AL baselines are acknowledged as suboptimal for test generation (designed for prediction accuracy), which weakens their utility as competitive baselines. BOPE (Appendix I) is a stronger comparison, but the paper notes its performance is highly sensitive to design choices (phase switching). For an ICLR submission, including standard preference-aware BO baselines (e.g., PES, TSEI-P, or Thompson Sampling for pairwise GP classifiers) would provide a more level playing field. Currently, the baselines appear tuned or mismatched, making SEED-SET's advantage easier to achieve.
- **Evaluation circularity:** The primary metric is a handcrafted preference score $h(y)$ designed to be "proportional to $f_{\text{subj}}$" and to "approximate the LLM's preference function." Since the acquisition strategy is explicitly guided by the LLM's preferences, evaluating success against a function that was reverse-engineered to match those same preferences creates a circular validation loop. While TrueSkill ratings (Figure 7) offer an alternative, they are noisy, seed-dependent, and not directly comparable across methods. Statistical significance tests or confidence intervals across the 5 seeds are absent; mean ± shaded std dev is standard but often insufficient to claim superiority at ICLR.
- **Missing ablations:** The paper does not ablate key hyperparameters of the VGP (e.g., number of inducing points $M$, kernel choice, MC sample count for the acquisition expectation). Without these, it's unclear if gains come from the hierarchical/bed formulation or from standard GP hyperparameter tuning. Additionally, the claim of LLM robustness (Appendix F) only varies temperature and model, but LLM preference consistency under scenario complexity shifts or contradictory objectives is not stress-tested.
- Datasets and metrics are appropriate in scope but require tighter operational definitions, particularly for the coverage score $\tilde{c}(x)$, which is mentioned but not formally derived or normalized across dimensions.

### Writing & Clarity
- The paper is well-structured and follows a logical flow from motivation to methodology to experiments. The decomposition of objectives and subjective preferences in Section 4.1 is clearly articulated.
- **Problem formulation notation:** Problem 3.1 states $f: \Pi \times \mathcal{X} \to y \in \mathcal{Y}$, but later decomposes $f$ into $f_{\text{obj}}: \mathcal{X} \to \mathcal{Y}$ and $f_{\text{subj}}: \mathcal{Y} \to \mathbb{R}$. The mapping from $\Pi \times \mathcal{X}$ is inconsistent with the later hierarchical assumption, and the role of $\pi_S$ beyond being a fixed simulator is under-specified. Clarifying whether $x$ represents environmental parameters, policy hyperparameters, or both would remove ambiguity.
- Figure 1 effectively summarizes the pipeline. Figures 3–6 and 18 are informative, though overlapping lines in some plots reduce readability. The garbled rendering of Figure 9 is a parser artifact and does not hinder understanding due to the detailed textual description in Appendix C.
- No major clarity issues impede comprehension of the contribution, but a visual or mathematical breakdown of how Eq. (2) behaves in a 2D toy example (showing how the three terms interact spatially) would significantly improve intuition.

### Limitations & Broader Impact
- The authors appropriately acknowledge scalability beyond tens of thousands of points, the restriction of stationary kernels, the requirement of known objective metrics a priori, and LLM prompt sensitivity. These are honest and standard limitations.
- **Missed limitations:** The method assumes a stationary latent utility model per stakeholder (A2), but ethical preferences in high-stakes domains are often context-dependent, evolving, or contradictory. The framework does not account for preference drift over sequential testing, which is common when evaluators observe new failure modes. Additionally, the multi-stakeholder extension (Appendix G) still routes through a single LLM proxy asked to "compromise," which does not genuinely model inter-stakeholder bargaining or fairness guarantees.
- **Broader impact & failure modes:** The paper's ethics statement correctly avoids prescribing normative values and relies on user-provided criteria. However, if the acquisition function fails to explore low-probability, high-risk ethical failure regions (e.g., edge-case collisions in Fire Rescue), the system could produce falsely confident safety certifications. Discussing how to guarantee minimum exploration coverage for risk-averse testing, or how to detect when the LLM proxy diverges from actual stakeholder values in deployment, is critical for real-world applicability.

### Overall Assessment
SEED-SET addresses a timely and practically relevant problem: sample-efficient ethical benchmarking of autonomous systems under combined objective and subjective constraints. The hierarchical VGP formulation and joint acquisition strategy offer a principled alternative to decoupled preference exploration methods. However, for ICLR's acceptance bar, several key elements require strengthening. The acquisition function (Eq. 2) lacks theoretical justification for additively combining information-theoretic and utility terms, and its computational tractability under nested expectations and probit likelihoods is not detailed. The evaluation setup suffers from circular validation (handcrafted preference score mirroring the LLM prompt) and underpowered baselines; adding standard preference-BO baselines, formalizing the coverage metric, and reporting statistical significance across more seeds would substantially bolster empirical claims. Finally, toning down the "first to combine objectives and preferences" claim and explicitly discussing optimization failure modes (e.g., term scaling conflicts, low-probability risk region omission) would improve rigor and practical grounding. Despite these concerns, the core idea is sound, well-motivated, and aligns with ICLR's interest in principled experimental design for AI evaluation. With the outlined methodological clarifications and more robust empirical validation, this work has strong potential for publication.

# Neutral Reviewer
## Balanced Review

### Summary
This paper introduces SEED-SET, a Bayesian experimental design framework for evaluating the ethical alignment of autonomous systems under strict sample-constrained budgets. The authors propose a hierarchical Variational Gaussian Process (VGP) architecture that separately models objective system behaviors and subjective stakeholder preferences, guided by a novel joint acquisition criterion that balances information-theoretic exploration with preference-driven exploitation. Empirical evaluations across power grid allocation, drone fire rescue, and urban routing tasks demonstrate that the framework achieves higher preference alignment and search-space coverage compared to several active learning and Bayesian optimization baselines.

### Strengths
1. **Well-Motivated Hierarchical Formulation:** The decomposition of ethical testing into an objective layer ($x \to y$) and a subjective layer ($y \to z$) is conceptually sound and mirrors real-world evaluation pipelines where stakeholders judge observable metrics rather than raw design parameters. This separation improves interpretability and data efficiency, as explicitly discussed in Section 4.1.
2. **Comprehensive Empirical Validation:** The paper provides extensive experiments across three distinct domains, accompanied by meaningful ablations on acquisition components, LLM parameters, prompt variations, and multi-stakeholder criteria. Comparisons against diverse baselines (Single GP, VS-AL variants, BOPE variants) thoroughly benchmark sample efficiency and preference recovery (Sections 5, F, G, I).
3. **Explicit Uncertainty & Scalability Considerations:** The adoption of Variational GPs and sparse approximation appropriately addresses computational bottlenecks ($O(n^3) \to O(nm^2)$) in iterative experimental design. The framework explicitly models evaluation noise and propagates uncertainty through both GP layers, which is critical for safe, low-budget ethical testing in simulation (Sections 3, 4.1).

### Weaknesses
1. **Circular Evaluation & Lack of Real Human Ground Truth:** The framework's performance is evaluated using handcrafted deterministic preference score functions that approximate the LLM's preferences (Section 5, "Metrics"). This creates a circular validation loop where the model is optimized to please a synthetic proxy whose "ground truth" is linearly constructed by the authors. Without real human preference data, claims about actual ethical alignment or human value recovery remain unsubstantiated.
2. **Heuristic Acquisition Function with Limited Theoretical Grounding:** The joint acquisition criterion (Eq. 2) additively combines mutual information from both layers and an expected utility term: $V(x) = I(g_x; y) + \mathbb{E}[I(h_y; z) + \mathbb{E}[h_y]]$. While intuitive, this formulation lacks rigorous justification. The weighting, additivity assumption, and expectation operations are presented heuristically without theoretical guarantees (e.g., convergence properties, regret bounds, or connection to established composite BO objectives), which falls short of ICLR's typical standards for methodological rigor.
3. **Ambiguous Pair Selection Mechanism:** The framework relies on pairwise preference elicitation, yet the acquisition function $V(x)$ is defined over a single candidate. The text states that "two jointly evaluated candidates $x = [x_1, x_2]$ can be obtained by maximizing V," but does not explain the pairing strategy, batch acquisition formulation, or how pairwise dependencies/uncertainties are handled during selection. This gap obscures reproducibility and methodological clarity (Section 4.2, 5).
4. **Overstated Scalability Claims:** The abstract and conclusion claim scalability to "high-dimensional scenarios," yet the empirical evaluation caps at ~40 dimensions (30-Bus case). Variational GPs and kernel-based methods face well-documented curse-of-dimensionality challenges beyond 50-100 parameters. The paper does not analyze how inducing point selection or kernel hyperparameters degrade in truly high-dimensional spaces, making the scalability claim potentially misleading.

### Novelty & Significance
The hierarchical VGP architecture and joint acquisition strategy offer a principled, modular approach to separating observable metrics from subjective judgments, representing a practical and incremental contribution to composite Bayesian optimization and preference learning. However, the core machine learning novelty is constrained; hierarchical GPs, mutual information acquisition, and preference exploration via pairwise comparisons are established in the literature. The specific combination in Eq. (2) feels more like an applied engineering adaptation than a foundational algorithmic breakthrough. For ICLR, which typically prioritizes rigorous theoretical analysis or breakthrough empirical performance on core ML benchmarks, the paper's strength lies in its systems-level perspective on AI safety evaluation and thorough ablation. Its significance is moderate, contingent on framing it as a practical experimental design tool rather than a novel ML architecture.

### Suggestions for Improvement
1. **Clarify the Pair Selection Strategy:** Provide explicit mathematical or algorithmic detail on how the optimal pair $(x_1, x_2)$ is derived from $V(x)$. If selected independently or via greedy batching, discuss the approximation error regarding pairwise preference dependencies. Consider adapting established qEI/EUBO pair-selection techniques or explicitly formulating a joint $V(x_1, x_2)$ criterion.
2. **Strengthen Theoretical Justification for Eq. (2):** Relate the additive acquisition structure to established composite BO literature (e.g., EUBO, qNEI, or information-theoretic bounds). Provide a lemma or discussion justifying the additive combination of mutual information and expected utility, perhaps deriving it as an upper bound on expected utility improvement or an approximation to the Expected Information Gain over the latent preference parameters.
3. **Reframe or Augment Ground Truth Evaluation:** Clearly bound claims to state that SEED-SET efficiently approximates *simulated/LLM-encoding* stakeholder preferences. To strengthen the ethical testing claim, include at least one pilot experiment with real human annotators or analyze how performance degrades when the LLM proxy is replaced with noisy/misaligned human feedback (Appendix H partially touches this but focuses on mode choice rather than ethical preference).
4. **Improve Reproducibility & Open Science Practices:** ICLR strongly encourages open-source artifacts. Replace "code will be released upon request" with an anonymous GitHub or Zenodo link. Include the full set of prompts, VGP kernel/hyperparameter configurations, inducing point counts ($M$), and exact random seeds in the supplementary material to enable direct reproduction of Figures 3–7.
5. **Calibrate Dimensionality & Scalability Claims:** Update the abstract/conclusion to reflect the actual tested dimensionality (<50D). Add a brief empirical or theoretical discussion on how kernel choice, lengthscale priors, and inducing point placement ($M$) scale with dimensionality for the tasks considered, acknowledging GP limitations and explicitly stating where the method's practical scaling ceiling lies.

# Spark Finder Review
## How to Improve This Paper

### Missing Experiments (top 3-5 only)
1. **Human Expert vs. LLM Proxy Validation:** Add an experiment comparing LLM pairwise judgments to those of actual human domain experts for the same scenario outcomes. Without quantifying LLM-human divergence, the core claim that LLMs can reliably act as ethical proxies is unverified.
2. **Standard Multi-Objective BO Baselines:** Compare against dedicated Multi-Objective Bayesian Optimization baselines (e.g., EHVI, q-ParEGO) that optimize a surrogate utility function over the same objective space. Without showing SEED-SET outperforms standard MOBO, the hierarchical complexity and joint acquisition are not justified.
3. **Pareto-Optimal Ground Truth Comparison:** Validate discovery against a computed Pareto front of the objective metrics rather than a handcrafted linear preference score. The current validation is circular; demonstrating convergence to the true non-dominated set is required to claim effective ethical trade-off discovery.
4. **Empirical Computational Scaling:** Report wall-clock time, memory usage, and optimization overhead per iteration as a function of dataset size and design dimensionality. Without these metrics, the "Scalable" claim in the title and contributions remains theoretical rather than proven.

### Deeper Analysis Needed (top 3-5 only)
1. **Acquisition Function Term Magnitude Analysis:** Analyze the relative numerical scales of the three terms in Equation 2 across iterations. Without proving these terms contribute meaningfully rather than one dominating, the claimed "balanced exploration-exploitation" mechanism is numerically unsupported.
2. **Subjective GP Posterior Calibration:** Quantify the calibration of the Subjective GP uncertainty (e.g., Expected Calibration Error or CRPS plots). The acquisition strategy relies heavily on `q_psi` uncertainty to guide exploration; poor calibration would misdirect queries and invalidate sample-efficiency results.
3. **Objective Misspecification Robustness:** Test performance when the objective space `Y` is incomplete (e.g., omitting a key metric) or polluted with highly correlated/redundant variables. The framework assumes complete, known objectives (Assumption A3); quantifying degradation under misspecification is critical for real-world applicability.

### Visualizations & Case Studies
1. **Objective Space Trajectory Overlay:** Plot the sequence of queried points from SEED-SET versus baselines directly on the 2D/3D objective space with the underlying preference surface. This reveals whether the method navigates toward high-utility, uncertain regions or collapses into trivial local optima.
2. **LLM-Human Preference Divergence Map:** A confusion matrix or calibration plot showing where LLM preferences systematically diverge from human expert judgments on specific trade-off boundaries. This exposes the exact regimes where the LLM proxy fails as an ethical oracle.
3. **Dimensional Irrelevant Feature Stress Test:** Visualize performance degradation as the ratio of irrelevant design parameters increases significantly. This demonstrates whether the hierarchical decomposition truly isolates objective learning or suffers from the curse of dimensionality like the Single GP baseline.

### Obvious Next Steps
1. **Dynamic Criterion Adaptation:** The title and intro claim "Evolving Experimental Design" and "adapting value alignment," yet all experiments use static criteria per run. Testing mid-loop stakeholder preference shifts is required to validate the "Evolving" claim.
2. **Batched/Asynchronous Query Simulation:** Real-world system testing rarely supports strictly sequential, zero-delay feedback. Simulating batched BED with asynchronous updates is an immediate next step to bridge the gap from simulator to practical deployment.
3. **Non-Stationary Kernel Extension:** The limitations section notes that stationary RBF kernels cannot handle varying regimes. Implementing and evaluating input-warping or spectral mixture kernels is the logical next step to prove the framework handles complex, real-world ethical landscapes.

# Final Consolidated Review
## Summary
This paper proposes SEED-SET, a Bayesian experimental design framework for sample-efficient ethical testing of autonomous systems. It introduces a hierarchical Variational Gaussian Process (VGP) architecture that decouples objective system behaviors from subjective stakeholder preferences, guided by a joint acquisition function that balances information-theoretic exploration with utility exploitation. The method is evaluated across three simulated domains (power grid allocation, drone fire rescue, urban routing) using LLM-assisted pairwise elicitation and benchmarked against several active learning and preference optimization baselines.

## Strengths
- **Conceptually clean hierarchical decomposition:** Separating the mapping from design parameters to observable metrics ($x \to y$) from observable metrics to latent utility ($y \to z$) correctly mirrors real-world evaluation pipelines where stakeholders judge perceivable outcomes rather than raw design parameters. This modularity improves interpretability and isolates uncertainty propagation across layers.
- **Systematic empirical scope and ablations:** The paper conducts thorough component-wise ablations across acquisition terms, LLM temperature/prompts/models, and multi-stakeholder preference configurations. The consistent outperformance of the full SEED-SET pipeline against fragmented variants demonstrates that the proposed coupling of objective uncertainty reduction and preference exploitation is empirically cohesive within the tested regimes.

## Weaknesses
- **Heuristic and mathematically unjustified acquisition formulation:** Equation (2) additively combines mutual information terms (measured in nats/bits) with expected latent utility values (arbitrary, unnormalized scale) without weighting, normalization, or theoretical grounding. This scalarization is highly sensitive to kernel hyperparameters and inducing point counts, risking term dominance that would collapse the acquisition into pure exploration or pure exploitation. The paper provides no derivation, regret bounds, or analysis of term magnitudes across iterations to justify this heuristic combination.
- **Circular validation and absence of ground-truth ethical alignment:** Performance is primarily measured using handcrafted linear preference scores explicitly engineered to approximate the LLM prompt used for optimization. This creates a closed evaluation loop where the model is rewarded for matching a synthetic proxy it was directly trained against. While TrueSkill ratings and coverage metrics are reported, they lack statistical significance testing, cross-stakeholder generalization evidence, or comparison against computed Pareto fronts. Consequently, claims of "ethical alignment" remain methodologically unverified.
- **Undefined pair selection and opaque computational tractability:** The acquisition function $V(x)$ is formulated for single-candidate evaluation, yet the methodology claims to produce "two jointly evaluated candidates $x=[x_1, x_2]$ by maximizing $V$." The paper fails to specify whether pairs are selected independently, greedily, or via a joint criterion, leaving a critical reproducibility gap. Furthermore, the inner expectation $\mathbb{E}_{q_\phi(y|x)}[\cdot]$ and MI computation under probit GP classification require numerical approximation; the paper omits details on Monte Carlo sample counts, reparameterization tricks, gradient estimators, or wall-clock overhead per iteration, undermining the "scalable" claim.
- **Overstated novelty and misaligned baselines:** The positioning as the "first" framework to explicitly integrate objective and subjective ethical evaluation ignores established composite preference Bayesian optimization (e.g., Lin et al., 2022; Astudillo & Frazier, 2019). Additionally, baselines like VS-AL are acknowledged as designed for prediction accuracy rather than test generation, and BOPE variants are shown to be sensitive to arbitrary phase-switching heuristics. The absence of standard preference-aware BO or multi-objective BO baselines (e.g., PES, q-ParEGO) creates an uneven competitive landscape that inflates perceived gains.

## Nice-to-Haves
- Include a numerical analysis of the relative scales of the three terms in Eq. (2) across different iterations and domains to demonstrate balanced contribution.
- Evaluate robustness to objective space misspecification (e.g., omitted or highly correlated metrics), as Assumption A3 (complete objective knowledge a priori) is rarely met in practice.
- Provide empirical wall-clock and memory profiling to substantiate scalability claims, particularly as dataset size and dimensionality increase beyond the ~40D ceiling currently tested.

## Novel Insights
The paper correctly identifies that ethical testing of autonomous systems cannot be reduced to a single scalar loss; it requires explicitly modeling the dependency between measurable system behaviors and stakeholder value judgments. While the hierarchical VGP decomposition and LLM proxy integration are practically useful for sample-constrained simulation, the approach currently operates as an engineering adaptation of composite Bayesian optimization rather than a theoretically grounded advancement. The true insight lies in framing ethical testing as a joint uncertainty-reduction and preference-alignment task, but the execution is currently bottlenecked by ad-hoc scalarization, unverified proxy alignment, and ambiguous acquisition mechanics.

## Potentially Missed Related Work
- **Composite Preference Bayesian Optimization** (Lin et al., 2022; Astudillo & Frazier, 2019) — directly relevant for understanding established theoretical treatments of objective-to-preference mappings and EUBO variants.
- **Multi-Objective Bayesian Optimization** (ParEGO, EHVI, q-ParEGO) — standard baselines for optimizing trade-off surfaces without handcrafted utility functions; comparing against these would contextualize SEED-SET's empirical gains.
- **Active Learning for Preference Elicitation** (Chu & Ghahramani, 2005; Houlsby et al., 2011 (PES)) — provides established information-theoretic acquisition strategies that could inform a more rigorous derivation of Eq. (2).

## Suggestions
1. Formally define the pair/batch selection mechanism derived from $V(x)$. If candidates are optimized independently, provide a rigorous batching approximation analysis or reformulate $V$ as a joint criterion $V(x_1, x_2)$ that accounts for pairwise preference uncertainties.
2. Provide explicit computational details for maximizing Eq. (2): specify the Monte Carlo sample size for the inner expectation, the gradient estimator used during optimization, and how hyperparameters/inducing points ($M$) are selected or learned. Include a brief sensitivity analysis showing how term magnitudes in Eq. (2) behave across iterations to rule out trivial term dominance.
3. Replace or supplement the handcrafted preference score evaluation. Demonstrate convergence toward analytically computed Pareto fronts of the objective metrics, or run a pilot study with real human evaluators to quantify LLM-human preference divergence and calibrate the proxy's reliability.
4. Calibrate claims in the title and contributions to match the empirical evidence. Temper "Scalable" to reflect the ~40D limit and add a brief discussion on kernel/inducing-point behavior in higher dimensions. Reframe "Evolving Experimental Design" or add a dynamic preference-shift experiment, as all current evaluations use static criteria per run.
