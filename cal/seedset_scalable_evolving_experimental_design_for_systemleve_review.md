# Harsh Critic Review
## Section-by-Section Critical Review

### Title & Abstract
- **Does the title accurately reflect the contribution?** The title accurately captures the core methodology (experimental design/Bayesian optimization) and application domain (system-level ethical testing). However, "Evolving" is somewhat misleading given Assumption A2 explicitly assumes stationary preferences, and the experiments do not demonstrate temporal adaptation of criteria.
- **Does the abstract clearly state the problem, method, and key results?** The problem formulation (sample-efficient ethical benchmarking combining objective/subjective metrics) and method (hierarchical GPs, novel acquisition strategy) are clear. The key results claim "up to 2× optimal test candidates" and "1.25× improvement in coverage" lack necessary context (e.g., specific tasks, baseline definitions, or statistical significance), making them difficult to interpret at a glance.
- **Are any claims in the abstract unsupported by the paper?** The claim of "up to 2× optimal test candidates" is partially supported by Figure 3 and 5 preference scores, but the abstract does not clarify that "optimal" is measured against a handcrafted linear proxy that closely mirrors the LLM prompt. This circularity is not acknowledged in the abstract, potentially overinflating the perceived novelty of discovering *truly* ethically complex scenarios.

### Introduction & Motivation
- **Is the problem well-motivated? Is the gap in prior work clearly identified?** Yes. The intersection of ethical evaluation, sample efficiency, and the need to unify measurable objectives with subjective stakeholder values is well-motivated. The gap (existing methods are either sample-hungry, ignore objective structure, or assume abundant expert feedback) is clearly articulated and aligns with real-world deployment constraints.
- **Are the contributions clearly stated and accurate?** The three contributions (unified formulation, hierarchical VGP, joint acquisition criterion) are clearly listed. The claim to be the "first framework of its kind to explicitly consider both objective and subjective ethical evaluation criteria" is slightly over-stated. Preference-aware Bayesian Optimization (e.g., BOPE, Lin et al. 2022, cited extensively) already combines objective exploration with preference exploitation. The distinction here is the specific hierarchical VGP formulation and joint mutual information acquisition, which should be more precisely scoped.
- **Does the introduction over-claim or under-sell?** Slightly over-claims novelty relative to composite BO/PE literature. It under-sells the technical contribution by not clarifying early on that the framework is fundamentally a Bayesian optimization/experimental design loop adapted for hierarchical preference learning, rather than a standalone ethical evaluation metric.

### Method / Approach
- **Is the method clearly described and reproducible?** The hierarchical decomposition into Objective GP and Subjective GP is conceptually clear. However, the practical implementation of Equation (2) lacks algorithmic detail. Maximizing $V(x)$ over *pairs* of candidates $[x_1, x_2]$ in a high-dimensional space is non-trivial. The paper does not specify whether joint optimization, greedy pairing, or batch acquisition strategies are used, nor does it provide pseudocode for the experimental design loop. Reproducibility suffers without these details.
- **Are key assumptions stated and justified?** Yes. A1 (fixed policy/space) and A3 (known objectives) are standard in BO/PE and well-justified. A2 (stationary latent preferences) is explicitly stated but creates a conceptual tension with the paper's emphasis on "evolving" standards and stakeholder-specific subjectivity.
- **Are there logical gaps in the derivation or reasoning?** Equation (2) combines three terms: objective MI, subjective MI, and expected preference. The relative scaling/weighting of these terms is not discussed. Mutual information in GPs naturally scales with log-determinants of covariance matrices, while the expected preference term scales linearly. Without adaptive weighting or normalization, one term can dominate, effectively reducing the method to either pure exploration or pure exploitation depending on the dimensionality of $x$ vs $z$.
- **Are there edge cases or failure modes not discussed?** The method relies on fitting a VGP with a probit likelihood for the subjective layer. If stakeholder preferences are inconsistent or highly non-stationary, the variational approximation may collapse or produce uncalibrated uncertainty, breaking the acquisition strategy. This failure mode under label noise (beyond simple stochasticity) is not addressed.

### Experiments & Results
- **Do the experiments actually test the paper's claims?** The experiments test sample efficiency and preference maximization in simulated environments. They validate the acquisition strategy's ability to find high-proxy-score regions efficiently. However, they do not test *ethical alignment* in a broader sense, as the "ground truth" is a handcrafted linear function designed to match the prompt exactly.
- **Are baselines appropriate and fairly compared?** Single GP, VS-AL-1/2, and BOPE variants are reasonable baselines for preference learning and composite BO. The BOPE ablations (Appendix I) are particularly strong and fairly demonstrate the advantage of joint acquisition over decoupled phases. Tuning and budget allocation across baselines appear consistent.
- **Are there missing ablations that would materially change conclusions?** Yes. (1) *Acquisition weights*: How does performance change if the exploration vs exploitation terms in Eq (2) are manually weighted? (2) *Compute overhead*: The paper claims sample efficiency but ignores wall-clock time or fitting overhead for VGPs. (3) *LLM vs Human*: Without a human-in-the-loop baseline, the claim of "ethical testing" remains proxy-bound.
- **Are error bars / statistical significance reported?** Results show mean ± 1 std over 5 seeds (Figures 3, 5, 6). No formal statistical significance tests (e.g., paired t-tests or bootstrap confidence intervals) are provided, though the trends appear consistent across seeds.
- **Do the results support the claims made, or are they cherry-picked?** Results consistently support the claim that HVGP outperforms direct mapping (Single GP/VS-AL) in higher dimensions. This is a strong empirical contribution. However, claiming the method identifies "optimal test candidates" is only supported within the confines of the synthetic linear proxy, not necessarily for discovering ethically challenging edge cases that violate the linear assumption.
- **Are datasets and evaluation metrics appropriate?** Datasets are custom simulators (IEEE bus systems, Webots fire model), which the authors transparently acknowledge due to the lack of standard ethical benchmarking suites. The "preference score" metric is appropriate for optimizing BO loops but inadequate for validating true ethical reasoning. The coverage metric (cumulative standard deviation) is unconventional and lacks formal justification as a proxy for scenario diversity.

### Writing & Clarity
- **Are there sections that are confusing or poorly explained?** Section 4.2 introduces Equation (2) but does not explain how the nested expectations and mutual information terms are efficiently computed or optimized. The transition from theoretical EIG (Eq 1) to the proposed acquisition (Eq 2) feels abrupt. Readers are left to infer that this is a heuristic approximation of information gain tailored to the hierarchical structure, which should be explicitly stated and justified.
- **Are figures and tables clear and informative?** Figures 3, 5, and 6 clearly show convergence trends. Figure 4 (stakeholder contours) is conceptually strong but lacks axis labels and colorbar scales, making it difficult to quantitatively assess the separation of Pareto fronts. Table 1 (Appendix H) compares real vs. LLM-generated distributions but lacks statistical distance metrics (e.g., Wasserstein or MMD), relying solely on visual/mean comparison.

### Limitations & Broader Impact
- **Do the authors acknowledge the key limitations?** Yes. They acknowledge scalability limits of VGPs ($O(NM^2)$), kernel stationarity constraints, the requirement for known objectives a priori, and LLM prompt sensitivity.
- **Are there fundamental limitations they missed?** (1) The linear proxy validation fundamentally limits claims about discovering complex ethical trade-offs; if the true ethical landscape is non-convex or involves lexicographic thresholds not captured by linear weights, the acquisition strategy may fail. (2) The framework assumes preferences are stationary during testing, which contradicts real-world ethical deliberation where stakeholder values often shift as they observe system failures. (3) Using LLMs as ethical proxies without auditing for societal bias risks baking the LLM's pretraining biases into the "subjective" evaluation layer, which the ethics statement only briefly addresses.
- **Are there failure modes or negative societal impacts not discussed?** The paper does not discuss the risk of "ethics washing": system developers could use SEED-SET with narrowly scoped prompts to claim automated ethical validation while ignoring harm dimensions not included in the objective vector $y$. The framework optimizes for prompt alignment, not comprehensive harm mitigation.

### Overall Assessment
This paper presents a conceptually relevant and technically grounded approach to sample-efficient ethical evaluation by framing it as a hierarchical Bayesian experimental design problem. The proposal of a joint acquisition criterion for Variational Gaussian Processes is a meaningful contribution to the preference-aware optimization literature, and the experiments convincingly demonstrate that decomposing objective and subjective layers improves sample efficiency over direct mapping in high-dimensional settings. However, the empirical validation is constrained by reliance on handcrafted linear preference proxies that mirror the evaluation prompt, which limits the strength of claims regarding discovery of complex or novel ethical trade-offs. Additionally, the lack of algorithmic details for optimizing the pairwise acquisition function, unaddressed scaling between exploration/exploitation terms, and the tension between the "evolving" motivation and stationary preference assumptions temper the technical maturity. For ICLR, the work has merit but requires clarification on acquisition optimization mechanics, a more rigorous discussion of proxy validation limitations, and ideally, statistical tests or human-in-the-loop comparisons to solidify its claims of ethical alignment testing.

# Neutral Reviewer
## Balanced Review

### Summary
This paper introduces SEED-SET, a Bayesian experimental design framework that efficiently evaluates the ethical alignment of autonomous systems by jointly modeling objective performance metrics and subjective stakeholder preferences using a hierarchical Variational Gaussian Process (VGP). The method proposes a novel nested acquisition strategy that combines mutual information gain from both objective and subjective layers with expected preference utility, leveraging LLMs as scalable pairwise oracles. Experiments across power grid allocation, drone-based fire rescue, and routing tasks demonstrate that the framework consistently outperforms standard active learning and preference-based baselines in sample efficiency and coverage.

### Strengths
1. **Well-Motivated Hierarchical Decomposition:** Modeling ethical evaluation as a two-stage process ($x \rightarrow y \rightarrow z$) is highly principled. It grounds subjective utility in observable system behaviors rather than opaque design parameters, improving both interpretability and data efficiency (Section 4.1, Figures 1 & 4).
2. **Novel Joint Acquisition Strategy:** The proposed acquisition function $V(x)$ explicitly unifies exploration of objective uncertainty, exploration of subjective preference boundaries, and exploitation of high-utility regions (Eq. 2). Ablation studies (Section 5.2, Figure 5 right) rigorously demonstrate that this joint formulation outperforms isolated exploration-only or exploitation-only variants.
3. **Comprehensive and Rigorous Evaluation:** The paper evaluates across multiple realistic domains with appropriate baselines (Single GP, VS-AL variants, BOPE ablations) and includes extensive robustness checks for LLM evaluator perturbations (temperature, model, prompt in Appendix F), multi-stakeholder adaptation (Appendix G), and real-world data alignment (Appendix H).
4. **Strong Reproducibility Infrastructure:** The manuscript clearly specifies the computational stack (BoTorch, GPyTorch, pandapower, Webots), hardware, and provides complete LLM prompt templates. This transparency, combined with the availability of implementation details in appendices, strongly supports replication.

### Weaknesses
1. **Unscaled Summation in Acquisition Function:** Equation 2 sums mutual information (bits/nats) and expected utility (arbitrary preference score) without explicit normalization or weighting coefficients. Since these terms operate on different scales and sensitivities to kernel hyperparameters, the unweighted sum may yield unpredictable exploration-exploitation trade-offs.
2. **Reliance on Heuristic Linear Proxies for Ground Truth:** The primary evaluation metric is a handcrafted linear preference score (e.g., $\tilde{h}(y) = [0, -0.5, 1, 0]y$), used as a proxy for the LLM's true preferences. While TrueSkill rankings are provided, the absence of comparison against actual human expert judgments or a theoretically grounded utility function limits the strength of claims regarding "ethical alignment."
3. **Limited Empirical Demonstration of Scalability:** The paper claims scalability to high-dimensional spaces but only tests up to 40 dimensions (30-Bus scenario). Modern Bayesian optimization and experimental design literature routinely address 100+ dimensions. The VGP's $O(nm^2)$ complexity claim and the computational cost of pairwise candidate optimization lack empirical validation at larger scales.
4. **Restrictive Assumption on Objective Completeness:** Assumption A3 requires the full set of objective metrics to be known a priori. In real-world safety and ethics testing, relevant objectives are often contested, emergent, or discovered iteratively, which restricts the framework's applicability to fully exploratory or open-ended evaluation settings.

### Novelty & Significance
**Novelty** is moderate-high within the AI safety and preference-based optimization literature. While hierarchical VGP and composite Bayesian optimization are established, explicitly unifying them into a single nested BED acquisition function tailored for ethical benchmarking, coupled with LLM-based pairwise elicitation, represents a clear methodological advance. **Clarity** is generally strong; the problem formulation is precise, the pipeline is well-illustrated, and mathematical notation is consistent, though the derivation of Eq. 2 could benefit from a more explicit connection to standard expected information gain theory. **Reproducibility** is high, with detailed environment specifications, library choices, prompt designs, and ablation configurations provided. **Significance** is moderate-high for the ICLR community, as it addresses a critical bottleneck in empirical AI alignment: sample-efficient testing under subjective-objective dual constraints. The framework is highly applicable to robotics safety, policy testing, and automated LLM evaluation pipelines.

### Suggestions for Improvement
1. **Explicitly Address Scale Balancing in Acquisition:** Introduce a weighting or normalization mechanism for the MI and utility terms in Eq. 2 (e.g., adaptive temperature scaling, term-wise variance normalization, or multi-objective scalarization). Provide a sensitivity analysis showing how different balances affect convergence behavior.
2. **Strengthen Ground-Truth Validation:** Supplement the linear preference proxy with a more rigorous baseline, such as aggregated human expert rankings, a nonlinear synthetic ground truth with known curvature, or explicit calibration metrics for the LLM oracle's consistency against human judgments. Clarify the limitations of using LLM preferences as ethical ground truth.
3. **Demonstrate Higher-Dimensional Scalability:** Include experiments on standard high-dimensional synthetic benchmarks (e.g., 100+ dim Ackley or Branin variants adapted for preference learning) or explicitly integrate and evaluate dimensionality reduction techniques (e.g., random embeddings, additive kernels, or sparse inducing point scaling) to substantiate the "scalable" claim.
4. **Relax Objective Completeness Assumption:** Discuss how Assumption A3 could be practically relaxed in future work. For instance, propose integrating unsupervised objective discovery (e.g., PCA/VAEs on system traces) or multi-fidelity BO to handle missing or contested objectives, broadening the framework's applicability to exploratory ethical testing.

# Spark Finder Review
## How to Improve This Paper

### Missing Experiments (top 3-5 only)
1. **Validate with non-linear, human-derived preference functions instead of handcrafted linear scores.** Using a linear proxy ground truth (`h_tilde`) circularly validates a non-linear preference learning method and invalidates the core claim that SEED-SET captures real ethical complexity.
2. **Benchmark against modern Preferential Bayesian Optimization (PBO) and active RLHF methods.** The current baselines (VS-AL, Random, decoupled BOPE variants) are outdated or poorly matched to continuous preference optimization, making the "state-of-the-art" performance claim unconvincing.
3. **Quantify LLM-to-human expert alignment in each domain.** Without measuring correlation between LLM proxy judgments and actual domain experts, the claim that LLMs reliably substitute human evaluators in high-stakes ethical testing remains unsupported.
4. **Test robustness to preference noise and contradictory feedback.** Real stakeholder input is inconsistent and often violates assumption A2; evaluating performance under injected preference flips or multi-modal latent utilities is required to prove practical reliability.

### Deeper Analysis Needed (top 3-5 only)
1. **Provide a principled derivation or normalization for the composite acquisition function (Eq 2).** Summing mutual information terms and expected utility lacks scale invariance and implicit weighting; without theoretical justification or empirical sensitivity analysis, the exploration-exploitation balance is arbitrary.
2. **Quantify error and uncertainty propagation between the Objective GP and Subjective GP.** Hierarchical surrogate models amplify estimation variance; analyzing how Objective GP predictive uncertainty degrades preference ranking accuracy is necessary to trust the hierarchical design.
3. **Analyze behavior under strictly Pareto-conflicting objectives.** Ethical testing inherently involves trade-offs where no single outcome dominates; demonstrating how the acquisition function navigates flat preference plateaus or competing stakeholder criteria is missing.

### Visualizations & Case Studies
1. **Plot sampled scenarios on Pareto fronts across key objectives (e.g., Cost vs. Priority).** This reveals whether SEED-SET actually discovers meaningful ethical trade-off boundaries or simply optimizes along the dominant axis of the linear proxy.
2. **Visualize the magnitude of each acquisition term (MI1, MI2, Pref) over iterations.** Showing the dynamic contribution of each component proves the claimed "balanced exploration-exploitation" isn't dominated by a single heuristic term.
3. **Provide qualitative side-by-side comparisons of "ethical edge cases" found by SEED-SET vs. baselines.** Quantitative scores hide whether discovered scenarios represent genuinely challenging moral dilemmas or trivial objective extremes.

### Obvious Next Steps
1. **Replace synthetic preference scores with real human or high-fidelity crowd-evaluated feedback.** The paper's ethical testing premise cannot scale beyond simulation metrics without grounding in actual human preference data.
2. **Report wall-clock training time, GP fitting overhead, and memory usage vs. iteration count and dimensionality.** Claiming "scalable" requires empirical compute benchmarks, not just O(NM^2) asymptotic statements.
3. **Relax Assumption A1 by testing policy drift or adaptive system responses.** Real autonomous systems update during deployment; validating SEED-SET under shifting objective landscapes is essential before claiming real-world applicability.

# Final Consolidated Review
## Summary
The paper introduces SEED-SET, a Bayesian experimental design framework that efficiently evaluates autonomous systems by jointly modeling objective performance metrics and subjective stakeholder preferences via a hierarchical Variational Gaussian Process. It proposes a novel nested acquisition strategy that balances mutual information gain across both objective and subjective layers with expected preference utility, using LLMs as cost-effective pairwise oracles. Empirical results across power grid, fire rescue, and routing simulations demonstrate consistent gains in sample efficiency and coverage over standard active learning and preference-based baselines.

## Strengths
- **Principled Hierarchical Decomposition:** By explicitly modeling the pipeline $x \rightarrow y \rightarrow z$ (design $\rightarrow$ observable metrics $\rightarrow$ stakeholder preference), the framework grounds subjective ethical utility in interpretable system behaviors rather than opaque parameters. This separation naturally promotes data efficiency, as the subjective layer learns task-agnostic preferences that transfer across varying objective landscapes.
- **Joint Acquisition Criterion with Clear Empirical Utility:** Equation (2) unifies objective uncertainty reduction, subjective boundary exploration, and preference exploitation in a single BED loop. The ablation studies (Figure 5 right) convincingly isolate the contribution of each term, demonstrating that jointly optimizing all three components consistently outperforms decoupled exploration or exploitation strategies in higher-dimensional settings.
- **Rigorous, Well-Documented Experimental Pipeline:** The evaluation spans multiple realistic domains with appropriately scoped baselines, extensive LLM robustness checks (temperature, model, prompt variations in Appendix F), and transparent reproducibility infrastructure (full prompt templates, library stack, and environment specifications provided).

## Weaknesses
- **Unbalanced Scaling in the Acquisition Function:** Equation (2) sums mutual information terms (which scale logarithmically with determinant of covariance matrices and are sensitive to kernel bandwidths) with an expected utility term (which scales linearly with the probit likelihood) without any normalization or weighting coefficients. This matters because, depending on the domain scaling and GP hyperparameters, one term can easily dominate the acquisition landscape, effectively reducing the method to pure exploration or greedy exploitation and undermining the claimed balanced trade-off.
- **Validation Constrained to Circular Linear Proxies:** The primary evaluation metric relies on handcrafted linear preference functions (e.g., $\tilde{h}(y) = [0, -0.5, 1, 0]y$) that are explicitly constructed to mirror the LLM's prompt instructions. While TrueSkill rankings provide a secondary check, testing against a proxy that perfectly aligns with the optimization objective only validates instruction-following and basic convergence, not the capacity to discover non-linear, contested, or genuinely complex ethical trade-offs.
- **Unanalyzed Uncertainty Propagation Between Hierarchical Layers:** The Subjective GP receives predictive distributions from the Objective GP as inputs. The paper does not quantify how objective-layer predictive variance propagates through the probit likelihood or affects preference ranking reliability. In sample-constrained ethical testing, overconfident objective predictions in sparsely explored regions could lead the acquisition strategy to confidently query scenarios with fundamentally misaligned subjective utilities.

## Nice-to-Haves
- Reporting wall-clock training time and VGP fitting overhead per iteration would help contextualize the "scalable" claim beyond asymptotic $O(NM^2)$ complexity.
- A brief discussion on how Assumption A3 (complete knowledge of objective metrics a priori) might be relaxed in practice (e.g., via automated metric discovery or multi-fidelity proxies) would broaden the framework's applicability to fully exploratory safety auditing.
- Expanding the Ethics Statement to address the risk of "ethics washing" when developers use SEED-SET with narrowly scoped prompt/objective sets would strengthen the broader impact section.

## Novel Insights
The paper successfully reframes ethical evaluation from a post-hoc auditing problem to a proactive, sample-efficient experimental design task. Most alignment methodologies focus on training-time preference optimization (RLHF/DPO) or post-deployment metric reporting; SEED-SET instead asks how to *strategically query* a black-box policy to maximally reveal its value alignment under strict budget constraints. The hierarchical VGP structure is particularly insightful because it formalizes a key practical reality: stakeholders evaluate AI systems through observable outcomes, not latent policy parameters. By modeling the subjective layer over the objective manifold $y$, the acquisition function naturally navigates regions where measurable trade-offs align with stakeholder priorities, making the test generation process both interpretable and inherently adaptive to different value profiles without requiring objective-space re-parameterization.

## Suggestions
- Introduce a normalization or adaptive weighting mechanism for Equation (2) (e.g., variance-based scaling or a temperature parameter) and provide a sensitivity analysis showing stable convergence behavior across different term balances.
- Plot the relative magnitude of each acquisition component (MI_obj, MI_subj, E[Utility]) over training iterations to empirically verify that no single term dominates the search dynamics in practice.
- Include at least one evaluation against a non-linear synthetic ground truth or a small-scale human-in-the-loop pilot to demonstrate that SEED-SET can capture complex preference boundaries beyond linear prompt-matching.
- Add a brief paragraph specifying the numerical optimization routine used to maximize $V(x)$ over candidate pairs $[x_1, x_2]$ (e.g., gradient-based BO, greedy pairwise selection, or batch approximations) to ensure full methodological reproducibility.
