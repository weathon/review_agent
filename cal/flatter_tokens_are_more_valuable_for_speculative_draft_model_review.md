# Harsh Critic Review
## Section-by-Section Critical Review

### Title & Abstract
The title accurately reflects the core contribution, and the abstract clearly states the problem, insight, method (SFDD), and quantitative claims (2× training speedup at 50% data, ≤4% inference degradation). The claims are directly supported by Table 1 and Figure 4. The abstract effectively frames the paper as a data-centric efficiency contribution, which aligns well with current ICLR interests in efficient LLM serving and training. No major concerns here.

### Introduction & Motivation
The introduction successfully motivates the discrepancy between standard KD objectives (KL-divergence) and SD's actual objective (acceptance rate / $L_1$-norm). The pivot from "modifying the loss function" to "curating the training data" is logical and timely. 
**Concern:** The transition assumes that suboptimal $L_1$-loss performance (citing Zhou et al., 2023) directly implies data heterogeneity is the root cause. However, this causal link isn't rigorously established. It's possible that optimization dynamics, learning rate scheduling, or gradient scaling under $L_1$ loss cause the underperformance, not necessarily data value. A brief ablation or discussion isolating *why* direct $L_1$ training fails would strengthen the motivation for a data-centric solution over an objective-centric one.

### Preliminaries & Theoretical Analysis (Section 3)
The preliminary definitions (Eq. 1-2) are standard and correct. The constrained optimization formulation (Eq. 3) provides a clean toy model for a single KD step.
**Major Concern:** The theoretical derivation heavily relies on assuming categorical token distributions can be modeled as 1D Gaussians to analyze variance's effect on $\Delta L_1$. While analytically tractable, this abstraction is fundamentally mismatched with LLM outputs, which are high-dimensional categorical distributions over token IDs that lack an inherent metric ordering. You acknowledge in Appendix F.4 that computing variance over arbitrary token indices is meaningless, yet the entire Gaussian derivation (Appendix A) is built on variance as the key property. This creates a conceptual disconnect: the math proves variance matters for Gaussians, but the actual metric used is `flatness` (cosine similarity to uniform), which is mathematically equivalent to the inverse $L_2$ norm of the probability vector (or related to Rényi entropy of order 2). The Gaussian derivation, while elegant, does not rigorously generalize to discrete categorical distributions and feels like a post-hoc justification for a simpler concentration measure. A more direct theoretical analysis of how the gradient magnitude or $L_1$-reduction potential of KL-distillation scales with the concentration (e.g., collision probability or $L_2$ norm) of the categorical teacher distribution would be both more rigorous and more tightly coupled to Equation 6.

### Proposed Approach & Empirical Validation (Section 4)
The definition of `flatness` (Eq. 6) is computationally efficient and intuitively captures distribution dispersion. The sample-level aggregation (Eq. 8) is straightforward.
**Concern 1 (Validation Scale):** Figure 2 claims to empirically validate the theoretical insight, yet the text states it uses only *10 randomly selected samples*. For a core empirical claim that drives the entire paper, this sample size is insufficient to establish robust training dynamics across diverse text domains. The 10-point moving average and "Minimal/Greater change" annotations visually suggest strong separation, but the underlying scatter shows high overlap. 
**Concern 2 (Metric Comparison):** Figure 2d introduces a gap metric $g$ to show flatness outperforms entropy. While $g > 0$ consistently holds, the absolute magnitude of the improvement is not contextualized relative to training variance. The downstream gains in Table 1 over the next best baseline (Top-1 Probability) are modest (~0.18× in average speedup). Without reporting variance across multiple seeds, it's difficult to assess whether this improvement is robust or falls within standard training noise.

### Experiments & Results (Section 5 & Appendices)
The experimental setup follows the EAGLE-2 pipeline and covers multiple downstream tasks and retention ratios. Tables 1-3 and Figure 4 demonstrate consistent, albeit moderate, improvements.
**Concern 1 (Baselines):** The baselines (Entropy, Top-1, Margin, Energy, PPL) are reasonable but reflect older, static heuristics. For an ICLR submission, it's expected to compare against more modern, adaptive data selection methods used in LLM training/distillation, such as gradient-norm-based filtering, influence function approximations, or loss-based curriculum learning. Comparing solely to static distributional metrics may understate the competitiveness of SFDD.
**Concern 2 (Statistical Reporting):** All main tables report single-run point estimates. Differences in speedup (e.g., 2.41× vs 2.23×) are clinically meaningful but statistically unverified. ICLR standards typically require results averaged over ≥3 seeds with confidence intervals or standard deviations, especially when claiming a metric is "more effective." Appendix F.8 attempts to address a minor performance fluctuation but only runs 2 seeds on 2 datasets. This needs to be scaled across all main results.
**Concern 3 (Overhead Quantification):** Appendix D reports a 3.85% overhead for the scoring pass, which is acceptable. However, this is measured on the ShareGPT dataset. For real-world deployment, draft models are often trained on billion-token corpora. Explicitly stating the expected scaling of the offline pass (e.g., GPU-hours for 100B vs 10B tokens) would help practitioners assess practical feasibility.

### Writing & Clarity
The paper is generally well-structured and readable. The progression from theory to empirical validation to method to experiments is logical. The heavy mathematical derivations in Appendices A and B, while thorough, are partially undermined by the categorical disconnect noted above. Streamlining these to focus directly on the properties of categorical distributions would improve clarity and perceived rigor. Figures 1 and 2 are conceptually clear, though the parsing artifacts make exact interpretation difficult (I am ignoring these per instructions).

### Limitations & Broader Impact
**Concern 1 (Domain Shift Risk):** The method filters out low-flatness (sharply peaked) tokens. While efficient, many critical LLM applications (code generation, mathematical reasoning, factual QA) naturally produce highly peaked target distributions. Aggressively removing these tokens could degrade the draft model's ability to handle precise, low-entropy sequences, potentially causing acceptance rate drops on out-of-distribution or highly structured tasks. This failure mode should be explicitly acknowledged and ideally tested.
**Concern 2 (Impact of Data Selection Bias):** The broader impact section focuses solely on computational savings. Filtering training data based on distributional properties can inadvertently introduce domain or stylistic biases (e.g., if creative writing yields flatter distributions than technical documentation). A brief discussion on how SFDD might alter the draft model's representational landscape beyond just acceptance rate is warranted.

---

### Overall Assessment
This paper presents a well-motiated, practically useful idea: not all tokens contribute equally to speculative decoding draft training, and filtering out "sharp" tokens using a simple flatness metric can accelerate training with minimal inference degradation. The empirical results across retention ratios and tasks are consistent and demonstrate genuine efficiency gains. However, for ICLR acceptance, the paper needs to strengthen three key areas: (1) bridge the theoretical gap by deriving the concentration-insight directly for categorical distributions rather than relying on a 1D Gaussian proxy that doesn't map cleanly to token IDs; (2) bolster empirical rigor by adding multi-seed statistical testing with confidence intervals and comparing against more modern, adaptive data selection baselines; and (3) explicitly discuss and test the potential risks of filtering out low-flatness tokens on structured/precision-heavy domains. The core contribution is sound and valuable, and with these revisions to theoretical framing and experimental validation, it would meet ICLR's standards for publication.

# Neutral Reviewer
## Balanced Review

### Summary
The paper investigates data selection for training draft models in Speculative Decoding (SD). Through a theoretical analysis of a KL-constrained knowledge distillation step, the authors demonstrate that target tokens with flatter (more uniform) predictive distributions yield larger per-step reductions in the $L_1$-norm discrepancy, which directly correlates with SD acceptance rates. They propose a practical "flatness" metric (cosine similarity to a uniform distribution) and introduce SFDD, a sample-level filtering pipeline that retains only high-flatness training data. Experiments within the EAGLE-2 framework demonstrate that using 50% of the data selected via SFDD achieves over $2\times$ training speedup while maintaining inference speedup within 4% of the full-dataset baseline, outperforming several standard data-selection heuristics.

### Strengths
1. **Principled Theoretical Motivation for a Practical Bottleneck:** The paper correctly identifies a subtle mismatch between standard KD objectives and SD's acceptance rate objective ($L_1$-norm). The toy model analyzing $\Delta L_1$ under a KL-budget constraint provides a clear, intuitive foundation for why certain tokens offer more training headroom, effectively reframing the problem from loss engineering to data curation.
2. **Rigorous Empirical Validation & Clear Practical Impact:** The experimental design is comprehensive. Evaluations across multiple retention ratios (including extreme low-data regimes), multiple downstream tasks, and temperature variations consistently demonstrate SFDD's robustness. A $2.02\times$ training speedup with minimal inference degradation addresses a highly relevant pain point, especially as modern SD pipelines adopt multi-epoch SFT and RL-style training.
3. **Strong Reproducibility & Transparent Reporting:** The paper bridges continuous theory and discrete LLM outputs with asymptotic derivations (Appendix B) and thorough empirical tracking (gradient norms, loss curves, epoch-over-epoch $L_1$ reduction). Code is open-sourced, hyperparameters are explicitly listed, and timing protocols fairly include data-selection overhead, which aligns well with ICLR's reproducibility standards.

### Weaknesses
1. **Theoretical Leap from Continuous Variance to Discrete Cosine Similarity:** The core theoretical insight is derived from 1D Gaussian distributions, where "flatness" maps cleanly to variance. The practical metric, however, is the cosine similarity between a high-dimensional categorical distribution and a uniform vector. While the authors provide an asymptotic justification, the high-dimensional behavior and exact mathematical relationship between discrete variance, entropy, and cosine similarity are not fully disentangled. The empirical comparison shows flatness slightly outperforms entropy, but the underlying reason for this marginal gain lacks a rigorous theoretical or geometric explanation.
2. **Limited Validation Across Draft Architectures:** All primary experiments are conducted exclusively with the EAGLE-2 framework. The authors claim the method is orthogonal to other train-based SD approaches, but they do not validate this on alternative draft paradigms (e.g., Medusa's multi-head predictors, EAGLE-3's dynamic draft trees, or tree-based verification methods). The effectiveness of flatness-based filtering may vary depending on how different draft architectures consume or align with target logits.
3. **Lack of Analysis on Dataset Bias & Domain Shift:** Retaining only tokens with flatter target distributions may inadvertently skew the training corpus toward domains or linguistic patterns where the base model is inherently more uncertain. The paper addresses why filtering out low-flatness (peaked) tokens isn't harmful (due to early saturation), but it does not analyze whether SFDD degrades performance on highly deterministic or structured tasks (e.g., code completion, exact math) where sharply peaked target distributions are essential for precision.

### Novelty & Significance
The novelty is moderate but well-grounded and timely. While data selection and uncertainty metrics are established in ML, systematically applying a distribution-flatness criterion to SD draft model training is a fresh perspective that fills a gap in the literature. Prior SD training works largely focus on architectural tweaks or loss-function modifications; this paper offers a complementary, data-centric solution. The significance is high: as speculative decoding matures into more complex, resource-intensive training pipelines (e.g., RL alignment, multi-stage SFT), reducing training compute by half with negligible inference trade-offs provides immediate, practical value to the community. The work meets ICLR's bar for a clear, empirically validated contribution with actionable insights for efficient LLM inference.

### Suggestions for Improvement
1. **Deepen the Metric Comparison Analysis:** Provide a more detailed analysis of *why* cosine similarity to uniform consistently edges out entropy or perplexity. Is it more sensitive to the probability tail? Is it less affected by vocabulary size scaling? A controlled ablation investigating sensitivity to these factors would strengthen the choice of the `flatness` proxy.
2. **Expand Architectural Generalization:** To robustly support the claim of method orthogonality, evaluate SFDD on at least one alternative training-based SD framework (e.g., Medusa or a standard EAGLE-1 setup). This would confirm that the insight generalizes beyond EAGLE-2's specific token-predictor architecture.
3. **Analyze Training Data Composition & Out-of-Distribution Performance:** Report statistics on the linguistic/task diversity of retained vs. filtered samples to address potential bias concerns. Additionally, test SFDD on a highly structured or deterministic domain (e.g., code generation or formal reasoning) to ensure the method does not inadvertently remove critical low-entropy examples necessary for precise outputs.
4. **Clarify Scalability of the Offline Selection Step:** While the offline target pass overhead is negligible (~3.8%) for ShareGPT, clarify how SFDD scales to corpora with hundreds of millions of tokens. Briefly discussing distributed computation strategies or pre-computation pipelines would preempt scalability concerns and strengthen the practical deployment argument.

2. Compare static pre-filtering against online/dynamic data selection or curriculum learning, since the flatness value of a token is static but the draft's distribution evolves throughout training, making static filtering a potentially suboptimal approximation of true token value.

# Final Consolidated Review
## Summary
This paper addresses the computational inefficiency of training draft models for speculative decoding (SD) by introducing a data-centric filtering approach. Through a KL-constrained knowledge distillation toy model, the authors demonstrate that tokens inducing flatter (more uniform) target distributions yield larger per-step reductions in the $L_1$-norm, which directly governs SD acceptance rates. They propose a practical "flatness" metric (cosine similarity to uniform) and the SFDD pipeline to distill training data, empirically showing over $2\times$ training speedup at 50% retention while keeping inference speedup within 4% of the full-dataset baseline across multiple downstream tasks.

## Strengths
- **Clear, principled motivation for a practical bottleneck:** The paper correctly identifies that standard KD training wastes compute on tokens that quickly saturate, and successfully pivots the field's focus from loss-function engineering to data curation. The toy modeling $\Delta L_1$ under a budget constraint provides an intuitive, mathematically grounded reason why distributional flatness correlates with training headroom.
- **Transparent efficiency accounting and robust scaling behavior:** The reported $2.02\times$ training speedup fairly includes the offline scoring overhead (3.85% of total time), directly addressing a common pitfall in data-selection literature. Performance remains stable across extreme retention ratios (down to 5%) and temperature variations, confirming the metric's reliability in resource-constrained regimes.
- **Strong diagnostic validation of the underlying hypothesis:** The authors don't just present end-to-end results; they empirically track epoch-over-epoch gradient norms, loss curves, and $L_1$ reductions (Appendices F.3, F.5), quantitatively verifying the claimed saturation behavior of low-flatness tokens. This bridges the theoretical insight to actual training dynamics effectively.

## Weaknesses
- **Risk of domain/task bias from discarding low-flatness tokens:** Filtering out sharply peaked distributions systematically removes examples where the target model is highly confident, which often corresponds to structured, deterministic, or precision-critical content (e.g., code syntax, mathematical operators, exact formatting). While this saves compute, it risks biasing the draft model's representational landscape and degrading alignment on out-of-distribution or highly structured prompts. The paper lacks any analysis of the semantic composition of discarded tokens or evaluation on generation quality metrics to quantify this trade-off.
- **Limited architectural generalization beyond EAGLE-2:** All main experiments rely exclusively on the EAGLE-2 dynamic draft tree framework with a LLaMA3-8B-Instruct target. While the authors claim method orthogonality, the effectiveness of flatness-based filtering remains untested on fundamentally different draft paradigms (e.g., Medusa's multi-head predictors, standard separate draft models trained with vanilla KD, or tree-based verifiers). Alignment dynamics and gradient flow in these architectures may interact differently with filtered data, making it unclear whether the efficiency gains generalize universally.
- **Lack of theoretical or geometric justification for the marginal edge over entropy:** The flatness metric is mathematically closely related to distributional entropy and perplexity, and Appendix F.2 shows nearly identical training dynamic trends. While flatness consistently outperforms entropy in Figure 2d and Table 1, the paper does not explain *why* cosine similarity to uniform captures a distinct signal that entropy misses. Given the modest average speedup gap (~0.18×) over the second-best baseline, the marginal win risks appearing as an empirical artifact rather than a fundamentally superior proxy.

## Nice-to-Haves
- Provide a qualitative or statistical breakdown of the linguistic/task domains present in the filtered-out vs. retained samples to transparently address potential dataset bias.
- Evaluate SFDD on at least one non-EAGLE SD architecture (e.g., Medusa or a standard separate draft model) to empirically validate the claim of framework orthogonality.
- Report exact-match or structured quality metrics (e.g., pass@1 on code, exact solutions on math) alongside wall-clock speedup to confirm that data filtering does not introduce silent degradation in precision-critical generation.
- Quantify the direct correlation between the static flatness score and the empirical per-token acceptance rate on held-out generation traces, complementing the $\Delta L_1$ proxy used in the diagnostic analysis.
- Include variance bounds or multiple-run averages for the main benchmark results to contextualize whether the gains over static heuristics consistently exceed standard fine-tuning noise.

## Novel Insights
The paper's core novelty lies in diagnosing a fundamental mismatch in how draft models are currently trained: standard knowledge distillation objectives implicitly assume all tokens contribute equally to learning, yet speculative decoding's true success metric (acceptance rate via $L_1$ reduction) is disproportionately driven by tokens where the target model is uncertain. This reframes SD draft training from an architectural or loss-design challenge into a data-curation problem, challenging the conventional selective-learning heuristic that prioritizes high-confidence samples for stability. Instead, it shows that in the SD regime, high-uncertainty tokens provide the most optimization-efficient trajectory, and that this property can be isolated using a simple, permutation-invariant geometric proxy (cosine similarity to uniform) without requiring draft model warm-up, gradient tracking, or iterative re-scoring.

## Suggestions
- Conduct a targeted evaluation on structured/benchmark domains (e.g., code generation, formal math) with explicit generation quality metrics to empirically rule out the risk that SFDD degrades precision-critical capabilities when low-flatness tokens are filtered.
- Provide a brief theoretical or geometric analysis clarifying why cosine similarity to the uniform vector provides a strictly better selection signal than entropy or perplexity, even if both capture distributional dispersion, to solidify the metric choice beyond marginal empirical gains.
- Release or describe a breakdown of how the flatness distribution correlates with task type (creative vs. technical, long-context vs. short) to help practitioners anticipate and mitigate potential domain-skew when applying SFDD to custom corpora.
