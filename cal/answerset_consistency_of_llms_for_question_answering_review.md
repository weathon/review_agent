# Harsh Critic Review
## Section-by-Section Critical Review

**Abstract & Introduction**
The abstract clearly positions the problem: LLMs generate set-valued answers that violate expected set-theoretic relations. The formulation of "answer-set inconsistency" is well-motivated, and the distinction from standard factual accuracy is explicit. The five RQs are logically sequenced, and the contributions are clearly enumerated. However, the claim in the introduction that "newer or bigger models do not universally outperform older or smaller variants" requires careful qualification. While Table 3 supports non-monotonic scaling, this phenomenon is heavily confounded by differing refusal rates (`%IDK`). The introduction should explicitly flag this trade-off early on so readers interpret subsequent consistency scores with the necessary caution.

**Related Work**
The literature review adequately covers prior work on LLM consistency (boolean, paraphrase, preference orderings) and database query containment. It correctly identifies the gap: no prior work systematically evaluates *enumeration* consistency across set-theoretic relations. One area for strengthening: the paper draws an analogy to database query containment (Sec 2, Abiteboul et al., 1995), but does not sufficiently distinguish the evaluation paradigm. Database containment is schema- and data-invariant, whereas LLM containment relies on parametric knowledge that may be incomplete or evolving. Acknowledging that "consistency" here is inherently a *knowledge-aware* property rather than a purely syntactic/logical one would tighten the conceptual framing.

**Methodology & Dataset (Sections 3.1–3.2)**
- *Definition (Sec 3.1):* The formalization of consistency across equivalence, containment, disjointness, and overlap is clear and mathematically sound. The separation of "answer-set inconsistency" (vs. ground truth) from "answer-set contradiction" (model's prediction vs. its own output) is a valuable conceptual distinction.
- *Dataset Construction (Sec 3.2):* The ASCB dataset is a genuine contribution, but its validation pipeline raises methodological concerns for ICLR standards. The quadruples were generated via LLM assistance and manually curated (Sec 3.2, App B), but there is no report of inter-annotator agreement, nor systematic external verification that the intended set-theoretic relations strictly hold in reality. Natural language enumeration questions often have fuzzy boundaries (e.g., "tributaries" vs. "streams flowing directly," or geographic/political definitions of regions). If the ground-truth relations themselves are debatable, the consistency metrics become noisy. I recommend explicitly detailing how ambiguous cases were resolved, or providing a subset of the dataset with KG-verified ground truth to establish a lower-bound error rate for the benchmark itself.

**Evaluation Metrics & Tasks (Sections 3.3–3.4)**
- *Metrics (Sec 3.4):* The use of binary consistency rates and Jaccard similarity is appropriate. However, the decision to exclude `"idk"` and empty answers from the denominator of `CON(M)` and `SIM(M)` creates a known inflation bias. Table 3 shows GPT-5 has `~32%` IDK under Base and `~47%` under CtE. A model that refuses more questions will mathematically appear more consistent on the remaining subset. While `%IDK` is reported separately, the paper should report a *joint metric* (e.g., consistency weighted by attempt rate) or analyze consistency conditional on non-IDK responses to ensure fairness across strategies.
- *Stochasticity Control:* The inclusion of `E_{1,*}` as a control for generative nondeterminism is clever. However, the interpretation in Sec 4.2 ("The small gap between `E_{1,*}` and `E_{1,2}` suggests that stochasticity plays an important role") is slightly contradictory. If `E_{1,*}` consistency is low (~20-60%), and `E_{1,2}` is similarly low, the gap is indeed small, but this implies *both* are dominated by stochasticity/instability. The causal attribution in Sec 3.4 and App G remains largely indirect; the paper relies on literature rather than controlled interventions (e.g., repeated sampling vs. single-pass) to isolate stochasticity from semantic misunderstanding.

**Experiments & Results (Section 4)**
- *Classification (Sec 4.1, App D):* Results are straightforward and support RQ2/RQ3. `N_{4,1}` being the hardest aligns with the difficulty of reasoning over set complements/negations.
- *Answer-Set Consistency (Sec 4.2, Table 3):* The core finding is that CtE significantly outperforms Base, and sometimes even Oracle. The explanation ("forcing the LLM itself to reason...") is plausible but under-analyzed. Oracle prompts (App A.3) directly tell the model it was wrong and provide the correct relation, yet performance doesn't always improve. This paradox deserves deeper investigation. Possible alternative explanations not ruled out: (1) instruction-following degradation in multi-turn prompts, (2) cognitive overload from context switching, or (3) compliance bias where models ignore corrections if the initial answer feels confident. An ablation isolating the "reasoning" step (e.g., asking the model to generate a chain of thought about the relation before answering, without explicit relation prediction) would clarify whether the gain comes from *classification accuracy* or *structured generation*.
- *Hypothesis Testing & Correlations (Sec 4.3, Table 4):* Table 4 reports Pearson correlations between external benchmark scores (LiveBench) and consistency. The claim that this supports H2 ("LLMs with better general performance produce more consistent responses") conflates correlation with mechanism. Furthermore, LiveBench evaluates a mix of reasoning, coding, and math, which may not directly correlate with set-valued reasoning. The correlation is interesting but insufficient to claim a causal or architectural link. I recommend rephrasing H2 to focus on *empirical association* rather than implying a general performance guarantee, or adding a controlled comparison of model variants (e.g., 8B vs 70B of the same architecture) to isolate scaling effects.
- *Parsing & Normalization:* The evaluation pipeline (App A.4) converts LLM outputs to JSON lists and computes Jaccard/consistency. Appendix H notes "terminological inconsistency" (e.g., "Spain" vs "Kingdom of Spain") as a source of error. This is a critical vulnerability: string-level mismatch penalizes semantic equivalence. The paper does not specify whether entity normalization, fuzzy matching, or KG grounding is applied. For ICLR, a benchmark measuring logical consistency must separate *semantic inconsistency* from *lexical mismatch*. If not addressed, this could systematically underestimate true consistency.

**Discussion & Limitations (Section 5)**
The discussion effectively summarizes findings and acknowledges key limitations (single-turn, static domains, English-only). However, it misses a crucial operational limitation: the *consistency-accuracy-completeness trilemma*. A model can achieve perfect consistency by returning `"idk"` or trivially small sets, or by hallucinating uniformly. The paper notes `%IDK` but does not discuss how practitioners should navigate the trade-off between refusing to answer vs. answering consistently but incorrectly. Acknowledging this trilemma and suggesting future work on joint optimization would strengthen the practical relevance.

**Appendices & Reproducibility**
Appendices B–I provide necessary implementation details, prompts, and supplementary analyses. The multi-agent dataset pipeline (App B.2) is well-documented, though the prompts for Agent 3/4 rely on LLM self-verification, which circles back to the ground-truth validation concern. The statistical analysis (App E, McNemar tests) is appropriate for paired nominal data. Figure 4 (App F) and Tables 8–9 correctly show that misclassification correlates with lower consistency, but this is expected; a more insightful analysis would examine cases where classification is *correct* but consistency still *fails*, isolating execution/generation failure from reasoning failure.

---

### Overall Assessment
This paper presents a clearly motivated, well-structured investigation into a genuine and underexplored failure mode of LLMs: answer-set inconsistency across enumeration questions. The ASCB dataset, the formalization of set-theoretic consistency, and the CtE/Oracle mitigation strategies constitute a solid contribution that aligns well with ICLR's emphasis on systematic model evaluation and reliability analysis. However, several methodological gaps must be addressed to meet ICLR's rigor standards: (1) the dataset lacks explicit validation of ground-truth set relations and inter-annotator agreement, (2) exclusion of `"idk"` responses from consistency denominators inflates scores for high-refusal models, (3) the parsing pipeline's susceptibility to lexical mismatches vs. true semantic inconsistency is not mitigated or quantified, and (4) the CtE vs. Oracle paradox and the H2 correlation claims require deeper ablation and more precise framing. If these points are clarified—particularly around dataset validation, metric normalization, and controlled ablations of the prompting strategies—the paper will stand as a strong, reproducible evaluation study. As written, it is a promising foundation but requires tightening in experimental design and result interpretation.

# Neutral Reviewer
## Balanced Review

### Summary
This paper formalizes and evaluates "answer-set consistency" for LLMs, investigating whether generated answer lists for enumeration questions adhere to expected set-theoretic relations (equivalence, containment, disjointness, overlap, and ternary difference). The authors introduce the Answer-Set Consistency Benchmark (ASCB), comprising 600 manually curated question quadruples, and systematically evaluate 18 contemporary LLMs across base enumeration, classification-then-enumeration (CtE), and oracle strategies. Results reveal pervasive inconsistency across models, which the authors successfully mitigate via CtE prompting, while also disentangling stochastic generation noise from semantic misunderstanding.

### Strengths
1. **Clear Problem Formalization & Novel Evaluation Axis:** The paper effectively distinguishes answer-set consistency from prior consistency research focused on binary facts, negation, or single-entity cloze tests. By targeting enumeration questions and formalizing relations like containment ($\subseteq$) and disjointness ($\cap = \emptyset$), it addresses a practically relevant but underexplored gap in LLM reliability.
2. **Rigorous Benchmark Construction & Transparency:** ASCB is thoughtfully constructed from four sources (LC-QUAD 2.0, QALD, QAWiki, and synthetic) with explicit cardinality bounds (2–100 answers) and manual multi-researcher curation to ensure crisp, objective sets. The full dataset generation pipeline, prompts, and evaluation scripts are provided, strongly supporting reproducibility.
3. **Comprehensive Empirical Analysis & Statistical Rigor:** The evaluation spans 18 models across multiple families and scales, utilizing multiple complementary metrics (consistency rate, Jaccard similarity, %IDK, contradiction-free rates). The use of a temporal control relation ($E_{1,*}$) to isolate stochasticity from semantic misunderstanding is methodologically sound, and the application of one-sided McNemar tests appropriately validates the statistical significance of the CtE mitigation strategy.
4. **Actionable Mitigation Insights:** The CtE prompting strategy consistently and significantly improves consistency across nearly all models. The observation that CtE sometimes outperforms the Oracle condition provides a valuable insight into the benefit of self-generated relational reasoning over externally forced correction.

### Weaknesses
1. **Limited Scale and Domain Diversity:** At 600 quadruples (2,400 questions), the benchmark is relatively small for contemporary LLM evaluation, which often leverages thousands to tens of thousands of instances to capture long-tail failure modes. Furthermore, the questions are heavily skewed toward static factual/KG domains (geography, rivers, political entities), limiting claims about generalization to temporal, procedural, or highly abstract enumeration tasks.
2. **Decoupling of Consistency from Factual Correctness:** The paper explicitly states it does not require ground-truth answer sets to measure consistency. While theoretically clean, this design choice obscures a critical practical trade-off: a model can be perfectly consistent by confidently hallucinating the *same* incorrect entities across related questions. The %IDK metric partially mitigates this, but a joint analysis of consistency vs. ground-truth accuracy is missing.
3. **CtE's Success is Partly Driven by Conservative Fallbacks:** The authors note that CtE yields higher %IDK rates, which likely inflates consistency scores by avoiding uncertain answers rather than genuinely improving generative coherence. The paper acknowledges this but does not sufficiently ablate or control for the "safety valve" effect, leaving the exact mechanism of CtE's improvement underexplored.
4. **Unexplained Non-Monotonic Scaling Behavior:** Results show that larger or newer models do not universally outperform smaller/older variants (e.g., mid-tier models sometimes rival top-tier ones in consistency). The paper reports this phenomenon but lacks architectural or training-based hypotheses to explain it, which misses an opportunity aligned with current ICLR discussions on alignment tax and reasoning efficiency.

### Novelty & Significance
**Novelty:** High for the evaluation/reliability subfield. While logical consistency and single-fact factuality have been extensively studied, formalizing set-theoretic consistency over *enumeration* questions is a distinct and valuable contribution. The benchmark design and the decomposition of inconsistency sources (stochastic vs. semantic) are well-conceived.
**Clarity:** Excellent. Definitions are precise, examples are intuitive, metrics are clearly defined, and the paper structure logically flows from problem formulation → dataset → tasks → results → discussion. 
**Reproducibility:** Strong. All prompts, dataset construction steps, evaluation pipelines, and raw results are shared. The methodology relies on standard prompting and open/closed API models, making replication straightforward.
**Significance:** Solid fit for ICLR's expanding scope on reliable reasoning and LLM evaluation. The findings caution practitioners about the unreliability of raw LLM enumeration and provide a simple, effective mitigation strategy. The benchmark will serve as a useful community resource for tracking progress in structured reasoning and consistency.

### Suggestions for Improvement
1. **Introduce a Consistency-Correctness Joint Analysis:** Augment the evaluation with a ground-truth correctness check (even on a subset). Report metrics like "consistent & correct," "consistent & hallucinated," and "inconsistent & correct." This will clarify whether consistency improvements reflect genuine reasoning gains or merely reduced variance in hallucination.
2. **Ablate the CtE Mechanism vs. IDK Inflation:** Conduct a controlled experiment where the "idk" option is removed or penalized during CtE to isolate the effect of explicit relational reasoning from conservative fallback behavior. Alternatively, report consistency metrics conditional on non-IDK responses.
3. **Broaden Dataset Diversity and Scale:** Incorporate enumeration questions from temporal (e.g., "events in year X"), procedural (e.g., "steps to compile Y"), and conceptual domains to test the robustness of answer-set consistency beyond static factual triples. Expanding the quadruple count would also strengthen statistical power for subset analyses.
4. **Deepen the Scaling & Architecture Discussion:** Hypothesize why consistency does not scale monotonically with model size or parameter count. Consider analyzing factors like instruction-tuning alignment, RLHF/DPO trade-offs, or reasoning-specific fine-tuning as drivers of the observed plateau/inversion in consistency performance.
5. **Clarify the Stochasticity Control Setup:** Specify whether API calls for the $E_{1,*}$ control relation used identical model versions, deterministic routing, and disabled response caching to ensure that observed variance stems from sampling stochasticity rather than system-level nondeterminism or hidden version updates.

### Missing Experiments (top 3-5 only)
1. Add ground-truth accuracy and recall metrics for the enumerated sets because, without them, a model could achieve high consistency by hallucinating the exact same incorrect entities, rendering the consistency metric useless for practical applications.
2. Benchmark standard consistency baselines (e.g., self-consistency/majority voting over multiple samples and vanilla Chain-of-Thought) before claiming CtE as an effective mitigation, as CtE's gains may simply reflect longer context or generic reasoning structure rather than explicit relation-aware prompting.
3. Run multi-seed evaluations (≥5 independent generations per question at T=0) to rigorously decouple computational nondeterminism from semantic failure, since comparing a single paraphrase run (E1,2) to a temporal repeat (E1,*) conflates prompt-order sensitivity with true generation stochasticity.

### Deeper Analysis Needed (top 3-5 only)
1. Quantify and control for the selection bias introduced by the "idk" option, because the inflated %IDK in CtE systematically removes hard cases from the denominator, artificially inflating consistency rates and undermining the statistical validity of H1.
2. Analyze how consistency degrades as a function of ground-truth answer-set cardinality, because if inconsistency scales linearly with list length, the method's contribution is better framed as a scaling limitation of LLM enumeration rather than a novel logical reasoning failure.
3. Explain the mechanistic reason why CtE sometimes outperforms the Oracle strategy, as this counter-intuitive result suggests deeper model dynamics (e.g., self-consistency bootstrapping vs. rigid instruction following) that must be analyzed to support the paper's practical recommendations.

### Visualizations & Case Studies
1. Provide annotated, concrete examples of specific relation violations (e.g., exactly which hallucinated or omitted entities break containment/disjointness) to prove the benchmark captures genuine logical failures rather than superficial parsing or formatting inconsistencies.
2. Plot consistency rate vs. expected answer-set size for each model to visually expose whether performance collapses past a specific cardinality threshold, which would immediately reveal the boundary conditions of the observed inconsistency.
3. Include a domain-stratified breakdown (e.g., geography, biology, synthetic facts) to verify that the 600 quadruples are not biased toward low-knowledge-frequecy topics where models are expected to fail, ensuring the findings generalize to standard QA domains.

### Obvious Next Steps
1. Evaluate retrieval-augmented or tool-use baselines (e.g., LLM + external KG/API) to contextualize the findings, as ICLR reviewers will expect evidence on whether simple external grounding trivially resolves the consistency bottleneck before accepting purely prompt-based mitigation claims.
2. Implement a rigorous entity-normalization pipeline prior to set evaluation, because failures like "Spain" vs. "Kingdom of Spain" trivially break equivalence and disjointness checks, conflating surface-level string matching failures with actual logical inconsistency.
3. Report accuracy-conditional consistency (consistency rates computed only over correctly retrieved subsets) to isolate pure logical reasoning failures from knowledge gaps, which is essential for establishing whether the proposed method addresses reasoning or merely masks retrieval deficiencies.

# Final Consolidated Review
## Summary
This paper formalizes "answer-set consistency" as a novel evaluation axis for LLMs, measuring whether enumerated answer sets respect expected set-theoretic relations (equivalence, containment, disjointness, etc.). The authors introduce the manually curated ASCB benchmark, evaluate 18 contemporary models, and demonstrate that a Classification-then-Enumeration (CtE) prompting strategy significantly mitigates inconsistencies. The work provides a timely, systematic investigation into structural coherence for set-valued QA tasks.

## Strengths
- **Precise problem formalization and clear conceptual boundaries:** The paper successfully carves out answer-set consistency from prior work on binary fact-checking, paraphrase stability, and preference ordering. The mathematical definitions of consistency versus self-contradiction are rigorous and well-scoped.
- **Methodologically sound isolation of stochasticity vs. semantic failure:** The inclusion of a temporal control relation ($E_{1,*}$) alongside standard pairwise evaluation provides a clever mechanism to disentangle inherent generative nondeterminism from genuine logical/semantic misunderstanding.
- **Reproducible and empirically validated mitigation:** The CtE strategy yields statistically significant improvements across diverse model families, validated via appropriate paired tests (McNemar). The full release of the dataset, prompts, and evaluation pipelines strongly supports community replication and extension.

## Weaknesses
- **Evaluation pipeline conflates semantic inconsistency with lexical mismatch:** Consistency is computed via exact string matching on enumerated lists. As acknowledged in Appendix H, terminological variations (e.g., `"Spain"` vs. `"Kingdom of Spain"`) or minor formatting differences trivially violate disjointness or equivalence checks. Without entity normalization, fuzzy matching, or KG grounding, the benchmark systematically underestimates true logical consistency and penalizes surface-level paraphrasing variance rather than structural reasoning failures.
- **Exclusion of `"idk"`/empty responses from consistency denominators creates a refusal-coherence confound:** The core metrics `CON(M)` and `SIM(M)` drop abstentions. Consequently, strategies or models that frequently refuse (e.g., CtE reaches ~47% IDK for some models) appear artificially more consistent on the remaining non-empty subset. The absence of a joint metric (e.g., consistency weighted by attempt rate) or conditional analysis makes it difficult to disentangle whether performance gains stem from improved reasoning or simply a conservative "safety valve" behavior.
- **Limited benchmark scale and narrow domain coverage:** The dataset comprises 600 quadruples (2,400 questions) heavily skewed toward static, factual knowledge-graph domains (geography, hydrology, political entities). While sufficient for initial statistical significance testing, this scale is modest for contemporary LLM evaluation. More importantly, the narrow domain focus limits generalizability claims to temporal, procedural, or highly abstract enumeration tasks where set boundaries are inherently fuzzier or evolve over time, potentially overstating the benchmark's broader utility.

## Nice-to-Haves
- **Consistency conditioned on factual correctness:** Although explicitly scoped out, reporting a subset analysis of consistency on ground-truth-correct retrievals would help distinguish true reasoning stabilizers from models that simply hallucinate with lower variance.
- **Stronger prompting baselines:** Including self-consistency (majority voting over multiple samples) or standard Chain-of-Thought baselines would contextualize whether CtE's gains are specific to relation-aware prompting or generic artifacts of extended generation length.
- **Cardinality-scaling analysis:** Plotting consistency against ground-truth answer-set size would clarify whether inconsistency scales linearly with list length, framing the issue as an enumeration capacity bottleneck rather than purely a logical reasoning failure.
- **Domain-stratified breakdowns:** Reporting performance by source domain (e.g., geography vs. synthetic vs. biology) would verify that results are not driven by a few low-frequency knowledge areas where models universally struggle.

## Novel Insights
The paper surfaces a critical, often overlooked failure mode in LLMs: the inability to maintain structural coherence across related enumeration queries, even when factual knowledge is partially present. Perhaps the most compelling insight is the observation that forcing models to explicitly classify set-theoretic relations *before* enumerating (CtE) frequently outperforms oracle correction, where the model is directly told the correct relation. This suggests that self-generated relational reasoning acts as a more effective scaffolding for structured output generation than external compliance prompts, highlighting a potential alignment bottleneck where LLMs benefit more from internal cognitive organization than from direct instruction-following on corrections.

## Suggestions
- Implement a lightweight entity normalization or semantic similarity pipeline prior to set evaluation to decouple logical inconsistency from surface-level lexical variations, and report its impact on baseline consistency scores.
- Report consistency metrics conditional on non-abstention (excluding only `"idk"`/empty cases from analysis but weighting them in aggregate scores, or providing a joint "attempt × consistency" metric) to ensure fair comparison across strategies with divergent refusal rates.
- In future iterations or expansions, incorporate domains with dynamic boundaries or procedural steps to stress-test answer-set consistency beyond static factual triples, strengthening claims about general logical coherence.
