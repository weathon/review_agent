# Harsh Critic Review
## Section-by-Section Critical Review

### Title & Abstract
- **Does the title accurately reflect the contribution?** Yes. "MermaidFlow" + "Safety-Constrained Evolutionary Programming" + "Agentic Workflow Generation" precisely captures the core components: a DSL-based representation (Mermaid), an optimization mechanism (EP), and the target domain.
- **Does the abstract clearly state the problem, method, and key results?** The problem (brittle, unexecutable LLM-generated workflows due to implicit representations) and method (declarative Mermaid graphs + safety-constrained EP operators) are clearly articulated. Key results are summarized as consistent improvements in success rates and faster convergence.
- **Are any claims in the abstract unsupported by the paper?** The claim that MermaidFlow achieves improvements "without modifying task settings or evaluation protocols" is supported. However, the abstract implies that static verifiability directly translates to runtime reliability. While the paper shows strong empirical gains, the abstract slightly overstates the bridge between "statically verifiable" graphs and "robust" execution, as semantic/runtime failures (e.g., incorrect prompts or tool misuse) are not prevented by the static checks.

### Introduction & Motivation
- **Is the problem well-motivated? Is the gap in prior work clearly identified?** Yes. The motivation effectively highlights a recognized bottleneck in current agentic systems: collapsing planning, code realization, and runtime execution into imperative Python/JSON leads to entangled semantics and brittleness. The gap (lack of a declarative, structurally explicit, and pre-verifiable workflow representation) is clearly positioned against recent works like AFlow and ADAS.
- **Are the contributions clearly stated and accurate?** The three contributions (declarative representation, EP search framework, empirical superiority) are clearly listed and accurately reflect the paper's content.
- **Does the introduction over-claim or under-sell?** The claim that this is the "first agentic workflow framework to **guarantee** static graph-level correctness" is slightly strong for ICLR standards. The "guarantee" applies only to the structural/syntactic rules enforced by the custom checker and Mermaid parser, not to semantic validity or execution success. Additionally, the introduction frames the EP operators as formal mathematical constructs, but Section 4.2 reveals that candidate generation is primarily prompt-driven by an LLM, which only *approximates* these operators. This disconnect between formal guarantee and practical LLM-based generation should be tempered in the introduction to avoid over-promising.

### Method / Approach
- **Is the method clearly described and reproducible?** Largely yes. The graph formalization, node/edge definitions, and evolutionary loop (parent sampling, operator application, LLM-as-judge filtering, history buffer update) are well-documented. The appendix provides full prompts, checker rules (A.2), and pseudo-code (A.3), which greatly aids reproducibility.
- **Are key assumptions stated and justified?** The assumption that Mermaid's declarative syntax + custom soft/hard checks suffice to define a closed, valid search space is reasonable for syntactic structure. However, the paper assumes that an LLM, when prompted, will faithfully execute the defined EP operations (Lemma 1). This is not enforced programmatically; it's heuristic. The paper also assumes the LLM-as-judge can reliably score structural/semantic fitness without rollout execution, which is a strong assumption that warrants more justification or validation.
- **Are there logical gaps in the derivation or reasoning?** There is a notable gap between the formalized operators (Section 4.1) and their implementation. Lemma 1 states that the space `S` is closed under operators `O`, but the actual search loop generates candidates via LLM prompts (Algorithm 3), not by programmatically applying the operators. The "safety constraints" are enforced post-hoc via rejection sampling (re-generating until the checker passes). This means the theoretical closure property does not guarantee that the *search trajectory* is smooth or efficient; it only guarantees that accepted candidates are valid. The paper should clarify that the operators serve as *prompts/guidelines* rather than deterministic transformations.
- **Are there edge cases or failure modes not discussed?** The method does not address semantic drift: a graph can be structurally perfect and type-consistent but functionally useless (e.g., redundant nodes, contradictory prompt routing, or infinite logical loops). The LLM-as-judge attempts to filter these, but its scoring criteria (coherence, innovation, etc.) are subjective and unvalidated. Additionally, cyclic dependencies or disconnected components are caught by W2/W4, but conditional branching (if/else) and iterative loops are explicitly unsupported (noted in Appendix E), which limits applicability to complex real-world workflows.
- **For theoretical claims: are proofs correct and complete?** Lemma 1 is underdeveloped for ICLR standards. It reads more as an engineering assertion than a mathematical proof. The "proof" is essentially "by induction, if operators check types, validity is preserved." Given that the operators are not programmatically enforced but LLM-prompted, Lemma 1 should either be rigorously formalized (e.g., as a property of the valid Mermaid language subset) or replaced with a clear empirical/statistical analysis of operator success rates.

### Experiments & Results
- **Do the experiments actually test the paper's claims?** Yes. The experiments measure task success (accuracy/pass@1), search efficiency (token consumption, learning curves), and robustness across domains (math/code), directly addressing the claims of improved performance and search efficiency.
- **Are baselines appropriate and fairly compared?** The baselines are comprehensive and well-chosen. Using `gpt-4o-mini` consistently across methods ensures a fair model-level comparison. The authors correctly note that AFlow's iteration count is matched to 20. One minor caveat: the MBPP score for MaAS is taken from the original paper (`*` in Table 1) due to unavailable implementation, which slightly breaks the "identical evaluation protocol" claim for that single entry, but is understandable.
- **Are there missing ablations that would materially change conclusions?** **Yes, critical ablations are missing.** The paper combines three novel components: (1) the Mermaid representation, (2) the EP search loop, and (3) the safety constraints/checker. Without ablations isolating these, it's unclear where the gains come from. For example: Does Mermaid outperform Python simply because the LLM understands it better as a DSL, or is it the safety constraints? How much does the LLM-as-judge contribute vs. random selection or pure rollout-based selection? An ablation of `(Mermaid vs Python representation) × (With vs Without Safety Constraints)` would substantially strengthen the methodological claims.
- **Are error bars / statistical significance reported?** **No.** Table 1 reports averages over 3 runs, but no standard deviations or confidence intervals are provided. Given that the average improvement over the strongest baseline (MaAS) is 1.40%, and improvements on individual datasets range from ~0.14% (MBPP: 82.31 vs 82.17) to 2.61% (MATH), the lack of variance makes it impossible to assess statistical significance. ICLR reviewers heavily scrutinize marginal gains without error bars.
- **Do the results support the claims made, or are they cherry-picked?** The results appear consistent across all four benchmarks and the ablation on LLM scale (Table 2) supports the claim that the structured search space benefits from stronger optimizers. No obvious cherry-picking is detected.
- **Are datasets and evaluation metrics appropriate?** Yes. GSM8K, MATH, HumanEval, and MBPP are standard for this domain. Using Solve Rate and Pass@1 is appropriate. The MATH subset (difficulty level 5) follows established prior work.

### Writing & Clarity
- **Are there sections that are confusing or poorly explained?** The transition between the formal EP operators (Sec 4.1) and the actual LLM generation process (Sec 4.2, Algorithm 2) is confusing. The paper defines `O ∈ {Node Substitution, Edge Rewiring, ...}` as discrete operators, but the implementation relies on a monolithic `UPDATE_MERMAID_WORKFLOW` prompt that asks the LLM to "combine, modify, or innovate." The mapping between the formal operators and the prompt's instructions is loose. Clarifying that these operators are *semantic guidelines for the prompt* rather than *programmatic primitives* would resolve this. Additionally, Section 4.2 states that LLM-as-judge avoids expensive rollouts, but Algorithm 1 line 9 still calls `EvaluateWorkflow` on the selected child to update the history buffer. The trade-off (judge for screening 1 candidate per round vs. evaluating all candidates) should be explicitly stated and cost-analyzed.
- **Are figures and tables clear and informative?** Table 1 is well-formatted and easy to parse. The referenced figures (learning curves, case study graphs) are described well in-text. The case study (Appendix B) effectively demonstrates the Mermaid-to-Python translation and final workflow structure, enhancing clarity.

### Limitations & Broader Impact
- **Do the authors acknowledge the key limitations?** Yes. Section 6 and Appendix E acknowledge the lack of control flow (if/for) in the current Mermaid representation, the reliance on LLM-based translation to Python (which introduces the same brittleness they aim to avoid), and the need for a rule-based Mermaid-to-LangGraph converter.
- **Are there fundamental limitations they missed?** Several important limitations are under-discussed:
  1. **Compute & API Costs:** The optimization loop relies on closed-source models (GPT-4o-mini/Claude) for both optimization and execution. The cost of the `LLM-as-judge` + `checker` + `re-generation loops + single rollout per round` is not quantified, which is critical for practical adoption.
  2. **Semantic vs. Syntactic Safety:** The "safety constraints" only enforce graph topology and type signatures. They do not prevent runtime failures, security vulnerabilities (e.g., arbitrary code execution in generated Python), or logical deadlocks. Conflating "static graph correctness" with "workflow robustness" is a conceptual limitation that should be explicitly bounded.
  3. **Generalization to Open-Domain Tasks:** The evaluation is restricted to math and code-generation with well-defined inputs/outputs. Extending this to open-ended, multi-turn, tool-heavy, or human-in-the-loop workflows would likely expose the rigidity of the predefined node types.
- **Are there failure modes or negative societal impacts not discussed?** The paper briefly mentions integration with real-world systems but doesn't discuss the risk of over-automating brittle reasoning pipelines. The broader impact section is omitted, but given the technical focus, a brief statement on how verifiable workflows could reduce hallucination-driven automation failures (positive) vs. risks of propagating systematic errors if the base LLM or checker is flawed (negative) would be appropriate for ICLR.

### Overall Assessment
MermaidFlow presents a clear and timely contribution by introducing a declarative, graph-based workflow representation combined with an evolutionary search framework to improve agentic planning reliability. The motivation is well-grounded in the recognized brittleness of imperative/code-based workflow generation, and the empirical results on standard benchmarks demonstrate consistent improvements over strong baselines like AFlow and ADAS. However, to meet ICLR's rigorous standards, the authors must address several critical points: (1) provide error bars and statistical significance tests for the reported gains, as some improvements are marginal; (2) include targeted ablations isolating the contributions of the Mermaid representation, the safety constraints, and the LLM-as-judge; and (3) clarify the disconnect between the formally stated EP operators (which imply deterministic, programmatic transformations) and the actual LLM-prompted generation with rejection sampling. The claim of "guaranteed correctness" should also be refined to explicitly distinguish between syntactic/structural validity and semantic/runtime reliability. If these methodological and empirical clarifications are addressed, the paper offers a solid, reproducible advancement in verifiable agentic workflow generation.

# Neutral Reviewer
## Balanced Review

### Summary
MermaidFlow introduces a declarative, statically verifiable workflow representation using the Mermaid graph language, coupled with safety-constrained evolutionary programming to search a structurally valid agentic workflow space. By enforcing type-consistent graph operators and static parsing, the framework aims to reduce brittle, code-bound workflow generation and improve search efficiency. Experiments on four math and code benchmarks show consistent, albeit modest, improvements over strong baselines, alongside notable reductions in token consumption during optimization.

### Strengths
1. **Well-motivated shift to declarative IR:** The paper clearly identifies the core failure mode in current agentic systems: implicit, imperative code representations that collapse planning and execution, leading to runtime brittleness. The adoption of Mermaid as a lightweight, human-readable, and structurally explicit intermediate representation directly addresses this gap (Section 1 & 3.1).
2. **Formalized constraint-preserving search operators:** The evolutionary operators (node substitution, addition, deletion, edge rewiring, crossover) are rigorously defined with explicit type-matching conditions, and Lemma 1 formally proves closure under these operations. This ensures the search remains within a valid subspace by design, avoiding the frequent syntax/runtime errors seen in code-generation approaches (Section 4.1).
3. **Comprehensive empirical evaluation and token efficiency:** The method is compared against 13 baselines across GSM8K, MATH, HumanEval, and MBPP, consistently outperforming strong evolutionary baselines like AFlow and MaAS (Table 1). The reported ~50% reduction in token cost to reach competitive performance (Section 5.3) demonstrates practical efficiency gains from constrained graph-level search over raw code generation.
4. **High reproducibility and transparency:** The appendices provide exhaustive details including the Mermaid checker rules (soft/hard checks), full prompt templates, algorithmic pseudocode, baseline configurations, and concrete case studies with Mermaid/Python code pairs (Appendices A–C). This level of documentation substantially lowers the barrier for replication and practical adoption.

### Weaknesses
1. **Lack of statistical rigor and variance reporting:** While improvements over baselines are consistent, Table 1 reports only point averages without error bars, standard deviations, or significance tests across the three runs mentioned in the caption. Given the stochastic nature of LLM generation and evolutionary search, this omission makes it difficult to assess the robustness and statistical significance of the claimed gains.
2. **Unvalidated LLM-as-Judge selection mechanism:** The framework relies on an LLM-as-Judge to score and select candidates before full rollout evaluation (Section 4.2). However, the paper does not analyze judge-reward correlation, judge bias, or calibration. Optimizing for judge-preferred structures rather than empirically successful ones could introduce hidden selection pressures that compromise real task performance.
3. **Conflated claims of "static verification":** The abstract and introduction assert static graph-level correctness guarantees. While Mermaid's parser ensures syntactic and structural validity (e.g., connectivity, type matching), it cannot verify semantic correctness, prompt efficacy, or runtime LLM behavior. The paper acknowledges this implicitly through its retry mechanism and checker logs, but the framing overstates the safety guarantees relative to actual execution failure modes.
4. **Missing ablation isolating Mermaid's contribution:** It remains unclear whether performance gains stem from the Mermaid representation specifically or from the constrained EP framework and experience buffer alone. Without a baseline applying the same safety-constrained evolutionary search over alternative structured representations (e.g., JSON schema with type validation, DOT/Graphviz), the unique advantage of Mermaid is confounded.

### Novelty & Significance
**Novelty:** Moderate. Structured workflow search and evolutionary programming for LLM agents are established directions (e.g., AFlow, EvoFlow, GPTSwarm). The paper's novelty lies in coupling a lightweight, graph-oriented DSL (Mermaid) with formally verified, type-consistent evolutionary operators to guarantee syntactic search-space closure. This is a practical, incremental advancement rather than a conceptual breakthrough, but it is technically sound and well-integrated.
**Clarity:** High. The problem statement, mathematical formalization of the graph space, operator definitions, and pipeline algorithms are clearly presented. Figures and case studies effectively bridge the declarative representation and executable code. Minor PDF extraction artifacts do not impede comprehension.
**Reproducibility:** Good, with caveats. Detailed prompts, algorithms, checker rules, and baseline configs are provided. However, reliance on closed-source APIs, LLM-as-judge scoring, and fixed seed assumptions means exact reproducibility depends on API stability and strict prompt adherence. Open-sourcing the codebase would further strengthen this.
**Significance:** Practically significant. The framework offers a reproducible blueprint for building safer, interpretable, and token-efficient agentic workflows. The consistent gains across standard benchmarks validate its utility, though broader generalization to tool-use, web navigation, or open-ended planning remains untested. For ICLR, the work meets the empirical bar for applied agentic systems but would benefit from tighter statistical reporting and deeper analysis of the judge selection mechanism.

### Suggestions for Improvement
1. **Add statistical reporting:** Report mean ± standard deviation (or confidence intervals) across multiple random seeds for all main results. Include significance tests (e.g., paired t-test or bootstrap) against the top-performing baselines to substantiate the claimed improvements.
2. **Validate and calibrate the LLM-as-Judge:** Measure the correlation between judge scores and actual task solve rates. Test alternative selection strategies (e.g., validation-set scoring, Thompson sampling) and analyze how judge bias or prompt sensitivity affects evolutionary trajectory and final performance.
3. **Isolate the representation contribution:** Add an ablation where the same safety-constrained EP and experience buffer are applied to an alternative structured format (e.g., typed JSON or NetworkX/DOT graphs). This will clarify whether gains arise from Mermaid's syntax/parsing properties or primarily from the constrained search framework.
4. **Refine verification claims:** Explicitly distinguish between syntactic/structural guarantees (enforced by the Mermaid parser) and semantic/runtime guarantees (dependent on prompts and LLM execution). Discuss how the system handles prompt degradation, LLM hallucination, or tool mismatch post-translation.
5. **Expand task evaluation for generalizability:** Evaluate on at least one non-math/code benchmark (e.g., HotpotQA, ToolBench, or ALFWorld) to demonstrate that the Mermaid + constrained EP paradigm transfers to domains requiring tool retrieval, external API calls, or stateful environment interaction.

# Spark Finder Review
## How to Improve This Paper

### Missing Experiments (top 3-4 only)
1. Full compute-matched comparison reporting token usage, wall-clock time, and validation success across all baselines throughout the entire optimization curve, not just at a single threshold. Without this, claims of faster convergence and superior token efficiency are unsubstantiated.
2. Ablation decoupling the declarative representation from the safety-constrained checks (e.g., Mermaid without the static checker, and Python with identical type-enforcement rules) to isolate whether gains stem from true static verification or merely simpler text editing for the LLM.
3. Evaluation on multi-step tool-use or interactive agent benchmarks (e.g., WebArena, SWE-bench lite) to prove the representation captures genuine dependency complexity rather than just optimizing for parallel ensemble patterns in math/code.

### Deeper Analysis Needed (top 3-4 only)
1. Quantitative correlation between LLM-as-Judge proxy scores and ground-truth execution success across evolutionary generations. A weak or inverse correlation would invalidate the evolutionary selection mechanism and undermine search efficiency claims.
2. Precise failure attribution breaking down post-translation errors into: static verification bypass, semantic mismatch in prompts, and actual runtime/code execution faults. Without this, the core "guaranteed valid-by-construction" claim remains unverified.
3. Tracking of discovered topological metrics (graph depth, branching factor, unique node types) over iterations to determine if EP discovers non-trivial architectural motifs or merely trivially expands parallel branches and ensembles.

### Visualizations & Case Studies (top 3 only)
1. Parent-to-child evolution trajectories showing concrete before/after Mermaid graphs for each operator type, proving that crossovers and mutations yield meaningful structural adaptations rather than syntactic noise.
2. A side-by-side case study of a workflow that passes all static checks but fails at Python execution, explicitly highlighting which logical or semantic constraints the graph formalism failed to capture.
3. Operator contribution heatmap showing which evolutionary operations most frequently lead to validation score increases versus decreases, revealing whether the proposed operator set actually drives optimization or just adds search variance.

### Obvious Next Steps (top 3 only)
1. Fix or remove the completely truncated "Optimal Stopping Point Analysis" text before submission, as leaving core experimental analysis cut off directly contradicts claims of stable convergence and invalidates the ablation.
2. Replace the LLM-based Mermaid-to-Python translation step with a deterministic, rule-based compiler in the main pipeline, because delegating translation to an LLM reintroduces the exact brittleness and verification gaps the paper claims to eliminate.
3. Consolidate all prompts, operator definitions, and EP hyperparameters into a single, machine-readable configuration file or table rather than scattering them across fragmented appendix sections, as incomplete setup details violate ICLR reproducibility standards.

# Final Consolidated Review
## Summary
MermaidFlow introduces a declarative, graph-based intermediate representation (using the Mermaid DSL) for agentic workflows, coupled with a safety-constrained evolutionary programming search. By enforcing structural and type-level validity via static parsing, the framework aims to reduce thebrittleness of imperative Python/JSON workflow generation. Experiments on four math and code benchmarks show consistent improvements in task success rates and notable reductions in token consumption during optimization compared to strong code-centric baselines.

## Strengths
- **Well-motivated shift to a declarative IR:** The paper accurately identifies the core failure mode in current agentic systems—implicit, imperative code representations that collapse planning, semantics, and execution. Adopting Mermaid as a lightweight, human-readable, and structurally explicit intermediate representation directly targets this bottleneck and cleanly separates symbolic planning from runtime realization.
- **Strong baseline comparison & demonstrated token efficiency:** The method is evaluated against 13 diverse baselines across GSM8K, MATH, HumanEval, and MBPP, consistently outperforming evolutionary and hand-crafted baselines. The reported ~50% reduction in token cost to reach competitive performance empirically validates that constrained graph-level search is more sample-efficient than unconstrained code mutation.
- **High transparency & reproducibility:** The appendices provide exhaustive implementation details, including the Mermaid checker rules (soft/hard checks), full prompt templates, algorithmic pseudocode, baseline configurations, and concrete Mermaid-to-Python translation examples. This level of documentation substantially lowers the barrier for replication.

## Weaknesses
- **Confounded experimental design & missing critical ablations:** The framework bundles three distinct components: the Mermaid representation, the safety constraints/checker, and the evolutionary selection loop. Without ablations isolating these, it remains unclear where the empirical gains originate. Specifically, comparing Mermaid against an alternative structured IR (e.g., typed JSON or DOT) with identical safety constraints, and comparing LLM-as-judge selection against pure rollout-based selection, is necessary to disentangle representation benefits from constrained search mechanics.
- **Disconnect between formal guarantees and LLM-driven implementation:** The paper claims the search space is closed under evolutionary operators (Lemma 1) and formally defines deterministic transformations. However, the actual search relies on monolithic LLM prompts that approximate these operators, with validity enforced via post-hoc rejection sampling. Consequently, Lemma 1 describes a property of the *operator definitions*, not the *LLM generation trajectory*. The claimed "guaranteed" closure is thus an engineering artifact of filtering, not a mathematical property of the optimization process.
- **Unvalidated proxy selection & inflated verification claims:** The evolutionary loop relies heavily on an LLM-as-judge to filter candidates before costly evaluation, yet the correlation between judge scores and ground-truth execution success is never quantified. This leaves the selection mechanism unverified and prone to optimizing for structural aesthetics over functional reasoning capability. Furthermore, conflate "static graph correctness" with workflow robustness is overstated; static parsing cannot detect semantic failures (e.g., logical deadlocks, contradictory prompts, or tool mismatch), which remain the dominant failure mode in agentic systems.

## Nice-to-Haves
- Replace the LLM-based Mermaid-to-Python translation step with a deterministic, rule-based compiler (as noted in the authors' future work) to fully eliminate syntax-brittleness in the execution layer.
- Track and report topological evolution metrics (e.g., graph depth, branching factor, operator success/failure rates) across iterations to demonstrate whether the EP discovers non-trivial architectural motifs or merely trivially expands parallel ensemble branches.
- Consolidate prompts, node type definitions, and EP hyperparameters into a single, machine-readable configuration schema to streamline reproducibility and future extensions.

## Novel Insights
Constraining the architecture search space to a lightweight, compiler-verifiable DSL can significantly reduce the "exploration tax" of LLM-based workflow generation, cutting optimization token costs by roughly half while maintaining syntactic validity. However, the results reveal a critical bottleneck shift: once structural errors are eliminated by design, agentic performance becomes strictly bounded by semantic alignment. This underscores that verifiable graph topology is a necessary but insufficient condition for robust multi-agent reasoning, highlighting a clear trajectory toward integrating prompt-level and tool-level semantics directly into the representation's type system.

## Potentially Missed Related Work
- **DSPy Teleprompters & Optimizable Graphs:** DSPy's approach to optimizing LM programs via structured signatures and teleprompters shares the goal of decoupling logic from weights/prompts. Citing this would better contextualize the declarative IR space beyond graph-centric agent frameworks.
- **LangGraph / State Machine Formalisms:** Formal state/flow definitions for agent orchestration provide established semantics for control flow and conditional branching. Positioning MermaidFlow against these would clarify its trade-offs regarding expressive power versus static verifiability. (Present as contextual suggestions; not penalized.)

## Suggestions
- **Rigorous Statistical Reporting & Decoupled Ablations:** Report mean ± standard deviation across ≥5 random seeds and apply significance testing against top baselines. Crucially, run ablation studies that isolate the Mermaid IR from the safety checker and LLM-judge to prove the unique contribution of each component.
- **Validate the LLM-as-Judge & Scope Verification Claims:** Quantify the rank correlation between judge-proxy scores and actual validation accuracy across generations. Explicitly refine the manuscript's language to distinguish between syntactic/topological guarantees (enforced by the parser) and semantic/runtime reliability, avoiding overclaiming "guaranteed correctness" for end-to-end execution.
- **Reframe Lemma 1 or Align Implementation:** Either formalize Lemma 1 to reflect the prompt-based, rejection-sampled reality (e.g., framing it as an empirical closure property supported by checker pass-rate statistics), or implement the operators programmatically to match the claimed deterministic search guarantees.
