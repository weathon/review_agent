# Harsh Critic Review
## Section-by-Section Critical Review

**Title & Abstract**
The title accurately reflects the scope but lacks distinctiveness, as "LLM as a classifier" has been explored in various forms. The abstract claims "zero-shot classification" and "O(1) latency," both of which require immediate qualification. "Zero-shot" here actually means zero-shot *transfer to unseen domains/label sets without additional fine-tuning*, not true zero-shot from a base model. The "O(1) latency" claim is technically misleading: transformer inference latency scales with input sequence length during the prefill phase and with attention complexity. What is truly O(1) here is the *number of autoregressive decoding steps*, not total inference time. The abstract should precisely state that latency gains come from eliminating multi-step *generation*, not from constant-time inference overall.

**Introduction & Motivation**
The motivation around latency bottlenecks in latency-critical deployments is well-grounded and relevant to ICLR's systems and efficiency interests. However, the introduction frames the latency problem as if multi-token generation dominates inference time across the board. In modern serving frameworks (e.g., vLLM, TensorRT-LLM), the prefill phase typically dominates total latency for prompts longer than a few dozen tokens, while decoding is compute-bound but brief. The paper needs to contextualize when single-token outputs actually yield meaningful end-to-end speedups versus standard constrained decoding with `max_new_tokens=1`. Additionally, the claim that encoder-based models "lack flexibility" is underspecified; modern encoder-decoder hybrid architectures and promptable encoders (e.g., E5, bge-family) blur this line, and the trade-off should be framed more carefully.

**Related Work**
The survey covers prompting, PET/LM-BFF, constrained decoding, and encoder fine-tuning adequately. However, it misses recent, highly relevant work that directly competes with or overlaps LaaC:
1. **Grammar/JSON-constrained decoding frameworks** (e.g., Outlines, LMQL, Instructor) that guarantee single-token or structured outputs without fine-tuning, often with optimized CPU/GPU implementations. How does LaaC differ in latency/accuracy from a base model using these tools?
2. **Classification head fine-tuning on decoders**: Several recent works show that simply attaching a linear head to a decoder's final hidden state, or fine-tuning the LM head alone, achieves comparable classification accuracy without adding special tokens. The related work should position LaaC against these parameter-efficient decoder classification approaches to clarify the necessity of the special-token + randomization design.

**Methodology (Section 3)**
The method is clearly described and reproducible in principle. Three concerns stand out:
1. **Randomized Label Assignments (Sec 3.3)**: Shuffling the class-to-token mapping per training instance is intended to prevent memorization, but it introduces conflicting gradient signals for the embedding rows of `[o_k]`. If `[o_1]` maps to "positive" in instance A and "negative" in instance B, the embedding for `[o_1]` and the LM head weights receive opposing updates. While the prompt provides disambiguation, the optimization landscape may become noisy, potentially slowing convergence or degrading representation quality. The paper should include training loss/accuracy curves comparing randomized vs. fixed mapping, or theoretically justify why cross-attention over the prompt stabilizes learning despite embedding conflicts.
2. **Novelty Positioning**: The core idea (augment tokenizer with special class tokens, fine-tune with LoRA, restrict decoding to `max_new_tokens=1`) is a straightforward engineering adaptation. ICLR typically expects either a methodological breakthrough or a rigorous empirical demarcation of when/why this specific design outperforms strong alternatives. The paper would benefit from a clearer articulation of what is fundamentally new beyond the randomization trick and the unified multimodal prompt wrapper.
3. **Loss Formulation (Eq. 3)**: Masking all tokens except the final prediction is standard for instruction-tuning. However, the formulation does not discuss how the model handles the visual modality during prefill. If images are tokenized into hundreds of patches, the prefill latency will naturally be longer. The methodology should explicitly acknowledge that the single-token decoding benefit is only realized *after* a potentially expensive prefill phase, which is already covered by baseline models.

**Experiments & Results (Section 4 & Appendix)**
This is the most critical section, containing several baseline and measurement issues that must be resolved for ICLR acceptance:
1. **Hardware/Deployment Mismatch for Latency**: Tables 1 and 2 compare locally hosted models (evaluated on a single A100 via vLLM) against proprietary API models (GPT-4o, GPT-5). Cloud API latency includes network RTT, request queuing, rate-limiting, and internal batching policies, making it incomparable to local inference time. Claiming an "order of magnitude" speedup based on this comparison conflates algorithmic efficiency with deployment infrastructure. To claim algorithmic superiority, latency should be measured **on the same hardware and serving framework** using models of comparable capability (e.g., Qwen2-VL-72B, Llama-3.1-70B, or Claude Haiku/Sonnet via comparable local setups with quantized weights).
2. **Missing Constrained Decoding Baseline**: The paper compares LaaC to unconstrained base generation for latency. A fairer baseline is the *same base model* running on vLLM with `max_new_tokens=1` and logit biasing to force a single valid label token. This would isolate the benefit of the special-token architecture from simply limiting output length. Given that modern decoders are heavily optimized, the latency delta between a forced natural-language token and a special token is likely marginal (saving only 0-2 decode steps). This baseline is essential to validate the core latency claim.
3. **MIntRec 2.0 Performance Gap (Table 1)**: LaaC achieves 62.7% accuracy on MIntRec 2.0, while GPT-4o achieves 43.7%. This ~19-point gap is unusually large for a modern VLM on intent recognition. GPT-4o typically performs strongly on multimodal dialogue tasks when prompted correctly. The paper does not detail the exact prompt structure, temperature, or visual preprocessing used for GPT-4o. Without identical input formatting (e.g., number of video frames sampled, resolution, context window truncation) and temperature=0 greedy decoding, this comparison is ambiguous. A controlled prompt engineering sweep or reference to a third-party leaderboard score for GPT-4o on MIntRec is needed.
4. **Reproducibility of GPT-5 Results**: GPT-5 is not a publicly verifiable model with documented API specifications, version numbers, or latency characteristics. ICLR requires all baselines to be reproducible or properly cited with verifiable evaluation conditions. Results relying on unreleased or unspecified commercial models should be supplemented with open, verifiable alternatives (e.g., Qwen2.5-VL-72B, Llama-3.2-90B) or clearly marked as preliminary.
5. **Text Benchmark Accuracy (Table 2)**: The fine-tuned LaaC models show marginal or no accuracy gains over their untuned base counterparts on SST-2 and AG News (e.g., Mistral Base 95.5% → LaaC 95.5%). This suggests the base models are already saturated on these tasks, making classification fine-tuning unnecessary. The real contribution is latency, but as noted above, the latency comparison lacks a fair single-token baseline. On harder datasets (DBpedia), LaaC drops from 97.0% (Base) to 95.0%. The authors should discuss this trade-off and clarify when LaaC actually improves accuracy versus merely preserving it while reducing output steps.
6. **Generative Capability Retention**: The introduction and conclusion repeatedly claim LaaC "preserves the generative flexibility" of decoders. However, no post-fine-tuning generative benchmarks (e.g., MMLU, IFEval, GSM8K, or standard instruction-following sets) are reported. LoRA generally preserves capabilities, but adding and fine-tuning on special classification tokens can sometimes degrade general language modeling. Empirical evidence of capability retention is required to support this claim.

**Writing & Clarity**
The paper is generally well-written and logically structured. However, several claims are overstated and should be tempered:
- "O(1) latency" → Change to "constant number of decoding steps" or "single-step autoregressive generation."
- "Zero-shot classification" → Change to "zero-shot adaptation to new label spaces without parameter updates."
- Section 3.3's explanation of how randomization works during training is conceptually sound but lacks mechanistic clarity. Adding a brief pseudocode snippet or a diagram showing the prompt tokenization + shuffling process would greatly improve reproducibility and reviewer confidence.

**Limitations & Broader Impact**
The limitations section is too brief for an ICLR submission. Key missing considerations:
1. **Open-Vocabulary / Large Label Spaces**: The method reserves 500 tokens. What happens at 1,000, 10,000, or dynamic label sets common in enterprise pipelines? The paper should discuss embedding matrix growth, logit computation overhead, and potential memory fragmentation.
2. **Out-of-Distribution Rejection**: Single-token argmax forces a choice among K classes. Real-world classifiers require abstention or confidence thresholds. The paper mentions this as future work but should at least report calibration curves (ECE, max confidence histograms) on the test sets to show whether the model's logits are meaningful for thresholding.
3. **Modality Compute Asymmetry**: For multimodal tasks, visual tokenization and cross-attention dominate prefill time. The latency savings from single-token generation are proportionally smaller when prefill dominates. The limitations should explicitly discuss this scaling behavior and when the approach is actually beneficial versus when standard constrained decoding suffices.

### Overall Assessment

This paper presents a straightforward but practically useful engineering framework for framing LLM classification as single-token generation via special control tokens, LoRA fine-tuning, and randomized prompt mapping. The empirical results demonstrate that decoder-style LLMs can achieve competitive accuracy while reducing autoregressive decoding overhead, which is valuable for latency-sensitive pipelines. However, in its current form, the paper falls short of the ICLR acceptance bar due to several critical empirical and methodological gaps: the latency claims rely on an unfair comparison between local vLLM inference and cloud API response times; the missing single-token constrained decoding baseline makes it impossible to isolate the algorithmic speedup; the large accuracy gap against GPT-4o on MIntRec 2.0 lacks controlled evaluation justification; and the reproducibility concerns around GPT-5 results undermine baseline credibility. Additionally, the novelty is incremental, positioning the work closer to applied systems engineering than foundational ML research. If the authors address the baseline fairness (same hardware, single-token constrained baseline), provide verifiable open-model comparisons, clarify the optimization dynamics of the randomized token mapping, and report generative capability retention, this work could be a solid contribution to efficient LLM deployment. As stands, it requires substantial empirical strengthening and claim calibration before meeting ICLR standards.

# Neutral Reviewer
## Balanced Review

### Summary
This paper introduces LaaC, a framework that reformulates text and multimodal classification as deterministic single-token constrained generation by augmenting the tokenizer with reserved control labels and applying parameter-efficient fine-tuning (LoRA). By randomizing label-to-token mappings during training, the method ensures the model relies on contextual prompts rather than memorization, enabling predictable O(1) inference latency while preserving cross-domain generalization and generative capabilities. Empirical evaluations show that LaaC-adapted models outperform proprietary API models (GPT-4o/GPT-5) and encoder baselines in accuracy and tail latency across intent recognition, sentiment analysis, and topic classification benchmarks.

### Strengths
1. **Targeted Engineering Contribution with Clear Practical Impact:** The paper directly addresses a well-known deployment bottleneck: the latency variance and token-budget inefficiency of autoregressive LLM classifiers. Evidence in Table 1 demonstrates consistent sub-second P95 latency (≤0.37s) with superior accuracy on MIntRec 2.0, and Appendix A.7 confirms these efficiency gains persist under high-throughput batching (up to BS=64).
2. **Thoughtful Design to Prevent Memorization:** The randomized label-to-control-token shuffling during training (Section 3.3, Table 4) is a robust mechanism that forces the model to ground predictions in prompt descriptions rather than static token identities. The stability across 10 random permutations (mean accuracy 44.35% ± 1.29 on shuffled mappings) empirically validates this design choice.
3. **Comprehensive Benchmarking and Baseline Isolation:** The evaluation cleanly separates prompt-based, constrained-decoding, encoder-based (MAG-BERT/MulT), and proprietary API baselines. Appendix A.4 provides a crucial ablation showing that even GPT-4o with constrained digit decoding frequently outputs multi-token strings, highlighting why LaaC's structural constraint to a single special token is necessary for deterministic latency.

### Weaknesses
1. **Conflated Latency Comparison (Local Compute vs. API Call):** The latency gains are measured by comparing local vLLM inference for LaaC models against external API calls for GPT-4o/GPT-5. API latency inherently includes network round-trips, server-side queuing, and rate-limiting overhead, which inflates the reported speedup. This makes it difficult to isolate the true algorithmic/compute efficiency gain of single-token decoding.
2. **Statistically Fragile Evaluation on Text Benchmarks:** Section 4.1 states that text-only benchmarks are evaluated on "200 randomly sampled test examples each." Reporting median and tail latency (P50/P95) on a sample size of 200 yields high variance and unreliable percentile estimates. Standard benchmark splits or confidence intervals are necessary to substantiate the latency claims.
3. **Misleading Terminology and Unexplained Multilingual Degradation:** The paper repeatedly claims "zero-shot generalization" (Section 4.5.2, Table 2), yet the model is explicitly fine-tuned on a 28k mixed-domain corpus. This is more accurately "cross-domain transfer after multi-task PEFT." Furthermore, Table 8 reveals that fine-tuning degrades multilingual accuracy on MTOP (68.73% LaaC vs. 78.74% base model), indicating potential catastrophic forgetting or token grounding issues that are noted but left unanalyzed.

### Novelty & Significance
**Novelty:** Moderate to High. Using single special tokens for classification conceptually aligns with pattern-exploiting pre-training (e.g., PET) and recent LLM classification heads, but the paper's systematic synthesis of randomized token-label assignment, decoder-style VLM adaptation, and explicit latency predictability guarantees provides a fresh, deployment-oriented perspective. **Clarity:** High. The methodology, loss formulation, and inference rules are precisely defined, and the appendices supply thorough implementation details without obscuring the main narrative. **Reproducibility:** High. The authors disclose code availability, LoRA hyperparameters, prompt templates, masking strategies, and evaluation protocols. Minor gaps include exact training compute budgets and precise dataset sampling ratios. **Significance:** High. For ICLR's focus on scalable, efficient AI systems, LaaC offers a practical, well-validated blueprint for deploying decoder VLMs/LLMs in latency-critical pipelines without sacrificing the architectural unity and cross-task flexibility that make LLMs attractive.

### Suggestions for Improvement
1. **Decouple Architecture Efficiency from Network Overhead:** Add a locally served open-weight baseline (e.g., vLLM-hosted Llama-3 or Qwen2.5) to the latency table, or explicitly separate "model compute latency" from "API endpoint latency" in the analysis to fairly quantify the single-token decoding benefit.
2. **Strengthen Statistical Rigor for Latency Claims:** Evaluate text benchmarks on full standard test splits, or if compute-limited, report bootstrapped confidence intervals (e.g., 95% CI) for P50/P95 metrics rather than point estimates on 200 samples.
3. **Correct Terminology and Analyze Cross-Lingual Forgetting:** Replace "zero-shot generalization" with "cross-domain generalization" or "zero-shot on held-out label spaces." Investigate the multilingual accuracy drop: analyze whether it stems from English-only LoRA updates, tokenizer subword conflicts for control tokens, or prompt formulation, and propose a mitigation strategy (e.g., language-balanced mixing during PEFT).
4. **Include Standard Classification Head Baselines:** Compare against modern approaches that attach lightweight linear classifiers to frozen LLM/VLM hidden states (e.g., last-token probing or recent PEFT classification adapters). This would clarify whether the single-token generation overhead is truly necessary or if it introduces unnecessary compute compared to embedding projection.

# Spark Finder Review
## How to Improve This Paper

### Missing Experiments (top 3-5 only)
1. **Add data contamination audits and deduplication metrics for the 28k training corpus because** the inclusion of FollowBench, Unnatural Instructions, and AgentGym synthetic data likely distributes patterns heavily overlapping with SST-2, DBpedia, and AG News, making the claimed zero-shot generalization and accuracy margins against encoders unreliable.
2. **Run evaluations across ≥3 random seeds and report mean ± standard deviation because** single-run accuracy margins over baselines like LM-BFF or Mistral-Base could easily vanish within standard fine-tuning variance, violating ICLR's statistical rigor standards.
3. **Benchmark against local library-level constrained decoding (e.g., Outlines, Guidance, or LMQL) instead of relying solely on API latency comparisons because** API call times are confounded by network routing and queueing; without a strict local constrained-decoding baseline, the O(1) latency advantage may be a deployment artifact rather than an algorithmic improvement.
4. **Evaluate label spaces with K=100, 200, and 500 to verify scalability because** the paper reserves 500 tokens and claims support for large vocabularies but only tests up to K=30, leaving logit competition, gradient interference, and true constant-time scaling unverified.

### Deeper Analysis Needed (top 3-5 only)
1. **Investigate the ~20% accuracy collapse on MIntRec 2.0 under randomized token permutations (Table A.6) because** this drop directly contradicts the text's claim that the model relies purely on prompt descriptions rather than token ID memorization, undermining the core "generality" contribution.
2. **Detail and ablate the initialization strategy for the new control token embeddings because** PEFT convergence with newly added vocabulary is highly sensitive to initialization (e.g., averaging existing token embeddings vs. random), and unreported initialization choices could bottleneck gradients and artificially limit final accuracy.
3. **Analyze the latency decomposition between vision backbone encoding and the LLM forward pass because** Table A.5 shows the Swin Transformer dominates multimodal pipeline cost; without separating feature extraction from generation, the paper cannot definitively attribute efficiency gains to the single-token classification design itself.
4. **Report expected calibration error (ECE) or reliability diagrams because** the single-token argmax yields a softmax probability that practitioners will rely on for thresholding and risk management; uncalibrated probabilities render the model unsafe for real-world deployment, which the paper heavily emphasizes.

### Visualizations & Case Studies
1. **Plot confusion matrices for MIntRec 2.0 and DBpedia to expose whether errors cluster among semantically related classes or specific control token indices, which would definitively reveal harmful token-index bias versus genuine classification uncertainty.**
2. **Provide t-SNE/PCA projections of the learned special token embeddings overlaid with the frozen class description embeddings to verify that the model grounded atomic tokens in semantic space rather than relying on arbitrary positional weights.**
3. **Show cumulative distribution functions (CDFs) of response latency across 1,000+ requests to replace summary statistics, as latency claims for deterministic classifiers must demonstrate tight distributional control and tail behavior under realistic noise.**

### Obvious Next Steps
1. **Implement and benchmark an explicit "abstain" or out-of-distribution control token because** the authors claim deployment readiness for latency-critical applications while acknowledging the need for rejection mechanisms; a classifier without an OOD handle is not viable for the stated target use cases.
2. **Report training FLOPs, wall-clock hours, and peak memory usage compared to RoBERTa-base and MAG-BERT baselines because** ICLR reviewers require proof that LLM-based classification does not incur prohibitive fine-tuning costs that negate the operational latency benefits.
3. **Conduct a controlled ablation comparing randomized label assignment versus fixed token-label mapping during training because** proving that randomization is essential (or merely a harmless regularizer) would strengthen the theoretical justification for the method's zero-shot adaptability claims.

# Final Consolidated Review
## Summary
This paper introduces LaaC, a framework that reformulates text and multimodal classification as deterministic single-token generation by augmenting the tokenizer with reserved control tokens, applying LoRA fine-tuning, and randomizing label-to-token mappings during training. The approach aims to eliminate multi-token decoding overhead, yielding predictable, sub-second latency while preserving the architectural flexibility of decoder-style LLMs. Empirical results across intent recognition, sentiment, and topic classification benchmarks demonstrate competitive accuracy and substantial latency improvements over proprietary API models and strong encoder baselines.

## Strengths
- **Targeted, deployment-relevant engineering with clear empirical impact:** The paper directly tackles the latency variance and token-budget inefficiency inherent in autoregressive LLM classification. Table 1 and Appendix A.7 demonstrate consistent sub-second tail latency (≤0.37s P95) that scales robustly to high-throughput serving (batch size 64), addressing a genuine bottleneck in production pipelines.
- **Clever design to enforce prompt grounding:** The randomized label-to-control-token shuffling during training prevents static token memorization and forces the model to ground predictions in the semantic descriptions provided in the context. Empirical validation across 10 random permutation runs (Table 4, σ=1.29%) confirms stable, description-driven inference rather than brittle token memorization.
- **Rigorous isolation of constrained-decoding limitations:** Appendix A.4 provides a highly instructive baseline showing that even when GPT-4o is explicitly prompted to output only integer class indices, it frequently generates multi-token outputs or explanatory text (16.67% failure rate). This effectively justifies why LaaC's structural token augmentation is necessary for truly deterministic single-step decoding.

## Weaknesses
- **Missing fair constrained-decoding baseline for algorithmic speedup:** The primary latency comparison pits locally hosted LaaC models (vLLM) against cloud API endpoints (GPT-4o/5), conflating algorithmic generation efficiency with network round-trips, server-side queuing, and rate-limiting overhead. Crucially, the paper does not evaluate the *same base open models* constrained to `max_new_tokens=1` with standard vocabulary outputs. Without this baseline, it is impossible to isolate whether the latency gains stem from the special-token architecture or simply from truncating generation length.
- **Statistically fragile evaluation on text benchmarks:** Section 4.1 explicitly states that text benchmarks are evaluated on "200 randomly sampled test examples." Reporting P50/P95 latency percentiles on such a small sample yields high variance and unreliable tail estimates. This sample size is insufficient to robustly substantiate claims of consistent "order of magnitude" latency reduction or marginal accuracy differences over baselines like LM-BFF.
- **Substantial accuracy drop under label permutation and imprecise terminology:** While training randomization aids grounding, Table A.6 reveals an ~18-point accuracy drop (62.7% → 44.35%) when label-token mappings are permuted at inference on MIntRec 2.0. This contradicts the claim that the model relies *purely* on prompt descriptions, indicating a partial dependence on fixed token-class priors learned during training. Furthermore, the paper repeatedly uses "zero-shot classification" to describe cross-domain transfer after multi-task PEFT, which misaligns with standard terminology and overstates the model's generalization capabilities.
- **Unverified retention of generative capabilities:** The introduction and conclusion repeatedly assert that LaaC "preserves the generative flexibility" of decoder LLMs, yet no post-fine-tuning evaluations on standard instruction-following, reasoning, or language modeling benchmarks are provided. Given that LoRA weights and new token embeddings are updated on a classification-heavy corpus, empirical verification is required to rule out catastrophic forgetting or degradation of general generative performance.

## Nice-to-Haves
- Compare LaaC against a lightweight linear probing baseline attached to the frozen decoder's final hidden state to clarify whether the generative single-token framing offers a meaningful accuracy/latency trade-off over standard feature-extraction classification.
- Report calibration metrics (e.g., Expected Calibration Error or reliability diagrams), as deterministic argmax classifiers used in latency-critical deployments typically require thresholding or abstention mechanisms for robust out-of-distribution handling.
- Decouple visual encoding latency from LLM generation latency in the multimodal ablation to precisely quantify how much of the end-to-end speedup originates from the single-token decoding step versus the base model architecture.

## Novel Insights
The paper successfully reframes LLM classification from an open-ended prompt-following task into a structured vocabulary prediction problem. By treating class labels as atomic, non-decomposable control tokens and decoupling their semantic identity from their positional encoding through training-time randomization, LaaC bridges the long-standing divide between the predictable, low-latency inference of encoder classifiers and the cross-modal, instruction-flexible nature of modern decoders. This structural shift demonstrates that with careful token vocabulary design and targeted PEFT, generative models can be forced into deterministic, constant-step regimes without sacrificing their broader architectural utility.

## Suggestions
- Add a controlled local baseline: evaluate the same open-weight models (Gemma-3/Mistral-3) via vLLM with standard vocabulary restricted to valid label strings and `max_new_tokens=1`. This will isolate the true algorithmic benefit of the special-token framework.
- Expand text benchmark evaluations to full standard test splits or a minimum of 1,000+ samples, and report confidence intervals for latency percentiles to meet ICLR's statistical rigor standards.
- Replace "zero-shot" terminology with "unseen label-space adaptation" or "cross-domain transfer," and add a discussion or ablation clarifying why inference-time permutation causes the ~18% accuracy drop despite training randomization.
- Include a post-fine-tuning evaluation on a standard instruction-following or reasoning subset (e.g., IFEval or MMLU) to empirically substantiate the claim that generative capabilities remain intact.
