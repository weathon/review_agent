# Harsh Critic Review
## Section-by-Section Critical Review

### Title & Abstract
- The title is catchy but overstates the result. "Bayes Always Wins the Lottery" implies a deterministic or guaranteed victory across regimes, which the empirical results and theoretical reality do not support. ICLR reviewers expect claims to be tightly constrained to what is demonstrated.
- The abstract outlines the problem, method (HMC/BNN on LTH masks), and results. However, it contains an unsupported claim: "HMC... has proven guarantees of convergence to the true posterior distribution, which will allow for achieving the same performance on a pruning mask." While MCMC has asymptotic convergence guarantees in ideal settings, deep neural network posteriors are highly multimodal and non-log-concave. In practice, HMC frequently fails to explore all relevant modes or mixes slowly; these practical limitations are glossed over in favor of theoretical idealism.
- There is also an internal inconsistency: the abstract claims a "3% over the original lottery-ticket initialization" improvement, while Section 5 and the Conclusion claim up to 4% or even 10% depending on the metric/baseline. The abstract should report the most representative and verifiable figures.

### Introduction & Motivation
- The motivation is clear: LTH masks are highly sensitive to their seed initialization, limiting their interpretability and reuse. The goal to decouple mask performance from the original seed is well-motivated.
- The gap in prior work is only partially established. The introduction implies that gradient descent's sensitivity to initialization is the primary bottleneck and that a "more general approach" (Bayesian treatment) solves it. However, it fails to contrast the proposed approach against strong deterministic alternatives like aggressive learning rate warmup, multiple random restarts, or advanced optimizers (e.g., AdamW, SGD with cosine annealing) that already mitigate initialization sensitivity in pruned networks. Without this comparison, the necessity of switching to Bayesian inference is not rigorously justified.
- The introduction over-claims in framing the contribution. Phrases like "ensure the optimal initialization distribution is found" mischaracterize BNN training. The goal of a BNN is to approximate the posterior over weights conditioned on data and architecture, not to find an "optimal initialization." An initialization is a prior; the posterior is what the method targets. This conceptual conflation persists throughout the early sections and should be corrected.

### Method / Approach
- The method is partially reproducible but suffers from logical and conceptual gaps. Section 3.2 is particularly confusing. The authors state: "Once the posterior is found... we can then use these as the initialization distribution, and not do any backpropagation based gradient descent training." This contradicts standard practice and even the authors' own use of SVI (which explicitly uses backpropagation to optimize variational parameters). The authors likely mean they sample from the posterior and use the posterior mean/mode as final point estimates without a subsequent deterministic fine-tuning phase. This needs to be explicitly clarified. Otherwise, the methodology reads as if training stops arbitrarily.
- The application of the pruning mask during HMC/SVI is not formally described. Are masked weights fixed to exactly zero (and excluded from sampling/updating), or are they still assigned a distribution but multiplied by zero during forward passes? This affects the dimensionality of the posterior and the efficiency of the sampler. If they are excluded, the method is effectively training a smaller deterministic network with Bayesian regularization, which should be stated clearly.
- The theoretical claims regarding convergence require tempering. While HMC has geometric ergodicity under strict conditions (e.g., Lipschitz gradients, tail bounds), these are violated in deep networks. The paper asserts HMC will "surely converge to a true posterior" and therefore "give optimal weights regardless of the initial distribution." For ICLR, this theoretical overstatement is a red flag. The authors must discuss multi-modality, the risk of chain collapse, and how they verify convergence in practice (e.g., Gelman-Rubin, effective sample size). Currently, they rely on fixed sample counts (1400–2800), which may be insufficient for even the small networks tested, as evidenced by the high variance reported on LeNet5 in Section 5.

### Experiments & Results
- The experiments only partially test the paper's claims. The evaluation is restricted to MNIST and CIFAR-10 with LeNet variants (and ResNet-18 only for SVI). For ICLR, which expects strong empirical grounding, this scope is insufficient to support broad claims about a "generalized framework for training lottery ticket pruned networks."
- Critical baseline is missing: The paper compares HMC/SVI against Lottery Initialization, Random Initializations, and deterministic networks trained for 50/100 epochs. It lacks a properly tuned deterministic baseline trained *longer* or with modern LR schedules on the same masks. The claim that "further training yielded no benefit" for deterministic networks (Section 4) is unsupported without loss curves or early stopping validation. Given LTH's sensitivity to optimization dynamics, it is possible that extended training or optimizer tuning narrows or eliminates the gap between Bayesian and deterministic training on masks.
- Statistical rigor is lacking. Table 1 reports means but omits uncertainty for HMC and SVI methods. Figures 2 and 3 lack error bands. Given the reported variance (up to 5% accuracy drop across HMC runs on LeNet5 in Section 5), the claim that HMC "consistently outperforms" baselines needs statistical validation (e.g., paired t-tests or confidence intervals).
- The SVI hyperparameter tuning introduces unquantified overhead. Section 4 notes that a population-based training method was used to find the optimal KL-divergence normalization factor. While necessary for BNNs to scale, this tuning process is itself computationally expensive and algorithm-dependent. The paper does not ablate the sensitivity of results to this scalar, making it difficult to assess if the performance gains over baselines are inherent to the Bayesian formulation or a byproduct of manual tuning.
- Absolute performance on harder masks is low. On CIFAR-10, LeNet300-100 achieves ~53–56% accuracy. While the relative improvement over baselines holds, the low ceiling suggests the mask or model family might be severely capacity-constrained. The discussion should address whether Bayesian advantages persist when the unpruned/full network is highly overparameterized versus when the mask is near the information bottleneck.

### Writing & Clarity
- The core methodological narrative in Section 3.2 is obscured by imprecise terminology. Equating "initialization" with "prior" and implying that HMC finds an "optimal initialization" rather than the posterior over weights will confuse readers familiar with Bayesian deep learning. The text should be revised to consistently distinguish between: (1) the seed/initial point for the sampler, (2) the prior distribution, and (3) the posterior approximation.
- The transition between HMC and SVI is abrupt. While Section 3.3 describes SVI, it does not clarify how the pruning mask is handled within the variational objective or how the KL normalization interacts with the sparse structure. Are the KL terms computed only for unmasked weights?
- Figure and table references are functional, but Table 1 includes "NA" for HMC on ResNet-18 without clarifying that this was a hardware constraint until later sections. Clarity would benefit from integrating the limitation or explicitly noting the architectural split in the table caption.

### Limitations & Broader Impact
- The authors acknowledge compute/dataset limitations and the lack of convergence guarantees for SVI. This is honest and appropriate.
- A fundamental limitation missed is the **posterior mode collapse in BNNs**. HMC in non-convex, high-dimensional spaces often explores a single local mode rather than the full multi-modal posterior. Given the 5% variance observed on LeNet5, it is plausible the chains are stuck in different local optima rather than converging to a single global posterior. The paper should discuss whether the performance gains come from Bayesian averaging or simply finding a different, favorable local mode that deterministic gradient descent missed. Techniques like temperature scaling or parallel tempering could be mentioned as necessary future work to address exploration.
- Broader impact/energy considerations are omitted. HMC is computationally intensive (Section 6.2 notes "several days" vs 30 minutes for SGD). Given current emphasis on sustainable AI, the trade-off between marginal accuracy gains and massive compute increase should be explicitly discussed as a limitation.

### Overall Assessment
This paper proposes an interesting idea: using Bayesian inference to train Lottery Ticket Hypothesis masks independently of their seed initialization. While the concept is valid and the initial results are suggestive, the paper currently falls short of ICLR standards due to significant theoretical overclaims, methodological ambiguities, and narrow empirical validation. The assertion that HMC guarantees convergence to the optimal posterior ignores the well-known challenges of multi-modality and poor mixing in deep Bayesian neural networks. Section 3.2 confuses initialization, priors, and training phases, which obscures the actual algorithm and raises reproducibility concerns. Experimentally, the lack of properly tuned deterministic baselines, missing error bars, and restriction to small models make it difficult to verify that the observed gains are inherent to the Bayesian framework rather than optimization artifacts or hyperparameter tuning. To meet the review bar, the authors must: (1) correct theoretical claims regarding MCMC convergence in deep nets and explicitly discuss mode-exploration limitations; (2) clarify the exact training pipeline for masked weights, removing the claim of "no backpropagation"; (3) add robust deterministic baselines with extended training/optimization schedules; (4) report statistical significance and uncertainty for all methods; and (5) expand experiments to at least one medium-scale benchmark to support generalizability claims.

# Neutral Reviewer
## Balanced Review

### Summary
This paper investigates whether Bayesian Neural Networks (BNNs) can be trained on pruning masks generated via the Lottery Ticket Hypothesis (LTH) to achieve high accuracy without relying on the specific original weights used during mask discovery. By employing Hamiltonian Monte Carlo (HMC) and Stochastic Variational Inference (SVI), the authors demonstrate that BNN training can match or exceed the performance of LTH-initialized deterministic networks. The work highlights a theoretical advantage of Bayesian training (initialization independence via posterior convergence) and compares the practical trade-offs between HMC's asymptotic guarantees and SVI's computational efficiency.

### Strengths
1. **Clear Motivation and Conceptual Framing:** The paper directly addresses a well-known practical bottleneck of LTH (initialization dependency) and proposes a principled Bayesian solution. Treating the initialization as a prior and searching for a posterior effectively decouples mask utility from weight starting points.
2. **Honest and Useful Algorithmic Comparison:** The contrast between HMC (theoretically guaranteed convergence but computationally prohibitive) and SVI (faster, scalable, but lacking strict convergence guarantees) provides practical insight. The successful scaling of SVI to ResNet-18 on CIFAR-10, outperforming LTH initialization, is a strong empirical result that highlights the viability of variational approaches for pruned BNNs.
3. **Transparent Reporting of Limitations:** The authors explicitly acknowledge computational bottlenecks (HMC memory constraints, 2-day training times vs. minutes for SGD) and practical non-convergence (LeNet-5 variance dipping 5% across runs). This candid discussion aligns well with ICLR's emphasis on realistic method evaluation.

### Weaknesses
1. **Limited Empirical Scope and Outdated Benchmarks:** Experiments are confined to MNIST/CIFAR-10 and legacy architectures (LeNet-300-100, LeNet-5). ICLR typically expects validation on modern, deeper architectures and more complex benchmarks to prove practical relevance. While ResNet-18 is tested, 200 epochs is generally insufficient for thorough BNN training on CIFAR, and no larger-scale evaluation is provided.
2. **Theoretical Claims vs. Practical Reality:** The paper heavily emphasizes HMC's "convergence guarantees" as a theoretical foundation. However, asymptotic convergence to the true posterior assumes infinite samples and stationary dynamics. In high-dimensional, non-convex neural network landscapes, this is practically unattainable with finite compute, a fact the authors themselves observe via LeNet-5 instability. The claim is theoretically true but misleading in the context of deep learning practice.
3. **Insufficient Statistical Rigor and Reproducibility Details:** Reported accuracy gains (0.5%–4%) lack statistical validation (e.g., confidence intervals from ≥5 seeds, hypothesis testing). Additionally, key implementation details are missing: the exact iterative pruning schedule (percentage removed per iteration, retraining epochs before mask update), exact prior hyperparameters per layer, the procedure for extracting deterministic weights from the posterior (mean, mode, or ensemble?), and the specific KL-weight normalization protocol for SVI.

### Novelty & Significance
**Novelty:** Moderate. Applying BNNs to pruning masks is not entirely new, but systematically framing LTH as an initialization-sensitivity problem and using MCMC to decouple mask evaluation from specific weights is a fresh, well-motivated perspective. The comparative study of HMC vs. SVI for this specific task adds value.
**Significance:** Moderate to Low for broad impact. While conceptually sound, the work does not overcome the fundamental scalability barriers of Bayesian deep learning. HMC remains impractical for modern models, and SVI's performance relies heavily on manual hyperparameter tuning (KL vs. CE weighting). The observed performance gains over standard methods are marginal and do not yet justify the added complexity in large-scale settings.
**Clarity & Reproducibility:** Clarity is generally good, though the theoretical bridge from BNN posterior to optimal deterministic weight recovery is loosely argued. Reproducibility is hindered by omitted hyperparameter details, insufficient experimental seeds reported, and lack of code reference.

### Suggestions for Improvement
1. **Strengthen Empirical Rigor:** Expand experiments to at least one contemporary architecture/benchmark (e.g., ResNet-50 or a modest Vision Transformer on CIFAR-100/ImageNet subsets). Report results with mean ± standard deviation over multiple random seeds (≥5) and include statistical significance testing to substantiate the claimed 0.5%–4% improvements.
2. **Reframe Theoretical Claims Practically:** Instead of leaning solely on asymptotic HMC convergence, explicitly discuss the *effective* convergence in practice. Provide a theoretical or empirical analysis of why BNNs mitigate initialization sensitivity (e.g., loss landscape smoothing, implicit regularization from the prior/posterior variance). Clearly state how final predictions are made (posterior mean vs. predictive averaging vs. single-sample forward pass).
3. **Improve Reproducibility and Experimental Details:** Provide exact pruning schedules (mask update frequency, sparsity increments), prior distributions and their scaling parameters, full SVI hyperparameter search ranges, and hardware specifications. If possible, release experimental code and pruning masks to enable independent verification.
4. **Address SVI Stability and Baselines:** Since SVI requires careful KL/CE balancing to work well, include this tuning cost in the efficiency comparison against deterministic methods. Additionally, compare against strong modern pruning baselines (e.g., one-shot magnitude pruning with learning rate warmup, or gradient-based salience pruning) to ensure LTH remains the strongest comparison.

# Spark Finder Review
## How to Improve This Paper

### Missing Experiments (top 3-5 only)
1. **Standard HMC Convergence Diagnostics**: Report $\hat{R}$ (Gelman-Rubin) statistics, Effective Sample Size (ESS), and chain trace plots for all experiments, because the paper's core claim of leveraging HMC's "convergence guarantees" is fundamentally undermined by the admitted 5% run-to-run variance and lack of diagnostic proof that the chains actually reached the posterior.
2. **Statistically Robust Seed & Mask Aggregation**: Present mean and standard deviation across at least 5-10 independent seeds per pruning mask, because the Lottery Ticket Hypothesis is notoriously seed-dependent and reporting only single-run averages or "best-case" improvements hides the true variance required to trust the claim of consistent superiority.
3. **Strong Non-Bayesian Retraining Baselines**: Add comparisons against modern mask-retraining protocols (e.g., aggressive learning rate warmups, cosine annealing, or large LR restarts), because the observed gains could simply stem from HMC exploring the landscape longer rather than an inherent Bayesian advantage over well-tuned SGD.
4. **Prior Sensitivity Table**: Systematically report performance for every prior tested (Normal, Uniform, Glorot-modified) instead of selecting the best-performing prior, because the claim of removing "initialization dependency" is vacuous if the method critically relies on correctly guessing a prior distribution that aligns with the optimal weight region.

### Deeper Analysis Needed (top 3-5 only)
1. **Computational Efficiency Trade-off Analysis**: Quantify the gradient update or FLOP budget required for HMC to match SGD, because without showing that the accuracy gains aren't solely a function of massively higher compute, the paper fails to prove methodological superiority.
2. **Predictive Uncertainty & Calibration Metrics**: Report Expected Calibration Error (ECE), Brier Scores, or Negative Log-Likelihood (NLL), because the primary justification for using Bayesian Neural Networks is proper uncertainty quantification, and high accuracy alone does not prove the posterior inference is functioning correctly or providing the claimed theoretical benefits.
3. **SVI KL-Weight Sensitivity Analysis**: Provide a sensitivity curve showing how the KL divergence scaling constant affects accuracy, because the authors state SVI success required PBT optimization of this weight, implying the results are fragile hyperparameter artifacts rather than robust properties of variational inference.
4. **Loss Landscape Geometry Comparison**: Analyze the local curvature (e.g., Hessian eigenvalues or gradient variance) of the final solutions found by HMC versus SGD, because the authors speculate that Bayesian averaging "smooths" the loss landscape but offer zero empirical evidence for this mechanism.

### Visualizations & Case Studies
1. **Posterior Variance vs. Mask Overlay**: Visualize the learned posterior variance of weights that were kept versus those forced to zero by the mask, because if the posterior for "pruned" slots is not sharply constrained, it reveals a fundamental conflict between the deterministic mask and the Bayesian weight distributions.
2. **Training Loss/Cost Curves on Shared Axis**: Plot training loss and validation accuracy against equivalent compute budgets for HMC, SVI, and SGD, because this is the only way to immediately distinguish whether the method finds better minima or merely spends significantly more resources searching.
3. **Uncertainty on Misclassified Hard Examples**: Show predictive entropy or variance for samples where HMC significantly outperforms SGD, to reveal if the Bayesian method genuinely captures epistemic uncertainty on hard cases (supporting the theoretical claim) or is simply reducing variance noise.

### Obvious Next Steps
1. **Mini-Batch/Stochastic HMC Implementation**: Implement SG-HMC or a scalable alternative and validate it on ImageNet-scale or modern transformer architectures, because full-batch NUTS is restricted to toy models ($<$1M params), rendering the "convergence guarantee" claim practically irrelevant for the real-world scale problems cited in the motivation.
2. **Comparison to Gradient-Free/Black-Box Mask Optimizers**: Benchmark HMC against evolutionary strategies or random search on the same masks, because the authors cite similar work (Lange & Sprekeler, 2023) but do not compare against them, leaving unclear whether expensive HMC sampling offers any efficiency advantage over simpler gradient-free optimization.
3. **Generalization to Modern One-Shot Pruning Masks**: Test the framework on masks generated by SNIP, GraSP, or magnitude pruning at initialization, to demonstrate that the "always wins" property extends beyond specific Iterative Magnitude Pruning (IMP) trajectories and represents a robust retraining framework rather than a niche fix for LTH.

# Final Consolidated Review
## Summary
This paper investigates whether Bayesian Neural Networks (BNNs) trained via Hamiltonian Monte Carlo (HMC) and Stochastic Variational Inference (SVI) can effectively learn on Lottery Ticket Hypothesis (LTH)-derived pruning masks without requiring the original initialization. The authors demonstrate on small architectures that BNN training can match or exceed the accuracy of LTH-initialized deterministic networks, framing the approach as a theoretically grounded method to decouple mask performance from seed dependency.

## Strengths
- **Clear, well-targeted motivation:** The work directly addresses a practical bottleneck in sparse training—the reliance on specific seed initializations to "win the lottery"—and frames it through a principled Bayesian lens that treats initialization sensitivity as an optimization landscape problem.
- **Transparent empirical trade-off analysis:** The honest comparison between HMC’s asymptotic theoretical properties and SVI’s practical scalability, alongside explicit acknowledgment of memory constraints, training time disparities (days vs. minutes), and observed HMC chain variance, provides a grounded baseline for evaluating Bayesian sparse training.

## Weaknesses
- **Empirical scope and baselines are insufficient for ICLR standards:** The evaluation is confined to legacy architectures (LeNet variants) and small datasets (MNIST/CIFAR-10). More critically, the paper lacks strong non-Bayesian retraining baselines with modern optimization dynamics (e.g., aggressive LR warmups, cosine schedules, or extended training). The unsupported claim that "further training yielded no benefit" for deterministic networks is a major gap; without loss curves or properly tuned baselines, it is impossible to attribute observed gains to the Bayesian formulation rather than superior exploration or compute budgets.
- **Theoretical overclaims contradict practical convergence evidence:** The paper asserts HMC guarantees convergence to the "optimal initialization distribution" and true posterior regardless of starting point. In high-dimensional, non-convex deep network landscapes, HMC’s theoretical ergodicity requires infinite time and is frequently obstructed by multi-modality and poor mixing. The authors themselves report up to 5% accuracy variance across HMC runs on LeNet-5 and use fixed sample counts (1400–2800) without reporting Gelman-Rubin ($\hat{R}$) statistics or Effective Sample Size (ESS). This directly undermines the central premise that convergence guarantees translate to reliable, initialization-independent performance in practice.
- **Lack of statistical rigor and methodological clarity:** Results are presented without multi-seed aggregation, confidence intervals, or statistical significance testing, making the claimed 0.5%–4% improvements indistinguishable from noise. Additionally, key implementation details are omitted: how masked weights are handled during sampling (fixed to zero vs. excluded from the posterior), how final predictions are generated (posterior mean, mode, or predictive averaging), and the full protocol for SVI’s KL-weight tuning via population-based training. Without these, the work is not reproducible, and the claimed robustness remains unverified.

## Nice-to-Haves
- Report calibration and uncertainty metrics (e.g., ECE, Brier score, posterior predictive entropy) to substantiate the claim that Bayesian training provides meaningful benefits beyond pointwise accuracy on sparse subnetworks.
- Provide a systematic prior sensitivity analysis across tested distributions to empirically verify the claimed initialization independence.
- Include compute-normalized comparisons (FLOPs or wall-clock time vs. accuracy) to fairly benchmark Bayesian methods against heavily optimized deterministic retraining.

## Novel Insights
The paper’s most valuable conceptual contribution is reframing the Lottery Ticket Hypothesis as an initialization-sensitivity problem rather than a purely architectural one, demonstrating that treating weights as distributions can decouple mask utility from specific starting points. The empirical observation that deterministic iterative magnitude pruning masks transfer surprisingly well to BNNs—and that SVI’s implicit loss smoothing via weight averaging allows it to rival or surpass SGD and LTH initialization despite lacking strict convergence guarantees—suggests that Bayesian training can act as a robust, initialization-agnostic retraining paradigm for highly sparse subnetworks, potentially opening new avenues for mask analysis that were previously confounded by optimization path dependency.

## Potentially Missed Related Work
- **Scalable SG-MCMC for BNNs:** Betancourt (2015) and Chen et al. (2014) on Stochastic Gradient HMC should be discussed more centrally as necessary bridges to overcome the full-batch NUTS memory constraints that currently restrict HMC to toy-scale models.
- **Optimization geometry of sparse networks:** Recent work on the loss landscape smoothness of pruned architectures (e.g., studies on flat minima and gradient flow in sparse regimes) could help explain why Bayesian averaging outperforms point-estimation SGD on fixed masks.

## Suggestions
1. **Validate empirical claims statistically:** Run all methods across ≥5 independent seeds, report mean ± standard deviation with error bands, and include paired statistical tests to verify that observed improvements over LTH/random baselines are significant.
2. **Add convergence diagnostics & clarify methodology:** Report $\hat{R}$ and ESS for HMC chains to confirm mixing. Explicitly detail how the pruning mask is applied during sampling, specify the exact prediction rule (posterior mean vs. ensemble), and provide full SVI hyperparameter search ranges and tuning costs.
3. **Include properly tuned deterministic baselines:** Compare against SGD/AdamW with modern schedules (linear warmup + cosine decay, LR restarts, and extended epochs) to isolate whether Bayesian training provides genuine representational advantages or merely benefits from longer/more diverse optimization trajectories.
4. **Temper theoretical language:** Remove claims of guaranteed convergence to "optimal" distributions. Explicitly state that asymptotic MCMC guarantees do not hold in finite-time, multi-modal deep learning settings, and frame results as empirical demonstrations of improved optimization robustness rather than theoretical certainties.
