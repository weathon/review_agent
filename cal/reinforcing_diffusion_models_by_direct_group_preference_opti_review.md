# Harsh Critic Review
## Section-by-Section Critical Review

### Title & Abstract
The title accurately reflects the core contribution: adapting group-level preference optimization to diffusion models without relying on stochastic policies. The abstract clearly states the motivation (policy gradient mismatch in diffusion), the key insight (group relative information is the active ingredient), and the claimed benefits (efficiency, convergence speed, ODE compatibility). The empirical claims (e.g., 63% → 97% on GenEval, ~20–30× speedup) are bold and set a high bar for verification. No major issues, but the abstract could briefly note that the method relies on a surrogate objective (derived via Jensen’s inequality), which is important context for reviewers assessing theoretical tightness.

### 1. Introduction & Motivation
The introduction effectively frames the gap between RLHF for LLMs and diffusion models. The critique of forced SDE rollouts for GRPO (inefficiency, weak exploration signal, trajectory-level cost) is well-reasoned and aligns with known practical bottlenecks. However, the claim that *"GRPO’s practical success stems less from its policy-gradient formulation, and more from its ability to utilize fine-grained relative preference information within groups"* slightly oversimplifies the role of policy gradients. Policy gradients provide theoretically grounded exploration and natural gradient updates in high-dimensional continuous spaces; dismissing them as secondary risks underplaying why gradient estimation is hard in continuous action/generation spaces. Clarifying that DGPO trades off explicit likelihood-ratio gradient estimation for a direct preference surrogate would strengthen the motivation. Also, the introduction should briefly acknowledge that policy-advantage methods (e.g., DDS, DPOC) have already reduced reliance on full trajectory training, positioning DGPO more precisely.

### 2. Preliminaries
Standard and well-structured. The transition from RLHF → GRPO → DPO sets up the technical problem cleanly. One minor point: the statement *"unlike LLMs, which directly output distribution, the stochasticity in DM’s policy comes from model-agnostic Gaussian noise"* conflates sampling noise with policy stochasticity. In continuous diffusion, the "policy" is typically defined by the reverse ODE/SDE transition density, and the exploration comes from injected noise at each step, not a vocabulary distribution. While the practical implication is correct (likelihood ratios over noisy transitions are hard to stabilize), the phrasing could be tightened to focus on the intractability of $\log \frac{p_\theta}{p_{\text{ref}}}$ over multi-step trajectories rather than attributing it solely to "model-agnostic noise."

### 3. Method
This is the core technical contribution. The derivation is mostly sound, but several points require clarification:
- **Jensen’s Inequality & Surrogate Loss (Eq. 15 → 16):** You correctly apply Jensen’s inequality using the convexity of $-\log \sigma(\cdot)$ to obtain an upper bound $L(\theta) \le -\mathbb{E}\log\sigma(\dots)$. Optimizing an upper bound is standard, but the paper does not discuss the *tightness* of this surrogate. Since diffusion trajectories can exhibit high variance across timesteps, the gap between $\mathbb{E}[-\log \sigma(Y)]$ and $-\log \sigma(\mathbb{E}[Y])$ may be substantial. Explicitly framing this as a tractable surrogate objective and briefly discussing its implications for optimization (e.g., potential bias or slower gradient alignment when trajectory variance is high) would strengthen theoretical rigor.
- **Weight Design & Edge Cases (Eq. 14, Sec 3.2):** Using $w(\mathbf{x}_0) = |A(\mathbf{x}_0)|$ elegantly cancels $Z(\mathbf{c})$ because $\sum_{G^+} A = \sum_{G^-} |A|$ under zero-mean normalization. However, Algorithm 1 lines 6–7 compute advantages using group `std`. If the rewards within a group are nearly identical (e.g., early training or highly deterministic prompts), `std` approaches zero, risking division-by-zero or unstable weights. A small $\epsilon$ regularization or fallback strategy should be documented. Additionally, what happens when all $A_i > 0$ or $A_i \le 0$? The algorithm bins all samples into $G^+$ or $G^-$, potentially degenerating the preference signal. Handling these edge cases would improve robustness.
- **Approximation in Eq. 10:** Replacing $\mathbb{E}_{p_\theta(\mathbf{x}_{1:T}|\mathbf{x}_0)}$ with $\mathbb{E}_{q(\mathbf{x}_{1:T}|\mathbf{x}_0)}$ is inherited from Diffusion DPO. It is practical but changes the objective from reverse-path likelihood matching to forward-path denoising matching. Since DGPO groups samples by $\mathbf{x}_0$ reward, this approximation is acceptable, but the paper should explicitly state that the group preference is optimized over an *approximated* path likelihood.
- **Timestep Clip Strategy (Sec 3.2):** Sampling $t \in [t_{\min}, T]$ is a pragmatic engineering choice to avoid overfitting to few-step rollout artifacts. It works empirically (Fig. 4), but lacks theoretical grounding. Is there sensitivity to $t_{\min}$? A brief discussion or ablation across different $t_{\min}$ values would clarify its necessity versus being a fixed heuristic.

### 4. Experiments & Results
The empirical evaluation is comprehensive and generally supports the claims, but several areas need strengthening for ICLR standards:
- **GenEval Jump (0.63 → 0.97):** This is a massive improvement. Table 1 shows SOTA performance, but the paper does not specify whether the reward function used for compositional training *is* the GenEval scoring script or a separate VLM/reward model. If optimizing directly on GenEval’s rule-based metrics (object detectors, layout checkers), the result may reflect metric overfitting rather than improved compositional reasoning. Clarifying the exact reward source per task and discussing potential metric leakage is critical.
- **Statistical Rigor:** The paper reports point estimates without error bars or multiple random seeds. Given the stochastic nature of diffusion sampling, group partitioning, and reward evaluation, ICLR typically expects results averaged over $\ge 3$ seeds with standard deviations, especially for claims of SOTA and large speedups.
- **Compute Comparison:** The "20× faster" claim relies on wall-clock GPU hours (Figs. 1, 3). While practical, this conflates algorithmic efficiency with potential implementation differences (e.g., kernel fusion, data loading, framework overhead). Reporting FLOPs or theoretical gradient computation steps per iteration, alongside wall-clock time, would make the efficiency claim more rigorous and reproducible.
- **Ablations & OOD Metrics:** The OOD metrics (Aesthetic, DeQA, ImageReward, UnifiedReward) are a strong point and effectively guard against reward hacking. However, the quantitative impact of the Timestep Clip strategy is underreported (only a 0.01 OCR Acc drop is mentioned, while visual degradation is qualitative). Adding FID or aesthetic score comparisons for the ablation would strengthen this claim. The offline vs. online comparison (Fig. 5) is clear but lacks discussion on *why* DGPO struggles offline (likely due to distribution shift without online exploration).

### Writing & Clarity
The paper is well-organized and the narrative flow is strong. Notation is dense but consistent. The derivation from Eq. 11 to 17 is compact and could benefit from a step-by-step breakdown in the main text (or clearer signposting to Appendix C). The claim that DGPO "eliminates" the intractable partition is accurate due to the weight design, but the reliance on a convex surrogate bound should be highlighted earlier so readers understand the theoretical trade-off. No major phrasing or structural issues impede understanding.

### Limitations & Broader Impact
This section is currently underdeveloped for an ICLR submission. Stating that the method "has the potential to be adapted to text-to-video synthesis" is a future work suggestion, not a limitation. Key limitations missing:
- **Reward Model Dependence:** DGPO inherits DPO's sensitivity to reward quality and calibration. How does it perform with noisy, sparse, or adversarial rewards?
- **Group Size & Partitioning Sensitivity:** The method assumes $G$ is large enough to form non-trivial $G^+/G^-$ splits. Small $G$ or homogeneous reward distributions degrade the signal.
- **Compute vs. Quality Trade-off:** Using ODE rollouts with few steps (10) speeds training but may limit exploration. How does this scale to high-fidelity, long-horizon generation (e.g., 50+ steps)?
- **Societal/Ethical Considerations:** Even if no human subjects were used, faster RL alignment of text-to-image models has implications for synthetic media generation, bias amplification via preference optimization, and compute democratization. A brief discussion aligns with ICLR's expectations.

### Overall Assessment
This paper presents a principled and practically impactful contribution to RL post-training for diffusion models. By recognizing that group-level relative preferences—not policy gradients per se—are the engine of GRPO’s success, the authors design DGPO to bypass stochastic policy constraints, enable ODE sampling, and reduce per-iteration cost. The mathematical framing is creative, particularly the advantage-based weight design that elegantly cancels the intractable partition function. Empirically, DGPO demonstrates strong performance gains and significant speedups over Flow-GRPO, with careful out-of-domain checks to mitigate reward hacking. However, to meet the ICLR acceptance bar robustly, the authors must: (1) explicitly frame the Jensen-derived objective as an upper-bound surrogate and discuss its tightness/variance implications; (2) clarify the reward sources for GenEval vs. human preference tasks and address potential metric overfitting; (3) include multi-seed results with error bars and theoretical compute metrics (FLOPs) to substantiate efficiency claims; and (4) significantly expand the limitations section to address reward sensitivity, edge-case handling in group partitioning, and compute-quality trade-offs. With these revisions, DGPO would be a strong candidate for acceptance.

# Neutral Reviewer
## Balanced Review

### Summary
The paper introduces Direct Group Preference Optimization (DGPO), an online reinforcement learning framework designed to post-train diffusion models without relying on stochastic policies. By extending DPO to incorporate group-level relative preferences and using advantage-based weighting to cancel the intractable partition function, DGPO enables the use of efficient deterministic ODE samplers and avoids costly full-trajectory training. Empirical results demonstrate that DGPO achieves approximately 20–30× faster training than Flow-GRPO while significantly improving performance on compositional generation, text rendering, and human preference benchmarks.

### Strengths
1. **Clear Motivation & Conceptual Shift:** The paper correctly identifies a structural mismatch between policy-gradient RL (GRPO) and practical diffusion sampling (deterministic ODEs). By reframing success as leveraging group-level preference information rather than stochastic exploration, the authors propose a principled alternative that aligns with how modern samplers operate (Section 1 & 3).
2. **Strong Empirical Performance & Efficiency Gains:** DGPO achieves state-of-the-art results on GenEval (0.97 vs 0.95 for Flow-GRPO and 0.84 for GPT-4o, Table 1) and consistently improves OCR accuracy and PickScore while maintaining out-of-domain quality metrics (Table 2). The reported ~20–30× reduction in training time is highly compelling for practical adoption.
3. **Thoughtful Algorithmic Design:** The advantage-based weighting strategy in Section 3.2 elegantly eliminates the intractable partition $Z(c)$ by exploiting the zero-mean property of normalized advantages. Additionally, the Timestep Clip Strategy (Section 3.2 & Figure 4) provides a simple yet effective solution to few-step rollout artifacts, demonstrating careful engineering for training stability.
4. **Reproducibility & Transparent Setup:** The paper promises open-sourced code built on an established codebase, provides detailed hyperparameters (group size $G=24$, 10-step ODE rollout, LoRA rank 32, $\beta=100$, Appendix E), and includes focused ablations on key components (ODE vs SDE, online vs offline, component removal), meeting ICLR's reproducibility expectations.

### Weaknesses
1. **Theoretical Approximations Lack Analysis:** The derivation relies on Jensen's inequality to upper-bound the objective (transition from Eq. 15 to 16), but the manuscript does not discuss the tightness of this bound, its impact on gradient estimation, or whether it introduces optimization bias. Additionally, the zero-mean advantage weighting assumes a reasonably symmetric reward distribution; its behavior under skewed rewards or smaller group sizes is unexamined.
2. **Limited Baseline Scope:** The primary comparisons are restricted to Flow-GRPO and standard Diffusion-DPO. Given the rapid evolution of diffusion alignment methods, missing comparisons with other contemporary approaches (e.g., Diffusion-KTO, ReFL, or DReaM) weakens the claim of broad superiority and leaves readers uncertain whether gains stem from DGPO specifically or from general advances in preference fine-tuning.
3. **Compute Breakdown Ambiguity:** While the relative speedup (20–30×) is prominently featured, the absolute compute cost and a quantitative breakdown of where the efficiency originates (e.g., ODE rollout savings vs. trajectory-free loss vs. reduced gradient passes) are not disentangled. This makes it difficult to verify whether the speedup is algorithmic or primarily implementation-specific.
4. **Concurrent Work Differentiation:** The brief citation of Chen et al. (2025a), which also explores group preferences in DPO, lacks explicit technical contrast. The manuscript should clearly differentiate DGPO's single group-level reward formulation and analytical derivation from concurrent pairwise enumeration strategies to firmly establish novelty.

### Novelty & Significance
**Novelty:** High within the generative alignment space. The adaptation of GRPO's group-relative information into a DPO-style objective specifically for diffusion models is conceptually clean and mathematically sound. Avoiding the policy-gradient framework while retaining relative preference signals is a meaningful departure from recent Flow-GRPO variants.
**Significance:** Strong for ICLR. Efficient, scalable post-training for diffusion models is a highly active research area. The demonstrated speedups, combined with SOTA benchmark performance and preservation of out-of-domain quality, make DGPO practically valuable and methodologically instructive. It offers the community a viable path to reinforce diffusion models using efficient ODE pipelines rather than computationally prohibitive SDE trajectories.

### Suggestions for Improvement
1. **Analyze the Jensen Bound Tightness:** Provide a theoretical note or empirical validation on how the upper-bound approximation (Eq. 15 → 16) affects gradient variance or convergence behavior. A small ablation comparing exact vs. approximated loss on a synthetic reward task would strengthen the methodological rigor.
2. **Broaden Baseline Comparisons:** Include at least 1–2 recent diffusion alignment methods (e.g., Diffusion-KTO, ReFL, or DReaM) in the main results tables to robustly position DGPO against the current state-of-the-art beyond the policy-gradient lineage.
3. **Quantify Efficiency Components:** Add a compute ablation table or subsection breaking down training time/memory by component (rollout inference, loss computation, gradient steps) for DGPO vs. Flow-GRPO. This will transparently attribute the 20–30× speedup to algorithmic design rather than implementation shortcuts.
4. **Clarify Reward Model Implementation:** Explicitly specify the exact reward models and versions used for each task (e.g., which OCR engine/model computes OCR accuracy, whether HPSv2 or a variant is used, exact PickScore checkpoint). This is critical for full reproducibility and fair comparison.
5. **Expand Sensitivity Analysis:** Discuss and potentially ablate the method's sensitivity to group size $G$ and the Timestep Clip threshold $t_{\min}$. A brief analysis or plot showing performance across different $G$ values would address concerns about reward distribution skew and variance.

# Spark Finder Review
## How to Improve This Paper

### Missing Experiments (top 3-5 only)
1. **Evaluate on larger backbones at native resolution:** Restricting experiments to LoRA rank 32 on 512x512 resolution falls short of ICLR's scaling expectations; you must run DGPO on SD3.5-L or FLUX at 1024x1024 to prove the method holds at production scale.
2. **Provide compute-fair baseline comparisons:** The claimed 20-30x speedup conflates algorithmic efficiency with the inherent inference cost difference between ODE and SDE samplers; run Flow-GRPO with identical rollout steps and group sizes to isolate DGPO's true sample efficiency.
3. **Report multi-seed statistical significance:** GenEval gains (0.63→0.97) and out-of-domain improvements are reported as single numbers; provide mean ± std across ≥3 seeds to confirm these marginal gains are not statistical flukes from favorable rollouts or hyperparameter tuning.
4. **Ablate group size ($G$) and weighting sensitivity:** DGPO's core premise relies on "group information," but fixing $G=24$ and using $|A_i|$ without baseline comparisons leaves the design unverified; show performance curves for $G \in \{4, 8, 16, 24\}$ and alternative weights to prove group leverage is necessary and monotonic.

### Deeper Analysis Needed (top 3-5 only)
1. **Analyze the Jensen's inequality gap:** The final objective relies on moving the expectation outside $\log \sigma$ via Jensen's inequality, introducing a loose surrogate bound; quantify how large this gap becomes during training, as a wide gap could mean gradients consistently misdirect optimization away from true group preferences.
2. **Empirically justify the Timestep Clip Strategy:** The claim that clipping $t_{\min} > 0$ prevents overfitting to few-step artifacts is purely heuristic; analyze gradient variance or signal-to-noise ratio across timesteps $t \in [0, t_{\min}]$ to demonstrate mathematically why early-step gradients degrade training.
3. **Characterize $\beta$ (KL regularization) trade-offs:** You show one reward-hacking failure with small $\beta$, but no systematic sweep exists; provide a stability analysis across $\beta \in [10, 500]$ to prove DGPO maintains output diversity and prevents mode collapse under different exploration-exploitation regimes.

### Visualizations & Case Studies
1. **Showcase spatial relation & counting failure/success cases:** Provide side-by-side breakdowns of complex prompts (e.g., "blue burger on head of red dog") where DGPO succeeds and Flow-GRPO fails; this directly proves compositional reasoning improvement rather than just aesthetic smoothing.
2. **Visualize latent trajectories during 10-step rollouts:** Plot gradient norms or latent space paths across the clipped timesteps to reveal whether DGPO genuinely corrects early diffusion dynamics or merely over-optimizes high-confidence signals near $t_{\max}$.
3. **Map reward distributions to visual failure modes:** Generate a scatter plot of reward scores vs. out-of-domain quality metrics with sampled images at extremes; this exposes whether high DGPO scores correlate with actual semantic fidelity or mask subtle reward-hacking artifacts.

### Obvious Next Steps
1. **Implement offline-to-online initialization:** Initialize the online DGPO policy with high-quality offline preference data to stabilize early exploration; this two-stage pipeline is standard in RLHF and necessary to make DGPO deployment-ready for real-world training.
2. **Validate with human pairwise preferences:** Replace automatic proxies (OCR accuracy, PickScore) with real human judgment studies; without human evaluation, claims of "aligning diffusion models" remain unverified due to potential metric gaming by proxy reward models.
3. **Extend to text-to-video architectures:** The current framework is limited to 2D image DiT/U-Net models; a preliminary adaptation to a temporal diffusion transformer (e.g., HunyuanVideo) would prove the method's generality and significantly increase impact.

# Final Consolidated Review
## Summary
This paper introduces Direct Group Preference Optimization (DGPO), a reinforcement learning framework for post-training diffusion models that replaces the policy-gradient machinery of GRPO with a DPO-style direct preference objective. By leveraging advantage-based weighting to cancel the intractable partition function and operating entirely with deterministic ODE samplers, DGPO bypasses the inefficiencies of stochastic SDE rollouts and full-trajectory training. The method achieves state-of-the-art compositional generation performance (0.97 on GenEval) alongside a reported 20–30× reduction in training time compared to Flow-GRPO, while maintaining image quality across multiple out-of-distribution metrics.

## Strengths
- **Principled architectural alignment:** The paper correctly identifies that GRPO's empirical success in LLMs stems from group-level relative preference signals rather than its policy-gradient formulation. DGPO cleanly transplant this insight into diffusion models, enabling native compatibility with efficient deterministic ODE samplers and avoiding the weak exploration signals of model-agnostic Gaussian noise (Sec 1 & 3).
- **Elegant mathematical formulation:** The advantage-based weight design ($w(\mathbf{x}_0) = |A(\mathbf{x}_0)|$) in Sec 3.2 elegantly cancels the intractable partition function $Z(\mathbf{c})$ by exploiting the zero-mean property of normalized advantages. This preserves fine-grained within-group reward granularity while maintaining a tractable, trajectory-free optimization objective.
- **Rigorous empirical validation & reward-hacking safeguards:** DGPO demonstrates strong performance across three distinct tasks (composition, text rendering, preference alignment) with clear SOTA gains. Crucially, the authors evaluate four independent out-of-domain metrics (Aesthetic, DeQA, ImageReward, UnifiedReward) on DrawBench to explicitly guard against reward hacking, showing that quality is preserved even as in-domain scores improve (Table 2 & Sec 4.2).
- **Practical engineering choices backed by ablation:** The Timestep Clip Strategy ($t \in [t_{\min}, T]$) and online rollout setup address real-world training bottlenecks (few-step artifact overfitting, inference cost). The ablations in Figs 4 & 5 convincingly isolate the contributions of ODE vs SDE rollouts, online vs offline training, and the proposed components against Diffusion-DPO baselines.

## Weaknesses
- **Unquantified surrogate gap from Jensen's inequality:** The final objective is derived by applying Jensen's inequality to move the trajectory expectation outside the log-sigmoid (Eq 15 → 16), yielding a tractable upper bound. While mathematically sound, the paper does not discuss the tightness of this surrogate or how the gap behaves when group rewards exhibit high variance. If the bound is loose, gradient directions may systematically misalign with the true underlying preference likelihood, particularly in high-entropy generation regimes.
- **Ambiguity in reward formulation for GenEval:** The paper attributes the massive GenEval jump (0.63 → 0.97) to a "rule-based reward" but does not explicitly specify the exact reward function or its mapping to the GenEval evaluation pipeline. Clarifying whether the training reward is identical to the test metric or a differentiable proxy is necessary to rule out direct metric overfitting versus genuine compositional improvement.
- **Numerical robustness of advantage-based partitioning:** Algorithm 1 partitions samples into $G^+$ and $G^-$ based on the sign of $A_i$, which relies on group standard deviation in the denominator. In early training or highly deterministic prompts where rewards cluster, standard deviation may approach zero (causing instability), or all advantages may share the same sign (collapsing the preference signal to a single group). The absence of a smoothing term (e.g., $\epsilon$ denominator) or fallback thresholding leaves a minor robustness gap.

## Nice-to-Haves
- Reporting results across multiple random seeds with variance/error bars to strengthen statistical confidence in the reported margins.
- Providing a compute breakdown (rollout inference vs. backward pass vs. data loading) or theoretical FLOP comparison to disentangle algorithmic efficiency from implementation-level optimizations.
- Including contemporaneous preference-tuning baselines (e.g., Diffusion-KTO, ReFL) to further contextualize DGPO's gains within the broader alignment landscape.
- A brief ablation or sensitivity analysis on group size $G$ and the $t_{\min}$ threshold to establish practical deployment guidelines.

## Novel Insights
DGPO successfully decouples the "group-relative preference" mechanism from the "policy-gradient" framework, demonstrating that diffusion models do not require stochastic policies to benefit from fine-grained within-group reward normalization. This reveals a more general principle for RLHF in continuous, deterministic-generation spaces: alignment can be framed as direct likelihood matching against advantage-weighted group preferences, yielding a trajectory-free objective that naturally synergizes with modern high-fidelity ODE samplers. The work provides a practical, mathematically grounded blueprint for scaling reinforcement post-training in diffusion pipelines without the computational overhead of SDE exploration or full-trajectory value estimation.

## Suggestions
1. **Clarify the exact GenEval reward formulation:** Explicitly state the mathematical form of the reward function used during compositional training and detail how it differs from (or matches) the official GenEval evaluation script. This will preempt concerns about metric leakage and clarify whether gains stem from improved spatial/counting reasoning or reward proxy optimization.
2. **Address advantage partitioning edge cases:** Add a small constant $\epsilon$ to the standard deviation denominator in Eq 12 to prevent division-by-zero instability, and specify a fallback behavior (e.g., treating the prompt as uninformative or using a fixed threshold) when all $A_i$ share the same sign.
3. **Discuss the Jensen surrogate's optimization behavior:** Add a paragraph in Sec 3 or the Appendix discussing the theoretical implications of the upper-bound objective. If feasible, include a small experiment measuring the empirical gap between the true log-likelihood ratio and the Jensen-derived proxy over training steps, or at least acknowledge how high trajectory variance might affect gradient alignment.
