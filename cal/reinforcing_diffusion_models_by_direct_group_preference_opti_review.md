# Harsh Critic Review
## Section-by-Section Critical Review

### Title & Abstract
- **Accuracy:** The title accurately captures the core contribution (direct group preference optimization for diffusion models). 
- **Clarity:** The abstract clearly states the problem (mismatch between GRPO's policy-gradient framework and deterministic diffusion sampling), the proposed method (DGPO, which bypasses stochastic policies and optimizes group-level preferences directly), and the claimed outcomes (~20-30× training speedup, GenEval boost from 0.63 to 0.97).
- **Support for Claims:** The claim of boosting GenEval to 0.97 (Abstract and Introduction) is striking given the strong baselines (GPT-4o: 0.84, Flow-GRPO: 0.95). While Table 1 reports this, such a near-perfect score on an automated compositional benchmark often signals reward hacking or evaluation artifacts. The abstract mentions maintained out-of-domain performance, which partially mitigates concern, but this specific claim requires stringent justification in the main text (addressed below).

### Introduction & Motivation
- **Motivation & Gap:** The motivation is well-grounded. The authors correctly identify that recent GRPO-style adaptations for diffusion models (e.g., Flow-GRPO, DanceGRPO) force SDE-based sampling to satisfy policy-gradient requirements, introducing computational overhead and relying on model-agnostic noise. Bridging this by reinterpreting GRPO's core value as *group relative information* rather than *policy gradients* is a conceptually sound and timely insight.
- **Contributions:** Clearly stated and well-aligned with the paper's structure. The three claimed benefits (efficient sampling, fast convergence, efficient training) logically follow from the proposed methodology.
- **Claim Calibration:** The introduction slightly overstates the nature of diffusion stochasticity. It claims that ODE samplers "do not naturally provide a stochastic policy," yet DGPO still samples a group of $G$ images from different initial noise vectors $\mathbf{x}_T$. The exploration is thus still driven by initial randomness, not the trajectory dynamics. The real advantage of ODE over SDE here is *trajectory determinism* (which avoids full-trajectory backpropagation and yields higher-quality intermediates), not the absence of stochasticity. Clarifying this distinction would strengthen the theoretical framing.

### Method / Approach
- **Clarity & Reproducibility:** The method is mathematically well-structured. Algorithm 1, the advantage-based weighting scheme, and the final objective (Eq. 17) are clearly presented. The derivation from Eq. 11 to Eq. 17 is logically sound under standard Diffusion-DPO assumptions.
- **Key Assumptions:** 
  1. **Reward Approximation (Eq. 10):** The method relies on approximating the inversion chain $p_\theta(\mathbf{x}_{1:T}|\mathbf{x}_0)$ with the forward diffusion $q(\mathbf{x}_{1:T}|\mathbf{x}_0)$, a known bias introduced in Diffusion-DPO. While standard, this approximation should be explicitly acknowledged as a source of potential gradient bias.
  2. **Partition Function Cancellation:** The choice of $w(\mathbf{x}) = |A(\mathbf{x})|$ elegantly cancels $Z(\mathbf{c})$ because $\sum_{G^+} |A_i| = \sum_{G^-} |A_i|$ for zero-mean normalized advantages. This is mathematically correct.
- **Logical Gaps & Edge Cases:**
  - **Empty Groups & Division by Zero:** Algorithm 1 (Lines 6-7) divides by `std({r_j})`. If all $G$ samples receive identical rewards, `std = 0`, causing a crash. Furthermore, if all $A_i > 0$ or all $A_i \leq 0$ (common early in training or with a flat reward landscape), either $G^+$ or $G^-$ becomes empty, breaking the BT loss in Eq. 8. The paper lacks a fallback strategy (e.g., skip update, revert to pairwise DPO, or add pseudo-counts), which is a critical implementation concern for stable RL training.
  - **Jensen's Inequality Surrogate (Eq. 16):** Moving the expectation over $q(\mathbf{x}_{1:T}|\mathbf{x}_0)$ outside $-\log \sigma(\cdot)$ relies on convexity, yielding an upper bound. Optimizing a tight upper bound is common, but the paper does not discuss whether this surrogate meaningfully changes the optimization landscape or gradient variance compared to Monte Carlo estimation inside the sigmoid.
- **Timestep Clip Strategy:** The intuition is plausible (avoiding overfitting to low-timestep artifacts from cheap 10-step rollouts), but the mechanism is under-explained. Generating $\mathbf{x}_0$ with a 10-step solver and then noising it to $t < \text{generation\_steps}$ creates a distributional mismatch because the 10-step trajectory does not exactly match the true continuous-time marginals. The clip strategy mitigates this, but a brief theoretical or empirical justification for why $t_{\min}$ resolves the mismatch (rather than just hiding low-$t$ errors) is needed.

### Experiments & Results
- **Testing Claims:** The experiments directly evaluate efficiency (Fig. 3), compositional quality (Table 1), text rendering, and human preference (Table 2). Ablations on ODE vs. SDE rollouts and offline vs. online settings are appropriate and support the core claims.
- **Baselines & Fairness:** Flow-GRPO is a strong, directly comparable baseline. Including Diffusion DPO (pairwise) effectively isolates the benefit of *group* information. However, it's unclear if Flow-GRPO's training curve in Fig. 3 was optimized with its default hyperparameters or aggressively tuned. ICLR expects evidence that baselines were given a fair computational/training budget.
- **Missing Ablations & Statistical Rigor:**
  - **Seed Sensitivity & Error Bars:** RL for diffusion models is notoriously seed-sensitive. All figures and tables report single-run values without error bars, variance estimates, or statistical significance testing. This is a notable gap for an ICLR submission.
  - **Group Size ($G$) Sensitivity:** The method scales with $G$, but the impact of group size on convergence rate and OOD metric stability is not studied. Is $G=24$ optimal? Does performance degrade sharply at $G=8$ or plateau at $G=32$?
  - **$\beta$ & EMA Sensitivity:** Appendix E mentions $\beta=100$ and a switch from identity to EMA updates. How do results change if $\beta$ is too small (Appendix D hints at reward hacking) or too large (vanishing gradients)? A sensitivity analysis for these crucial RL hyperparameters is expected.
- **GenEval Score of 0.97:** While Table 1 reports this, it significantly exceeds current SOTA and approaches theoretical saturation for an automated detector. The authors should explicitly detail the GenEval evaluation protocol (e.g., exact detection thresholds, prompt sets, whether detector weights were frozen) to rule out overfitting to the evaluation metric, even if OOD metrics appear stable.

### Writing & Clarity
- **Clarity of Exposition:** The narrative flows logically from problem identification to method derivation. The use of Algorithm 1 and structured equation numbering aids readability.
- **Figures & Tables:** Figures 1-5 effectively communicate the main results. Figure 4 visually demonstrates the timestep clip strategy's importance. However, Figure 3's claim of "~19× Faster" and "~14× Faster" lacks context on what metric threshold defines "completion." Are these times to reach a specific GenEval score, or just to finish a fixed number of iterations? This should be explicitly labeled in the caption or axis.
- **Minor Conceptual Clarification:** Section 3 states "sampling from the inversion chain $p_\theta(\mathbf{x}_{1:T}|\mathbf{x}_0)$ is expensive." It's worth briefly noting that exact inversion is intractable in general, but DDIM inversion provides a deterministic approximation widely used in editing. Approximating with forward noise $q$ is faster but shifts the reward definition. A single sentence clarifying this trade-off would help practitioners.

### Limitations & Broader Impact
- **Acknowledged Limitations:** The authors only mention the scope restriction to text-to-image and potential future application to video. This is insufficient for the depth of the method.
- **Missed Limitations:** 
  - **Empty partition failure modes** (discussed in Method) should be formally acknowledged as a training instability risk in the limitations.
  - **Reward Model Dependence:** DGPO inherits the biases and failure modes of the online reward function $r_\phi$. If the reward model is poorly calibrated or exploits spurious correlations, DGPO's group optimization will efficiently amplify these failures (as partially shown in Appendix D, though only with $\beta$ tuning).
  - **Memory/Compute Scaling:** While training per iteration is faster, maintaining $G=24$ full latent trajectories in GPU memory during the forward pass of the reward model and denoising network scales linearly. Memory constraints could limit group size on standard hardware.
- **Broader Impact:** The statement is minimal. RL alignment of generative models carries risks of amplifying societal biases present in the reward data, facilitating automated generation of misleading/harmful content with higher fidelity, and centralizing access due to compute requirements. A standard ICLR-level broader impact paragraph addressing these would be appropriate.

### Overall Assessment
This paper presents a conceptually elegant and practically effective method for reinforcement post-training of diffusion models. By decoupling GRPO's core strength (group-relative preference learning) from its rigid policy-gradient machinery, DGPO enables the use of efficient ODE rollouts, eliminates full-trajectory backpropagation, and achieves substantial training speedups while maintaining or improving generation quality. The mathematical derivation is sound, and the empirical comparisons against Flow-GRPO and Diffusion-DPO are compelling. However, the paper currently falls slightly short of ICLR's rigorous empirical standards due to the absence of error bars/multi-seed analysis, unaddressed edge cases in group partitioning (empty groups/zero variance), and limited ablations on critical hyperparameters ($G$, $\beta$, EMA decay). The extraordinarily high GenEval score (0.97) also warrants explicit validation of the evaluation protocol to rule out detector-specific overfitting. If the authors address these statistical, implementation, and evaluation clarity concerns—particularly by adding variance estimates, handling partition edge cases, and justifying the GenEval result—the contribution firmly meets ICLR's acceptance bar for both novelty and practical impact.

# Neutral Reviewer
## Balanced Review

### Summary
This paper introduces Direct Group Preference Optimization (DGPO), a reinforcement learning framework that aligns diffusion models by directly optimizing group-level preference signals without relying on stochastic policy gradients. By leveraging advantage-based sample weighting to cancel the intractable DPO partition function and enabling efficient ODE-based sampling paired with a timestep clipping strategy, DGPO achieves substantially faster convergence. Empirical results demonstrate strong performance gains on compositional generation and text rendering benchmarks while maintaining competitive out-of-domain image quality.

### Strengths
1. **Well-Motivated and Technically Sound Formulation:** The paper correctly identifies the mismatch between GRPO's requirement for stochastic policies and diffusion models' preference for deterministic ODE samplers. The derivation in Section 3.2 elegantly uses GRPO-style advantage normalization as sample weights to mathematically cancel the intractable partition function $Z(c)$, yielding a tractable objective (Eq. 17) that bridges DPO and group-RL concepts without policy gradients.
2. **Compelling Empirical Performance and Efficiency:** DGPO demonstrates a dramatic improvement on GenEval (63% to 97% overall, Table 1) and consistently outperforms Flow-GRPO and baseline DPO across compositional, text-rendering, and human preference tasks. The reported 20–30× reduction in GPU training time (Figures 1 & 3) is a highly impactful practical contribution for the post-training community.
3. **Practical and Effective Design Choices:** The proposed "Timestep Clip Strategy" effectively addresses the degradation caused by few-step online rollouts. The ablation (Figure 4) clearly shows that restricting training timesteps to $[t_{\min}, T]$ prevents the model from overfitting to rollout artifacts while preserving instruction-following accuracy, demonstrating careful engineering.
4. **Comprehensive Evaluation Protocol:** The inclusion of multiple independent out-of-domain metrics (Aesthetic, DeQA, ImageReward, UnifiedReward, Table 2) shows awareness of reward hacking risks and provides a holistic view of model behavior beyond the target reward.

### Weaknesses
1. **Surrogate Loss Tightness Not Discussed:** The optimization objective relies on Jensen's inequality to move the expectation inside the log-sigmoid (Eq. 15 to 16), turning the loss into an upper-bound surrogate. While standard in DPO-derivations, the paper does not analyze or discuss the tightness of this bound or its optimization implications, leaving the theoretical gap between the true Bradley-Terry objective and the optimized loss unaddressed.
2. **Baseline Fairness and Speedup Definition:** The claimed 20–30× speedup compared to Flow-GRPO is compelling, but the experimental setup lacks details on whether Flow-GRPO was run with matched compute budgets, identical hardware constraints, and tuned hyperparameters. Without a matched-compute convergence plot, it is unclear how much of the advantage stems from algorithmic efficiency versus baseline sub-optimization.
3. **Marginal Out-of-Domain Gains:** While the method maintains out-of-domain quality, the improvements on independent metrics are minimal or occasionally slightly lower than the baseline (e.g., Aesthetic score drops from 5.39 to 5.31 for the GenEval-tuned variant in Table 2). This pattern is noted but not thoroughly analyzed to determine if the aggressive in-domain optimization trades off subtle generative diversity or visual fidelity.
4. **Incomplete Reproducibility Details:** Key hyperparameters necessary for exact reproduction are missing from the main text and appendix, including the exact value of $t_{\min}$, learning rate, batch size, optimizer configuration, and the specific number of environment steps per rollout. Appendix E mentions an A100 and LoRA rank, but omits critical training configuration details.

### Novelty & Significance
**Novelty:** The contribution is a methodologically sound and well-executed synthesis of existing paradigms (DPO's implicit reward formulation + GRPO's group advantage mechanics) tailored to the diffusion setting. While the core idea of using advantage weights to eliminate $Z(c)$ builds on known off-policy RL literature, its application to bypass stochastic policy requirements in diffusion models is novel and practically valuable. The work is less of a radical theoretical breakthrough and more of a highly effective, diffusion-native algorithmic adaptation.

**Significance:** High. RL post-training for diffusion models is a rapidly evolving area, and the inefficiency of SDE-based gradient rollouts is a recognized bottleneck. DGPO provides a clear, implementable path to faster alignment with measurable benchmark improvements. If the experimental claims hold under rigorous scrutiny, this work will likely become a strong baseline or component in future diffusion alignment research, meeting ICLR's threshold for impactful algorithmic advances.

### Suggestions for Improvement
1. **Analyze the Surrogate Bound:** Add a brief theoretical discussion or empirical validation on the tightness of the Jensen's inequality bound. For example, provide a gradient similarity analysis between the exact BT loss and the surrogate, or experimentally verify that optimizing the bound monotonically improves preference accuracy.
2. **Clarify Baseline Comparisons and Speedup Metrics:** Explicitly state the compute budget, hyperparameter tuning scope, and iteration counts used for Flow-GRPO in your experiments. Add a convergence curve normalized by wall-clock time to transparently show the speedup, and clarify whether "20× faster" refers to time-to-target-performance or iterations-per-hour.
3. **Detail Missing Experimental Configurations:** Include $t_{\min}$ value, learning rate, batch size, optimizer type (e.g., AdamW), weight decay, and exact rollout scheduler in the main text or a comprehensive training details table. Specify how prompts are sampled per batch and whether reward model inference is parallelized.
4. **Deepen Out-of-Domain Analysis:** Investigate the slight degradation/plateau in metrics like Aesthetic Score. Provide qualitative examples or analyze reward-hacking patterns when $β$ is varied, discussing whether group advantage weighting inadvertently penalizes aesthetic diversity. A brief analysis of sample diversity (e.g., via pairwise similarity or variance in reward scores) would strengthen the fairness claim.

## How to Improve This Paper

### Missing Experiments (top 3-5 only)
1. Add training curves tracking KL divergence to the reference model alongside in-domain and out-of-domain rewards for both DGPO and Flow-GRPO. Without step-by-step KL/reward trade-off plots, the claim of stable alignment cannot be distinguished from early-stage reward hacking.
2. Conduct a systematic ablation on group size ($G$) and rollout steps, measuring how GenEval/OCR scores and convergence speed change. The claimed $20-30\times$ efficiency and performance hinges entirely on these hyperparameters, and the lack of sensitivity analysis makes the speedup claims unverifiable.
3. Benchmark against modern diffusion RLH F baselines beyond Flow-GRPO and standard DPO (e.g., ReFL, Diffusion-KTO, or SPIN). Showing improvement over only one GRPO variant does not establish DGPO as a general advance in the preference optimization landscape.

### Deeper Analysis Needed (top 3-5 only)
1. Rigorously analyze the impact of applying Jensen’s inequality to move the expectation inside the log-sigmoid (Eq. 15 → 16). This transforms the exact MLE objective into a loose upper bound, and without quantifying how this approximation affects gradient variance or optimization stability, the mathematical justification is incomplete.
2. Justify and analyze the sensitivity of the absolute advantage weighting $w(\mathbf{x}) = |A(\mathbf{x})|$. This choice is derived solely to cancel the partition function, but it ignores reward scale shifts during online training and may amplify gradient noise from outlier samples without variance control.
3. Quantify how the Timestep Clip Strategy ($t_{min} > 0$) differentially affects low-level structural fidelity versus high-level compositional reasoning. Skipping early timesteps is a heuristic fix that may prevent the model from learning foundational image priors, and a $t_{min}$ sweep is required to prove it doesn't bottleneck generalization.

### Visualizations & Case Studies
1. Report per-prompt diversity metrics (e.g., intra-prompt LPIPS variance or FID across seeds) and explicitly show failure cases for both reward types. Deterministic ODE rollouts with shared noise risk severe mode collapse, and without diversity quantification, the method may simply be overfitting to narrow reward peaks.
2. Visualize the evolution of denoising trajectories or latent feature distributions for DGPO vs. SDE-based baselines across training steps. This would directly expose whether ODE sampling actually preserves sufficient exploration or merely accelerates convergence to degenerate modes.
3. Provide side-by-side gradient norm heatmaps across timesteps for DGPO vs. Flow-GRPO. This would verify the claim that single-timestep training efficiently directs optimization without introducing high-variance updates that destabilize early denoising steps.

### Obvious Next Steps
1. Conduct standardized human pairwise evaluations (e.g., Pick-A-Pic or crowd-sourced win rates on GenEval prompts). RLHF papers submitted to ICLR must validate proxy reward gains with actual human judgments to prove alignment improvements aren't artifacts of reward model bias.
2. Extend experiments to larger foundation models (e.g., FLUX.1-Dev or SD3.5-Large) to verify that the single-timestep gradient signal scales effectively with model capacity. Results on SD3.5-M alone do not demonstrate utility for state-of-the-art generation pipelines.
3. Provide an isolated compute breakdown distinguishing time saved by ODE sampling from time saved by single-timestep training. Without profiling the two efficiency claims separately, the reported $30\times$ speedup could be misleading regarding the actual algorithmic contribution.

# Final Consolidated Review
## Summary
This paper introduces Direct Group Preference Optimization (DGPO), a reinforcement learning framework for post-training diffusion models that replaces policy-gradient updates with direct optimization of group-level preference signals. By deriving an advantage-based weighting scheme that cancels the intractable DPO partition function, DGPO enables efficient ODE-based sampling and single-timestep gradient updates, eliminating the need for stochastic SDE rollouts and full-trajectory backpropagation. The method claims ~20–30× faster training than Flow-GRPO while achieving strong gains on compositional generation, text rendering, and human preference benchmarks.

## Strengths
- **Conceptually elegant algorithmic bridge:** The paper correctly identifies that GRPO's practical success stems from leveraging group-relative advantage signals rather than strict policy-gradient mechanics. Reformulating this as an implicit preference objective with advantage-based weights to cancel the intractable partition $Z(\mathbf{c})$ is mathematically sound and directly addresses the stochasticity bottleneck in diffusion RLHF.
- **Strong empirical performance and efficiency:** DGPO demonstrates substantial improvements on GenEval (0.63 → 0.97) and consistently outperforms Flow-GRPO and pairwise DPO across multiple tasks. The reported wall-clock speedup is practically significant, and the use of deterministic ODE rollouts paired with a timestep clipping strategy offers a clear, implementable path to faster alignment.
- **Effective mitigation of rollout artifacts:** The proposed Timestep Clip Strategy is well-motivated and empirically validated (Fig. 4). It successfully prevents overfitting to low-quality few-step rollout artifacts while preserving instruction-following accuracy, showing careful engineering aligned with the constraints of online diffusion training.

## Weaknesses
- **Theoretical gaps in surrogate objective and weighting design:** The derivation relies on Jensen's inequality to push the expectation inside the log-sigmoid (Eq. 15 → 16), converting the exact Bradley-Terry objective into a loose upper bound. The paper provides no analysis of bound tightness, gradient variance, or optimization implications. Furthermore, the absolute advantage weighting $w(\mathbf{x}) = |A(\mathbf{x})|$ is chosen purely for algebraic convenience to cancel $Z(\mathbf{c})$, ignoring potential gradient noise amplification from outliers and reward scale shifts during online training.
- **Lack of statistical rigor and critical reproducibility details:** All tables and figures report single-run results without error bars, variance estimates, or multi-seed validation. For an online RL method claiming dramatic speedups, the absence of KL-divergence tracking against the reference model, systematic ablations over group size $G$, and sensitivity analysis for critical hyperparameters ($t_{\min}$, $\beta$, EMA decay) severely undermines trust in the stability and generalizability of the results. Additionally, implementation edge cases (e.g., zero-reward variance causing division-by-zero, or empty $G^+/G^-$ partitions crashing the BT loss) are neither discussed nor mitigated.
- **Evaluation saturation and unaddressed diversity trade-offs:** The near-perfect GenEval score (0.97) approaches automated detector saturation, raising concerns of reward hacking despite stable out-of-domain scores. Out-of-domain metrics show minimal gains or slight regressions (e.g., Aesthetic Score drops from 5.39 to 5.31), yet this trade-off is not analyzed. Given deterministic ODE rollouts and shared-noise group sampling, the method risks mode collapse or overfitting to narrow reward peaks. Without intra-prompt diversity metrics (e.g., LPIPS/FID variance) or human pairwise validation, it remains unclear whether DGPO achieves genuine alignment or proxy-metric optimization.

## Nice-to-Haves
- Explicitly profile the $20\times$ speedup to disentangle gains from ODE sampling efficiency versus single-timestep training versus baseline sub-optimization.
- Provide KL-divergence vs. reward curves across training steps to transparently demonstrate alignment stability and rule out early reward hacking.
- Include a brief ablation on how the Timestep Clip Strategy scales across different rollout step counts (e.g., 5 vs. 10 vs. 20) to confirm robustness.

## Novel Insights
The core conceptual contribution is the decoupling of group-relative preference learning from the rigid policy-gradient machinery historically required for RLHF. By recognizing that diffusion post-training does not need stochastic trajectory exploration to benefit from group advantages, the authors successfully reframe GRPO's relative signal as a tractable, group-weighted DPO objective. This shifts the paradigm from exploring noisy diffusion trajectories to efficiently harvesting high-quality deterministically generated samples and directly optimizing their rank-ordered preferences. The insight effectively transforms a fundamental mismatch between RLHF frameworks and diffusion samplers into a streamlined, single-timestep optimization pipeline that preserves fine-grained reward signals without paying the computational cost of stochastic rollouts.

## Potentially Missed Related Work
- **SPIN (Iterative Self-Play Fine-Tuning)** and **ReFL (Reward Fine-tuning of Language/Diffusion models)** — relevant for contrasting online/offline self-improvement paradigms and discussing how group sampling differs from pairwise self-play.
- **Variance-reduction techniques in DPO/KTO** (e.g., reference model anchoring, beta scheduling, or advantage clipping) — worth discussing to contextualize the sensitivity of the $|A(\mathbf{x})|$ weighting scheme and potential alternatives for stabilizing online updates.

## Suggestions
1. **Theoretical/Ablation Rigor:** Add an empirical analysis of the Jensen surrogate by comparing gradient similarity or convergence behavior against a Monte Carlo-estimated exact BT loss. Sweep $\beta$ and $G$ to demonstrate robustness and justify the chosen $|A(\mathbf{x})|$ weighting beyond algebraic convenience.
2. **Statistical Validation & Edge Case Handling:** Run experiments across $\geq 3$ seeds with reported error bars. Add KL-divergence tracking to explicitly monitor reference model drift. Implement and document fallback strategies for zero-variance rewards and empty group partitions (e.g., pseudo-counts or graceful skipping).
3. **Diversity & Human Evaluation:** Report intra-prompt diversity metrics (e.g., LPIPS variance across seeds) to verify that ODE rollout + group weighting does not induce mode collapse. Supplement proxy reward gains with at least one human pairwise preference evaluation on a hold-out prompt set to confirm genuine alignment.
4. **Reproducibility & Baseline Transparency:** Explicitly state missing training details (optimizer, LR, batch structure, exact $t_{\min}$ value). Clarify whether Flow-GRPO baselines were run with matched compute budgets, identical rollout steps, and tuned hyperparameters, and label speedup axes with explicit performance thresholds.
