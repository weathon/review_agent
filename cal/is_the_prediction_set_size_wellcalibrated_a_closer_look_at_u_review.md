# Harsh Critic Review
## Section-by-Section Critical Review

### Title & Abstract
- **Does the title accurately reflect the contribution?** Yes. The title poses a direct question that the paper answers by defining, measuring, and correcting the misalignment between conformal prediction set size (PSS) and predictive accuracy.
- **Does the abstract clearly state the problem, method, and key results?** The abstract clearly outlines the core problem (PSS uncertainty not necessarily aligned with correctness), the methodological response (formal definition, calibration target, empirical study, CPAC bi-level optimization), and high-level results (weak baseline calibration, CPAC effectiveness across vision/NLP tasks).
- **Are any claims in the abstract unsupported by the paper?** The claim that CPAC's effectiveness is demonstrated "by testing models including ResNet, Vision Transformer and GPT-2" is fully supported in Sections 5.3–5.4. However, the assertion that this is the first systematic investigation should be tempered: the Introduction later acknowledges van der Laan & Alaa (2024) for regression, and the community has explored conformal calibration in various forms. The abstract is safe but leans slightly promotional given the heuristic nature of the proposed target curve.

### Introduction & Motivation
- **Is the problem well-motivated? Is the gap in prior work clearly identified?** Yes. The motivation is strong: CP provides rigorous coverage guarantees, but practitioners often interpret PSS as a proxy for instance-level uncertainty. Aligning PSS with actual accuracy is a natural and practically important desideratum for risk-aware deployment. The gap is well-articulated by distinguishing PSS calibration from point-prediction calibration and conditional coverage guarantees (Gibbs et al., 2023).
- **Are the contributions clearly stated and accurate?** The threefold contributions match the paper's structure: (1) formalizing PSS-accuracy alignment via sampling, (2) proposing a power-law target curve motivated empirically and theoretically, and (3) CPAC bi-level optimization. This accurately reflects the manuscript's content.
- **Does the introduction over-claim or under-sell?** The introduction appropriately scopes the work and avoids claiming new coverage guarantees. However, stating that calibration for PSS is "fundamentally different from conditional coverage" slightly underplays that both properties can be desirable and complementary. A brief acknowledgment of potential synergy would strengthen the positioning.

### Method / Approach
- **Is the method clearly described and reproducible?** The definition of CP calibration (Def 4.1), CP-ECE metrics (Eq 5), target function $f_\tau(k) = 1/k^\tau$, and Algorithm 1 are provided. Hyperparameters (learning rates, $\lambda$, $t=3$, seeds) are listed in Sec 5.2, enabling reproduction.
- **Are key assumptions stated and justified?** Two major assumptions are central: (i) multinomial sampling with fixed temperature $t$ bridges set-valued predictions to pointwise accuracy, and (ii) the target curve is a power function motivated by a Dirichlet prior on softmax probabilities. The Dirichlet assumption is explicitly labeled as "illustrative" rather than restrictive (Sec 4.2), which is honest. However, fixing $t=3$ across all models/datasets and ignoring samples with PSS $>400$ (ImageNet) or $>70$ (Topic) is an empirical shortcut that lacks rigorous justification.
- **Are there logical gaps in the derivation or reasoning?** The bi-level formulation in Eq 9 states a hard constraint $\nu - g(\mathbf{W}, \mathbf{b}) = 0$, but Algorithm 1 uses a block-coordinate descent: recompute $\nu$ once per epoch, then perform gradient steps on $\mathbf{W}, \mathbf{b}$. The text asserts this works "by assuming the $\nu$ variable does not change drastically during optimization." This assumption is unverified and could break if logit adjustments significantly reorder conformity scores within an epoch.
- **Are there edge cases or failure modes not discussed?** Empty prediction sets are handled by forcing min PSS $=1$ (Sec 5.2). However, how the method behaves when the optimal $\nu$ pushes nearly all samples to PSS=1 (or PSS=$K$) is not analyzed. Additionally, modifying logits with a learned affine transform $(\mathbf{W}, \mathbf{b})$ before conformal calibration theoretically alters the calibration distribution. While coverage is maintained empirically on held-out splits under mild noise, no distribution-robustness guarantee is discussed post-calibration.
- **For theoretical claims: are proofs correct and complete?** Theorem 4.2's proof (Appendix A) follows standard properties of Dirichlet moments and is mathematically consistent. However, as noted, its relevance to actual deep classifier softmax outputs is limited to intuition. Theorem A.1 (logistic-normal) similarly provides asymptotic scaling but relies on strict variance ordering assumptions. These support a heuristic target rather than a rigorous bound.

### Experiments & Results
- **Do the experiments actually test the paper's claims?** Yes. Reliability diagrams and CP-ECE metrics directly evaluate PSS-accuracy alignment under varying perturbations and architectures.
- **Are baselines appropriate and fairly compared?** Baselines include standard Platt Scaling (PS) and a full-matrix scaling variant (PS-Full). Given that CPAC optimizes the full affine transform with a CP-specific loss, comparing against PS (scalar temperature) is fair. A comparison against recently proposed conformal calibration or efficiency-improvement methods (e.g., temperature-aware CP methods like Xi et al., 2024; Dabah & Tirer, 2024) would strengthen the empirical grounding, though the authors note those focus on coverage/efficiency, not PSS-accuracy alignment.
- **Are there missing ablations that would materially change conclusions?** 
  1. *Sensitivity to $t$:* The paper claims results for Top-1 sampling but lists $t=3$ as the operational temperature in Sec 5.2. The relationship between $t$, the target curve, and optimization stability is not ablated.
  2. *High-PSS filtering:* Excluding high-PSS samples shifts the optimization objective. An ablation showing Uni CP-ECE with/without this filter would clarify if improvements stem from CPAC or from ignoring difficult samples.
  3. *Coverage trade-offs:* Table 4 shows controlled coverage at 90%, where CPAC increases PSS significantly compared to PS. The paper acknowledges this but argues it "won’t diminish our major contributions." Quantifying the efficiency-calibration trade-off more systematically would be valuable.
- **Are error bars / statistical significance reported?** Yes, standard deviations over five random seeds are reported in all main tables, lending credibility to the trends.
- **Do the results support the claims made, or are they cherry-picked?** Results consistently show reduced Uni CP-ECE. However, the claim that CPAC "maintains the accuracy" (Sec 5.4) is not fully supported by the data. In Table 1 (ViT-L, Clean), accuracy drops from 80.35% (PS) to 77.68% (CPAC), a ~2.7% absolute drop. Under Norm-0.8, it drops from 12.30% to 12.50% (stable), but under Drop-5, it drops from 35.38% to 32.77%. The method improves PSS calibration but at a non-trivial cost to pointwise accuracy. This trade-off should be explicitly acknowledged rather than minimized.
- **Are datasets and evaluation metrics appropriate?** Datasets (CIFAR-100, ImageNet-1k, Topic Classification) are standard. The introduction of Uni CP-ECE vs. Std CP-ECE is thoughtful, though heavily weighting rare, high-PSS bins (Uni) can optimize for curve-fitting aesthetics at the expense of population-level reliability (Std). The choice is justified for "fairness" and "curve-fitting," but ICLR reviewers may question whether optimizing for unweighted bin error aligns with practical decision-making, where sample-weighted risk dominates.

### Writing & Clarity
- **Are there sections that are confusing or poorly explained?** Eq 9 and Algorithm 1 require clarification. The bi-level structure suggests joint optimization, but Algorithm 1 operates as an alternating update. How exactly $\nu$ is computed during the inner loop (line 2 re-runs quantile search each epoch; is it fixed during the $T$ batch updates?), and how gradient propagation accounts for the discrete nature of the $(1-\alpha)$-quantile operator, is unclear. Quantile functions are piecewise constant; the paper mentions using cross-entropy for PSS=1 to avoid zero gradients, but the gradient behavior for PSS>1 under the squared target loss is not explained.
- **Are figures and tables clear and informative?** Reliability diagrams (Figs 2–5, 7–14) effectively visualize PSS vs. accuracy alignment. Tables are dense but readable. Some parser artifacts scramble table borders, but the numerical content remains interpretable. The distinction between PS, PS-Full, and CPAC in table legends could be explicitly expanded for standalone clarity.

### Limitations & Broader Impact
- **Do the authors acknowledge the key limitations?** The authors note in the Conclusion that theoretical convergence/generalization of the bi-level optimization is an open question. They do not discuss the accuracy-PSS trade-off or the heuristic nature of high-PSS filtering as limitations.
- **Are there fundamental limitations they missed?** 
  1. *Accuracy degradation:* Forcing PSS to follow a specific decay curve inherently constrains the logit space. If the model's uncertainty is genuinely poorly aligned, calibration may only be achieved by collapsing useful set information or sacrificing discriminative power.
  2. *Distribution shift robustness:* CPAC fits $(\mathbf{W}, \mathbf{b})$ on a specific calibration split. Conformal prediction guarantees hold under exchangeability, but affine logit transforms learned on one split may not generalize well to shifted distributions, potentially breaking both calibration and coverage guarantees more severely than standard CP.
- **Are there failure modes or negative societal impacts not discussed?** If deployed in high-stakes domains (e.g., medical diagnosis, autonomous driving), a model with artificially calibrated PSS but degraded accuracy could give users false confidence in incorrect predictions when PSS is small. The paper should caution against treating CPAC as a silver bullet for uncertainty and emphasize that calibration here is a property of the specific dataset and preprocessing pipeline.

### Overall Assessment
This paper addresses a timely and practically relevant question: whether the prediction set size in conformal classification reliably reflects predictive accuracy. The authors successfully formalize PSS calibration, propose a bi-level optimization algorithm (CPAC), and demonstrate consistent reductions in unweighted calibration error across multiple architectures and perturbation regimes. The novelty lies in shifting focus from pure coverage/efficiency to the reliability of the set size as an uncertainty signal.

However, several methodological and empirical concerns must be addressed for ICLR standards. The bi-level optimization relies on a heuristic alternating scheme that assumes the conformity quantile remains stable during gradient updates, yet the quantile operator is discrete and non-differentiable. The theoretical justification for the power-law target curve is limited to Dirichlet/logistic-normal assumptions that rarely hold for modern deep classifiers. Most importantly, the experiments reveal a noticeable drop in pointwise accuracy (e.g., ~2–3% on clean ImageNet), which contradicts the claim that CPAC "maintains accuracy." Optimizing for unweighted bin error (Uni CP-ECE) improves curve fitting but ignores the sample distribution, raising questions about its alignment with real-world risk. 

The contribution stands as a conceptually valuable step toward richer conformal calibration, but I recommend **major revisions** focusing on: (1) clearer treatment of gradient flow through the discrete quantile step, (2) explicit quantification and justification of the accuracy-calibration trade-off, (3) ablation on high-PSS filtering and sampling temperature $t$, and (4) discussion of how the logit transformation affect exchangeability and coverage under shift. With these addressed, the paper would be significantly stronger and better positioned for acceptance.

# Neutral Reviewer
## Balanced Review

### Summary
This paper investigates a critical but underexplored property of Conformal Prediction (CP): whether the Prediction Set Size (PSS), which serves as CP's uncertainty metric, is well-calibrated with respect to predictive accuracy. The authors formalize CP calibration via multinomial sampling from prediction sets, derive a power-law target mapping $f(k) = 1/k^\tau$, and propose CP-Aware Calibration (CPAC), a bi-level optimization procedure that adjusts model logits to align PSS with accuracy. Comprehensive experiments across vision transformers, ResNets, and GPT-2 under clean and corrupted settings demonstrate that CP significantly reduces PSS calibration error while preserving nominal coverage guarantees.

### Strengths
1. **Strong Problem Formulation & Clear Definitions:** The paper successfully identifies a blind spot in the CP literature. While coverage guarantees are well-understood, the alignment between PSS and actual correctness has been largely ignored. Definition 4.1 and the two proposed CP-ECE metrics provide a concrete framework for evaluating this alignment, filling a meaningful conceptual gap.
2. **Comprehensive & Rigorous Empirical Validation:** The experimental design is thorough and aligns well with ICLR's emphasis on robust empirical evaluation. The authors test multiple architectures (ResNet50/101, ViT-Base/Large, GPT-2) across three diverse datasets (CIFAR-100, ImageNet-1K, Topic Classification) and systematically evaluate under distribution shifts (Gaussian noise, blur, pixel dropout, text typos). Tables 1, 2, and 5 consistently show CPAC improves calibration metrics without compromising accuracy or coverage.
3. **Practical & Plug-and-Play Method:** CPAC is designed as a lightweight post-processing calibration step that operates directly within the standard split-CP pipeline (Section 4.3, Algorithm 1). By constraining deviations from identity initialization with regularization, it mitigates overfitting to the calibration set and remains computationally feasible for modern deep classifiers.

### Weaknesses
1. **Theoretical Grounding for Calibration Target is Heuristic:** The derivation of $f(k) = 1/k^\tau$ relies on Theorem 4.2, which assumes both the predictive distribution $\mathbf{p}$ and sampling distribution $\mathbf{q}$ are drawn from Dirichlet distributions sharing the exact same underlying mass vector $\mathbf{a}$. This is a strong simplification of real neural network outputs, and while the authors acknowledge this limitation in the "Remark," it weakens the theoretical justification for the target function. The result is presented as a theorem but functions more as an empirical prior.
2. **Approximation in Bi-Level Lacks Convergence Guarantees:** Algorithm 1 freezes the quantile threshold $\nu$ during the inner optimization loop, assuming it remains stable while updating $\mathbf{W}$ and $\mathbf{b}$. The authors concede this is only empirically validated (Section 6) and lack any convergence analysis or sensitivity bounds for $\nu$ drift. Given ICLR's emphasis on rigorous optimization analysis, this approximation is left under-theorized.
3. **Metric Justification and Baseline Comparisons Need Strengthening:** The paper advocates heavily for Uniform CP-ECE over Standard CP-ECE based on fairness/minority-group arguments, but does not connect this choice to downstream risk-sensitive tasks where CP is typically deployed. Furthermore, comparisons are primarily against Platt Scaling (PS) and PS-Full; direct comparisons to other modern post-hoc calibration methods adapted for sets (e.g., temperature scaling variants for CP) or ablations isolating the impact of full-matrix optimization vs. scalar temperature would strengthen the empirical claims.

### Novelty & Significance
**Novelty:** High. The explicit focus on PSS calibration, rather than just coverage or efficiency, introduces a fresh perspective on CP's practical reliability. The multinomial sampling bridge between sets and accuracy, coupled with the $1/k^\tau$ target, is conceptually original, even if the theoretical derivation leans heuristic.
**Significance:** High. As CP becomes a standard tool for uncertainty quantification in production ML systems, ensuring that its uncertainty proxy (set size) reliably tracks true accuracy is crucial for risk-aware decision-making. The proposed method is directly applicable to existing CP pipelines, making it highly relevant to both practitioners and researchers.
**ICLR Fit:** The paper meets ICLR standards through its clear problem identification, extensive multi-modal evaluation, and practical algorithmic contribution. While the theoretical components are not fully rigorous, the empirical depth, reproducibility, and clear writing make it a strong candidate for acceptance.

### Suggestions for Improvement
1. **Reframe Theoretical Derivation or Add Robustness Analysis:** Either soften the presentation of Theorem 4.2 to position it as a structural motivation rather than a rigorous proof, or add a sensitivity analysis showing how deviations from the identical-$\mathbf{a}$ Dirichlet assumption affect the optimal $\tau$. This would ground the empirical success of the power-law target more securely.
2. **Empirical Validation of the $\nu$-Freezing Approximation:** Track and plot the evolution of the quantile threshold $\nu$ during CPAC optimization across different datasets and perturbation levels. Demonstrating empirically that $\nu$ remains stable (or quantifying its variance) would justify the approximate bi-level scheme and address the convergence concern.
3. **Expand Baseline Comparisons & Ablation Studies:** Include direct comparisons against alternative CP calibration baselines such as Conformal Temperature Scaling or APS variants with dynamic temperature. Additionally, provide an ablation isolating the effect of full-matrix optimization vs. a scalar temperature parameter, and clarify the computational overhead of CPAC relative to standard Platt scaling to better inform practical adoption.

# Spark Finder Review
## How to Improve This Paper

### Missing Experiments (top 3-5 only)
1. Test empirical coverage on the held-out test set across all perturbation levels, because Algorithm 1 optimizes $(W, b)$ on the calibration set while fixing $\nu$, which inherently breaks CP’s exchangeability-based $(1-\alpha)$ coverage guarantee.
2. Compare CPAC against modern CP-specific calibration baselines (e.g., RAPS with ECE minimization, Venn-Abers predictors, or conformalized temperature scaling) because standard Platt scaling does not represent the current standard for set-based uncertainty quantification.
3. Sweep experiments across multiple confidence levels ($1-\alpha \in \{0.5, 0.8, 0.9, 0.95, 0.99\}$) rather than reporting a single $\alpha$, since a valid conformal method must demonstrate consistent calibration-coverage trade-offs across the entire risk spectrum.

### Deeper Analysis Needed (top 3-5 only)
1. Validate the Dirichlet and logistic-normal assumptions with empirical goodness-of-fit tests (e.g., QQ plots or KL divergence metrics) because deriving the $1/k^\tau$ target function from unrealistic distributional assumptions makes the target curve arbitrary rather than theoretically grounded.
2. Analyze the trade-off between minimizing Uniform CP-ECE and degrading standard weighted accuracy or set efficiency on the true test distribution, as uniformly weighting high-PSS bins may artificially distort performance on the dominant data manifold.
3. Examine the gradient properties of the bi-level objective, since the lower-level quantile computation $\nu = g(W, b)$ involves discrete set-inclusion thresholds that are non-differentiable and can cause unstable or vanishing gradients without specialized subgradient approximations.

### Visualizations & Case Studies
1. Provide concrete before-and-after prediction set examples for easy, medium, and hard samples to expose whether CPAC genuinely tightens misaligned sets or merely overfits the calibration set to mimic the $1/k^\tau$ curve.
2. Visualize the distribution of logit shifts (magnitude and direction across all $K$ classes) induced by CPAC to confirm that the optimization meaningfully re-ranks class probabilities instead of applying trivial scaling artifacts.
3. Plot reliability diagrams for at least three different $\alpha$ values overlaid in one figure, demonstrating that the improved calibration curves are stable and not just an artifact of tuning to a single operating point.

### Obvious Next Steps
1. Derive a finite-sample bound that quantifies the maximum coverage violation introduced by optimizing $(W, b)$ on $D_{cal}$, as any practical CP modification must provide theoretical or empirically bounded guarantees on exchangeability violations.
2. Evaluate CPAC with alternative non-conformity scores (e.g., RAPS, threshold-straddling scores, or SQR) to prove the bi-level framework generalizes beyond the specific APS scoring routine used in the current implementation.
3. Conduct a systematic hyperparameter sensitivity analysis for $(\tau, \lambda, M_{opt})$ with transfer results across datasets, since the current reliance on per-dataset grid search limits practical reproducibility and deployment.

# Final Consolidated Review
## Summary
This paper investigates a critical underexplored property of Conformal Prediction (CP): whether the Prediction Set Size (PSS), commonly used as a proxy for instance-level uncertainty, is accurately aligned with predictive correctness. The authors formalize PSS calibration via multinomial sampling from prediction sets, propose a power-law calibration target $f(k)=1/k^\tau$ motivated by distributional assumptions, and introduce CPAC, a bi-level optimization procedure that adjusts model logits pre-CP to minimize calibration error. Extensive experiments across vision transformers, ResNets, and GPT-2 under clean and perturbed conditions demonstrate consistent improvements in PSS-accuracy alignment while maintaining nominal coverage.

## Strengths
- **Conceptual Gap Identification & Formalization:** The paper clearly articulates and addresses a blind spot in CP literature. While coverage guarantees are well-theorized, PSS calibration has been largely overlooked. Definition 4.1 and the proposed CP-ECE metrics provide a concrete, reproducible framework for evaluating set-size reliability, filling a meaningful conceptual void.
- **Comprehensive & Rigorous Empirical Validation:** The experimental design is thorough and well-aligned with ICLR standards. The authors evaluate multiple architectures (ResNet, ViT-Base/Large, GPT-2) across three diverse datasets and systematically test under controlled distribution shifts (Gaussian noise, blur, pixel dropout, text typos). Results are reported over five random seeds, demonstrating robust and consistent calibration gains.
- **Practical, Plug-and-Play Algorithmic Contribution:** CPAC is designed as a lightweight post-processing step that integrates directly into standard split-CP pipelines without requiring model retraining. The regularization towards identity initialization effectively mitigates overfitting on the calibration split, making it highly deployable for existing conformal workflows.

## Weaknesses
- **Unquantified Accuracy-Calibration Trade-off Contradicts Claims:** The text asserts that CPAC "maintains the accuracy," yet empirical results (e.g., Table 1 for ViT-L on ImageNet) show measurable accuracy drops in several settings (up to ~2.5% absolute on clean data under full affine optimization). Forcing logits to adhere to a strict $1/k^\tau$ decay inherently constrains the predictive distribution, which can degrade pointwise discriminative power. This trade-off is under-discussed and should be explicitly quantified rather than minimized.
- **Heuristic Target & Approximate Bi-Level Optimization:** The theoretical motivation for $f(k)=1/k^\tau$ relies on Dirichlet/logistic-normal assumptions that the authors themselves label "illustrative" rather than restrictive. Furthermore, Algorithm 1 decouples the discrete CP quantile computation ($\nu$) from the gradient updates by freezing $\nu$ per epoch. While this alternating scheme is empirically stable, the paper lacks any convergence analysis, sensitivity bounds, or discussion of how discrete threshold shifts might destabilize optimization when logit adjustments re-rank conformity scores.
- **Metric Choice Prioritizes Curve-Fitting Over Population Risk:** Heavy emphasis on Uniform CP-ECE optimizes for equal weighting across all PSS bins, justified via "fairness" and curve-fitting aesthetics. However, this can mask suboptimal calibration in low-PSS bins where the majority of samples reside. Given that Std CP-ECE improvements are marginal, it remains unclear whether the proposed optimization translates to improved reliability for population-weighted decision-making, which is the primary use case for CP in high-stakes applications.

## Nice-to-Haves
- **Multi-$\alpha$ Sweep & Alternative Baselines:** Evaluating CPAC across a range of confidence levels (e.g., $1-\alpha \in \{0.5, 0.8, 0.9, 0.95\}$) would demonstrate calibration stability across the risk spectrum. Including direct comparisons against modern CP-aware calibration baselines (e.g., RAPS with ECE minimization, conformalized temperature scaling) would better isolate CPAC's specific advantages over existing set-calibration techniques.
- **Mechanistic Analysis of Optimization:** Tracking the drift of the quantile threshold $\nu$ during CPAC epochs or visualizing the distribution of learned logit shifts would empirically justify the alternating optimization scheme and clarify whether the method meaningfully re-ranks probabilities or relies on trivial scaling.
- **High-PSS Filtering Generalization Check:** Since optimization explicitly excludes samples with very large PSS (e.g., $>400$ on ImageNet), a brief ablation showing how this filtering impacts out-of-sample calibration error would strengthen claims about the method's robustness.

## Novel Insights
The paper successfully shifts the conformal prediction discourse from purely coverage/efficiency metrics to the *reliability of the uncertainty proxy itself*. By bridging set-valued predictions and pointwise correctness through temperature-scaled multinomial sampling, the authors reveal that modern classifiers (especially pre-trained ones) often exhibit counterintuitive PSS-accuracy misalignments under perturbations. The proposed power-law target and bi-level affine optimization provide a practical pathway to enforce monotonic uncertainty calibration post-hoc, offering a conceptually fresh toolkit for risk-aware CP deployment.

## Suggestions
- **Revise Claims & Explicitly Quantify Trade-offs:** Update the abstract and Section 5.4 to acknowledge the observed accuracy drops under CPAC. Include a concise analysis or table explicitly reporting the calibration-accuracy-efficiency trade-off so practitioners can make informed deployment decisions.
- **Clarify Optimization Dynamics:** Add a short paragraph or appendix figure demonstrating the stability of the quantile $\nu$ across optimization epochs on a representative dataset. This will empirically validate the alternating scheme assumption and address convergence concerns without requiring full theoretical proofs.
- **Contextualize Uniform CP-ECE Usage:** Explicitly discuss when practitioners should prioritize Uniform vs. Standard CP-ECE. If the goal is population-level risk, consider reporting a hybrid metric or acknowledging that optimizing for uniform bin alignment may not always align with downstream utility.
