# Harsh Critic Review
## Section-by-Section Critical Review

### Title & Abstract
- **Does the title accurately reflect the contribution?** Yes, it clearly indicates the focus on adversarial attacks applied to Tensor Ring (TR) decomposition.
- **Does the abstract clearly state the problem, method, and key results?** Yes. It identifies the gap (vulnerability of TR decomposition is unstudied), introduces the asymmetric max-min formulation (AdaTR) and the approximate gradient variant (FAG-AdaTR), and summarizes empirical validation across decomposition, completion, and recommendation tasks.
- **Are any claims in the abstract unsupported by the paper?** The claim that this is the first systematic study of TR vulnerability is somewhat strong given prior work on adversarial matrix factorization (NMF) and PCA, but within the TR literature specifically, it is acceptable. However, the abstract implies the methods broadly "validate attack effectiveness," which is supported by experiments, though the transferability claims need tighter bounds in the main text.

### Introduction & Motivation
- **Is the problem well-motivated? Is the gap in prior work clearly identified?** The motivation is reasonable: adversarial robustness has been studied for learned models and NMF, but optimization-based, non-parametric decompositions like TR remain underexplored in adversarial settings. The gap is adequately framed.
- **Are the contributions clearly stated and accurate?** Contributions are clearly bulleted. However, the claim that this provides the "first evidence that tensor decomposition models are susceptible to adversarial attacks" is an overreach. Adversarial attacks on PCA, subspace learning, and matrix completion have been extensively documented (e.g., Li et al., 2020; Pimentel-Alarcón et al., 2017), which already establish vulnerability of low-rank approximations. The contribution is better framed as extending this to *Tensor Ring* structures with a bilevel optimization framework.
- **Does the introduction over-claim or under-sell?** Slightly over-claims on novelty/vulnerability uniqueness. Otherwise, the introduction under-sells the practical challenge: differentiating through iterative, non-convex ALS solvers is non-trivial, and this practical contribution is not highlighted until Section 4.

### Method / Approach
- **Is the method clearly described and reproducible?** The bilevel formulation in Eq. (10) is clear. However, Section 4.2 states gradients are computed via "PyTorch’s automatic differentiation engine" over TR-ALS updates. TR-ALS relies on closed-form least-squares steps (Eq. 8) involving pseudo-inverses. The paper does not explain how autodiff is implemented through these non-differentiable or numerically unstable operations (e.g., SVD/pinv backprop). This omission hinders reproducibility.
- **Are key assumptions stated and justified?** 
  - **FAG-AdaTR Assumption (Sec 4.3):** The method assumes $\mathbf{G}_n^{(=t=T-1)}(E)$ and $\mathbf{G}_n^{(=t=T)}(E)$ are independent of $E$ when deriving Eq. (16). This is a severe approximation: if $G$ is treated as fixed w.r.t $E$, the gradient no longer captures how the defense adapts to perturbations. The paper claims this "reduces computational overhead" but does not quantify the approximation error or justify why the resulting gradient direction remains effective. Table 4 shows FAG-AdaTR sometimes underperforms AdaTR (e.g., Video Table 8), suggesting this assumption is non-negligible and lacks theoretical backing.
  - **ATTR collapse claim (Sec 4.1):** The claim that ATTR "degenerates into maximizing the difference between successive perturbations" is stated without derivation or citation.
- **Are there logical gaps in the derivation or reasoning?** Theorem 1's proof (App B) contains dimensional inconsistencies. The budget constraint is $\|E\|_F^2 \le \epsilon$, yet Eq. (6) states $\epsilon < \sqrt{\delta - \|R_2\|_F}$. If $\delta = \|R_1\|_F$ (per Eq 20), then $\sqrt{\delta}$ has units of $\sqrt{\text{error}}$, while $\epsilon$ bounds squared error. The condition should read $\sqrt{\epsilon} < \sqrt{\delta} - \|R_2\|_F$, assuming $\delta = \|R_1\|_F^2$ or similar. As written, the inequality mixes units and obscures the regime where ATTR improves performance.
- **Are there edge cases or failure modes not discussed?** The inner TR-ALS problem is non-convex. Bilevel optimization assumes the inner solver converges to a continuous, differentiable trajectory $\mathcal{G}^{(T)}(E)$. In practice, small $E$ could push ALS to a different local minimum or alter convergence paths discontinuously, invalidating smoothness assumptions required for Theorem 2. This is not discussed.

### Experiments & Results
- **Do the experiments actually test the paper's claims?** Yes, the experiments cover decomposition, completion, and recommendation. However, they lack comparisons to standard adversarial baselines. Gradient ascent on reconstruction error (without unrolling/ALS adaptation) is a strong, standard baseline often called PGD or direct gradient attack. The paper only compares to ATTR (which is shown to be flawed) and Gaussian noise. Without a direct PGD/FW baseline, it's unclear if the asymmetric bilevel design offers meaningful gains over a simple first-order attack.
- **Are baselines appropriate and fairly compared?** ATTR is a fair theoretical baseline. The defense methods (TRPCA-TNN, HQTRC, etc.) are appropriate for transferability tests. However, for the RecSys task (Sec 5.4), the paper abruptly switches to NMF (AdaNMF) without justification. TR decomposition is widely used in recommender systems (user×item×context tensors). Evaluating AdaTR on actual TR-based recommendation models would have been more consistent and rigorous.
- **Are there missing ablations that would materially change conclusions?** Yes. 
  1. **Inner-loop iterations ($T_{in}$):** How many ALS steps are needed before gradients stabilize? If $T_{in}$ is too small, $\mathcal{G}^{(T)}(E)$ is a poor approximation of the final decomposition, potentially weakening the attack. 
  2. **Perturbation scale normalization:** $\epsilon=500$ or $\epsilon=66$ is reported, but $\|X\|_F$ is not. Is $\epsilon/\|X\|_F \approx 0.01$ or $0.5$? Without signal-to-perturbation ratio (e.g., dB or relative $\ell_F$ norm), perceptibility claims ("tiny perturbations") are ungrounded. 
  3. **Ablation of rank mismatch:** Sec 5.5 tests robustness to varying defense ranks, but attack rank is fixed to 5. A more comprehensive study of target-vs-defense rank gaps would strengthen the vulnerability claims.
- **Are error bars / statistical significance reported?** Table 1 reports mean±var, which is good. However, Tables 2, 5, 6, 8, 9, and 10 lack variance or statistical significance testing. Given the stochasticity in tensor initialization and sampling (noted in App H.2), reporting only point estimates weakens confidence in marginal improvements.
- **Do results support the claims, or are they cherry-picked?** Results generally support the main claim that AdaTR degrades reconstruction more than baselines. However, in Table 2 (RecSys), ATNMF actually achieves higher RMSE degradation than AdaNMF, while AdaNMF only wins on Precision/Recall. The paper labels AdaNMF as achieving "superior results" but selectively highlights different metrics per method. A unified statement of which metric defines "successful attack" is needed.

### Writing & Clarity
- **Are there sections that are confusing or poorly explained?** Sections 4.2 and 4.3 are conceptually dense but lack mathematical rigor in key transitions. Specifically, Eq. (16) in FAG-AdaTR presents an explicit gradient formula, but the derivation skips the step where mode-$n$ unfolding interacts with the perturbation tensor. The notation $\mathbf{G}_n^{(=t)}(E)$ is used inconsistently with the chain rule notation earlier. Clarifying the difference between exact gradient (via autodiff) vs. approximate gradient (via frozen factors) with a clear algorithmic comparison box would help.
- **Are figures and tables clear and informative?** Figures 1, 3, 4, 5 are clear and support the narrative. Table 1 is well-structured. Table 2's mixed metric highlighting is slightly confusing (bold indicates best attack, but RMSE $\uparrow$ vs P@10 $\downarrow$ requires careful reading). I am intentionally ignoring parser-induced garbling in tables and equations per instructions.

### Limitations & Broader Impact
- **Do the authors acknowledge the key limitations?** The appendix (H.3) reports runtime and peak memory, acknowledging AdaTR's high overhead (up to 8.57GB for video). This is appropriately flagged.
- **Are there fundamental limitations they missed?** Yes. The method assumes white-box access to the tensor decomposition solver's update rules and hyperparameters (learning rate $\eta$, rank $R$, inner iterations $T_{in}$). In many real-world scenarios, attackers only have black-box access or face randomized ranks/hyperparameters. The paper does not discuss black-box adaptability or hyperparameter sensitivity from an attacker's perspective.
- **Are there failure modes or negative societal impacts not discussed?** The failure mode where perturbations cause the inner ALS solver to diverge or hit a completely unrelated local minimum (destroying the attack objective rather than maximizing it) is not analyzed. Societal impact is briefly noted (security of compression/recommendation), but the paper does not discuss how these attacks could impact critical tensor-based pipelines in healthcare (e.g., neuroimaging tensor decomposition) or safety-critical systems, which would be valuable for ICLR's ethics standards.

### Overall Assessment
This paper tackles a relevant and timely problem: the adversarial vulnerability of non-parametric, optimization-based tensor decomposition methods, specifically Tensor Ring. The empirical study is extensive, covering images, videos, tensor completion, and recommendation, with strong visual and numerical evidence that the proposed AdaTR attacks significantly degrade reconstruction quality compared to naive baselines. However, several substantive issues must be addressed before the paper meets ICLR's acceptance bar. First, the claims of novelty and "first evidence" of vulnerability are overstated given prior work on adversarial PCA/NMF. Second, the theoretical foundation has inconsistencies, notably Theorem 1's dimensional mismatch and the lack of rigorous justification for FAG-AdaTR's independence assumption, which decouples the gradient from the adaptive defense. Third, the experiments miss critical baselines (standard PGD/first-order attacks on the reconstruction objective) and fail to contextualize perturbation budgets relative to data scale, making "tiny perturbation" claims unverifiable. Finally, the abrupt switch to NMF for the recommendation task, while practically motivated, weakens the paper's internal consistency. With clearer justification of approximations, inclusion of standard adversarial baselines, proper normalization of $\epsilon$, and discussion of black-box/non-convexity limitations, this work would make a solid contribution to the adversarial robustness and tensor learning literature. In its current form, the empirical strength is notable, but theoretical and experimental gaps require significant revision.

# Neutral Reviewer
## Balanced Review

### Summary
This paper investigates the vulnerability of Tensor Ring (TR) decomposition to adversarial perturbations and proposes AdaTR, an asymmetric bilevel optimization framework that directly maximizes the low-rank reconstruction error. To alleviate the computational burden of iterative gradient tracking, a Faster Approximate Gradient variant (FAG-AdaTR) is introduced with closed-form updates. The authors provide formal convergence guarantees and empirically validate the attacks across tensor decomposition, completion, and recommender system tasks, demonstrating substantial degradation in reconstruction and recommendation metrics under small perturbation budgets.

### Strengths
1. **Clear identification of prior failure modes:** The authors rigorously prove (Theorem 1) why symmetric min-max formulations (ATTR) can paradoxically *improve* reconstruction under small perturbations, providing a strong theoretical motivation for the asymmetric max-min design.
2. **Practical algorithmic efficiency:** FAG-AdaTR decouples perturbation dependencies across ALS iterations via an approximate gradient, achieving significant speedups and memory reductions (Table 4) while preserving attack strength (Table 1, Fig. 3).
3. **Comprehensive empirical robustness testing:** The attack's transferability is evaluated across multiple TR-based defenses (TRPCA-TNN, TRNNM, HQTRC, LRTC-TV), and sensitivity to TR-rank, lossy compression (JPEG/PNG), and perturbation norms ($F$ vs $L_\infty$) is thoroughly analyzed (Sec. 5.5–5.6, Appendix H.6).
4. **Theoretical convergence guarantees:** The paper provides structured proofs for monotonic ascent, Cauchy convergence, and KKT stationarity for both AdaTR and FAG-AdaTR (Theorems 2–4), aligning with ICLR's emphasis on theoretically grounded algorithmic design.

### Weaknesses
1. **Unverified differentiability assumption:** Theorem 2 assumes the map $E \mapsto \mathcal{G}^{(T)}(E)$ is differentiable with bounded Jacobian. However, TR-ALS relies on pseudo-inverses ($\mathbf{G}_{\neq n}^\dagger$), which are non-differentiable at singular points. The analysis lacks discussion of degenerate cases or regularization needed to ensure differentiability in practice.
2. **Approximation error not quantified empirically or theoretically:** FAG-AdaTR's approximate stationarity bound $\mathcal{O}(\sqrt{\epsilon}\epsilon_g)$ depends on the gradient mismatch $\epsilon_g = \|\nabla g(E) - \nabla\tilde{g}(E)\|_F$, but $\epsilon_g$ is neither theoretically bounded with respect to iterations nor measured in experiments. Without this, the practical trade-off between speed and optimality remains opaque.
3. **Limited adversarial baselines:** The paper only compares against ATTR and Gaussian noise. Missing are standard first-order attacks adapted to tensors (e.g., PGD with iterative Frobenius/L_inf projection, or step-size tuned FGSM variants), making it difficult to isolate the true advantage of the asymmetric formulation vs. generic gradient ascent.
4. **Dataset scale and threat model realism:** Experiments are confined to 8 images, 7 short video clips, and MovieLens-100K. While sufficient for a proof-of-concept, ICLR typically expects broader validation. Additionally, the practical attack scenario (how an adversary would inject bounded perturbations into collaborative filtering matrices or live video streams) is under-specified, limiting the real-world security implications.

### Novelty & Significance
**Novelty** is solid: shifting from symmetric adversarial training to an asymmetric bilevel attack tailored for non-parametric, iterative tensor factorization is a meaningful conceptual contribution. The explicit targeting of low-rank approximation error rather than factor deviation fills a clear gap in the literature. **Clarity** is generally high; the mathematical progression is logical, though some notation inconsistencies (e.g., unfolding vs. unfolding dagger, occasional typographical errors like "extention"/"tecomositions") slightly hinder readability. **Reproducibility** is well-supported via appendices containing algorithm pseudocode, hyperparameter tables, and convergence plots; however, meeting ICLR's reproducibility bar would be strengthened by releasing implementation code or a Docker environment. **Significance** is moderate-to-high within the tensor learning and robust optimization communities, highlighting an understudied vulnerability in data-driven low-rank modeling. While immediate impact on mainstream deep learning is limited, the findings are relevant for tensor-compressed models, video analysis pipelines, and recommendation systems.

### Suggestions for Improvement
1. **Address differentiability rigorously:** Discuss how non-differentiability of pseudo-inverses at singularities is handled (e.g., via Tikhonov regularization $\mathbf{G}^\dagger \approx (\mathbf{G}^\top\mathbf{G}+\lambda\mathbf{I})^{-1}\mathbf{G}^\top$) and verify whether the convergence proofs remain valid under smoothed ALS updates.
2. **Empirically measure gradient mismatch:** Track and report $\epsilon_g = \|\nabla g(E_t) - \nabla\tilde{g}(E_t)\|_F$ across outer iterations in a small ablation. This will validate the practical reliability of FAG-AdaTR and contextualize the $\mathcal{O}(\sqrt{\epsilon}\epsilon_g)$ approximation bound.
3. **Include standard adversarial baselines:** Adapt PGD and FGSM to the tensor input space with matched budgets and step projections, and add these to Tables 1 and 2. This will demonstrate that the performance gap is truly due to the asymmetric objective rather than just a stronger optimizer.
4. **Clarify threat model and expand scope:** Explicitly define the adversary's access capabilities and realistic injection vectors for tensor-structured data. If space allows, include one larger dataset (e.g., MovieLens-1M, a standard video benchmark like UCF-101 subsets, or a public tensor recommendation dataset) to align with ICLR's empirical scale expectations.

## How to Improve This Paper

### Missing Experiments (top 3-5 only)
1. Implement and compare against a standard projected gradient ascent (PGD) attack directly targeting the TR-ALS reconstruction loss; without this standard gradient-based baseline, the claim that the proposed asymmetric bilevel formulation is necessary or superior to simple iterative attacks is unsupported.
2. Evaluate on modern, large-scale tensor datasets (e.g., larger recommendation benchmarks or high-resolution video datasets) instead of relying primarily on DIV2K, MovieLens-100K, and synthetic data; the current scale is insufficient to validate real-world vulnerability claims at an ICLR standard.
3. Ablate the number of inner ALS iterations (`T_in`) and explicitly report the empirical gradient error `||∇g - ∇g̃||` for FAG-AdaTR to prove the approximate gradient assumption actually preserves attack effectiveness as computational costs drop.

### Deeper Analysis Needed (top 3-5 only)
1. Conduct a spectral or condition number analysis of the mode-n unfoldings before and after the attack to explain the mechanistic reason why the perturbation amplifies reconstruction error, rather than treating the low-rank breakdown as a black box.
2. Address the fundamental non-differentiability of the ALS pseudoinverse in your convergence theorems; the claim that `E ↦ G(E)` is differentiable with a bounded Jacobian is mathematically invalid at rank-deficient points, severely undermining the theoretical convergence guarantees.
3. Empirically validate the `O(√(ϵεg))` approximate stationarity bound (Theorem 4) by measuring the actual distance to KKT conditions across iterations; without this, the theoretical convergence claims for FAG-AdaTR remain purely asymptotic and unverified.

### Visualizations & Case Studies
1. Visualize the learned adversarial perturbation tensor `E` itself, not just the attacked output, to reveal whether the method injects structured artifacts or behaves like high-frequency noise; this is essential to verify if the attack exploits tensor-specific structural vulnerabilities.
2. Provide a side-by-side failure case where the independence approximation in FAG-AdaTR clearly misestimates the gradient direction compared to the full backpropagation of AdaTR, exposing the practical limits of the speedup.

### Obvious Next Steps
1. Incorporate Tikhonov regularization or a smooth surrogate for the matrix pseudoinverse in the inner ALS loop to make the bilevel objective well-posed and align the implementation with the smoothness assumptions required by your proofs.
2. Define a unified, explicit threat model (attacker knowledge, precise norm constraints, and evaluation protocol) and apply it consistently across modalities instead of switching between `F`-norm and `L∞`-norm post-hoc in the appendix.
3. Test the transferability of attacks generated against TR-ALS to other decomposition optimizers (e.g., ADMM, randomized ALS, or deep tensor autoencoders) to determine if the vulnerability stems from the TR model structure or the specific ALS optimization dynamics.

# Final Consolidated Review
## Summary
This paper introduces AdaTR, an asymmetric bilevel optimization framework designed to attack Tensor Ring (TR) decomposition by directly maximizing the low-rank reconstruction error with respect to an adversarial perturbation tensor. To mitigate the computational bottleneck of backpropagating through iterative ALS updates, the authors propose FAG-AdaTR, which derives a closed-form approximate gradient by decoupling factor dependencies. The paper provides formal convergence guarantees, proves why symmetric adversarial training baselines collapse for this problem, and empirically demonstrates substantial degradation across decomposition, completion, and recommendation tasks.

## Strengths
- **Correct problem formulation for non-parametric solvers:** The authors rigorously identify why symmetric min-max objectives (ATTR) fail for TR decomposition—degenerating into perturbation difference maximization rather than degradation (Theorem 1). The shift to an asymmetric max-min bilevel design properly externalizes the perturbation as the attacker while treating the deterministic factorization solver as an inner loop.
- **Practical efficiency with theoretical grounding:** FAG-AdaTR significantly reduces peak memory and runtime (Table 4) by approximating the gradient through frozen factor updates. The method is supported by convergence analysis (Theorems 2–4) demonstrating monotonic ascent and KKT stationarity, aligning algorithmic design with optimization theory.
- **Broad empirical validation across defenses and modalities:** Experiments systematically evaluate attack transferability against robust TR variants (TRPCA-TNN, HQTRC, LRTC-TV), TR-rank sensitivity, and lossy compression (JPEG/PNG). The attacks consistently outperform baselines and remain effective across visual and tabular tensor data.

## Weaknesses
- **Absence of standard first-order adversarial baselines:** The evaluation only compares against the flawed ATTR formulation and Gaussian noise. A direct projected gradient ascent (PGD) targeting the reconstruction objective $\min_{\mathcal{G}} \|X+E - \text{TR}(\mathcal{G})\|_F$ is missing. Without this critical baseline, the paper cannot disentangle gains attributable to the bilevel optimization structure from those achieved by a well-tuned iterative optimizer. This omission severely weakens the core claim that asymmetric unrolling is necessary for effective attack.
- **Theoretical assumptions contradict actual solver dynamics:** The convergence proofs assume the mapping $E \mapsto \mathcal{G}^{(T)}(E)$ is differentiable with a bounded Jacobian. However, TR-ALS relies on matrix pseudo-inverses ($\dagger$), which are provably non-differentiable at singular/rank-deficient points and numerically unstable near zero singular values. The paper provides no smoothing, regularization, or discussion of degenerate cases, rendering the formal guarantees mathematically misaligned with the non-smooth optimization reality.
- **Unquantified approximation error in FAG-AdaTR:** The faster variant assumes $\mathbf{G}_n^{(=t)}(E)$ is independent of $E$ to derive Eq. (16), completely ignoring the true dependency path. The resulting gradient mismatch $\epsilon_g$ is neither theoretically bounded relative to iteration depth nor empirically tracked. Table 8 and Fig. 3 reveal noticeable performance gaps between AdaTR and FAG-AdaTR, confirming the approximation is non-negligible, yet the paper lacks ablation or error monitoring to justify the speed-accuracy trade-off.
- **Perturbation budgets lack signal context:** Attack strengths are reported as absolute $\epsilon$ values (e.g., $\epsilon=500, 66$), with no normalization against $\|X\|_F$ or SNR. Given inputs are normalized to $[0,1]$ and span millions of elements, these budgets correspond to substantial noise injections, contradicting claims of "tiny" or stealthy perturbations. Without relative budget reporting, the practical threat model and perceptibility claims are unverifiable.

## Nice-to-Haves
- Spectral or condition number analysis of mode-$n$ unfoldings pre/post-attack to mechanistically explain how the perturbation amplifies reconstruction error.
- Direct visualization of the learned perturbation tensor $E$ to distinguish structured adversarial artifacts from generic high-frequency noise.
- Consistent evaluation using TR decomposition for the recommendation task (rather than switching to NMF), or explicit justification for why tensor factorization was abandoned in that setting.
- Testing attack transferability to non-ALS TR optimizers (e.g., randomized ALS, ADMM, or deep tensor autoencoders) to isolate whether vulnerability stems from model structure or specific solver dynamics.

## Novel Insights
The paper's most substantive conceptual contribution is recognizing that adversarial robustness analysis for *non-parametric, iterative factorization* models cannot be naively ported from parametric neural network settings. Because TR decomposition recomputes factors from scratch for each input via ALS, a symmetric adversarial training objective paradoxically acts as a regularizer rather than an attack, converging to a cleaner low-rank fit under small budgets. The proposed asymmetric bilevel formulation correctly treats the factorization solver as a fixed computational graph while optimizing the perturbation to maximize the final reconstruction residual. This perspective generalizes naturally to other ALS-driven tensor/network decompositions where parameters are emergent rather than stored.

## Potentially Missed Related Work
- **Adversarial Subspace/PCA Attacks (e.g., Li et al., 2020; Pimentel-Alarcón et al., 2017):** Directly relevant for contextualizing low-rank vulnerability beyond NMF and for comparing principal angle maximization vs. reconstruction error objectives (partially addressed in Appendix, but merits earlier positioning).
- **Implicit Differentiation for Iterative Solvers & Smooth Pseudo-inverse Approximations (e.g., Kreutz-Delgado, 2009; Blondel et al., 2020/2022):** Highly relevant for rigorously differentiating through ALS updates, addressing the non-smoothness gap in the convergence proofs, and enabling more accurate gradient unrolling without full backpropagation.

## Suggestions
1. **Implement and report a standard tensor-PGD baseline:** Run iterative gradient ascent with Frobenius/$L_\infty$ projection directly on the TR-ALS reconstruction loss. This will isolate whether the asymmetric bilevel structure provides meaningful gains over first-order optimization.
2. **Align theory with implementation regarding non-differentiability:** Incorporate Tikhonov regularization $(\mathbf{G}^\top\mathbf{G} + \lambda\mathbf{I})^{-1}\mathbf{G}^\top$ into the inner ALS loop to ensure the $E \mapsto \mathcal{G}(E)$ map is Lipschitz and differentiable, or revise the convergence proofs to accommodate non-smooth dynamics and subgradient conditions.
3. **Normalize perturbation budgets and track approximation error:** Report $\epsilon / \|X\|_F$ or input-aware SNR to ground "tiny perturbation" claims. Additionally, plot $\|\nabla g(E_t) - \nabla\tilde{g}(E_t)\|_F$ across iterations in an ablation to empirically validate Theorem 4's $\mathcal{O}(\sqrt{\epsilon}\epsilon_g)$ bound and clarify the reliability of FAG-AdaTR.
