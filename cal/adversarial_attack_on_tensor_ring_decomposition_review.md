# Harsh Critic Review
## Section-by-Section Critical Review

### Title & Abstract
- The title accurately reflects the core contribution. It correctly signals that the paper focuses on adversarial attacks specifically targeting Tensor Ring (TR) decomposition.
- The abstract clearly states the problem (vulnerability of TR decomposition), the proposed method (asymmetric max-min objective AdaTR, plus the faster FAG-AdaTR), and the key results (increased reconstruction error across decomposition, completion, and recommendation tasks, supported by convergence guarantees).
- The claim of providing the "first evidence that tensor decomposition models are susceptible to adversarial attacks" is slightly overbroad. Established literature on adversarial attacks for PCA and NMF already demonstrates vulnerability for matrix factorization. The abstract should be scoped to clarify that the novelty lies in the higher-order TR structure and the asymmetric bilevel formulation, rather than claiming the first discovery of vulnerability in decomposition models broadly.

### Introduction & Motivation
- The problem is well-motivated. The gap in systematically studying adversarial perturbations for tensor-specific low-rank approximations is clearly identified, and the transition from matrix (ATNMF) to tensor (ATTR) baselines is logical.
- The contributions are explicitly listed and align with the method and experiments.
- RQ1 ("Are matrix/tensor factorization vulnerable to adversarial attacks?") is answered somewhat trivially by construction: any optimization-based method targeting reconstruction error will increase it. The real scientific question is not binary susceptibility, but rather the *efficiency*, *robustness*, and *failure modes* of symmetric vs. asymmetric adversarial objectives. The introduction should reframe RQ1 to focus on why standard symmetric formulations fail to degrade performance meaningfully and under what structural conditions tensor models are highly sensitive. The motivation would be strengthened by explicitly contrasting tensor ring's cyclical dependency structure with linear matrix factorizations, explaining why adversarial propagation differs fundamentally.

### Method / Approach
- The method is generally well-described and reproducible, but several key theoretical and algorithmic assumptions require more rigor.
- Section 4.1 claims that ATTR "degenerates into maximizing the difference between successive perturbations, i.e., $\|E^{(t)} - E^{(t-1)}\|_F$." This is a pivotal assertion driving the proposed asymmetric objective, yet it is neither derived nor empirically illustrated. A brief theoretical justification or empirical gradient trajectory analysis is needed.
- Theorem 1's condition $\epsilon < \sqrt{\delta} - \|R_2\|_F$ (Eq. 6) relies on quantities ($\delta$, $R_2$) that are unknown a priori. While the proof in Appendix B is mathematically sound (triangle inequality), the theorem is descriptive rather than actionable. Practitioners cannot use it to predict when ATTR will fail or succeed. Clarifying whether this condition holds in the reported experiments would strengthen its relevance.
- The FAG-AdaTR approximation (Section 4.3) assumes the TR factors $\mathbf{G}^{(T)}(E)$ are independent of $E$ during gradient computation. This is a standard stop-gradient approximation, but the paper does not quantify the induced gradient error, nor does it discuss when this assumption breaks (e.g., slow ALS convergence, ill-conditioned ranks). The derivation of the closed-form gradient in Eq. (16) skips intermediate algebraic steps, hindering verification.
- The convergence guarantees (Theorems 2–4) assume differentiability and Lipschitz continuity of the map $E \mapsto \mathbf{G}^{(T)}(E)$. Since TR-ALS relies on mode unfoldings and pseudo-inverses, these properties can fail in rank-deficient or highly ill-conditioned regimes. Explicit discussion of how the method behaves near singularity boundaries would improve theoretical completeness.

### Experiments & Results
- The experiments demonstrate attack effectiveness, but lack critical baselines expected in ICLR adversarial literature. Comparing only against ATTR and Gaussian Noise omits standard white-box optimization attacks such as PGD, FGSM, or AutoAttack applied directly to the TR reconstruction loss. Without these, it is unclear whether the asymmetric bilevel formulation provides meaningful gains beyond standard gradient ascent with projection.
- The perturbation budget $\epsilon=500$ for color images (normalized to $[0,1]$, size $\sim$2M elements) is reported without scaling context. While normalized for tensor size, the actual per-element/pixel perturbation magnitude is not quantified, making perceptual imperceptibility hard to assess. The Appendix H.6 $L_\infty$ analysis is valuable, but the main experiments should translate $F$-norm budgets into interpretable SNR or average pixel-change metrics.
- Key ablations are missing. There is no quantitative analysis of how FAG-AdaTR's approximation quality degrades relative to AdaTR as a function of $T_{\text{in}}$ or TR rank. A direct comparison of gradient error, step-wise objective improvement, or computational cost vs. attack strength would materially justify the faster variant.
- Section 5.4 extends the attack to recommender systems but replaces TR with NMF (AdaNMF). While labeled as an extension, this drifts from the tensor ring focus. If the goal is to demonstrate TR vulnerability in recommendation settings, experiments should maintain TR decomposition on appropriately reshaped interaction tensors.
- Error bars (mean ± variance) are appropriately reported, and results do not appear cherry-picked. However, statistical significance testing for transferability claims (Table 1) would strengthen the assertion that AdaTR "consistently produces the strongest attack results" across defenses.

### Writing & Clarity
- The narrative flow is generally clear, but Section 4.1's unsupported claim about ATTR's degeneration creates a logical gap. The introduction and method sections would benefit from a short empirical or analytical illustration of why symmetric objectives fail before presenting Eq. (10).
- Figures and tables are informative. Figure 1 cleanly contrasts methods under varying budgets. Table 1/2 present metrics clearly. Figure 6 (convergence plots) lacks run variance, which is important for assessing empirical stability.
- Notation transitions between tensor $\mathcal{E}$ and mode-$n$ unfolded matrices $\mathbf{E}_{[n]}$ in Section 4.3 could be more explicitly mapped. The reuse of $g$ for both the maximization objective and loss function across sections occasionally requires careful tracking but does not fundamentally impede understanding.

### Limitations & Broader Impact
- The authors acknowledge computational overhead and address it with FAG-AdaTR, but omit a critical limitation: the attack is strictly white-box. It requires full access to the ALS update loop for gradient computation or approximation. Real-world tensor pipelines are often deployed as black-box APIs or run offline, which changes the threat model considerably. This should be explicitly discussed.
- Defense strategies are underexplored. While ATTR is tested as a potential defense in Appendix H.5, no robust decomposition methodology is proposed or critically examined. Brief discussion of potential mitigations (e.g., input purification, randomized smoothing, or robust tensor completion) would contextualize the attack's practical impact.
- The ethics statement is standard but brief. Given the stated applications to recommender systems and LLM compression, a slightly deeper reflection on how adversarially perturbed tensors could degrade downstream decision-making or fairness in deployed systems would align well with ICLR's broader impact expectations, even if kept high-level.

### Overall Assessment
This paper presents a well-motivated and technically structured framework for adversarial attacks on tensor ring decomposition, introducing an asymmetric bilevel objective that effectively overcomes shortcomings of symmetric min-max training. The convergence analysis and empirical validation across vision and recommendation tasks are strong, and the faster approximate gradient variant addresses a practical bottleneck. However, to meet ICLR's acceptance bar, the paper must address several substantive gaps: it lacks standard adversarial baselines (PGD/FGSM) to properly contextualize the claimed superiority, relies on an under-justified assumption about ATTR's degeneration, and does not quantify the approximation error or failure regimes of FAG-AdaTR. The theoretical claims, while formally correct, depend on unobservable residuals and smoothness conditions that need empirical or analytical validation near ill-conditioned ranks. With stronger comparative baselines, quantitative ablations of the gradient approximation, and tighter scoping of novelty relative to established matrix factorization attacks, this work would constitute a solid ICLR contribution. As currently written, the contribution is promising but requires these revisions to fully substantiate its empirical and theoretical claims.

# Neutral Reviewer
## Balanced Review

### Summary
This paper proposes AdaTR, an asymmetric bilevel adversarial attack framework for Tensor Ring (TR) decomposition that directly maximizes reconstruction error by optimizing against the low-rank approximation solver's output. To mitigate the high computational cost of backpropagating through iterative ALS updates, the authors introduce FAG-AdaTR, a closed-form approximate gradient variant with provable convergence guarantees. Comprehensive experiments across image/video decomposition, tensor completion, and recommendation tasks demonstrate that small perturbations can severely degrade performance and transfer to various tensor-based downstream methods.

### Strengths
1. **Strong Theoretical Motivation & Problem Formulation:** The paper correctly identifies a fundamental flaw in symmetric min-max formulations (ATTR) for non-parametric decomposition, proving in Theorem 1 that small perturbations can paradoxically improve reconstruction. This rigorously justifies the proposed asymmetric max-min bilevel objective (Eq. 10).
2. **Practical & Efficient Algorithm Design:** Recognizing the memory bottleneck of autodiff through ALS loops, FAG-AdaTR (Section 4.3) derives a tractable closed-form gradient. The efficiency gains are quantified in Appendix H.3, showing substantial reductions in runtime and peak memory (e.g., from 8.57 GB to 999 MB on video data) without sacrificing attack strength.
3. **Rigorous Convergence Analysis:** The paper provides thorough theoretical guarantees (Theorems 2–4 and supporting Lemmas in Appendices C–E) for both the exact and approximate methods, proving monotonic ascent, Cauchy convergence, and KKT stationarity. This level of mathematical rigor is commendable for an adversarial paper.
4. **Broad Empirical Validation:** Experiments span multiple domains (color images, videos, recommender systems), tasks (decomposition, completion), and perturbation types ($F$-norm, $L_\infty$). Results consistently show performance degradation over baselines, with Appendix H.7 and H.4 confirming effectiveness against alternative attack strategies and tensor formats.

### Weaknesses
1. **Weakened Baseline Comparisons:** The primary adversary for comparison is ATTR, which the authors themselves prove is fundamentally flawed (Theorem 1). While Appendix H.7 includes a principal angle attack, the paper lacks comparisons with strong, adaptive adversaries tailored to tensor structures (e.g., PGD-style attacks on the factorized space or recent adversarial matrix factorization methods). This makes the performance gains appear somewhat inflated.
2. **Under-specified Threat Model & Perturbation Realism:** The attack predominantly uses an unconstrained Frobenius norm, which allows perturbations to concentrate spatially, potentially creating visually obvious artifacts (as seen in Appendix Fig. 8). While Appendix H.6 explores $L_\infty$ constraints, the practical stealthiness and physical-world feasibility of the attack are not thoroughly discussed, especially for recommendation systems where assuming direct white-box access to inject perturbations into the observed rating tensor is a strong assumption.
3. **Lack of Quantitative Analysis on Gradient Approximation Error:** FAG-AdaTR's core assumption treats core factors as independent of $E$ beyond the current step (Section 4.3). While Theorem 4 bounds the stationary point error by $O(\sqrt{\epsilon}\varepsilon_g)$, the paper does not empirically measure the gradient mismatch $\varepsilon_g$ across iterations, leaving a gap between theoretical bounds and practical behavior.
4. **Reproducibility Gaps:** Despite claims in the Reproducibility Statement, no code repository, exact hyperparameter sweeps, or seed values are provided. The inner-loop iteration count $T_{in}$ and learning rate $\eta$ are critical for the bilevel dynamics, but their sensitivity analysis is minimal (only $\epsilon$ is varied in Fig. 1), falling short of ICLR's standards for full reproducibility.

### Novelty & Significance
The paper's novelty lies in reframing adversarial attacks on non-parametric, iterative solvers as an asymmetric bilevel optimization problem, contrasting with standard symmetric attacks used in parametric deep learning. The theoretical identification of the "improvement paradox" under symmetric attacks is a meaningful contribution to the adversarial robustness literature. Significance is moderate-to-high for the tensor decomposition, compressed sensing, and recommendation communities, demonstrating a critical vulnerability in widely used low-rank approximation pipelines. However, its broader impact on core ICLR research is somewhat limited by the niche focus, though the methodological insights could inspire attacks on other iterative algorithmic pipelines (e.g., optimization-based reconstruction or classical ML solvers).

### Suggestions for Improvement
1. **Release Code & Detail Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** Provide an anonymized code repository to meet ICLR reproducibility expectations. Additionally, expand the ablation to include sensitivity analysis for the learning rate $\eta$ and inner ALS iterations $T_{in}$, as bilevel convergence heavily depends on the solver's approximation fidelity.
2. **Strengthen Baselines & Threat Model Discussion:** Include a PGD adaptation or other strong white-box tensor attacks as baselines to better contextualize AdaTR's strength. Explicitly discuss the stealthiness trade-offs between $F$-norm and $L_\infty$ constraints for visual tasks, and clarify the practical threat model for recommendation systems (e.g., data injection vs. model poisoning).
3. **Empirically Quantify Gradient Approximation:** Add a plot or table measuring the actual gradient error $\| \nabla g(E) - \nabla \tilde{g}(E) \|$ during FAG-AdaTR training. This would directly validate Theorem 4 and clarify the practical regimes where the approximation holds.
4. **Clarify Evaluation Terminology & Defense Claims:** In Sections 5.1 and 5.3, methods like TRPCA-TNN and TRNNM are framed as "defenses," but they are actually robust completion/recovery baselines not trained against AdaTR. Rename these to "Downstream Robust Recovery Methods" to avoid misleading terminology, and briefly discuss whether incorporating AdaTR into an adversarial training loop could genuinely harden TR decomposition.

# Spark Finder Review
## How to Improve This Paper

### Missing Experiments (top 3-5 only)
1. Compare against a standard PGD/FGSM attack that directly maximizes reconstruction error without a bilevel formulation. Without this, the claim that the asymmetric max-min framework is necessary rather than overcomplicated is unconvincing.
2. Evaluate under standardized perceptual adversarial budgets (e.g., $L_\infty \leq 8/255$ for images) in the main text. Current Frobenius-norm budgets are scale-dependent, leaving the practical "vulnerability to small perturbations" claim unverified against standard ML threat models.
3. Quantify the empirical gradient mismatch $\|\nabla g - \nabla \tilde{g}\|$ for FAG-AdaTR across varying $\epsilon$ and ranks. Without measuring this, the theoretical approximation bounds in Theorem 4 lack empirical grounding, making convergence claims unreliable.
4. Report CP and Tucker decomposition attack results in the main body instead of the appendix. Burying these experiments weakens the core contribution's generality claim across ALS-based tensor models.

### Deeper Analysis Needed (top 3-5 only)
1. Analyze how the perturbation alters TR-core ranks or singular value spectra of mode-$k$ unfoldings. Without this structural analysis, it remains unclear whether the attack genuinely breaks low-rank geometry or merely adds high-energy noise that inflates reconstruction metrics.
2. Test the attack against non-ALS tensor solvers (e.g., randomized SVD, gradient-based decomposition, or convex relaxations). If the attack fails on these, the demonstrated vulnerability applies to the ALS optimizer's instability rather than tensor decomposition itself, invalidating the generalization claims.
3. Characterize the Lipschitz constant $L$ and gradient error $\epsilon_g$ used in Theorems 2-4. Presenting generic smoothness proofs for projected gradient ascent without bounding these parameters for the specific non-convex TR-ALS landscape makes the theoretical guarantees unverifiable.

### Visualizations & Case Studies
1. Visualize the learned perturbation tensor $E$ heatmaps across tensor modes alongside clean and reconstructed outputs. This directly reveals whether the method learns structured adversarial patterns or acts as unstructured noise, exposing the actual attack mechanism.
2. Provide a systematic failure-case analysis showing specific datasets, ranks, or initialization conditions where the attack degrades minimally or defenses succeed. Absence of this obscures practical limitations and artificially inflates perceived effectiveness.
3. Plot reconstruction error versus perturbation budget $\epsilon$ at fine granularity around the theoretical collapse threshold $\sqrt{\delta - \|R_2\|_F}$. This empirically validates the claimed transition where ATTR improves performance while AdaTR consistently degrades it.

### Obvious Next Steps
1. Benchmark the attack against robust tensor completion/ALS variants (e.g., Huber-loss, trimmed least-squares, or nuclear-regularized solvers). Demonstrating vulnerability without testing standard robust defenses leaves the security narrative incomplete and limits ICLR-impact.
2. Normalize perturbation budgets to relative data energy and report standardized attack success rates (e.g., $\epsilon$-success curves). This aligns the experimental evaluation with conventional adversarial machine learning reporting standards.
3. Attack parametric tensorized neural networks (e.g., tensor-train/TT-compressed LLMs) where decomposition factors are fixed or fine-tuned downstream. Since the paper cites model compression as a primary motivation, this extension is required to prove real-world relevance beyond offline data factorization.

# Final Consolidated Review
## Summary
This paper investigates the vulnerability of Tensor Ring (TR) decomposition to adversarial perturbations, proposing AdaTR, an asymmetric bilevel optimization framework that maximizes reconstruction error by optimizing the perturbation against the solver's converged output. To address the high memory/compute cost of backpropagating through iterative ALS updates, the authors introduce FAG-AdaTR, a faster closed-form approximate gradient variant with provable convergence guarantees. Experiments across image/video decomposition, tensor completion, and recommendation tasks demonstrate that the proposed attacks significantly degrade performance and transfer across multiple downstream tensor methods.

## Strengths
- **Rigorous identification of symmetric adversarial failure:** The paper formally proves (Theorem 1) that standard min-max adversarial training (ATTR) can paradoxically improve reconstruction performance under small perturbation budgets for non-parametric solvers. This provides a mathematically grounded justification for abandoning symmetric objectives in decomposition settings.
- **Well-structured bilevel formulation with practical acceleration:** The asymmetric max-min objective directly targets the low-rank approximation error via the ALS update mapping. The derivation of FAG-AdaTR (Section 4.3) successfully circumvents the computational bottleneck of autodiff through iterative updates, yielding empirically verified speedups (~1.5-3x faster) and drastic memory reductions (Appendix H.3) while maintaining attack strength.
- **Strong theoretical convergence guarantees:** The paper provides comprehensive convergence analysis (Theorems 2–4) proving monotonic ascent, Cauchy convergence, and KKT stationarity for both the exact and approximate gradient methods under standard smoothness and boundedness assumptions. This level of formal rigor is uncommon and valuable for optimization-based adversarial frameworks.

## Weaknesses
- **Insufficient comparative baselines for adversarial context:** The primary baseline is ATTR, which the authors themselves prove is suboptimal, alongside trivial Gaussian noise. Standard white-box attacks (e.g., PGD or FGSM applied directly to the reconstruction loss $\|\mathcal{X} - \text{TR}([\mathbf{G}])\|_F^2$) are absent from both the main text and appendix. Without these, it remains unclear whether the claimed superiority stems from the asymmetric bilevel formulation or simply from stronger gradient-based optimization.
- **Unsupported core motivation claim:** Section 4.1 asserts that ATTR "degenerates into maximizing the difference between successive perturbations, i.e., $\|E^{(t)} - E^{(t-1)}\|_F$." This pivotal claim lacks both theoretical derivation and empirical trajectory analysis. While Theorem 1 correctly identifies that ATTR can reduce reconstruction error under small budgets, the specific mechanism of "degeneration" is asserted without evidence, weakening the logical bridge to the proposed asymmetric objective.
- **Perturbation budget scaling and perceptual realism:** The main image/video experiments default to a Frobenius norm budget of $\epsilon=500$ on tensors normalized to $[0,1]$ (~2M elements). This corresponds to a per-element RMS perturbation of $\sim 0.35$, which is substantial and likely visually perceptible. The paper's claim of degrading performance with "tiny perturbations" is not substantiated in the main text with interpretable metrics (e.g., relative energy, PSNR deltas, or standard $L_\infty$ bounds), obscuring the practical threat model.
- **Unverified empirical behavior of the gradient approximation:** FAG-AdaTR approximates the gradient by treating TR factors as independent of $E$ beyond the current step. While Theorem 4 bounds the resulting stationary point error by $O(\sqrt{\epsilon}\varepsilon_g)$, the paper does not empirically measure the gradient mismatch $\|\nabla g(E) - \nabla \tilde{g}(E)\|$ across iterations or ranks. This leaves a gap between the theoretical guarantee and actual algorithmic stability, particularly in ill-conditioned regimes.

## Nice-to-Haves
- Move the CP, Tucker, and TT decomposition attack results (currently Appendix H.4) to the main text to better support the generality claim across ALS-based tensor models.
- Provide a sensitivity analysis for the inner-loop ALS iterations $T_{\text{in}}$ and learning rate $\eta$, as bilevel dynamics are highly sensitive to solver convergence fidelity.
- Include structural analysis (e.g., singular value spectra or TR-core rank shifts) to clarify whether the perturbation genuinely disrupts low-rank geometry or primarily injects high-energy noise that inflates reconstruction error.
- Release anonymized code and exact random seeds to meet standard reproducibility expectations.

## Novel Insights
The paper highlights a fundamental shift required for adversarial attacks on non-parametric, iterative solvers: unlike parametric neural networks where perturbations target fixed weights or embeddings, decomposition models must be attacked along their optimization trajectories. By reframing the problem as an asymmetric bilevel objective that explicitly optimizes the perturbation against the *converged solver's output mapping*, the work circumvents the inherent "improvement paradox" of symmetric min-max formulations. This insight—that vulnerability in classical optimization-based pipelines is best exposed by attacking the solver's fixed-point mapping rather than a static objective—provides a conceptual template for evaluating robustness beyond deep learning architectures.

## Suggestions
1. **Add a direct PGD baseline:** Implement a standard projected gradient ascent attack maximizing the raw reconstruction loss with identical budget constraints. Compare its trajectory, final error, and visual artifacts against AdaTR/FAG-AdaTR to rigorously isolate the benefit of the bilevel formulation.
2. **Substantiate the ATTR degeneration claim:** Provide either a short analytical derivation of the gradient update behavior for symmetric min-max, or include an empirical plot showing perturbation update norms $\|E^{(t)} - E^{(t-1)}\|_F$ vs. true reconstruction error during ATTR training.
3. **Contextualize perturbation budgets:** Translate the $\epsilon$ values used in main experiments into relative energy ($\|\mathcal{E}\|_F / \|\mathcal{X}\|_F$) and report corresponding PSNR/SSIM of the perturbed inputs $\mathcal{X}+\mathcal{E}$. Consider running a representative main-text experiment under standardized $L_\infty$ constraints (e.g., $8/255$) to verify vulnerability under strictly imperceptible budgets.
4. **Empirically quantify gradient approximation error:** Add a figure or table plotting $\|\nabla g(E) - \nabla \tilde{g}(E)\|_F$ over training steps for FAG-AdaTR across different ranks and budgets. This will directly validate Theorem 4 and clarify practical operating regimes.
