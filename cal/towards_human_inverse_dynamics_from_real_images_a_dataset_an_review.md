# Harsh Critic Review
## Section-by-Section Critical Review

### Title & Abstract
- Does the title accurately reflect the contribution? Yes. The title clearly communicates the focus on vision-based inverse dynamics, the introduction of a dataset, and a benchmark.
- Does the abstract clearly state the problem, method, and key results? Yes. It identifies the limitation of prior lab/reliant methods, introduces the VID dataset and VID-Network baseline, and reports state-of-the-art results across three evaluation levels.
- Are any claims in the abstract unsupported by the paper? The claim that the baseline achieves "state-of-the-art performance on almost all the evaluation criteria" is technically supported by Tables 2–4, but the abstract does not qualify that the compared baselines operate on different input modalities (explicit kinematics vs. raw images), which is a critical contextual gap for readers evaluating the strength of the result.

### Introduction & Motivation
- Is the problem well-motivated? Is the gap in prior work clearly identified? The motivation is strong. The paper effectively contrasts sEMG, marker/force-plate inverse dynamics, and simulation-based imitation learning, highlighting their reliance on specialized hardware or suffering from sim2real gaps. The need for a purely vision-based pipeline for real-world deployment is clear and relevant.
- Are the contributions clearly stated and accurate? Yes. The triad of contributions (dataset, benchmark, baseline) is explicitly listed and aligns with the rest of the manuscript.
- Does the introduction over-claim or under-sell? The introduction is reasonably calibrated, though stating that existing methods are "impractical for use in outdoor sports" slightly overstates the limitation of modern markerless MoCap pipelines. More importantly, it frames the vision-based approach as eliminating "the need for marker entities," which implies end-to-end image-to-torque mapping, yet later methodology relies on height/mass metadata and auxiliary kinematic supervisors. This tension should be acknowledged early.

### Method / Approach
- Is the method clearly described and reproducible? Partially. The two-stage training paradigm and high-level architecture (backbone → spatial probabilistic model → pose estimator/marker regressor → TorqueInferNet) are described, but several tensor shape and data-flow details are ambiguous or inconsistent, hindering exact reproducibility:
  - Sec 4.1 Eq. 2 defines `τ = VID(I_t, Pos_t, Velc_t, H, M)`, but immediately states "all motion information except for I_t can be used as supervisory signals." This is contradictory. If `Pos` and `Velc` are not model inputs, they should not appear in the forward function definition.
  - The marker regressor is defined as `f: R^{B×21×3} → R^{B×M×3}` (Sec 4.2). The origin of the `21` dimension is unclear. Is it 21 anatomical joints from the 3D pose estimator? If so, how do 21 joints map to 51 physical markers? This projection is unexplained.
  - The TorqueInferNet input combines "flattened spatial probabilistic features" and marker coordinates, but the exact dimensionality after flattening and the projection layer are not specified. Given the Transformer's `head=8, dim=128`, readers cannot reconstruct how 3D probability volumes are converted to sequence tokens.
- Are key assumptions stated and justified? Height and mass (`H, M`) are used in Eq 2. In real unconstrained scenarios, these are rarely available. The paper should clarify whether they are treated as ground-truh biometrics or predicted/estimated from visual cues.
- Are there logical gaps in the derivation or reasoning? The spatial probabilistic model outputs a tensor of shape `[B, J·D, H', W']`, yet the subsequent soft-argmax expects `[B, J, D, H, W]`. The reshaping operation is implied but mathematically loose. Additionally, training only uses `L_marker` and `L_torque` in stage two, meaning the pose estimator weights may freeze or drift, yet no mechanism (e.g., gradient flow control or joint optimization) is described.
- Are there edge cases or failure modes not discussed? Yes. The method assumes single-subject, full-body visibility, consistent camera viewpoint, and static lighting. Occlusion, dynamic backgrounds, multi-person interactions, and clothing variations are not addressed in the architecture or loss design.

### Experiments & Results
- Do the experiments actually test the paper's claims? The three-tier evaluation (overall, joint-specific, action-specific) appropriately tests torque prediction quality. However, the experimental design has a critical misalignment:
- Are baselines appropriate and fairly compared? Dino and ImDy are described as relying on "labeled motion data" (ground-truth markers/kinematics). Comparing a vision-based method directly to methods that ingest exact kinematic ground truth is inherently uneven. If the vision model outperforms kinematics-fed models by ~40%, either (a) the baselines were not fine-tuned/adapted to this specific dataset, (b) the normalization/metric favors the proposed architecture, or (c) there is a data leakage issue. ICLR reviewers expect either a fair modality-normalized comparison (e.g., running baselines on predicted poses) or an explicit acknowledgment that the comparison measures vision robustness vs. kinematic precision, not pure model quality.
- Are there missing ablations that would materially change conclusions? Yes:
  - Removing anthropometric inputs (`H, M`) to show the network can learn scale invariance or quantify their contribution.
  - Replacing the Transformer with a simpler temporal model (e.g., TCN or LSTM) to justify architectural choice.
  - Testing sensitivity to pose estimator errors (e.g., injecting synthetic 3D pose noise to simulate real-world vision inaccuracies).
- Do the results support the claims made, or are they cherry-picked? Tables 3–4 show consistent wins except for `walkingTS1/2`. This looks robust, but:
- Error bars / statistical significance reported? None. Deep learning baselines should report mean ± std over at least 3 seeds. Given the modest dataset size (63k frames), variance could meaningfully affect the claimed ~40% improvement.
- Are datasets and evaluation metrics appropriate? mPJE normalized by body weight is standard. However, the 8:2 train/test split across 9 subjects likely mixes subjects rather than holding out unseen individuals. Sec 5.1 does not specify cross-subject generalization. If frames are randomly split, the model benefits from subject-specific anthropometrics and motion patterns in both sets, inflating performance and weakening the claim of "practical deployment in wild scenarios."

### Writing & Clarity
- Are there sections that are confusing or poorly explained? Sec 4.2's data flow is the primary clarity barrier. The exact tensor shapes at each stage, the source of the marker regressor's input dimension, and how spatial features are tokenized for the Transformer are vague. A clear forward-pass diagram with explicit dimensions (or pseudocode) would resolve this.
- Are figures and tables clear and informative? Fig 2 provides a good high-level schematic but lacks dimension annotations. Tables 3 and 4 are informative but contain formatting artifacts from parsing; aside from that, the joint names (e.g., `hip ~~f~~lexion ~~r~`) suggest inconsistent LaTeX rendering, though this is noted as a parser issue. Table 1's dataset comparison is excellent and clearly motivates the contribution.

### Limitations & Broader Impact
- Do the authors acknowledge the key limitations? The conclusion mentions cross-subject generalization, in-the-wild evaluation, and multi-modal extensions as future work. This appropriately flags the current scope limits.
- Are there fundamental limitations they missed? Yes:
  - Heavy reliance on controlled lab-captured "real images" (likely fixed background/single camera) despite claims of unconstrained applicability.
  - Dataset scale: 63k frames from only 9 subjects is small for training a vision-to-dynamics mapper that must generalize across body types, ages, and ethnicities.
  - Dependency on OpenSim-derived ground truth: Inverse dynamics via OpenSim introduces its own modeling assumptions and filtering biases. These propagate to the supervision signal and are not discussed as a source of label noise.
- Are there failure modes or negative societal impacts not discussed? Privacy concerns around biometric data extraction from images, consent for medical/sports applications, and the risk of deploying biomechanical torque estimates in clinical or athletic coaching without validated uncertainty quantification should be briefly noted.

### Overall Assessment
This paper addresses a genuinely important and underexplored problem: bridging computer vision with biomechanical inverse dynamics via a curated real-image dataset. The VID dataset and the three-tier benchmark represent a useful contribution that will facilitate future work. However, the current submission falls short of ICLR's empirical rigor standards primarily due to an uneven baseline comparison (vision vs. ground-truth kinematics), an unspecified train/test subject-split strategy that likely inflates results through identity leakage, and several ambiguities in the method's tensor flow and module design. The claimed ~40% improvement is striking but lacks statistical validation and architectural justification. To meet ICLR's bar, the authors must clarify the evaluation protocol (ideally implementing cross-subject splits), fairly adapt or contextualize baseline comparisons, add error bars across multiple seeds, provide explicit dimension specifications for the network pipeline, and discuss the limitations of OpenSim-derived supervision. If these core empirical and methodological gaps are addressed, the dataset and benchmark will stand as a valuable foundation for the community.

# Neutral Reviewer
## Balanced Review

### Summary
This paper introduces the Vision-based Inverse Dynamics (VID) dataset and a corresponding benchmark for predicting human joint torques directly from monocular RGB images, bypassing traditional lab-grade motion capture or sEMG setups. The authors propose a two-stage baseline architecture (VID-Network) that combines a spatial probabilistic feature extractor, a marker position regressor, and a Transformer-based temporal predictor. The submission establishes a three-tiered evaluation protocol (overall, joint-specific, and action-specific) and reports substantial error reductions over existing kinematics-driven baselines.

### Strengths
1. **Clear Real-World Motivation & Practical Gap Addressed:** The paper correctly identifies that current inverse dynamics pipelines are constrained to laboratory environments due to hardware dependencies (mocap, force plates, sEMG). Proposing an end-to-end vision-based pipeline directly targets a meaningful bottleneck in biomechanics, robotics, and sports science.
2. **Structured Benchmark & Granular Evaluation Protocol:** The three-level evaluation criteria provide a diagnostic lens beyond aggregate metrics. Table 3 and Table 4 effectively break down performance by joint and action type, revealing nuanced model behavior (e.g., strong performance on lumbar extension and down jumping, but higher error on transitional walking steps). This granularity will benefit future research iteration.
3. **Transparent Data Processing & Strong Ablation Design:** Section 3 details rigorous preprocessing (Savitzky–Golay filtering, cubic spline interpolation for gaps, manual synchronization), which is critical for biomechanical data quality. The ablation study (Table 5) cleanly isolates the contribution of the pose estimator pre-training and the marker regressor, validating the architectural design choices with empirical evidence.

### Weaknesses
1. **Unfair Baseline Comparison Due to Modality Mismatch:** Section 5.3 states that Dino and ImDy "rely on labeled motion data" while the proposed method uses "real images." If baselines are fed ground-truth keypoint/marker coordinates from VID, they operate with privileged information not available to a vision-only model. This makes the reported ~40% improvement methodologically inconsistent; a fair comparison must evaluate all methods from the same input modality (e.g., feeding predicted poses to the baselines or comparing against vision-only torque estimators).
2. **Unresolved Monocular Scale Ambiguity:** Equation 2 lists height ($H$) and mass ($M$) as inputs, but the pipeline claims to be "purely visual." Monocular images lack absolute metric scale without camera intrinsics or known anthropometric priors at inference. It is unclear how $H$ and $M$ are obtained in unconstrained settings, or how the network resolves depth-scale ambiguity to output physically meaningful torque units (N·m). This omission undermines the physical validity of the predictions.
3. **Limited Dataset Scale & Ambiguous Generalization Protocol:** The dataset comprises ~63,369 frames from only 9 subjects (roughly 10.5 minutes of motion at 100 FPS). While carefully curated, this scale is small for training deep Transformer architectures and raises overfitting concerns. Furthermore, the train/test split is only described as an "8:2 ratio" without specifying whether it is subject-independent. In biomechanics, subject-dependent splits artificially inflate performance and do not reflect real-world deployment to new users.
4. **Under-Specified Training Regimen & Loss Weighting:** Section 4.2 mentions pre-training the pose estimator on "several large-scale 3D pose datasets" but never names them or details the fine-tuning protocol, hindering reproducibility. Additionally, the multi-task loss $L_{\text{total}} = 0.5 L_{\text{marker}} + 0.5 L_{\text{torque}}$ combines heterogeneous units (spatial coordinates in meters vs. torque in N·m) with fixed weights. Without justification or sensitivity analysis, this arbitrary balancing may dominate gradient updates and mask optimization inefficiencies.

### Novelty & Significance
- **Novelty:** The core novelty is dataset curation and the formulation of a vision-only inverse dynamics benchmark. Architecturally, the pipeline relies on well-established components (ResNet backbones, heatmap-based spatial probability, Transformer temporal modeling), making the methodological novelty incremental rather than groundbreaking.
- **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-structured, with clear problem framing and readable evaluation tables. However, the contradiction between the "purely visual" claim and Eq. 2's inclusion of height/mass, alongside vague pre-training details, reduces technical clarity.
- **Reproducibility:** Reproducibility is moderate. While the loss formulation, optimizer settings, and preprocessing pipeline are described, the omission of specific pre-training datasets, scale resolution strategy, and exact train/test split methodology makes exact replication difficult without the author's supplementary materials.
- **Significance:** The dataset fills a recognized niche and holds promise for applied computer vision and biomechanics. However, its immediate impact as a reliable benchmark is currently limited by the small subject count, modality-mismatched baselines, and unresolved scale ambiguity. Addressing these would substantially elevate its significance for the ICLR community and applied ML research.

### Suggestions for Improvement
1. **Equalize Input Modalities for Baselines:** Re-run Dino and ImDy using poses/markers estimated by a standard off-the-shelf vision model (or your own stage-1 pose estimator) rather than ground-truth mocap. Alternatively, add an "oracle ablation" showing the performance gap strictly attributable to the vision-to-pose estimation step versus the prediction-to-torque step.
2. **Explicitly Address Metric Scale & Anthropometrics:** Clarify how absolute scale is handled. If subject height/mass are required at inference, explicitly frame this as an anthropometric prior and evaluate a "vision-only + scale prediction" variant. Discuss how camera calibration or depth priors could be integrated to ensure torque predictions maintain physical unit consistency.
3. **Implement & Report Subject-Independent Splits:** Biomechanical models must generalize to unseen individuals. Replace or supplement the random 8:2 split with a Leave-One-Subject-Out (LOSO) or strict subject-independent protocol. Report cross-subject mPJE to demonstrate practical generalizability.
4. **Detail Pre-Training & Loss Strategy:** Name the specific 3D pose datasets used for stage-1 pre-training and document the fine-tuning schedule. Replace the fixed 0.5/0.5 loss weighting with either a rationale based on gradient magnitude normalization, uncertainty-weighted loss, or a short hyperparameter sensitivity study to justify the chosen balance.
5. **Expand Benchmarking & Computational Profiling:** To strengthen the submission for ICLR, include inference latency (FPS) and FLOPs/memory footprint analysis. Given the paper's emphasis on real-world applicability, understanding the computational cost of running a Transformer over 13 frames of 2048-D features is essential. Additionally, outline a clear roadmap for scaling the dataset beyond 9 subjects in future releases.

# Spark Finder Review
## How to Improve This Paper

### Missing Experiments (top 3-5 only)
1. Add an experiment comparing your method against baselines (Dino, ImDy) that also receive *predicted* keypoints from images rather than ground truth. Without this, your SOTA claim is fundamentally misleading because you are comparing vision-predicted inputs against oracle kinematic inputs.
2. Conduct a Leave-One-Subject-Out (LOSO) cross-validation across the 9 subjects. Using an 80/20 random frame split on a small cohort allows the model to memorize subject-specific appearances and morphologies, invalidating claims of real-world or unconstrained generalization.
3. Include an ablation study that explicitly removes height and mass ($H, M$) inputs to the network. Without this, it is unclear whether the model learns true inverse dynamics or simply memorizes subject-specific scale priors from the training data, which would cause catastrophic failure on new anthropometries.

### Deeper Analysis Needed (top 3-5 only)
1. Analyze and report performance under a strict *trial-wise* or *block-wise* split rather than a frame-wise split. Given your 13-frame Transformer window, frame-wise splitting introduces severe temporal data leakage, artificially inflating the reported 39.81% improvement and undermining trust in the results.
2. Correlate prediction errors with dynamic regimes (e.g., peak joint accelerations and ground reaction forces). The drastically higher error in "DownJump" (Table 4) cannot be explained by mPJE alone; quantifying whether failures stem from impact transients or motion blur is essential to trust the model's robustness.
3. Quantify the baseline noise floor introduced by Savitzky-Golay filtering and numerical differentiation of kinematics. If the ground truth torque contains smoothing artifacts, the model's "ceiling" accuracy is compromised, and this measurement is needed to contextualize the 1.76 N·m/kg error metric.

### Visualizations & Case Studies
1. Overlay predicted versus ground truth torque trajectories over time for high-impact actions (e.g., DownJump). This exposes whether the model captures sharp physiological impact peaks or merely outputs smoothed averages that artificially depress the mPJE score.
2. Map mPJE errors onto 2D image heatmaps to visualize performance degradation across different camera viewpoints and joint occlusions. This reveals whether your spatial probabilistic model actually resolves self-occlusion or if the reported average error masks complete failure under specific visual angles.

### Obvious Next Steps
1. Explicitly document how the kinematic-dependent baselines (Dino, ImDy) were adapted for image-based evaluation, including the exact 3D pose estimator used to feed them. Without this adaptation protocol, the baseline results are not reproducible or comparable. 
2. Benchmark against a standard kinematics-to-torque inverse dynamics pipeline (e.g., OpenSim or Newton-Euler formulation) using the *predicted* 3D poses as input. This isolates how much error stems purely from visual feature extraction versus the neural dynamics regression itself.
3. Provide a transparent table mapping subject demographics (height, weight) to train/test assignments. Without this, reviewers cannot verify that the dataset split avoids bias, such as placing all subjects with extreme morphologies solely in the training set.

# Final Consolidated Review
## Summary
This paper introduces the Vision-based Inverse Dynamics (VID) dataset and a corresponding benchmark to predict human joint torques directly from monocular RGB images, bypassing traditional motion capture or sEMG dependencies. The authors propose a two-stage baseline architecture (VID-Network) combining spatial probabilistic feature extraction, marker regression, and a Transformer-based temporal predictor. Experiments report substantial error reductions over existing kinematics-driven methods across overall, joint-specific, and action-specific evaluations.

## Strengths
- **Addresses a high-impact, underexplored gap:** The paper correctly identifies that current inverse dynamics pipelines are bottlenecked by lab-grade hardware dependencies. Framing joint torque estimation as a vision-only regression task directly targets a meaningful barrier for real-world biomechanics, sports science, and robotics deployment.
- **Carefully curated dataset & diagnostic benchmark:** The curation pipeline demonstrates rigor, employing manual synchronization, Savitzky–Golay filtering, and outlier correction. The three-tier evaluation protocol (overall, joint-specific, action-specific) provides a structured, granular diagnostic tool that moves beyond aggregate metrics, making the dataset genuinely useful for future methodology iteration (Tables 2–4).
- **Validated architectural design:** The ablation study (Table 5) cleanly isolates the contribution of the spatial probabilistic features, pose estimator pre-training, and marker regressor, empirically confirming that decomposing vision-to-torques into intermediate kinematic representations improves performance over direct regression.

## Weaknesses
- **Fundamentally unfair baseline comparison due to modality mismatch:** The claimed ~40% improvement over Dino and ImDy is methodologically invalid. Section 5.3 explicitly states the baselines "rely on labeled motion data" while VID-Network uses "real images." Comparing a vision-based model against baselines ingesting ground-truth kinematic coordinates conflates the benefits of oracle inputs with architectural quality. Without re-evaluating baselines on *predicted* poses or introducing vision-only torque estimators, the core empirical claim of state-of-the-art performance is unsupported and misleading.
- **Severe data leakage risk in train/test protocol:** The paper only specifies an "8:2 ratio" split without clarifying whether it is subject-independent, trial-wise, or random frame-wise. Given the small cohort (9 subjects) and the 13-frame temporal window used by the Transformer, a random frame split almost certainly leaks identity-specific anthropometrics, gait patterns, and overlapping temporal context into the test set. This artificially inflates mPJE scores and directly contradicts the stated goal of "practical deployment in unconstrained/wild scenarios."
- **Contradictory scale dependency & anthropometric priors:** Equation 2 and the methodology list height and mass ($H, M$) as explicit inputs, yet the introduction claims a "purely visual" approach that eliminates marker/external dependencies. Monocular RGB lacks absolute metric scale, and the paper never explains how $H$ and $M$ are obtained in real-world settings, nor whether the network learns true inverse dynamics or simply memorizes subject-specific scale priors. The omission of an ablation removing $H, M$ leaves the physical validity of the torque predictions (N·m/kg) unverified.
- **Under-specified training regimen & lack of statistical rigor:** Stage 1 pre-training uses "several large-scale 3D pose datasets" which are never named, hindering exact reproducibility. Stage 2 combines heterogeneous loss units (spatial coordinates in meters, torque in normalized N·m/kg) with fixed, uncalibrated weights ($\lambda_1 = \lambda_2 = 0.5$), risking gradient dominance by one task over the other. Furthermore, all results report single-run point estimates without standard deviations across multiple random seeds, making the claimed improvements impossible to contextualize against stochastic variance, especially on a dataset of this size.

## Nice-to-Haves
- Reporting inference latency (FPS), memory footprint, and FLOPs to contextualize the Transformer's viability for real-time or edge deployment.
- Quantifying the baseline accuracy ceiling introduced by Savitzky–Golay smoothing and numerical differentiation of the OpenSim ground truth, which would help contextualize the 1.76 mPJE metric.
- Briefly addressing privacy/consent considerations when extracting biodynamic parameters from unconstrained imagery.

## Novel Insights
The paper's most meaningful contribution is not architectural novelty—the backbone relies on well-established ResNet heatmaps and Transformer temporal encoding—but rather the successful formulation of biomechanical inverse dynamics as a multi-stage vision prediction pipeline. By demonstrating that spatial probabilistic features can serve as a bridge between raw pixels and physics-aware marker trajectories, the work provides a tangible proof-of-concept that deep vision representations contain sufficient latent kinematic structure to approximate rigid-body dynamics without explicit force plates. The structured, action- and joint-level evaluation framework further establishes a much-needed diagnostic standard for this emerging cross-disciplinary niche.

## Potentially Missed Related Work
- **PhysCap (2022) / VideoPose3D (2021) dynamics extensions** — Relevant for recent approaches combining monocular video with physics priors or 3D pose smoothing, which offer alternative baselines for vision-to-dynamics mapping without relying on mocap.
- **Markerless biomechanics estimation (e.g., recent OpenCap/DeepSimulator pipelines)** — These works explore recovering musculoskeletal forces from standard video; comparing your pipeline to their open-source reconstructions would ground the benchmark in current clinical/markerless workflows.
- **Uncertainty-aware pose & dynamics estimation** — Given the reliance on monocular prediction, methods quantifying epistemic/aleatoric uncertainty in 3D pose lifting could provide a valuable alternative to deterministic marker regression.

## Suggestions
- **Equalize input modalities for baselines:** Re-run Dino and ImDy using keypoints/markers predicted by an off-the-shelf vision model (or your Stage 1 estimator) rather than ground-truth coordinates. Report the performance delta to isolate the true contribution of your architecture from the noise introduced by visual pose estimation.
- **Enforce strict generalization splits:** Replace the ambiguous 8:2 split with Leave-One-Subject-Out (LOSO) or trial-wise cross-validation. Report mPJE under these conditions to prove the model generalizes to unseen anthropometries and motion styles rather than memorizing cohort-specific patterns.
- **Clarify and ablate anthropometric inputs:** Explicitly document how $H$ and $M$ are obtained in deployment (e.g., user input, estimated from visual cues like camera intrinsics/reference object). Conduct an ablation removing $H, M$ entirely to verify the network learns dynamics rather than scaling factors.
- **Complete the training specification & statistical reporting:** Name all pre-training datasets used for Stage 1, replace the fixed 0.5 loss weighting with gradient-norm balancing or a sensitivity sweep, and report mean ± standard deviation over at least 3 training seeds across all tables.
- **Resolve formulation contradictions:** Either update Equation 2 to reflect the actual visual-only input flow or clearly label $H, M$ as anthropometric priors rather than model inputs, ensuring terminology matches implementation throughout.
