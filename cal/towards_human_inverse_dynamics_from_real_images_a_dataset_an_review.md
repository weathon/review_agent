# Harsh Critic Review
## Section-by-Section Critical Review

### Title & Abstract
- **Does the title accurately reflect the contribution?** Yes. The title clearly signals a dataset, benchmark, and focus on image-based inverse dynamics.
- **Does the abstract clearly state the problem, method, and key results?** The problem (limitations of mocap/EMG/sim-dependence) and contributions (VID dataset, 3-level benchmark, baseline network) are stated. However, the claim that the baseline "achieves the state-of-the-art performance on almost all the evaluation criteria" lacks critical context. Tables 2–4 compare an image-based method against baselines that explicitly use ground-truth kinematic inputs. Stating this as SOTA without clarifying the input modality gap is potentially misleading in an abstract.
- **Are any claims in the abstract unsupported by the paper?** The abstract implies a direct end-to-end mapping from image to torque, but the method actually relies on an intermediate marker regression stage and pre-trained 3D pose features. This architectural detail is absent from the abstract and slightly overstates the "purely visual" simplification.

### Introduction & Motivation
- **Is the problem well-motivated? Is the gap in prior work clearly identified?** Yes. The limitation of lab-bound mocap/force plates and the sim-to-real gap of imitation learning are well-articulated. The need for unconstrained, vision-only torque estimation is strongly justified.
- **Are the contributions clearly stated and accurate?** The three contributions (Dataset, Benchmark, Baseline) are clearly listed and align with the paper's content.
- **Does the introduction over-claim or under-sell?** The introduction slightly over-claims the practical readiness: it states the solution "eliminates the need for marker entities and enables practical deployment in real-world or wild exercise scenarios." However, the experiments are confined to a controlled, synchronized dataset of 9 subjects performing structured tasks. Generalization to truly in-the-wild conditions is untested and should be tempered in the motivation section to match the empirical scope.

### Method / Approach
- **Is the method clearly described and reproducible?** Partially. The two-stage training pipeline (pre-training on 3D pose datasets, then joint optimization of marker and torque) is described. However, the exact data flow in Stage 2 is under-specified. It is unclear how the high-dimensional spatial probabilistic features (Eq. 3–6 region) are concatenated or projected with the predicted 3D marker coordinates before entering the Transformer encoder. The phrase "flattened and project each frame" lacks dimension details necessary for reproduction.
- **Are key assumptions stated and justified?** Equation 2 lists image, marker position, marker velocity, height, and mass as inputs to the prediction function. However, Section 4.1 states: *"Since we propose a purely visual approach, $I_t$ is the model’s input, and all motion information except for $I_t$ can be used as supervisory signals."* This is a direct contradiction. If $Pos$ and $V_{elc}$ are not provided at inference, the formulation in Eq. 2 is inaccurate and should be rewritten to reflect the actual autoregressive/multi-task pipeline. Additionally, the assumption that demographic priors ($H, M$) are available at inference is not discussed.
- **Are there logical gaps in the derivation or reasoning?** The spatial probabilistic module output is initially described as $[B, J \cdot D, H', W']$, but later reshaped to $[B, J, D, H, W]$ for soft-argmax. The transition and notation shift is inconsistent. Furthermore, the choice of a centered prediction window ($T=13$ frames) targeting the middle frame is practical but lacks justification over causal (past-only) autoregressive approaches, which are critical for online deployment.
- **Are there edge cases or failure modes not discussed?** Boundary conditions (first/last 6 frames where a full 13-frame window isn't available) are not addressed. The impact of severe occlusion or extreme viewpoint changes on joint probability heatmaps and subsequent torque regression is also omitted.

### Experiments & Results
- **Do the experiments actually test the paper's claims?** The experiments test overall, joint-specific, and action-specific error, which aligns with the benchmark proposal. However, they do not adequately test the *real-world applicability* claim, as the dataset remains lab-synchronized.
- **Are baselines appropriate and fairly compared?** This is a major concern. Dino and ImDy are trained/evaluated using ground-truth kinematic inputs (mocap data), while VID-Net uses predicted kinematics from images. Table 2–4 presents them side-by-side as direct competitors. To claim SOTA, the authors must either (a) clarify that the baselines represent an *oracle upper bound* using perfect kinematics, or (b) evaluate the baselines on predicted/noisy kinematics to ensure a fair modality-to-modality comparison. Without this, the comparison is apples-to-oranges.
- **Are there missing ablations that would materially change conclusions?** Important ablations are missing: 
  1. A split using only predicted 2D keypoints vs. predicted 3D markers to isolate the value of 3D projection.
  2. An analysis of inference latency or FLOPs, especially since the method claims potential for real-world deployment yet uses a ResNet-101 backbone + Transformer.
  3. The pre-training dataset composition and its sensitivity. The ablation in Table 5 only removes auxiliary branches but does not ablate the pre-training strategy itself.
- **Are error bars / statistical significance reported?** No. All results appear to be from single training runs. Given the stochastic nature of neural network training and the small dataset size (63K frames), reporting mean ± std over multiple seeds is standard practice at ICLR and necessary to validate the reported 39.81% improvement.
- **Do results support claims, or cherry-picked?** The authors honestly report underperformance on `walkingTS1` and `walkingTS2` in Table 4, which is commendable. However, the 8:2 train/test split on only 9 subjects raises serious reproducibility concerns regarding data leakage. Table 6 shows frame counts per subject but does not specify if the split is *subject-independent* or *frame-shuffled*. If it is frame-shuffled, the model is evaluated on subjects it has seen during training, which artificially inflates performance and does not test generalization.
- **Are datasets and evaluation metrics appropriate?** The mPJE metric normalized by body mass is reasonable. The dataset's manual synchronization and smoothing steps (Savitzky-Golay, spline) are well-documented. However, the lack of a held-out subject set for evaluation severely limits the benchmark's utility for assessing practical deployment viability.

### Writing & Clarity
- **Are there sections that are confusing or poorly explained?** Section 4.1's formulation (Eq. 2) contradicts the stated inference pipeline. The description of the `TorqueInferNet` input fusion is vague. Additionally, the notation in Tables 3 and 4 contains garbled formatting (e.g., `hip ~~f~~exion ~~r~~`), which, while likely a parser artifact, obscures joint/action names and requires cross-referencing with the text. Figure 3 is referenced as showing performance visualization, but without visual context, it's unclear if this refers to error trajectories, torque waveforms, or pose overlay.
- **Are figures and tables clear and informative?** Table 1 is clear and establishes the dataset gap. Tables 2–4 provide comprehensive breakdowns but would benefit from visualizing torque waveforms (predicted vs. ground truth) to show *where* temporal dynamics fail, rather than relying solely on scalar error metrics. Table 6 is useful but lacks the crucial train/test partitioning overlay.

### Limitations & Broader Impact
- **Do the authors acknowledge the key limitations?** The authors correctly identify the need for cross-subject generalization, in-the-wild extension, and multi-modal inputs as future work.
- **Are there fundamental limitations they missed?** 
  1. **Evaluation Protocol:** The potential for subject leakage in the 8:2 split fundamentally undermines the generalization claims.
  2. **Input Disparity:** The lack of a fair baseline comparison (image-to-image vs. kinematic-to-kinematic) leaves the true value of the proposed architecture ambiguous.
  3. **Computational Cost:** No discussion of model size or inference speed, which contradicts the push toward real-world/deployment applications.
- **Broader Impact / Failure Modes / Negative Societal Impacts:** Not discussed. In biomechanics and sports medicine, incorrect torque estimation could lead to faulty injury risk assessments or misleading athletic training feedback. The paper should at least acknowledge these risks, especially when advocating for unconstrained, real-world use. Privacy implications of releasing real human images also warrant brief mention.

### Overall Assessment
This paper addresses a highly relevant and challenging problem in computational biomechanics: bridging the gap between controlled lab-based inverse dynamics and unconstrained visual observation. The introduction of the VID dataset and the structured benchmark is a genuine contribution to the community, filling a clear void in available resources. The baseline method, while architecturally standard (leveraging proven heatmap and Transformer components), effectively demonstrates the feasibility of vision-driven torque estimation.

However, the paper currently falls short of ICLR's standards for rigorous empirical validation and methodological clarity. The most critical issues are the ambiguity surrounding the train/test split (potential subject leakage), the unfair cross-modality baseline comparison (image-predicted vs. GT kinematics), and the lack of statistical robustness (no error bars/seeds). Additionally, contradictions in the mathematical formulation (Eq. 2 vs. text) and missing details on feature fusion hinder reproducibility. If the authors can rigorously address the evaluation protocol (ensuring subject-independent testing), provide statistically validated results, clarify the input/output pipeline, and contextualize the baseline comparisons fairly, this work will be a strong candidate for acceptance. As it stands, the contribution is promising but the experimental validation requires substantial strengthening.

# Neutral Reviewer
## Balanced Review

### Summary
This paper introduces VID, the first dataset and benchmark designed for estimating human joint torques directly from real monocular images, bypassing traditional dependencies on motion capture or EMG. The authors present a two-stage neural baseline (VID-Network) that combines pose-pretrained spatial feature extraction, marker regression, and a Transformer-based temporal predictor to regress joint torques from image sequences. Extensive experiments demonstrate that the proposed method outperforms existing kinematic-driven baselines by approximately 40% in mean Per-Joint Error (mPJE), establishing a foundational pipeline for vision-based biomechanical analysis.

### Strengths
1. **Addresses a Clear Gap in Cross-Disciplinary Research:** The paper tackles a highly relevant problem at the intersection of computer vision and biomechanics. By releasing the first synchronized dataset of real images, kinematics, and inverse dynamics (Table 1), it removes a major barrier to deploying torque estimation in unconstrained, real-world settings.
2. **Rigorous Dataset Curation and Processing:** The authors detail substantial effort in data synchronization, noise removal, and biomechanical validation. Techniques like Savitzky-Golay filtering, cubic spline interpolation for short gaps, and OpenSim-based torque extraction (Sec 3) demonstrate careful attention to data quality, which is critical for dynamic modeling.
3. **Comprehensive and Structured Evaluation Protocol:** The three-tier evaluation framework (overall, joint-specific, action-specific) with mass-normalized mPJE (Eq 8) provides a standardized, interpretable benchmark. Tables 3 and 4 offer granular insights into model behavior across anatomical joints and movement categories, facilitating clear downstream comparisons.
4. **Pragmatic and Well-Validated Architecture:** The two-stage training strategy effectively leverages existing 3D pose priors, and the ablation study (Table 5) clearly demonstrates the contribution of the marker regressor and pre-trained pose estimator. The design choices are transparent, reproducible, and grounded in established CV practices (Sec 4.2).

### Weaknesses
1. **Lack of Cross-Subject Generalization Evaluation:** The 80/20 split methodology (Sec 5.1) is not explicitly defined as subject-wise or frame-wise. Without a holdout on unseen subjects, performance likely suffers from data leakage due to intra-subject action repetition. Given ICLR's emphasis on robust generalization, the omission of cross-subject evaluation significantly limits the claim of real-world applicability. The authors relegate this to "future work" (Sec 6), but for a benchmark paper, it is a necessary baseline metric.
2. **Mismatched Input Modalities in Baseline Comparisons:** The paper compares its vision-only model against Dino and ImDy, which consume ground-truth kinematic data (Sec 5.1, Table 2). While the authors acknowledge this discrepancy, it conflates the difficulty of the *vision-to-pose* subproblem with the *pose-to-torque* subproblem. The performance gain may stem from the benchmark's favorable visual conditions rather than superior inverse dynamics modeling.
3. **Inconsistencies and Missing Details in Dataset/Method Description:** Section 2 states subjects performed "seven types of movements," yet Section 6 and Appendix Table 6 describe 16 actions. Table 3 evaluates 11 joints, but OpenSim extracted 35 joint torques (Sec 3). The paper does not clarify which joints were selected for evaluation or why, raising questions about reporting bias. Additionally, the exact train/test subject allocation and preprocessing pipeline details (e.g., OpenSim scaling parameters, marker set definitions per subject) are absent.
4. **Limited Physical Consistency Checks:** Inverse dynamics outputs should ideally satisfy basic biomechanical constraints (e.g., joint torque limits, temporal smoothness, or energy balance). The paper relies purely on frame-wise MSE without evaluating physical plausibility, which is a standard requirement in biomechanical ML applications.

### Novelty & Significance
The primary novelty and significance lie in the **dataset and benchmark formulation**, not the neural architecture. The VID-Network employs well-established components (ResNet, volumetric heatmaps, soft-argmax, Transformer), making the modeling contribution incremental. However, establishing a standardized pipeline to estimate joint torques from pixels is a significant step for computational biomechanics, sports analytics, and assistive robotics. If paired with rigorous evaluation (e.g., cross-subject splits, fair cascaded baselines), this work could become a foundational reference point for vision-based dynamics research and meets ICLR's threshold for impactful dataset/benchmark submissions.

### Suggestions for Improvement
1. **Implement Subject-Wise Data Splitting:** Re-evaluate the model using a strict leave-one-subject-out (LOSO) or subject-disjoint 80/20 split. Report cross-subject generalization metrics alongside the current results to prove the model learns transferable dynamics rather than memorizing subject-specific kinematics.
2. **Add a Fair Cascaded Baseline:** Construct a baseline that chains a state-of-the-art image-to-marker/pose estimator (e.g., a modern 3DPose foundation model) with a traditional or learned torque regressor. This isolates the contribution of the VID-Network's integrated training strategy versus a naive two-stage pipeline.
3. **Resolve Reporting Inconsistencies and Document Splits:** Clarify the number of action types evaluated, explicitly list the train/test subject IDs used, and justify the selection of the 11 joints reported in Table 3. Provide a supplementary table mapping OpenSim's 35 outputs to the 11 evaluated metrics.
4. **Incorporate Physical Plausibility Metrics:** Augment the evaluation with biomechanically relevant metrics such as joint torque smoothness (e.g., jerk penalization), adherence to physiological torque limits, and ground reaction force consistency if applicable. This strengthens the claim of biologically meaningful predictions.
5. **Discuss Limitations Explicitly:** Address monocular depth ambiguity, the impact of clothing/occlusion on marker regression, and error propagation from the spatial probabilistic model to the torque head. Discuss how VID-Network handles subjects with body proportions outside the 1.60–1.85 m range collected in the dataset.

# Spark Finder Review
## How to Improve This Paper

### Missing Experiments (top 3-5 only)
1. Conduct strict leave-one-subject-out (LOSO) cross-validation instead of the implied frame-wise random split. Without it, the reported results likely suffer from severe temporal and subject-level data leakage, making the claimed state-of-the-art performance and "real-world" deployability unconvincing.
2. Re-evaluate the baselines (Dino, ImDy) using predicted poses from a standard off-the-shelf vision model rather than ground-truth markers. Comparing your image-to-torque pipeline against marker-input methods is an apples-to-oranges comparison; a fair baseline is required to prove your architecture, not just your input modality, drives the improvement.
3. Train a direct regression baseline (e.g., ResNet/ViT + Transformer) that maps raw image features straight to torques, bypassing the spatial probabilistic model and marker regressor. Without this ablation, there is no proof that the complex auxiliary modules improve biomechanical modeling rather than simply adding network capacity or leveraging pose pre-training priors.

### Deeper Analysis Needed (top 3-5 only)
1. Quantify error propagation by correlating 3D marker position RMSE with final joint torque RMSE across subjects and actions. Without isolating how much of the final torque error stems from visual pose estimation versus the temporal dynamics regressor, the paper cannot identify the true performance bottleneck.
2. Supplement MSE/mPJE with biomechanically meaningful metrics like normalized cross-correlation, phase-locked error, and R². Torques are high-frequency dynamic signals; relying solely on L2 distance fails to prove the model captures correct timing, push-off/landing peaks, or physiologically plausible profiles.
3. Provide a sensitivity analysis of input image conditions (camera viewpoint, occlusion, clothing, lighting) on torque prediction accuracy. The paper claims to enable "unconstrained/wild" applications, yet provides no evidence that the model doesn't collapse when faced with the visual degradations typical of real-world footage.

### Visualizations & Case Studies
1. Plot full time-series torque curves (predicted vs. ground truth) across complete action cycles (e.g., one squat repetition or gait stride) for at least three representative joints. Static error tables cannot reveal whether the model produces physiologically realistic dynamic patterns or merely learns a smoothed average/magnitude.
2. Show side-by-side visualizations of input frames, predicted 3D marker overlays, and resulting torque deviations for high-error sequences. This directly exposes whether prediction failures are caused by visual perception breakdowns (e.g., self-occlusion) or by the torque regression network failing despite accurate spatial features.

### Obvious Next Steps
1. Formalize the benchmark leaderboard to require subject-independent evaluation (LOSO) before public release. A biomechanics dataset without strict cross-subject validation will not see adoption, as community members need to trust that methods generalize to unseen individuals rather than exploiting subject-specific kinematic biases.
2. Document and release the exact synchronization pipeline between camera timestamps and OpenSim dynamics outputs, including handling of frame offsets and smoothing parameters. Without a transparent, reproducible alignment protocol, the dataset's quality claims are unverifiable and risk hidden manual curation artifacts.
3. Benchmark modern vision backbones (e.g., Vision Transformers, masked autoencoders) against the ResNet-101 baseline. Claiming a "vision-based" solution requires demonstrating robustness to architecture choice and showing that performance scales appropriately with contemporary representation learning models.

# Final Consolidated Review
## Summary
This paper introduces VID, the first synchronized dataset mapping real monocular images to human inverse dynamics ground truth, alongside a structured three-tier benchmark and a two-stage baseline network. By combining pre-trained spatial probability estimation, anatomical marker regression, and temporal modeling, the work demonstrates that vision-only pipelines can approximate joint-level physical forces, aiming to bridge biomechanics with unconstrained computer vision applications.

## Strengths
- **Fills a genuine cross-disciplinary data gap:** The dataset provides meticulously synchronized and validated pairs of real human images, kinematics, and dynamic outputs (including Savitzky-Golay smoothing and manual validation), offering a tangible resource where previously only lab-bound or simulated alternatives existed.
- **Diagnostic evaluation protocol:** The three-tier benchmark (overall, joint-specific, action-specific) paired with mass-normalized mPJE moves beyond scalar aggregation, enabling granular analysis of model behavior across anatomies and movement categories (Tables 3 and 4).
- **Pragmatic and well-ablated architecture:** The two-stage training strategy effectively leverages 3D pose priors without over-engineering, and the ablation study clearly isolates the individual contributions of the spatial probabilistic model and marker regressor (Table 5), validating the design choice to decompose vision-to-torque into spatial and temporal sub-tasks.

## Weaknesses
- **Ambiguous and likely leaky evaluation split:** The paper states an 80/20 train/test ratio but never specifies whether this is subject-disjoint or frame-randomized. Given the dataset structure (Table 6 shows dense sequential frames per subject across actions), a randomized frame split would cause severe temporal and intra-subject leakage, artificially inflating performance and rendering the benchmark useless for assessing true generalization. For a dataset/benchmark paper, the primary evaluation metric must reflect cross-individual generalization; relegating this to "future work" undermines the protocol's core utility.
- **Unresolved internal contradictions and reporting gaps:** Section 2 states subjects performed "seven types of movements," while Section 6 and Appendix A.2 explicitly detail 16 actions. Additionally, Table 3 evaluates only 11 joint torque components out of the 35 extracted via OpenSim, with no justification for this subset selection. These inconsistencies raise legitimate concerns about reporting transparency and reproducibility, as readers cannot verify whether omitted joints or actions exhibit different failure modes.
- **Mathematical formulation contradicts the stated pipeline:** Equation 2 explicitly lists marker positions and velocities as model inputs (`Pos`, `Velc`), yet the text in Section 4.1 states: *"Since we propose a purely visual approach, $I_t$ is the model’s input, and all motion information except for $I_t$ can be used as supervisory signals."* This directly conflicts with the end-to-end inference claim and must be corrected to accurately reflect that kinematics are training targets, not inference-time inputs.
- **Cross-paradigm baseline comparison requires explicit framing:** The SOTA claims compare the proposed vision-only pipeline against Dino and ImDy, which consume ground-truth mocap markers. While demonstrating that a visual pipeline can surpass oracle kinematic methods is an interesting finding, presenting the results without emphasizing the modality gap conflates perception accuracy with dynamics modeling. The evaluation section needs to explicitly frame this as a cross-paradigm benchmark (vision vs. lab instrumentation) rather than a direct architectural comparison.

## Nice-to-Haves
- Reporting performance variance (e.g., mean ± std across 3+ seeds) to confirm the 39.81% improvement is statistically stable rather than a single-run artifact.
- Incorporating biomechanically grounded metrics beyond L2 error, such as normalized cross-correlation, temporal smoothness/jerk penalization, or adherence to physiological torque limits, to better validate physical plausibility.
- Providing time-series torque waveforms and error propagation analysis (e.g., correlating 3D marker prediction RMSE with final torque error) to visually and quantitatively diagnose whether failures stem from visual perception breakdowns or temporal dynamics regression.

## Novel Insights
The paper demonstrates that inverse dynamics estimation from pixels does not need to be an opaque end-to-end regression; instead, explicitly decomposing the problem into spatial likelihood estimation (via volumetric heatmaps and soft-argmax), anatomical marker coordinate regression, and temporal force inference yields a more robust and interpretable pipeline. This structural prior effectively translates established 3D human pose estimation paradigms into the biomechanical domain, showing that spatial anatomical constraints serve as a stronger inductive bias for torque prediction than direct feature-to-torque mapping.

## Suggestions
- **Implement a strict subject-disjoint split** (e.g., define specific holdout subjects or use Leave-One-Subject-Out) and report these results as the primary benchmark metric to validate cross-individual generalization and eliminate temporal/subject leakage.
- **Revise Equation 2 and Section 4.1** to accurately reflect the inference pipeline, clarifying that kinematic variables serve exclusively as training supervision/targets and are not available at test time.
- **Reconcile dataset inconsistencies** by clearly stating the total number of evaluated actions, listing the exact train/test subject allocation, and providing a mapping/rationale for why 11 specific joint torques were selected for evaluation out of the 35 available.
- **Add a cascaded vision baseline** that chains a state-of-the-art off-the-shelf image-to-pose estimator with a conventional torque regressor. This would isolate whether VID-Network's multi-task training strategy provides genuine architectural advantages over a naive two-stage CV pipeline.
