# Harsh Critic Review
## Section-by-Section Critical Review

### Title & Abstract
- **Title Accuracy:** The title accurately reflects the core contribution: a differentiable method to decompose dense kernels into sparse sequences ("Kernel Complex") and an efficient mechanism for spatially-varying filtering.
- **Abstract Clarity:** The abstract clearly states the problem (quadratic cost of dense convolution), the proposed method (differentiable optimization, shape-aware initialization, filter-space interpolation), and reports key outcomes (higher fidelity than simulated annealing, lower cost than low-rank, practical for real-time/mobile use).
- **Supported Claims:** The claim of "up to a 20 × speedup" is stated but not explicitly anchored to a specific baseline in the abstract. It is crucial to clarify whether this refers to inference speed compared to direct dense convolution, a specific baseline (LowRank/PST), or includes precomputation overhead. The transparency regarding the assumption ("assuming the target dense kernel is known for both optimization and filtering") is appreciated and accurately scoped.

### Introduction & Motivation
- **Motivation & Gap:** The motivation is well-structured. The authors correctly identify the limitation of specialized Gaussian approximations, the density bottleneck of low-rank methods, and the heuristic/iterative inefficiency of parallel simulated annealing (PST). The gap for a principled, gradient-based, sparse decomposition framework is clearly established.
- **Contributions:** The three listed contributions directly map to the methodology and experiments. They are accurately bounded and do not overclaim generality.
- **Claims Calibration:** The introduction carefully positions the method within computational photography and graphics. It does not over-sell neural network integration beyond stating it "integrates cleanly," which is reasonable given the image-agnostic, LSI-based formulation. The scope is appropriate and well-calibrated for ICLR's applied systems/graphics track.

### Method / Approach
- **Reproducibility & Description:** The mathematical formulation is mostly clear, but a critical implementation detail is missing: **how non-integer spatial offsets are handled during convolution**. Optimizing continuous offsets $\mathbf{o}_{l,i}$ (Eq. 2, 5) requires a differentiable sampling/splatting mechanism (e.g., bilinear grid sampling, Gaussian interpolation) to route gradients from the impulse loss back to the offsets. Section 4.1 and 4.4 do not specify this, which blocks exact reproducibility.
- **Key Assumptions:** The assumption of a known target kernel and shift-invariance (Sec 4.4) is explicitly stated and justified via LSI system properties. However, the derivation assumes the multi-pass sequence $F_\approx = K_{s,1} * \dots * K_{s,L}$ commutes with boundary handling. Real images have finite boundaries; multi-pass convolution inherently expands the effective receptive field and interacts with padding strategies. If the impulse response is computed with `same` padding but natural images use different boundary conditions, the approximation fidelity will degrade near edges. This is not discussed.
- **Logical Gaps / Interpolation:** Filter-space interpolation (Sec 4.3, Eq. 9) synthesizes per-pixel kernels via convex combinations: $f(x,y) = \sum \alpha_k f_k$. The paper does not explain how $\alpha(x,y)$ is derived from $P(x,y)$ (linear vs. higher-order? barycentric?). More importantly, interpolating *sparse kernel parameters* (offsets + weights) assumes linearity in the filter-response space. While computationally efficient, geometrically interpolating offsets can break the optimal structural arrangement found during optimization, potentially introducing intermediate kernels that violate the sparsity or fidelity guarantees. The approximation error of this linearization should be quantified or bounded.

### Experiments & Results
- **Claim Testing:** Experiments directly test the single-kernel approximation and SV filtering claims. Fig. 2–5 demonstrate clear visual and perceptual improvements over PST and LowRank across diverse kernel shapes.
- **Baseline Fairness:** Comparisons to PST and LowRank are appropriate. However, the optimization budget asymmetry (1,000 iterations for Ours vs. 100,000 for PST, Sec 5.1) favors Ours heavily. While this demonstrates efficiency, a fairer comparison would report performance *at equal iteration counts* or compute time, rather than just showing Ours converges faster with fewer steps.
- **Missing Ablations:** 
  - **Variance/Significance:** Results show single-run values. No standard deviations or results over multiple random seeds are reported for the optimization process, which is critical for gradient-based methods in non-convex landscapes.
  - **Loss & Boundary Handling:** The choice of Charbonnier L1 (Eq. 11) over L2 or frequency-domain losses (common in kernel approximation) is not ablated. Additionally, there is no evaluation of edge artifacts on full images vs. center crops, which would validate the LSI/impulse response assumption in practice.
  - **Runtime Setup:** Fig. 3 reports inference latency on a Snapdragon 8 Gen 3. It is unclear if baseline methods (LowRank, PST-optimized filters) were implemented with equally optimized tensor operations or memory layouts on the same hardware, which could skew latency claims.
- **Metric Appropriateness:** PSNR, LPIPS, and FLIP-LDR are standard and well-chosen. The LPIPS results consistently favor Ours, supporting the fidelity claims.

### Writing & Clarity
- **Structural Clarity:** The paper is logically organized, moving cleanly from single-kernel decomposition to SV filtering. The impulse-response training paradigm (Sec 4.4) is explained concisely.
- **Clarity Gaps Impeding Understanding:**
  - As noted in Method, the lack of detail on differentiable offset rendering (Eq. 2, 5) is a significant clarity gap for researchers attempting to reimplement the work.
  - Section 4.1 redundantly restates the parameter set $\Theta$ in both text and Eq. 4/5. This is minor but adds noise.
  - The derivation of interpolation weights $\alpha(x,y)$ is mentioned vaguely ("determine a corresponding vector..."). Providing the exact mapping or interpolation scheme is necessary for clarity.
- **Figures/Tables:** Figures 2, 4, and 5 are highly informative and visually demonstrate the contribution effectively. Fig. 3 is clear but lacks explicit annotation of what the "20x" claim refers to. Convergence plots in the ablations (Fig. 6/7) are understandable despite parser artifacts, but labeling axes and legends more robustly in the final version would help.

### Limitations & Broader Impact
- **Acknowledged Limitations:** The authors explicitly acknowledge the need for access to the target dense kernel during optimization. This is honest and properly scopes the contribution to non-blind approximation/filtering design.
- **Missed Limitations:** 
  - **Edge/Boundary Propagation:** As discussed, the multi-pass nature of the sparse complex interacts poorly with standard image padding strategies, potentially causing border artifacts not visible in center-cropped metrics.
  - **Generalization to Neural Pipelines:** While claimed to integrate into learning pipelines, the method is formulated as an offline optimization of static kernels. If embedded in a trainable network where kernels must adapt dynamically to input data, the optimization overhead and fixed initialization would need re-evaluation.
  - **Hardware Sparsity Mapping:** Optimized sparse patterns are irregular. On modern SIMT/GPU architectures, highly irregular memory access patterns can negate theoretical FLOP reductions due to memory bank conflicts or lack of vectorization. The paper assumes uniform speedup across configurations, but hardware-aware sparsity scheduling could yield different results.
- **Broader Impact:** The societal impact is appropriately narrow and positive, focusing on computational efficiency for imaging and graphics. No negative impacts are reasonably foreseeable.

### Overall Assessment
This paper presents a practical, well-motivated framework for efficient kernel approximation that addresses genuine limitations in heuristic sparse sampling and dense low-rank methods. The differentiable formulation is a sensible direction, and the empirical results convincingly demonstrate higher fidelity and faster convergence than the primary baseline (PST). However, the contribution currently falls short of the reproducibility and rigorous evaluation standards expected at ICLR due to several unresolved details: the exact differentiable sampling mechanism for continuous offsets is omitted, the linear interpolation assumption in the spatially-varying extension lacks error analysis, and the experiments lack statistical variance reporting and fair iteration-budgeted comparisons. Additionally, the interaction between multi-pass convolutions and image boundary conditions remains unexamined, which could affect real-world image quality. Addressing these implementation and evaluation gaps—particularly providing a clear algorithmic specification for offset gradients, adding standard deviations/seeded runs, and explicitly bounding runtime comparison conditions—will significantly strengthen the paper and make it a compelling, reproducible contribution to efficient vision systems.

# Neutral Reviewer
## Balanced Review

### Summary
The authors propose DISK, a differentiable optimization framework that approximates dense, arbitrary 2D convolution kernels by composing a sequence of sparse kernels with learnable spatial offsets and weights. By replacing heuristic search with gradient-based training and introducing a shape-aware initialization strategy, the method achieves high-fidelity kernel approximation with substantially fewer optimization steps than prior work. The framework is further extended to spatially-varying filtering via a precomputed basis and linear interpolation, effectively decoupling per-pixel kernel synthesis cost from image resolution.

### Strengths
1. **Efficient Differentiable Formulation:** Casting kernel decomposition as an end-to-end optimization problem over offsets and weights (Eq. 4-5) enables stable, gradient-driven convergence. Empirically, this requires only 1,000 steps versus 100,000 for the Parallel Tempering baseline (Sec 5.1), demonstrating a clear efficiency gain without sacrificing fidelity.
2. **Targeted Initialization Strategy:** The combination of layer-wise radial expansion and rejection sampling from the target kernel’s non-zero support (Sec 4.2) directly addresses the poor local optima and vanishing gradient issues common in sparse optimization. The ablation study (Fig 6) quantitatively validates its superiority over random baselines for both PST and the proposed method.
3. **Practical Spatially-Varying Extension:** The filter-space interpolation approach (Sec 4.3, Eq 9) cleverly decouples kernel generation complexity from spatial resolution. By precomputing a compact basis and performing on-the-fly convex combination of offsets/weights, it enables real-time SV rendering with minimal overhead, as demonstrated on mobile hardware (Fig 3).
4. **Comprehensive Empirical Validation:** The evaluation covers a diverse set of kernels (Gaussian, non-convex, optical PSFs) and reports standard perceptual/numerical metrics (LPIPS, PSNR, FLIP-LDR) alongside real-device latency. The speed-quality trade-offs are clearly visualized and consistently favor the proposed method across varying sparsity levels.

### Weaknesses
1. **Unvalidated Linearity Assumption in SV Interpolation:** The method linearly interpolates sparse kernel offsets and weights across a parameter space (Sec 4.3). While empirically effective for smooth parameter changes, the paper provides no theoretical justification or empirical error analysis for this assumption. Linear blending of spatial coordinates may fail for kernels with topological differences or large parameter jumps, potentially introducing unquantified approximation artifacts.
2. **Limited Relevance to Modern Deep Learning Pipelines:** The paper claims the method “integrates cleanly into learning pipelines” and is “usable as a trainable layer” (Abstract, Sec 6). However, Sec 5 exclusively evaluates static image reconstruction against ground-truth kernels. No experiments demonstrate gradient flow through the optimized filters during end-to-end training, nor is there evaluation on downstream ML tasks (e.g., classification, segmentation, or neural rendering) where such efficient convolutions would typically be deployed.
3. **Dated and Narrow Baseline Comparisons:** The competitive landscape is restricted to Low-Rank decomposition (2015) and Parallel Simulated Tempering (2020). The evaluation omits modern alternatives highly relevant to ICLR, such as dynamic depthwise separable convolutions, implicit neural filter representations, or recent sparse/efficient CNN architectures. This makes it difficult to assess whether the proposed method offers clear advantages over contemporary learned efficient operators.
4. **Reproducibility & Implementation Gaps:** Critical implementation details are missing or underspecified. The exact boundary handling/padding during the impulse-response optimization (Sec 4.4) is unclear, the memory footprint and data structure of the LUT basis are not quantified, and hyperparameter sensitivity is not discussed. Furthermore, no code or optimized inference implementation is referenced, which limits reproducibility.

### Novelty & Significance
**Novelty:** Moderate/Incremental. The core concept of sparse kernel approximation directly builds upon Schuster et al. (2020), with the primary novelty shifting the optimization paradigm from heuristic search to differentiable gradient descent coupled with a structured initialization. The SV foundation via precomputed linear interpolation is a practical engineering contribution rather than a fundamental algorithmic or theoretical breakthrough.
**Clarity:** High. The paper is well-structured, with clear mathematical formulations, intuitive visualizations, and a logical progression from single-kernel approximation to spatially-varying extensions. The impulse-response optimization strategy is particularly well-explained and easy to follow.
**Reproducibility:** Moderate. While the optimization objective, optimizer, and iteration counts are specified, key practical details (impulse padding, LUT storage format, gradient clipping/thresholding, and hardware-specific kernel implementations) are omitted. Without released code or detailed sensitivity analysis, faithful reproduction and fair comparison may be challenging.
**Significance:** Moderate for ICLR. The method is highly relevant to real-time rendering, computational photography, and edge AI deployment. However, ICLR’s acceptance bar typically prioritizes broader impacts on machine learning methodology, optimization theory, or novel learned representations. The work’s applied focus and lack of integration benchmarks limit its potential impact on the core ICLR community, placing it more comfortably in specialized tracks for efficient computing or graphics-oriented ML.

### Suggestions for Improvement
1. **Analyze & Quantify Interpolation Stability:** Provide an empirical error analysis of the linear interpolation step as a function of basis density and parameter distance. If linear blending of offsets proves unstable, consider a learned interpolation mechanism, spline-based blending, or a metric-aware basis selection strategy.
2. **Demonstrate End-to-End Learning Integration:** Validate the claim of seamless pipeline integration by embedding the differentiable sparse kernel complex within a standard CNN or a differentiable renderer (e.g., a lightweight classification backbone or NeRF). Report training convergence, gradient norm stability, and downstream task performance to prove compatibility with modern DL workflows.
3. **Expand Baseline Comparisons & Contextualize within Modern DL:** Include comparisons against contemporary efficient convolution methods (e.g., dynamic convolutions, implicit filters, or hardware-aware sparse operators) to clearly position DISK’s advantages in the current research landscape. Discuss how it complements or differs from standard architectural efficient designs like grouped or depthwise separable convolutions.
4. **Enhance Reproducibility & Theoretical Grounding:** Release the optimization code and inference implementations. Add explicit details on impulse boundary conditions, memory/compute overhead of the LUT, and formally derive the computational complexity vs. approximation error trade-off (FLOPs/MACs as a function of L and N). A brief discussion on convergence properties or condition numbers for the sparse parameterization would strengthen the paper’s academic rigor.

# Spark Finder Review
## How to Improve This Paper

### Missing Experiments (top 3-5 only)
1. End-to-end training in a downstream vision/GDN task (e.g., blind deblurring or video interpolation). The paper claims seamless pipeline integration but provides zero evidence that backpropagation through DISK improves task metrics or converges stably without the precomputed target kernel.
2. Actual hardware latency runs using a real sparse convolution backend (e.g., TensorRT, TVM, or a custom CUDA sparse operator). Standard dense `Conv2d` calls cannot exploit arbitrary irregular masks; without true sparse execution, the "20× speedup" claim is an unverified theoretical projection.
3. Comparisons against modern efficient convolution baselines like FFT/Wavenet filtering, Winograd minimal filtering, or adaptive/dynamic convolutions. Comparing only to 2015 low-rank and 2020 heuristic methods understates the current state-of-the-art and weakens the superiority claim.

### Deeper Analysis Needed (top 3-5 only)
1. Quantitative interpolation error bounds vs. basis count ($M$) and parameter distance. The SV filtering claim hinges on optimized kernels "varying more linearly," but no metric validates this interpolation smoothness, leaving the required basis size and fidelity trade-off completely opaque.
2. Optimization landscape and gradient flow analysis across layers. While differentiability is argued as superior to simulated annealing, absent gradient norm trajectories or Hessian conditioning analysis, it's unclear if the method genuinely navigates the non-convex landscape or exploits flat loss plateaus that overfit the impulse response.
3. Memory footprint and arithmetic intensity breakdown for the SV Filter-Space LUT. Decoupling kernel generation from resolution is meaningless if the stored basis and blending weights exceed mobile RAM/VRAM limits or cause bandwidth bottlenecks during runtime interpolation.

### Visualizations & Case Studies
1. Exact learned sparse support patterns (offset coordinates mapped to weights). Rendering where samples actually land reveals if DISK learns physically meaningful distributions matching kernel contours or collapses to degenerate, highly clustered configurations that are impossible to map to real hardware memory layouts.
2. Continuous interpolation sweeps between basis filters. Plotting or animating the synthesized kernel shape as the control parameter transitions between two basis points exposes hidden ringing, topological flips, or discontinuities that single-point PSNR/LPIPS scores mask.
3. Explicit failure cases on highly multi-modal, discontinuous, or extreme-scale kernels. Demonstrating where the method degrades defines the operational envelope and prevents overgeneralization from the curated set of smooth and star-shaped test kernels.

### Obvious Next Steps
1. Provide a deployable sparse convolution implementation or tight integration with a standard sparse tensor library, because claiming mobile real-time performance without demonstrating how arbitrary learned offsets are actually executed leaves the core efficiency claim unbuilt and unverified.
2. Derive an empirical scaling law or closed-form heuristic for selecting layer count ($L$) vs. samples per layer ($N$) given a target error budget, replacing the current ad-hoc grid search so practitioners can scale DISK to new compute constraints.
3. Ablate the Charbonnier L1 loss against frequency-weighted or standard perceptual losses. Different loss functions fundamentally alter passband/stopband behavior and ringiness in synthesized kernels, making the choice arbitrary without proving it optimizes the downstream rendering or vision quality.

# Final Consolidated Review
## Summary
This paper introduces DISK, a differentiable optimization framework that decomposes dense, arbitrary 2D convolution kernels into sequences of sparse layers with learnable continuous offsets and weights. By replacing heuristic search with gradient descent and introducing a topology-aware initialization scheme, the method achieves high-fidelity kernel approximation with orders-of-fewer optimization steps than parallel simulated annealing. It is extended to spatially-varying filtering via precomputed basis interpolation, claiming significant inference speedups on mobile hardware.

## Strengths
- **Efficient Differentiable Formulation:** Casting sparse kernel approximation as an end-to-end optimization over continuous spatial coordinates and weights successfully bypasses the combinatorial bottlenecks of prior heuristic methods, achieving stable convergence in $\sim$1,000 iterations versus 100,000+ for Parallel Simulated Tempering.
- **Topology-Respecting Initialization:** The dual initialization strategy (progressive radial expansion plus rejection sampling from non-zero target support) directly addresses the vanishing gradient and poor local optima problems that typically plague sparse filter optimization, consistently outperforming random baselines across convex and non-convex shapes.
- **Resolution-Decoupled SV Filtering:** The filter-space interpolation mechanism provides a practical engineering solution to the per-pixel kernel synthesis bottleneck, enabling complex spatially-varying effects (e.g., tilt-shift, anisotropic bokeh) with negligible overhead during inference.

## Weaknesses
- **Unsubstantiated Hardware Efficiency Claims:** The reported "up to 20× speedup" and mobile latency metrics rely on theoretical MAC reductions ($\sum N_l \ll M^2$), but arbitrarily sparse patterns with continuous offsets incur severe memory access overhead and irregular memory banking on SIMT/GPU architectures. The paper does not describe a custom sparse convolution backend or optimized memory layout used to realize these speedups. Standard dense or blocked convolution routines cannot natively exploit the learned irregular masks, rendering the practical efficiency claims speculative without detailed implementation disclosure or verified sparse execution timing.
- **Unbounded Linearity Assumption in SV Interpolation:** The spatially-varying extension synthesizes per-pixel kernels via direct linear interpolation of basis offsets and weights (Eq. 9). This assumes the mapping from control parameters to optimal sparse geometry lies on a locally linear manifold. Without theoretical justification or empirical error bounds, blending continuous spatial coordinates linearly is geometrically unsound and prone to topological discontinuities, ringing, or structural collapse when basis filters diverge significantly in shape or scale. The empirical success on smooth Gaussian-like variations does not guarantee validity for the broader class of arbitrary kernels claimed.
- **Narrow Evaluation Scope & Lack of End-to-End Integration:** Despite claiming seamless integration into machine learning pipelines, the evaluation is strictly confined to offline, image-agnostic optimization against fixed ground-truth kernels. There are no experiments demonstrating stable gradient flow through the sparse complex during end-to-end training, nor evaluations on downstream vision or rendering tasks (e.g., neural fields, adaptive filtering, or deblurring). Furthermore, comparisons are limited to dated baselines (LowRank 2015, PST 2020), omitting contemporary learned efficient operators (e.g., dynamic/deformable convolutions, implicit filter representations, Winograd/FFT-based large-kernel methods), making it difficult to assess DISK's standing within current ML methodology.
- **Critical Reproducibility Omissions (Differentiable Sampling & Boundaries):** The mathematical formulation optimizes continuous 2D offsets but omits the exact differentiable sampling or splatting mechanism (e.g., bilinear grid sampling, kernel density estimation, or spatial transformers) required to compute gradients with respect to $\mathbf{o}_{l,i}$. Additionally, the impulse-response training assumes circular or zero-padded boundaries, while multi-pass sparse convolution on finite images inherently expands the receptive field and interacts unpredictably with standard padding strategies. Unquantified boundary degradation in SV applications remains unaddressed.

## Nice-to-Haves
- Report optimization variance across multiple random seeds to confirm non-convex training stability.
- Ablate the Charbonnier L1 loss against frequency-weighted or perceptual objectives to justify the choice for kernel approximation.
- Provide exact derivation of interpolation weights $\alpha(x,y)$ and quantify the LUT memory footprint vs. basis size $M$.
- Release reference code and the mobile inference implementation to enable fair hardware benchmarking.

## Novel Insights
The paper successfully reframes sparse kernel decomposition from a discrete combinatorial search problem into a continuous geometric parameter learning task. By optimizing offsets directly in coordinate space and initializing them to respect target topology, the method exposes a tractable pathway to high-fidelity filter approximation that aligns classical signal processing constraints with modern gradient-based optimization. However, the reliance on linear interpolation for spatial variation and the lack of differentiable sampling formalization highlight a recurring tension in efficient vision systems: theoretical FLOP reductions often outpace the development of robust, hardware-aware continuous operators capable of stable integration into broader learning pipelines.

## Potentially Missed Related Work
- **Deformable & Dynamic Convolutions** (e.g., Dai et al., 2017; Yang et al., 2019; Chen et al., 2021) — Directly relevant as they also optimize continuous spatial offsets and sampling weights via gradient descent; a comparison would clarify whether DISK's multi-pass decomposition offers structural advantages or merely trades depth for offset flexibility.
- **FFT & Winograd Minimal Filtering** — Standard highly optimized techniques for large-kernel convolution in modern frameworks; benchmarking against these is necessary to contextualize the "20x speedup" claim in practical deep learning workloads.
- **Implicit Neural Representations for Filters/Kernels** (e.g., continuous filter networks, SIREN-based PSF modeling) — Relevant for understanding whether precomputed basis interpolation outperforms learned implicit functions for spatially-varying parameter mapping.

## Suggestions
1. **Provide a Differentiable Sampling Formalization & Boundary Analysis:** Explicitly state and implement the grid-sampling/splatting function used for backpropagating through continuous offsets. Conduct an empirical evaluation comparing center-cropped fidelity vs. full-image boundary behavior under different padding strategies, especially in the multi-pass SV configuration.
2. **Open the Inference Implementation & Real Hardware Benchmarking:** Release the actual sparse convolution kernel or specify the exact backend optimizations used. If custom hardware scheduling wasn't applied, reframe claims to theoretical complexity reductions and report measured latency using standard PyTorch/TensorRT fallbacks. Provide an equal-compute-time comparison against PST to validate superiority beyond iteration count differences.
3. **Quantify Interpolation Error & Linearity Limits:** Derive an empirical error bound for Eq. 9 as a function of basis density $M$ and parameter distance. Include continuous interpolation sweeps between basis filters to visually and quantitatively expose topological breaks or synthesis artifacts. If linear blending is unstable for complex shapes, propose or evaluate spline-based or attention-weighted interpolation mechanisms.
4. **Expand Baselines and Demonstrate ML Utility:** Compare against at least one modern efficient convolution paradigm (dynamic/deformable convs or FFT/Wavenet filtering) and include one end-to-end training experiment where the sparse complex is embedded in a standard backbone or differentiable pipeline to validate the claimed integrative capability for ICLR audiences.
