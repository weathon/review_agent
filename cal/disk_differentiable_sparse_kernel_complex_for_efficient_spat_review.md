# Harsh Critic Review
## Section-by-Section Critical Review

### Title & Abstract
- **Does the title accurately reflect the contribution?** Yes. The title precisely captures the method (differentiable, sparse kernel complex) and target application (spatially-variant convolution).
- **Does the abstract clearly state the problem, method, and key results?** The problem and method are clearly stated. Key results mention "up to a 20× speedup" and "higher fidelity than simulated annealing," but these lack context. The 20× figure has no baseline specified (vs. dense? vs. low-rank? vs. PST?). 
- **Are any claims in the abstract unsupported by the paper?** The claim that the method "integrates cleanly into learning pipelines" appears in both the abstract and conclusion, but the paper contains zero experiments demonstrating integration into a neural network, gradient flow through the layer during training, or downstream impact on a standard vision pipeline. This is a significant unsupported claim for an ICLR submission.

### Introduction & Motivation
- **Is the problem well-motivated? Is the gap in prior work clearly identified?** The motivation for accelerating complex, non-convex convolutions is clear and relevant to both graphics and vision. The gap identifying the limitations of heuristic search (PST) and the inflexibility of low-rank/O(1) methods is well-articulated.
- **Are the contributions clearly stated and accurate?** Yes. The three contributions (differentiable decomposition, initialization scheme, filter-space interpolation) are explicit.
- **Does the introduction over-claim or under-sell?** The introduction leans heavily into graphics/rendering applications (Depth of Field, PSFs) and mobile imaging, which dilutes its positioning for ICLR. While it mentions DL integration, it under-sells how this could serve modern neural architectures (e.g., as a parameter-efficient convolutional layer or for dynamic PSF estimation networks). The introduction accurately reflects the paper's scope but misaligns with typical ICLR expectations regarding learning-centric contributions.

### Method / Approach
- **Is the method clearly described and reproducible?** The mathematical formulation is sound, but critical implementation details are missing. Specifically, offsets $\mathbf{o}_{l,i}$ are treated as continuous 2D vectors. Standard deep learning frameworks do not natively convolve with non-integer grid offsets. Do you use bilinear grid sampling, scatter-add operations, or learned grid offsets? Without specifying how gradients flow through non-integer spatial sampling, the method is not reproducible.
- **Are key assumptions stated and justified?** The assumption that the target dense kernel $K_{tgt}$ is known for *every* parameter value during optimization and runtime is explicitly stated but practically restrictive. In many SV scenarios (e.g., real-world lens PSFs, learned deconvs), the ground-truth dense kernel is unknown or expensive to acquire. This limits applicability to scenarios where an analytical or pre-calibrated PSF model exists.
- **Are there logical gaps in the derivation or reasoning?** 
  1. **Support Expansion:** The composite kernel $F_{approx}$ is the convolution of $L$ sparse layers. Convolution inherently expands spatial support. If $K_{s,l}$ spans a radius $r_l$, the final support scales roughly with $\sum r_l$. The paper does not discuss how boundary conditions, padding, or cropping are handled to ensure $F_{approx}$ aligns with the target $K_{tgt}$'s spatial extent.
  2. **Offset Interpolation Validity:** Equation 9 proposes linearly interpolating both weights and offsets between basis filters. Linearly interpolating spatial offsets is geometrically non-linear with respect to the resulting convolutional response. Moving two sparse centers linearly does not guarantee the intermediate PSF linearly transitions between the two shapes. This could introduce artifacts or break the approximation property. The paper assumes it works empirically but lacks theoretical or practical justification for why offset interpolation preserves filter structure.
- **Are there edge cases or failure modes not discussed?** The method does not address zero-sum or bandpass kernels (e.g., edge detectors, Laplacians). The Charbonnier L1 loss may struggle to enforce exact zero-mean constraints common in such filters.

### Experiments & Results
- **Do the experiments actually test the paper's claims?** The single-kernel and SV filtering experiments test fidelity and latency. They do not test the claim of DL pipeline integration. The latency experiments test rendering speed, which is valid for the claimed mobile/graphics use case but less aligned with ICLR's standard evaluation norms.
- **Are baselines appropriate and fairly compared?** Baselines (PST, LowRank) are standard for graphics filtering but somewhat dated. The paper mentions depthwise separable convolutions and factorized networks in Related Work but excludes them from comparison. For ICLR, comparing against modern factorized or dynamic convolution baselines (e.g., DynConv, spatially-adaptive separable filters) would strengthen the evaluation.
- **Are there missing ablations that would materially change conclusions?** Yes. 
  1. A critical ablation for Sec 4.3 is missing: interpolating only weights while keeping offsets fixed vs. interpolating both. This would isolate whether the performance gain comes from the differentiable optimization or the interpolation scheme itself.
  2. No ablation on the sensitivity to the Charbonnier loss parameters vs. standard L1/L2.
- **Are error bars / statistical significance reported?** No. Optimization is stochastic (Adam), and initialization may vary. LPIPS/PSNR are reported as single values. No variance, confidence intervals, or multi-run averages are provided, making it impossible to assess robustness.
- **Do the results support the claims made, or are they cherry-picked?** Results are generally consistent, but the "20× speedup" claim lacks a precise breakdown in the text. Figure 3 shows latency vs. quality, but the absolute latency numbers are highly hardware-dependent and don't quantify the 20× figure relative to a specific baseline.
- **Are datasets and evaluation metrics appropriate?** PSNR, LPIPS, and FLIP-LDR are appropriate for image fidelity. However, relying solely on synthetic/analytical kernels and a handful of high-res photos limits generalization claims. Real-world PSF datasets or standard vision benchmarks would be stronger.

### Writing & Clarity
- **Are there sections that are confusing or poorly explained?** Section 4.3 (Spatially Varying Filtering) needs clearer exposition on how the "parameter space" maps to physical filter properties. The transition from optimizing individual basis filters to runtime interpolation is conceptually clear, but the practical mechanism of blending two sets of discrete offsets and weights into a single functional kernel at runtime deserves a concrete example or pseudocode.
- **Are figures and tables clear and informative?** Figures 2, 4, and 5 effectively visualize qualitative improvements. However, convergence plots (Fig 6/7, despite parsing artifacts) would benefit from logarithmic iteration axes to better contrast the 1000 vs 100,000 step disparity. Tables are currently missing from the text, so quantitative comparisons rely on figure inlays.

### Limitations & Broader Impact
- **Do the authors acknowledge the key limitations?** They acknowledge the requirement for a known target dense kernel during both optimization and filtering.
- **Are there fundamental limitations they missed?** 
  1. **Optimization Overhead:** The paper treats the 1000-step optimization as trivial, but generating a basis for multi-dimensional SV filtering (e.g., varying $\sigma$ AND orientation) requires $M$ independent optimizations. The offline compute cost and storage for large $M$ are not quantified.
  2. **Non-analytical Kernels:** In real optical systems or neural feature spaces, the dense PSF may not be available in closed form. The method cannot approximate kernels that must be learned jointly with network weights unless the dense kernel is explicitly computed each forward pass, negating efficiency gains.
- **Are there failure modes or negative societal impacts not discussed?** Failure modes for highly anisotropic or extremely sparse kernels (where $N_l \ll$ required samples) aren't discussed. Broader impact is standard; efficient filtering reduces compute energy, which is positive. No major negative impacts identified.

### Overall Assessment
This paper proposes a well-motivated, differentiable approach to approximating dense convolutional filters via composed sparse layers, offering a clear improvement over heuristic simulated annealing in both fidelity and optimization stability. The core idea of filter-space interpolation for efficient spatially-varying filtering is elegant and practically useful for graphics pipelines. However, for ICLR, the paper faces significant hurdles: (1) the claim of seamless DL integration is entirely unsubstantiated experimentally; (2) critical implementation details regarding continuous offset handling, support boundary management, and the validity of linearly interpolating sparse offsets are missing; (3) the evaluation lacks statistical rigor and omits modern deep learning baselines. The work is technically sound within the graphics/imaging domain but currently reads as an application-specific engineering contribution rather than a core machine learning advancement. With rigorous ablation of the interpolation scheme, clarification of the differentiable sampling mechanism, and experiments demonstrating integration into a learning pipeline or real-world PSF estimation task, this could be a strong method. As submitted, it is likely below ICLR's acceptance bar due to insufficient ML validation and unresolved technical details.

# Neutral Reviewer
## Balanced Review

### Summary
The paper introduces DISK, a differentiable framework that approximates complex, dense convolution kernels by learning a sequence of sparse layers via gradient-based optimization. The authors propose a two-part initialization strategy to handle non-convex kernel shapes and a filter-space interpolation mechanism to enable efficient, resolution-independent spatially varying filtering. Evaluated across analytical, geometric, and optical kernels, DISK demonstrates higher fidelity and faster convergence than simulated annealing and low-rank baselines, with reported speedups suitable for mobile hardware.

### Strengths
1. **Efficient Gradient-Based Optimization:** Replacing heuristic search (parallel simulated annealing) with an end-to-end differentiable formulation yields dramatically faster convergence and more stable solutions. The paper demonstrates this concretely: DISK requires only 1,000 optimization steps versus 100,000 for the PST baseline (Sec. 5.1, Sec. 5.2), while consistently achieving lower LPIPS and higher PSNR across Gaussian and arbitrary kernels (Fig. 2, Fig. 4).
2. **Thoughtful Initialization for Non-Convex Kernels:** The proposed Sparse Sampling (SS) initialization, which restricts initial offsets to the support of the target kernel, directly addresses the vanishing gradient/local optimum problem common in sparse filter learning. Ablation results in Fig. 6 clearly show SS outperforms Random and Radial initializations and significantly accelerates convergence for complex shapes like hearts and optical PSFs.
3. **Practical Mobile Benchmarking & SV Decoupling:** The paper includes real-world latency measurements on a Snapdragon 8 Gen 3 SoC (Sec. 5.1), validating claims of practical efficiency. The filter-space interpolation strategy (Sec. 4.3) successfully decouples per-pixel kernel synthesis cost from image resolution, enabling complex spatially varying effects (e.g., rotational bokeh, tilt-shift) with minimal runtime overhead, as shown qualitatively and quantitatively in Fig. 5.

### Weaknesses
1. **Missing Details on Gradient Computation for Continuous Offsets:** The method treats spatial offsets $\mathbf{o}_{l,i}$ as continuous learnable parameters (Eq. 4, Sec. 4.1). However, the paper does not specify how gradients are backpropagated through non-integer pixel coordinates. Standard differentiable approaches (e.g., deformable convolutions, bilinear interpolation splatting) or straight-through estimators are not described, making it unclear how the "end-to-end differentiable" claim is technically realized or how discretization is handled at inference vs. training.
2. **Lack of Machine Learning Pipeline Integration:** Despite claiming the method "integrates cleanly into learning pipelines" (Abstract) and positioning it for modern deep learning, all experiments are purely optimization-based using impulse responses or static images. There are no experiments embedding DISK as a trainable layer within a neural network (e.g., for differentiable rendering, denoising, or adaptive style transfer) to demonstrate gradient stability, impact on downstream task loss, or joint optimization with backbone networks. For ICLR, this limits the method's demonstrated relevance to the ML community.
3. **Narrow Baseline Comparisons:** The empirical evaluation primarily compares against classical/heuristic methods (Simulated Annealing/PST, SVD Low-Rank). While these are appropriate for kernel approximation, the paper cites but omits comparison to modern learned spatially-variant or dynamic convolution methods from the deep learning literature (e.g., Niklaus et al., 2017; Zhou et al., 2019; Dynamic Convolution approaches). This makes it difficult to assess whether the proposed sparse sequence offers advantages beyond traditional signal processing approximations in a learning-driven context.
4. **Empirical-Only Analysis of Interpolation:** The spatially varying filtering relies on linearly interpolating basis kernel offsets and weights (Eq. 9). While visually compelling (Fig. 5), the paper provides no theoretical bounds, smoothness analysis, or error metrics regarding interpolation fidelity across the parameter space. Linear interpolation of non-linear geometric offsets can introduce artifacts or break the target kernel's frequency response, yet this trade-off is unquantified.

### Novelty & Significance
**Novelty:** Moderate. Sparse kernel approximation and sequential multi-pass filtering are established concepts in graphics. The novelty lies in packaging these into a fully differentiable optimization routine with a dedicated initialization scheme and a parameterized interpolation basis for SV filtering. The gradient-based formulation is a principled upgrade over heuristic search, and the shape-aware initialization is a sensible, effective engineering contribution.  
**Clarity:** High. The paper is well-structured, with clear problem motivation, explicit mathematical formulation, and effective visualizations. The distinction between offline optimization and runtime synthesis is clearly explained.  
**Reproducibility:** Moderate. Hyperparameters (optimizer, LR schedule, steps) and hardware are provided. However, reproducibility is hindered by the missing details on continuous offset handling during backpropagation, the exact implementation of sparse convolution on discrete hardware/grid, and the lack of code/pseudocode for the rejection sampling procedure (noted as "Detailed procedure in appendix," which is not provided here).  
**Significance:** High for computational photography, real-time graphics, and mobile vision systems where arbitrary kernel application is a bottleneck. For ICLR, significance is moderate unless the differentiable properties are rigorously demonstrated within broader deep learning pipelines. The work bridges classical signal processing and differentiable programming, offering a practical, efficient module that could accelerate various vision pipelines if integrated effectively.

### Suggestions for Improvement
1. **Clarify Backpropagation through Continuous Offsets:** Explicitly describe the mechanism used to compute gradients w.r.t. continuous $\mathbf{o}_{l,i}$ (e.g., bilinear grid sampling, soft assignment, or STN-style interpolation). Provide a brief derivation or implementation snippet, and clarify how optimized continuous offsets are quantized/discretized for efficient integer-based inference on mobile GPUs.
2. **Demonstrate ML Pipeline Integration:** Add at least one experiment where DISK is used as a differentiable layer within a neural network training loop. For example, fine-tune a lightweight denoiser or image-to-image translation model where the DISK parameters are jointly optimized with network weights using a task-specific loss, demonstrating that gradients flow stably and downstream performance improves or matches dense baselines.
3. **Expand Baseline Comparisons & Interpolation Analysis:** Compare against 1-2 modern dynamic/kernel prediction methods to contextualize performance within current ML standards. Additionally, include a quantitative analysis of the filter-space interpolation, such as measuring interpolation error (LPIPS/PSNR difference between interpolated and directly optimized kernels) across the parameter range, or discuss conditions under which linear combination of sparse parameters breaks down.
4. **Release Implementation/Pseudocode for Reproducibility:** Ensure the supplementary material contains complete pseudocode for the training loop, offset gradient computation, sparse convolution implementation, and the exact rejection sampling initialization. This is crucial for verifying the claimed efficiency gains and facilitating adoption by the community.

# Spark Finder Review
## How to Improve This Paper

### Missing Experiments (top 3 only)
1. Run end-to-end training on a standard downstream task (e.g., image denoising or super-resolution) using DISK as a trainable layer. Without demonstrating actual gradient propagation and task performance in a learning pipeline, the "integrates cleanly into learning pipelines" claim remains theoretically asserted rather than empirically validated.
2. Compare against modern efficient adaptive convolution methods (e.g., Involution, Dynamic Convolution, or depthwise-separable + spatial attention baselines). Relying solely on 2020 heuristics and linear SVD decomposition makes the efficiency gains unmeasured against current vision standards expected at ICLR.
3. Add an ablation varying the number of basis filters ($M$) and interpolation schemes (nearest, linear, cubic) for spatially-varying cases. The paper claims seamless multi-dimensional SV filtering but provides no evidence of how basis density or interpolation choice affects transition quality or runtime cost.

### Deeper Analysis Needed (top 3 only)
1. Explicitly derive the gradient computation mechanism for learnable spatial offsets in discrete convolution. Clarifying whether bilinear sampling, soft masks, or differentiable splatting is used for backpropagation is essential to trust the reported stability on non-convex shapes.
2. Provide formal FLOP/MAC counts and memory bandwidth equations as a function of layers ($L$), samples per layer ($N$), and basis size ($M$). The "up to 20× speedup" and "decoupled from image resolution" claims lack theoretical grounding and cannot be audited without explicit complexity analysis.
3. Analyze how convex interpolation of offset-weight pairs preserves valid sparse sampling and avoids weight cancellation or offset collisions. Without mathematical or empirical bounds on interpolation error, the SV filtering approach risks introducing ringing or ghosting artifacts that metrics may mask.

### Visualizations & Case Studies
1. Plot optimization trajectories showing offset migration and weight convergence across iterations for highly non-convex targets. This would expose whether gradient descent genuinely escapes local minima or if the results are highly fragile to initialization.
2. Provide high-contrast error maps specifically at spatially-varying transition boundaries (e.g., sharp-to-blur gradients). This would reveal whether filter-space interpolation causes blending artifacts, discontinuity ringing, or loss of structural coherence.
3. Showcase explicit failure cases using extreme-frequency PSFs or kernels with deep interior holes. Establishing hard sparsity limits prevents overgeneralization and clarifies where the approximation fundamentally breaks down.

### Obvious Next Steps
1. Disclose the exact runtime implementation for sparse, spatially-variant filtering on GPUs/mobile (e.g., custom CUDA kernel, hash-map lookup, or dense masking). Mobile latency claims cannot be reproduced or trusted without knowing how arbitrary offsets are actually executed in hardware.
2. Extend the formulation to multi-channel feature maps and explain how channel-wise interactions are handled without quadratic cost explosion. The impulse-response setup assumes single-channel processing, which is insufficient for modern RGB or deep feature pipelines.
3. Evaluate the framework under standard quantization (FP16/INT8) and mixed-precision inference. Real-time mobile deployment claims require evidence that the learned sparse kernels survive precision reduction without collapsing approximation fidelity.

# Final Consolidated Review
## Summary
DISK introduces a differentiable optimization framework that approximates arbitrary dense convolution kernels using a compositional sequence of sparse layers. By replacing heuristic simulated annealing with gradient-based learning and introducing a support-aware initialization strategy, the method achieves significantly faster convergence and higher reconstruction fidelity for both convex and non-convex kernels. The framework further extends to spatially-varying filtering via a filter-space interpolation scheme that decouples kernel synthesis cost from image resolution, demonstrating practical runtime speedups on mobile hardware.

## Strengths
- **Principled acceleration over heuristic search:** Reformulating kernel decomposition as an end-to-end gradient optimization replaces brittle parallel tempering. Empirical results (Sec. 5.2, Fig. 2/4) demonstrate dramatically faster convergence (1,000 vs. 100,000 steps) and consistently lower perceptual error (LPIPS/FLIP-LDR) across analytically defined and complex optical PSFs.
- **Effective handling of non-convex geometries:** The proposed sparse-sampling initialization restricts initial offsets to the non-zero support of the target kernel, directly addressing vanishing gradient and local optima pitfalls. Ablations (Fig. 6) confirm this strategy reliably stabilizes optimization for irregular shapes where radial or random initialization fails.
- **Resolution-decoupled spatially-varying filtering:** The filter-space interpolation mechanism (Sec. 4.3) successfully precomputes a compact basis of optimized sparse filters and synthesizes per-pixel kernels via convex combination. This enables complex SV effects (tilt-shift, rotational bokeh) with minimal runtime overhead, validated by latency measurements on a Snapdragon 8 Gen 3 SoC (Sec. 5.1).

## Weaknesses
- **Unspecified gradient flow through continuous offsets:** The method treats spatial offsets $\mathbf{o}_{l,i}$ as continuous, learnable parameters optimized via backpropagation (Eq. 4–5), yet omits how gradients are computed when sampling from a discrete pixel grid. Standard deep learning frameworks require bilinear grid sampling, splatting, or deformable convolution logic for non-integer offsets; without specifying the implementation, the reproducibility and technical validity of the "end-to-end differentiable" claim remain unclear.
- **Unsubstantiated claim of learning pipeline integration:** The abstract and conclusion emphasize that DISK "integrates cleanly into learning pipelines" and can serve as a trainable layer. However, all experiments are restricted to image-agnostic impulse-response optimization and static SV filtering. There is no demonstration of DISK operating within a neural network training loop (e.g., joint optimization for denoising, deblurring, or differentiable rendering), leaving its practical utility and gradient stability in modern DL systems unverified for an ML-focused venue.
- **Lack of quantitative analysis for spatial interpolation:** Runtime SV filtering relies on linearly interpolating both weights and geometric offsets across a precomputed basis (Eq. 9). Linearly interpolating spatial coordinates is mathematically non-linear with respect to the resulting convolutional response. While qualitative results are strong (Fig. 5), the paper provides no bounds, interpolation error metrics, or discussion of transition boundaries, making it difficult to assess when or how the blending strategy might introduce artifacts or break PSF coherence.

## Nice-to-Haves
- Clarify how optimized continuous offsets are discretized or mapped to integer coordinates for hardware-efficient inference on mobile GPUs.
- Include an interpolation ablation isolating weight-only blending vs. full offset+weight blending to quantify the contribution of geometric interpolation.
- Provide explicit FLOP/MAC complexity equations as a function of layers ($L$), samples per layer ($N_l$), and basis size ($M$) to analytically ground the reported speedup claims.

## Novel Insights
None beyond the paper's own contributions.

## Suggestions
- Explicitly detail the backpropagation mechanism used for continuous spatial offsets (e.g., grid sampling, soft assignment, or custom CUDA kernels) and specify how optimized floating-point offsets are deployed during low-precision runtime inference.
- Add a minimal experiment embedding DISK as a differentiable module within a standard vision training pipeline to empirically validate gradient flow, convergence stability, and downstream task performance during joint optimization.
- Provide a quantitative interpolation error analysis measuring the divergence (e.g., LPIPS or PSNR) between synthetically interpolated kernels and directly optimized ground-truth kernels across the parameter range, alongside a brief discussion of the geometric conditions where linear offset blending remains valid.
