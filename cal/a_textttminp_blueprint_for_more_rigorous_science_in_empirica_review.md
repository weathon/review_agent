# Harsh Critic Review
## Section-by-Section Critical Review

### Title & Abstract
- **Title Accuracy:** The title accurately reflects the paper's core structure: a detailed case study on min-p sampling followed by methodological recommendations. However, "Blueprint" implies a formal, structured framework, whereas the paper ultimately delivers a set of generalizable lessons and best practices. Slightly overstating the structural novelty of the "blueprint" may set expectations higher than the delivered content.
- **Abstract Clarity:** The abstract clearly outlines the problem (rigor crisis), the method (re-analysis of 4 evidence pillars), and key results (conclusions of the original paper are not supported by the data, and several methodological lessons are extracted).
- **Unsupported Claims:** The phrase "its conclusions are invalidated by its own data" in the abstract is stronger than what the subsequent analysis supports. Section 2.2 and Table 1 correctly show *insufficient evidence for consistent superiority*, not definitive invalidation. The data suggest parity or marginal variance depending on setting, rather than a clear refutation of min-p's utility in specific regimes. The abstract should be tempered to "fails to robustly support" or "undermines the claim of consistent superiority."

### Introduction & Motivation
- **Problem Motivation & Gap:** The motivation is strong and highly relevant to ICLR's current focus on reproducibility, statistical rigor, and methodological transparency. The gap is clearly identified: prominent sampling methods are often endorsed based on pooled statistics, insufficient hyperparameter control, and opaque human/LLM evaluation practices.
- **Contributions Stated:** The contributions are listed as a novel hyperparameter tuning control methodology, statistical testing best practices, and transparency recommendations. The claim of a "novel methodology for fairly comparing methods" (Intro, Paragraph 3) is slightly overstated. The "Best-of-N" sweep approach (Sec 3.1) is conceptually adapted from reinforcement learning/alignment literature (e.g., Stiennon et al., 2020; Hughes et al., 2024). Its application to LLM decoding hyperparameters is valuable and underexplored, but should be framed as an *adaptation* or *systematic application* rather than a wholly novel invention.
- **Over-claiming/Under-selling:** The introduction appropriately sets high stakes. It does not under-sell the computational effort (~6000 A100-hours), which demonstrates seriousness. The only minor risk is framing the critique as primarily about min-p rather than the broader evaluation practices, but the transition to general lessons in the final paragraph mitigates this.

### Method / Approach
- **Clarity & Reproducibility:** The re-analysis methodology is highly transparent. Data sources (original GitHub, Form responses), statistical tests (one-sided paired t-tests, Bonferroni, IUT), and NLP benchmark configurations (9 models, 2 stages, 4 samplers, 31 temperatures, 3 seeds) are explicitly detailed. The handling of the prompt template bug (Section 3.1, Appendix C) further strengthens reproducibility.
- **Assumptions & Justifications:** The focus on the "high" diversity setting is justified by author correspondence and min-p's stated value proposition. However, the assumption that equalizing the *volume* of hyperparameter search (Best-of-N across N=1 to 100) perfectly simulates fair comparison warrants deeper discussion. Practitioners rarely use brute-force sweeps; they rely on inductive biases or coarse-to-fine tuning. A random/grid sweep treats the hyperparameter space as uniformly uncertain, which may not reflect realistic research budgets. The method controls for *brute-force cherry-picking*, but not necessarily for *informed* tuning advantages. This assumption should be explicitly acknowledged.
- **Logical Gaps:** Section 4.1 correctly identifies the non-transitivity of LLM-as-a-judge evaluations (Xu et al., 2025) as a fatal flaw for indirect comparison designs (comparing all methods to a fixed baseline). The logic is sound, but the paper relies on "ongoing work to publish" (Sec 4.2) to support the exact win-rate calculations and confidence intervals. For a submission, the derivation of those CIs should be briefly outlined or referenced to an accessible technical appendix to avoid opacity.
- **Edge Cases/Failure Modes:** The paper acknowledges the shift in temperature ordering before/after truncation in the second human study (Sec 2.4), which is crucial. A missing discussion is how extreme low temperatures (e.g., greedy/T=0.1) interact with min-p vs. top-p; if the sweep is dominated by low-T regimes where all samplers converge, the "no consistent advantage" conclusion is expected. Figure 9 (Appendix D) addresses this partially, but the main text should explicitly state whether performance parity is driven by convergence at optimal temperatures.

### Experiments & Results
- **Tests of Claims:** The experiments directly target the original paper's four claims. The statistical re-evaluation of human scores (Table 1) correctly applies multiple comparison corrections and IUT. The NLP sweep (Figs 4, 5) directly tests robustness to search budget. The LLM-as-a-Judge analysis (Fig 6) highlights reporting inconsistencies.
- **Baselines & Fairness:** Baselines (basic, top-p, top-k) are standard and appropriate. The comparison is fairer than the original due to the controlled HP volume.
- **Missing Ablations:** 
  - *Computational Efficiency:* A key practical consideration for sampling methods is compute efficiency (latency, token throughput). The paper focuses purely on output quality/diversity and exact match accuracy. If min-p offers computational speedups or simpler hyperparameter tuning curves in practice, that trade-off is missing. Acknowledging this scope limitation is necessary.
  - *Sweep Granularity:* The sweep uses 6 discrete HP values per sampler. While reasonable, it doesn't test whether continuous optimization (e.g., TPE, CMA-ES) would reveal different optima or sharper loss landscapes for min-p vs. top-p.
- **Statistical Significance/Error Bars:** Table 1 correctly reports p-values and applies Bonferroni correction. Figures 4-5 show averaged sweep curves, which appropriately convey trends. However, confidence intervals or variance bands on the "Best-of-N" curves are missing; without them, the heavy overlap between lines (especially in Fig 5, where difference hovers near 0) is visually apparent but not statistically quantified. Reporting bootstrap CIs around the difference curves would materially strengthen the conclusion.
- **Data/Metrics Appropriateness:** GSM8K Exact Match is a strong, objective metric for reasoning. Human evaluation metrics (Quality/Diversity on a 1-10 scale) are standard but inherently noisy. The paper's re-analysis handles this appropriately via non-parametric/robust testing and explicit CI visualization (Fig 1).

### Writing & Clarity
- **Confusing Sections:** Section 4.1's critique of the "indirect methodology" is conceptually important but could be sharper. Explicitly stating why comparing Method A vs. Baseline and Method B vs. Baseline does not imply a transitive comparison between A and B (due to LLM judge inconsistency and baseline anchoring bias) would improve readability. Additionally, Figure 6's left panel caption is heavily garbled by the parser, but the text clarifies the 2x/10x tuning ratio claim. The authors should ensure this figure is unambiguous in the final version.
- **Figures & Tables:** Table 1 is exceptionally clear and directly answers whether "consistent outperformance" holds. Figure 2 (qualitative preferences) is intuitive, though the manual annotation process would benefit from a brief mention of inter-annotator agreement or a predefined coding rubric to assure objectivity. Figures 4 and 5 effectively communicate the core argument about hyperparameter volume.
- **Overall Clarity:** The writing is direct, academic, and avoids hyperbolic language in the main body. Claims are well-anchored to specific figures and data sources.

### Limitations & Broader Impact
- **Acknowledged Limitations:** Section 6 appropriately notes that conclusions are bound to the provided evidence and runs, and that future evidence could shift conclusions. This is standard and responsible.
- **Missed Limitations:** 
  - *Task Specificity:* Sampling performance is notoriously task-dependent (e.g., creative writing vs. math reasoning vs. code). The paper sweeps GSM8K and human creative evaluation, but does not claim broader coverage. It should explicitly state that the "no consistent advantage" conclusion is bounded to these domains and that min-p might still offer regime-specific utility not captured here.
  - *Reviewer/Incentive Structure:* The paper correctly identifies that inflated adoption claims influenced reviewers (Sec 5). A broader impact discussion could address how conference review norms incentivize broad adoption claims and how a "refutation track" (as cited in Schaeffer et al., 2025b) could systematically address this.
- **Societal/Failure Modes:** The paper promotes scientific rigor, which has a positive downstream impact on research integrity and reduces wasted compute on marginally effective methods. No significant negative societal impacts are evident; the tone is constructive and evidence-focused.

### Overall Assessment
This submission provides a thorough, well-executed, and highly relevant critique that aligns well with ICLR’s growing emphasis on methodological rigor and reproducibility. The re-analysis of human evaluations is statistically sound, the NLP benchmark sweeps required substantial compute and are transparently documented, and the identification of reporting inconsistencies in LLM-as-a-Judge evaluations highlights genuine flaws in current evaluation pipelines. The primary concerns are minor but important for calibration: the "blueprint" framing should be adjusted to reflect that the paper proposes a valuable checklist and methodological adaptation (controlled HP sweeps) rather than a novel theoretical framework; statistical uncertainty (confidence intervals) should be added to the Best-of-N difference plots to fully quantify the overlap observed; and the assumption that equal search volume perfectly simulates practitioner behavior should be explicitly acknowledged. Despite these points, the contribution stands strongly. It successfully demonstrates that without rigorous statistical correction, controlled hyperparameter budgets, and transparent data reporting, claims of "consistent superiority" in LLM sampling cannot be reliably supported. Acceptance at ICLR is warranted, as the paper will serve as a concrete, actionable reference for reviewers and researchers aiming to improve empirical standards in generative modeling.

# Neutral Reviewer
## Balanced Review

### Summary
This paper presents a comprehensive, multi-faceted re-evaluation of a high-visibility ICLR 2025 oral publication on min-p sampling, demonstrating that the original claims of performance superiority are unsupported due to omitted evaluation data, inappropriate statistical pooling, unequal hyperparameter tuning, and selective reporting. By conducting a large-scale computational sweep (~6000 A100-hours) and re-analyzing human evaluation data with proper statistical corrections, the authors extract a transparent, actionable blueprint for improving methodological rigor in empirical machine learning research. The work directly addresses critical reproducibility and fairness issues in sampler and LLM evaluation.

### Strengths
1. **Exceptional Transparency & Reproducibility**: The authors provide direct links to raw CSV data, annotation sheets, code repositories, and exact experimental configurations (Sec. 2.1-2.4, Sec. 3.1). The explicit reporting of compute cost, seeds, model variants, and prompt formatting sets a high standard for reproducible auditing in ML.
2. **Methodological Rigor in Statistical Testing**: The paper correctly identifies and remedies common pitfalls in human evaluation analysis, including inappropriate pooling across conditions and failure to correct for multiple comparisons. The application of Bonferroni corrections and Intersection-Union Tests (Sec. 2.2, Table 1) provides a mathematically sound refutation of the original "consistent outperformance" claims.
3. **Innovative Control for Hyperparameter Tuning Bias**: The introduction of the "Best-of-N" subsampling analysis (Sec. 3.1, Figs. 4-5) is a novel and highly practical contribution. It directly quantifies how apparent performance advantages frequently dissolve when the volume of hyperparameter search space is equalized across methods, addressing a widespread but rarely measured source of baseline unfairness.
4. **Actionable, Evidence-Based Guidelines**: The extracted "blueprint" (Sec. 6) is not purely theoretical; it is grounded in forensic analysis of real data. Recommendations on controlling tuning volume, applying correct statistical corrections, demanding full data transparency, and avoiding indirect LLM-as-a-judge designs are directly applicable to ICLR's submission standards.

### Weaknesses
1. **Restricted Benchmark Scope for the Primary Experimental Extension**: As explicitly stated in Sec. 3.1 ("Due to our compute budget, we only evaluated GSM8K CoT"), the extensive "Best-of-N" hyperparameter sweep is limited to a single math-reasoning benchmark. The absence of results on generative, multimodal, or coding tasks weakens the generalizability of the finding that samplers perform equally when tuning is equalized.
2. **Incomplete Re-analysis of LLM-as-a-Judge Evaluations**: While Sections 4.1-4.3 accurately diagnose methodological flaws (indirect comparisons, under-specification, non-transitivity risks, and selective score reporting), the critique remains largely observational. The authors note that a full re-run is "ongoing work to publish," leaving this section experimentally unvalidated compared to the human and GSM8K sections.
3. **Practicality of the Proposed Blueprint**: The "Best-of-N" framework and exhaustive sweeps require substantial compute resources that many researchers and practitioners lack. The paper does not discuss heuristic alternatives, early-stopping criteria, or statistical power analysis that would allow smaller labs to implement fair hyperparameter controls without a 6000 A100-hour budget.

### Novelty & Significance
**Novelty**: High. The "Best-of-N" methodology for controlling hyperparameter search volume is a fresh contribution to empirical evaluation design. Applying a multi-pronged forensic audit (human, automated, judge, and meta-claims) to a specific, high-profile publication provides a new template for rigorous post-publication verification.
**Clarity**: Excellent. The argument is structured logically around the four original evidentiary pillars, with clear figures, well-explained statistical procedures, and direct hyperlinks to source data. The narrative maintains scientific objectivity while delivering sharp critiques.
**Reproducibility**: Outstanding. Every claim is tied to publicly accessible data or explicitly described code. The detailed sweep parameters, random seeds, and formatting corrections enable exact replication. This work strongly aligns with ICLR's reproducibility standards and could serve as a benchmark for future audit papers.
**Significance**: Very High. The paper tackles systemic issues in LLM evaluation (hyperparameter cherry-picking, statistical misuse, opaque judge setups, and unretracted claims influencing reviewers). The lessons are directly relevant to authors, reviewers, and area chairs, offering concrete mechanisms to prevent similar methodological oversights from reaching top-tier publication venues.

### Suggestions for Improvement
1. **Broaden the Benchmark Sweep**: Include at least one non-math benchmark (e.g., a creative generation, instruction-following, or coding dataset) even on a reduced set of models or temperatures. This would empirically demonstrate that the hyperparameter volume control findings generalize beyond reasoning tasks.
2. **Provide Compute-Aware Implementation Guidelines**: Add a subsection to the blueprint outlining practical alternatives to exhaustive Best-of-N sweeps, such as Bayesian optimization with early stopping, statistical power calculators for determining minimum necessary hyperparameter candidates, or standardized reporting templates for tuning volume.
3. **Formalize the LLM-as-a-Judge Re-evaluation**: Either complete the direct pairwise comparisons with proper confidence intervals or provide a clear, step-by-step methodological template showing how future researchers should structure judge evaluations to avoid indirect comparison and non-transitivity confounds.
4. **Refine the Blueprint's Scope**: Explicitly separate empirical methodology fixes (statistical testing, hyperparameter control, prompt formatting) from meta-scientific practices (GitHub metric validation, transparency requirements). This will help reviewers differentiate between experimental flaws and broader publishing culture issues, making each recommendation more actionable.

# Spark Finder Review
## How to Improve This Paper

### Missing Experiments (top 3-5 only)
1. **Evaluate on Generative/Creative Benchmarks:** The hyperparameter sweeps exclusively use GSM8K CoT, a deterministic math task, while min-p's original claim targets open-ended creative text; adding generation benchmarks like AlpacaEval, MT-Bench, or open-ended story prompts is necessary to prove min-p fails in its intended domain.
2. **Conduct an Independent, Powered Human Evaluation:** Relying solely on re-running statistical tests on the original flawed dataset cannot resolve fundamental design issues like poor prompt selection and attention-check dropouts; a fresh, independently recruited human study with rigorous experimental controls is required to validly claim human preference parity.
3. **Re-Run LLM-as-a-Judge with Direct Comparisons & Bootstrapping:** The manuscript identifies missing uncertainty and indirect baselines but does not empirically re-evaluate the claims; running direct pairwise judge comparisons against min-p with bootstrapped confidence intervals is required to prove the reported win-rate differences are statistically indistinguishable from chance.
4. **Benchmark Under Equal Compute/Tuning Budgets:** Equating "hyperparameter volume" with fair comparison ignores that grid search efficiency varies drastically across methods; an experiment comparing samplers under fixed computational budgets (wall-clock time or GPU hours) would better reflect practical scientific standards and ICLR reproducibility expectations.

### Deeper Analysis Needed (top 3-5 only)
1. **Apply Mixed-Effects Modeling to Human Scores:** Using simple one-sided t-tests on human evaluation data ignores the nested, repeated-measures structure (multiple conditions per participant), which inflates Type II error; linear mixed-effects models with random participant intercepts are the ICLR standard for this data and are necessary to support statistical invalidation.
2. **Quantify Effect Sizes and Statistical Power:** Claiming samplers are "indistinguishable" solely on non-significant p-values commits an absence-of-evidence fallacy; reporting effect sizes (e.g., Cohen’s d) and conducting explicit power analyses is required to prove the evaluations were sensitive enough to detect the originally claimed advantages.
3. **Analyze Hyperparameter Robustness and Variance:** The "Best-of-N" methodology only compares peak performance, completely ignoring how sensitive each sampler is to tuning; analyzing expected value across the hyperparameter space, variance, or tuning efficiency would clarify whether min-p is truly equivalent or merely harder to optimize reliably.

### Visualizations & Case Studies
1. **Side-by-Side Generative Output Comparison:** Provide concrete, multi-prompt output examples from each sampler at optimized hyperparameters, explicitly annotated for coherence, novelty, and repetition, to empirically validate the claim that min-p provides no qualitative edge over baselines.
2. **Aggregated Cross-Model Performance Matrix:** The GSM8K results are fragmented across dozens of tiny subplots that obscure global trends; a unified win-rate heatmap or rank-distribution plot across all models and temperatures would clearly expose whether min-p systematically ties or loses.
3. **Structured Qualitative Feedback Analysis:** Summarizing open-ended human preferences via simple bar counts fails to capture *why* participants chose specific methods; running basic thematic coding or topic modeling on the raw qualitative responses would objectively verify or refute the claim that the original authors mischaracterized user feedback.

### Obvious Next Steps
1. **Release an Evaluation Toolkit Enforcing the Blueprint:** The current "blueprint" remains a retrospective checklist; packaging the hyperparameter budget control, automatic multiple-comparison correction, and standardized reporting into an open-source evaluation wrapper would elevate the work from critique to actionable field contribution.
2. **Generalize the Protocol to a Second High-Profile Method:** Demonstrating the rigor pipeline on only one paper risks perceptions of a targeted post-mortem; applying the exact same standardized protocol to a different, highly-cited empirical ML method would prove the blueprint's general utility and scientific maturity.
3. **Provide Theoretical or Mechanistic Grounding for the Findings:** The paper establishes empirical parity but lacks any explanation for why min-p, top-p, and basic sampling converge under fair tuning; adding a brief analysis of decision-boundary alignment or distributional overlap would transform statistical observations into the foundational insights expected at ICLR.

# Final Consolidated Review
## Summary
This paper presents a comprehensive forensic re-evaluation of a high-visibility ICLR 2025 publication on min-p sampling. By systematically re-analyzing the original study's human evaluations, NLP benchmarks, LLM-as-a-Judge results, and community adoption claims, the authors demonstrate that the original assertions of consistent superiority are unsupported due to omitted data, statistical missteps, unequal hyperparameter tuning, and selective reporting. The work distills these findings into a practical methodological blueprint for empirical ML, emphasizing the necessity of controlling for hyperparameter search volume, applying rigorous statistical corrections, and ensuring full data transparency.

## Strengths
- **Exceptional transparency and reproducibility**: Every major claim is anchored to publicly accessible raw data, annotation sheets, and explicit experimental configurations. The documentation of computational resources (~6000 A100-hours), model variants, random seeds, and prompt formatting corrections sets a high standard for reproducible auditing.
- **Effective control for hyperparameter tuning bias**: The introduction of the "Best-of-N" subsampling framework (Sec. 3.1) is a highly practical methodological contribution. It directly quantifies how apparent performance advantages frequently dissolve when the volume of hyperparameter search space is equalized across methods, addressing a common but rarely measured source of baseline unfairness.
- **Mathematically sound correction of statistical pitfalls**: The paper correctly identifies and remedies widespread errors in human evaluation analysis, including inappropriate pooling across conditions, failure to correct for multiple comparisons, and mischaracterization of qualitative feedback. The application of Bonferroni corrections and Intersection-Union Tests provides clear, statistically grounded refutations of the original "consistent outperformance" claims.

## Weaknesses
- **Benchmark scope mismatch for the primary experimental extension**: The extensive hyperparameter sweep is strictly limited to GSM8K Chain-of-Thought, a deterministic reasoning task. Since min-p's core claim centers on improving creative and open-ended generation, restricting the sweep to math reasoning leaves the method's intended operational domain empirically unexamined, limiting the generalizability of the parity conclusion.
- **Diagnostic rather than experimental validation for LLM-as-a-Judge**: While Sections 4.1-4.3 accurately diagnose critical flaws in the judge evaluation design (indirect comparisons, non-transitivity risks, and selective score reporting), the analysis remains observational. Without independent pairwise re-evaluation and bootstrapped uncertainty estimates, the section cannot definitively conclude statistical indistinguishability, relying instead on methodological critique.
- **High computational barrier for the proposed blueprint**: The "Best-of-N" control protocol requires exhaustive grid sweeps that are computationally prohibitive for most researchers and smaller labs. The paper does not offer heuristic approximations, power analysis guidelines, or compute-aware tuning alternatives to make the framework practically scalable beyond well-resourced groups.

## Nice-to-Haves
- Add bootstrap confidence intervals or variance bands to the Best-of-N difference curves (Figs. 4-5) to formally quantify the visual overlap between samplers and strengthen the parity claim statistically.
- Report basic effect sizes (e.g., Cohen’s d) alongside corrected p-values in the human evaluation re-analysis to clearly distinguish statistical non-significance from practical equivalence.
- Apply structured thematic coding or inter-annotator agreement metrics to the qualitative feedback analysis (Sec. 2.3) to move beyond manual frequency counts and more objectively verify claims about evaluator preferences.

## Novel Insights
The paper successfully shifts the empirical burden of proof in LLM research from demonstrating isolated peak performance to proving robustness under controlled search budgets. By coupling statistical forensics with a tuning-volume equalization metric and auditing meta-claims (e.g., GitHub adoption), it exposes how methodological opacity, indirect baselines, and selective reporting can artificially manufacture the appearance of progress. This creates a reproducible, multi-pillar template for post-publication verification that forces the field to separate genuine algorithmic gains from artifacts of unequal evaluation conditions.

## Suggestions
- Expand the hyperparameter sweep to include at least one open-ended or creative generation benchmark (e.g., AlpacaEval or MT-Bench) to verify whether the observed sampler parity holds in min-p's originally claimed domain.
- Supplement the blueprint with a "compute-aware" implementation guide that provides practical alternatives to exhaustive sweeps, such as sequential model-based optimization with early stopping, statistical power calculators for minimum candidate thresholds, and standardized reporting templates for tuning budgets.
- Formalize the LLM-as-a-Judge critique by providing a step-by-step, reusable protocol for designing direct pairwise judge evaluations that explicitly mitigate non-transitivity confounds and mandate reporting of bootstrapped win-rate confidence intervals.
