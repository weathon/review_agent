# Harsh Critic Review
## Section-by-Section Critical Review

### Title & Abstract
- **Title accuracy:** The title accurately reflects the paper's dual nature: a rigorous re-analysis of a specific high-profile method and a methodological blueprint for empirical ML.
- **Abstract clarity:** The abstract clearly states the problem (rigor crisis in empirical ML), the method (comprehensive re-examination of four lines of evidence from Nguyen et al., 2024), and the key results (omitted baseline data, vanishing superiority under controlled hyperparameter sweeps, inconsistent LLM-as-a-Judge reporting, retracted community adoption claims). The claims are well-supported by the subsequent sections.
- **Unsupported claims:** The phrase *"conclusions are invalidated by its own data"* is strong. While the human evaluation and LLM-as-a-Judge sections convincingly show methodological flaws and lack of statistical support, the NLP benchmark analysis demonstrates that min-p's advantage vanishes when hyperparameter tuning volume is equalized, rather than being strictly "invalidated" in a deterministic sense. This is a nuance the authors could soften, but it does not materially detract from the core argument.

### Introduction & Motivation
- **Problem motivation & gap:** The motivation addresses a widely recognized issue in ML: inflated claims due to inadequate baselines, selective reporting, and poor statistical practice. The citation dump at the start of Section 1 establishes context but leans heavily on blog posts, comment threads, and position papers. While relevant, the introduction would benefit from more focused discussion of *systematic* gaps in evaluation methodology rather than cataloging individual scandals.
- **Contributions:** Clearly stated: (1) fair comparison via hyperparameter volume control, (2) rigorous statistical practices, (3) data transparency demonstration, and (4) scrutiny of qualitative/unrealistic claims.
- **Over/under-claiming:** The introduction labels the Best-of-N hyperparameter subsampling analysis as "novel methodology." In reality, hyperparameter fairness analysis and "any-metric" or Best-of-N evaluations are established practices (cited as Nakano et al., 2021; Stiennon et al., 2020; Hughes et al., 2024). Framing this as novel is an overstatement. The true contribution lies in the *systematic, cross-method application* of these controls to dismantle a specific claim, not the invention of the technique itself. This should be calibrated.

### Method / Approach
- **Clarity & reproducibility:** The methodological approaches are generally clear and well-motivated. The statistical re-analysis (Table 1, IUT, Bonferroni correction) is transparent and reproducible. The NLP benchmark sweep (Sec 3.1) is thoroughly documented (9 models, 4 samplers, 31 temperatures, 6 HP values, 3 seeds, ~6000 A100-hours). Reproducibility is supported by linked GitHub repositories and appendices.
- **Assumptions & justification:** The core assumption is that equalizing the *number* of hyperparameter configurations tested equates to a *fair* comparison. While a practical proxy, this assumes uniform search budgets and ignores the computational cost or efficiency of finding good hyperparameters. The "Best-of-N" approach measures peak potential performance, not typical/expected performance under realistic tuning constraints. This distinction is not discussed.
- **Logical gaps:** The human evaluation critique correctly identifies improper pooling and lack of multiple comparison correction. However, the critique of the original paper's qualitative summary (Sec 2.3) relies on the authors' manual annotation scheme without inter-annotator agreement metrics or a pre-registered coding framework. While illustrative, qualitative coding without reliability scores leaves room for subjective interpretation bias.
- **Edge cases/failure modes:** The method does not address whether min-p might offer latent benefits in specific regimes not captured by discrete HP sweeps (e.g., very low-temperature coherent generation, or long-context stability). If the goal is to show min-p offers no *general* advantage, the scope of swept configurations should be justified as covering practically relevant operating regimes.

### Experiments & Results
- **Testing claims:** The experiments directly target the original paper's four pillars of evidence. The human evaluation re-analysis definitively shows indistinguishable performance after statistical correction. The NLP sweep shows convergence when HP volume is controlled. The LLM-as-a-Judge critique exposes reporting inconsistencies. The community adoption section validates retraction.
- **Baselines & fairness:** Baselines (basic, top-p, top-k) match the original paper. Controlling HP sweep volume is a significant strength and directly addresses a common reviewer blind spot.
- **Missing ablations & metric alignment:** This is the most substantive experimental concern. The original paper heavily emphasizes *creativity, diversity, and quality* in open-ended generation. Yet, the massive NLP benchmark sweep is restricted **solely to GSM8K Chain-of-Thought Exact Match**. GSM8K measures strict mathematical reasoning accuracy, which is famously insensitive to sampling diversity or creative coherence. While the authors note compute constraints (~6000 GPU hours), omitting generation-focused benchmarks (e.g., open-ended QA, summarization, or creative prompts measured via LLM-as-a-judge with proper controls) weakens the claim that min-p offers no quality/diversity advantage. The human evals cover this, but the NLP section does not test what min-p was claimed to optimize.
- **Error bars & significance:** Human evaluation p-values and CIs are properly reported. NLP sweep results (Figs 4, 5, App C/D) show smoothed curves but lack error bands across the 150 subsampling iterations or the 3 random seeds. Given high variance in LLM outputs, reporting variance around the Best-of-N curves is necessary to confirm the apparent convergence isn't within noise margins.
- **Cherry-picking/Results support claims:** The results strongly support the claim that the *reported* evidence does not justify the original conclusions. The authors effectively demonstrate that original results likely stem from uneven baseline tuning and reporting bias.

### Writing & Clarity
- **Clarity of explanation:** The manuscript is logically structured, mirroring the original paper's claims. The statistical arguments are accessible. However, **Section 4.1** references "ongoing work to publish" regarding the detailed LLM-as-a-Judge scrutiny. For a paper under peer review, relying on unpublished analysis weakens the evidentiary chain. All data and analytical steps cited must be fully contained in the submission or supplementary materials.
- **Figures and tables:** The figures effectively communicate the narrative (overlapping CIs, Best-of-N convergence, qualitative distribution). Table 1 is clear. The claim in Section 4.3 about selective reporting in Table 3(b) relies on verifying a Telegram link screenshot. While the authors document it, this should be formally archived in an appendix or public repository with timestamps to ensure long-term verifiability and avoid claims of relying on informal social media.

### Limitations & Broader Impact
- **Acknowledged limitations:** Section 6 appropriately notes that conclusions are bound by the available data and code, and that new evidence could theoretically change outcomes. Compute constraints limiting the NLP sweep to GSM8K CoT are mentioned.
- **Missed limitations:** 
  1. **Hyperparameter efficiency vs. volume:** The analysis controls for the *number* of HP configurations tried, but not the *computational budget* or *optimization efficiency*. A method that achieves comparable peaks with fewer evaluations is practically superior, even if Best-of-N curves converge. The paper does not discuss tuning efficiency.
  2. **Benchmark diversity mismatch:** As noted in Experiments, the restriction to a strict-reasoning benchmark (GSM8K) limits the ability to refute claims specifically about open-ended generation quality/diversity. This is a fundamental limitation that should be explicitly discussed in Section 6.
- **Societal impact:** The broader impact is well-handled. The paper advocates for transparency, correct statistics, and scrutiny of adoption metrics, which directly benefits the ML community's health. No negative impacts are apparent.

### Overall Assessment
This paper makes a strong, empirically grounded contribution to empirical rigor in ML research. It successfully demonstrates how omitted baselines, improper statistical pooling, uneven hyperparameter tuning, and selective reporting can collectively produce unsupported claims in high-visibility work. The statistical re-analysis of the human evaluations is robust, and the ~6000 GPU-hour NLP sweep provides compelling evidence against the necessity of min-p when baselines are fairly tuned. However, the paper has notable limitations: the reliance on GSM8K CoT (a strict accuracy metric) to critique claims about creative/diverse generation is misaligned; the "Best-of-N" approach measures peak potential rather than practical tuning efficiency or expected performance; and parts of the LLM-as-a-Judge critique currently reference "ongoing work" and informal sources (Telegram), which must be formalized for peer review. Additionally, the claim of a "novel methodology" overstates the novelty of Best-of-N hypervolume analysis. I recommend that the authors: (1) explicitly acknowledge the mismatch between GSM8K and open-ended generation claims, (2) add variance/error bands to the Best-of-N sweep figures, (3) formalize all LLM-as-a-Judge data and claims within the submission, and (4) tone down the novelty framing. Despite these points, the manuscript stands as a valuable, rigorous case study that aligns with ICLR's emphasis on methodological transparency and reproducible science.

# Neutral Reviewer
## Balanced Review

### Summary
This paper presents a systematic, multi-faceted empirical audit of a high-visibility ICLR 2025 Oral publication on min-p sampling, re-evaluating its claims across human studies, NLP benchmarks, LLM-as-a-judge evaluations, and community adoption metrics. Through extensive new hyperparameter sweeps (~6000 A100-hours), rigorous statistical re-analysis, and direct engagement with the original authors' data, the authors demonstrate that min-p's claimed superiority largely disappears when controlling for hyperparameter search volume and applying proper multiple comparison corrections. The work distills these findings into a practical blueprint outlining six core principles for more rigorous empirical evaluation in machine learning.

### Strengths
1. **Comprehensive and Methodologically Rigorous Re-Analysis:** The paper systematically audits four distinct lines of evidence rather than focusing on a single metric. It correctly identifies and corrects statistical errors in the original human evaluation, applying Bonferroni corrections and an Intersection-Union Test to appropriately test the claim of "consistent" superiority (Sec. 2.2, Table 1).
2. **Valuable Evaluation Framework for Hyperparameter Fairness:** The introduction of the "Best-of-N" subsampling methodology to control for unequal hyperparameter search volume directly addresses a pervasive and often overlooked issue in empirical ML. The results across 9 models (Sec. 3.1, Figs. 4-5) provide concrete, compute-intensive evidence that perceived gains in sampling methods frequently stem from asymmetric tuning rather than intrinsic superiority.
3. **Strong Commitment to Transparency and Constructive Dialogue:** The authors publicly release raw annotations, code references, and maintain an open feedback loop with the original authors, which led to factual updates in the camera-ready version (Sec. 2.1, 2.4). This level of openness and reproducibility aligns well with top-tier conference expectations and models healthy scientific discourse.
4. **Clear, Actionable Takeaways for the Community:** The distilled six-lesson blueprint (Sec. 6) translates specific audit findings into generalizable guidelines, offering reviewers and researchers concrete checks for statistical rigor, data completeness, and methodological clarity that are immediately applicable to future ICLR submissions.

### Weaknesses
1. **Limited Methodological Generalization Beyond the Case Study:** While the audit is thorough, the "blueprint" largely reiterates established best practices (e.g., correct stats, release data, control for tuning) rather than introducing novel evaluation theory or formalized testing protocols. The contribution leans heavily on identifying flaws in one paper rather than proposing a new, broadly applicable evaluation standard.
2. **Under-Detailed Experimental Reporting for Reproducibility:** Despite the large compute footprint, several experimental details necessary for full replication are underspecified. The LLM-as-a-judge analysis relies on "ongoing work" and lacks methodological details on how confidence intervals were computed (e.g., bootstrap vs. parametric, positional bias mitigation). Similarly, the GSM8K prompt formatting correction (Appendix C) is mentioned without providing the exact standardized templates used in the main text.
3. **Disproportionate Focus on Meta-Claims and Adversarial Framing:** Section 5 dedicates significant space to debunking retracted GitHub star/repo claims. While factually accurate, this focuses on peripheral meta-metrics rather than core scientific methodology, and may be perceived as overly adversarial by ICLR reviewers who typically prioritize constructive, forward-looking empirical contributions over reputational critiques.
4. **Narrow Baseline Selection:** The benchmark sweeps only compare min-p against elementary samplers (basic, top-p, top-k). The paper does not evaluate or contextualize findings against more recent, advanced decoding strategies (e.g., contrastive decoding, entropy-based sampling, or speculative variants), limiting the broader relevance of the conclusions to the modern sampling landscape.

### Novelty & Significance
**Novelty:** Moderate. The paper does not propose a new algorithm but contributes a rigorous, audit-driven methodology. The primary novelty lies in the systematic demonstration of how unequal hyperparameter search volume inflates perceived performance, operationalized through a practical "Best-of-N" subsampling protocol. **Clarity:** High. The logical flow is clear, statistical re-analyses are well-motivated, and figures effectively communicate the overlap between sampling methods. **Reproducibility:** High, but with caveats. The authors link to datasets and code, and the statistical re-evaluations are fully replicable. However, missing details on LLM-judge CI computation and exact prompt templates slightly hinder end-to-end replication of the benchmark sweeps. **Significance:** High for ICLR's evaluation and reproducibility standards. As empirical ML faces growing scrutiny over statistical rigor and selective reporting, this paper provides timely, hard evidence of systemic evaluation flaws and offers a structured pathway for improvement. It strongly aligns with ICLR's increasing emphasis on robust benchmarking and meta-scientific contributions.

### Suggestions for Improvement
1. **Formalize the Hyperparameter Control Protocol:** Elevate the "Best-of-N" analysis from a case-study tool to a generalizable framework. Provide explicit algorithmic or mathematical guidelines for how researchers and reviewers should apply it across domains (e.g., RL, vision), and include a recommended standard for reporting "search volume" in future empirical papers.
2. **Strengthen Statistical Transparency in LLM-as-a-Judge Analysis:** Detail the exact methodology used to compute the 95% confidence intervals (e.g., non-parametric bootstrap, number of resamples, handling of judge position bias). If this relies on external/ongoing work, summarize the full pipeline in an appendix to ensure self-containment and reproducibility.
3. **Reframe Narrative and De-emphasize Peripheral Claims:** Restructure the paper to position the case study primarily as an *illustrative motivator* for the evaluation blueprint. Condense or move Section 5 to an appendix to maintain a constructive, methodology-first tone that aligns better with ICLR's preference for forward-looking scientific contributions.
4. **Expand Baseline Discussion and Practical Reviewer Guidelines:** Briefly discuss how the audit's findings apply to more advanced sampling baselines, or explicitly acknowledge this limitation. Additionally, transform the six lessons in Section 6 into a concrete checklist or decision tree for reviewers, making the blueprint directly actionable during the peer-review process.

# Spark Finder Review
## How to Improve This Paper

### Missing Experiments (top 3-5 only)
1. **Extend hyperparameter sweeps to open-ended creative benchmarks** (e.g., AlpacaEval Creative, StoryGen), because evaluating a method explicitly designed for creativity/coherence solely on GSM8K (mathematics) severs the link between your refutation and the original paper’s core domain claim.
2. **Run a controlled, directly-paired LLM-as-a-Judge experiment** comparing min-p against baselines, because identifying the original paper's methodological ambiguity is insufficient; you must provide positive evidence showing how much the confounder (non-transitivity/indirect comparison) actually skewed the results.
3. **Conduct a statistical power analysis for the human evaluation studies**, because demonstrating "no statistical significance" does not prove equivalence or lack of superiority if the original or new studies were fundamentally underpowered to detect practical effect sizes.

### Deeper Analysis Needed (top 3-5 only)
1. **Quantify the sample efficiency or compute cost of the hyperparameter search**, because equalizing the raw *count* of configurations ignores that some samplers may have more sensitive or rugged loss landscapes, and equalizing volume does not necessarily equalize practical tuning effort.
2. **Justify or replace parametric paired t-tests with non-parametric alternatives** (e.g., Wilcoxon signed-rank), because human evaluation scores on bounded Likert scales violate t-test normality assumptions, and invalid statistical tests directly undermine the credibility of your claim that the original conclusions are false.
3. **Analyze performance interactions with model scale and instruction-tuning stage**, because the current analysis aggregates results across base and instruct models; if min-p interacts differently with aligned models, the broad claim that it offers "no apparent advantage" becomes overgeneralized and inaccurate.

### Visualizations & Case Studies
1. **Add variance shading or confidence intervals to the "Best-of-N" hyperparameter sweep plots**, because plotting only mean maxima hides the overlap distribution, leaving readers unable to visually verify that the samplers truly perform identically rather than just having similar averages.
2. **Plot explicit Pareto frontiers for Quality vs. Diversity**, because separate metric visualizations obscure the trade-off curve, which is the precise theoretical niche min-p claims to occupy; showing that no sampler dominates the Pareto set is the only way to visually kill the trade-off claim.
3. **Include side-by-side qualitative examples of high vs. low temperature generations**, because without seeing the actual text, reviewers cannot determine if "creativity" differences exist but are poorly measured by your metrics, or if they truly do not exist at all.

### Obvious Next Steps
1. **Develop and release a standardized, automated evaluation harness that enforces equal hyperparameter search budgets**, because presenting a conceptual "blueprint" lacks the tooling required for ICLR reviewers and authors to practically implement and verify rigorous comparisons in future work.
2. **Run the controlled sweep on a dedicated creativity benchmark and publish definitive default recommendations**, because concluding that "samplers are roughly equal" leaves the practitioner community without actionable guidance on which sampler to use or how to configure it efficiently.
3. **Formally integrate your "Best-of-N" subsampling methodology into a reproducibility checklist or metric**, as this addresses the cherry-picking vulnerability systematically rather than relying on ad-hoc re-analysis for every future suspect submission.

# Final Consolidated Review
## Summary
This paper conducts a comprehensive empirical audit of a high-visibility ICLR 2025 Oral publication on min-p sampling, systematically re-evaluating the original claims across human evaluations, NLP benchmarks, LLM-as-a-judge assessments, and community adoption metrics. Through extensive compute (~6000 A100-hours) and rigorous statistical re-analysis, the authors demonstrate that min-p's reported advantages largely collapse when baseline hyperparameter tuning volumes are equalized and proper multiple-comparison corrections are applied. The case study is used to derive a six-principle blueprint aimed at improving methodological transparency, statistical rigor, and reporting standards in empirical machine learning research.

## Strengths
- **Rigorous, transparent audit framework with high compute investment:** The paper systematically targets all four evidence pillars of the target study, publicly releasing raw annotations, code, and data. The ~6000 A100-hour hyperparameter sweep across 9 models and 4 samplers provides a substantial, reproducible empirical foundation rarely seen in critique literature.
- **Effective statistical correction and hyperparameter fairness protocol:** Correctly identifies and rectifies statistical flaws in the original human evaluation (improper pooling, lack of multiple comparison correction), applying Bonferroni adjustment and an Intersection-Union Test. The "Best-of-N" subsampling methodology concretely addresses a pervasive but frequently overlooked source of bias in ML evaluations: asymmetric tuning budgets masquerading as algorithmic superiority.
- **Actionable, community-facing methodological blueprint:** Successfully translates case-specific failures into six generalizable, immediately applicable principles for researchers and reviewers, promoting full data transparency, scrutiny of adoption metrics, and consistent reporting practices that directly address documented reproducibility gaps in the field.

## Weaknesses
- **Critical benchmark-claim mismatch undermines the central refutation:** The paper dedicates the vast majority of its compute to sweeping GSM8K CoT with Strict Exact Match to refute claims about *creativity and open-ended diversity*. GSM8K measures deterministic mathematical reasoning accuracy, which is largely insensitive to sampling temperature, creative coherence, or diversity variations. Failing to evaluate min-p on generation-focused or creative benchmarks (where it was explicitly claimed to excel) leaves the primary domain claim unchallenged and severely limits the scope of the refutation.
- **Statistical methodology in human evaluation lacks rigor for equivalence claims:** The critique correctly identifies improper pooling and multiple comparison errors but relies exclusively on parametric paired t-tests for bounded Likert-scale data without verifying normality or employing non-parametric alternatives (e.g., Wilcoxon signed-rank). More critically, demonstrating non-significant p-values does not validate "indistinguishable" or "no advantage" without formal equivalence testing (TOST) or statistical power analysis to rule out Type II errors from potentially underpowered sample sizes.
- **Conflates hyperparameter search volume with practical fairness:** The Best-of-N analysis controls for the *number* of configurations swept, assuming this equates to a fair comparison. However, it ignores computational cost, search efficiency, and landscape ruggedness. A method that reaches comparable peaks with fewer evaluations or a smoother optimization trajectory holds distinct practical value. By treating raw configuration count as the sole fairness metric, the paper overlooks search efficiency as a legitimate scientific property, weakening its claim to a fully equitable comparison.

## Nice-to-Haves
- Add variance shading or confidence bands to the Best-of-N sweep figures (Figs 4 & 5) to visually confirm convergence isn't masked by high variance across the 150 subsampling iterations and 3 random seeds.
- Formalize the LLM-as-a-Judge critique within the submission by detailing CI computation methods, positional bias mitigation, and replacing the reliance on "ongoing work" and ephemeral Telegram screenshots with archived, timestamped evidence.
- Calibrate the "novel methodology" framing in the introduction; Best-of-N subsampling for tuning fairness is established. The true contribution is the systematic, cross-method diagnostic application, which should be framed accordingly.
- Include explicit Pareto frontiers plotting Quality vs. Diversity to directly visualize whether any sampler dominates the trade-off curve, rather than relying on separate metric plots.

## Novel Insights
The paper's most valuable conceptual contribution is exposing a pervasive, often invisible evaluation bias in modern ML research: perceived algorithmic superiority frequently collapses when baseline hyperparameter tuning budgets are rigorously equalized. By operationalizing fairness through the volume of sampled hyperparameter space, the authors provide a tangible diagnostic tool for reviewers to detect cherry-picking and asymmetric engineering effort. This shifts the methodological discourse from "does the proposed method work?" to "how much computational tuning was invested to make it appear superior?", offering a critical meta-scientific lens for interpreting empirical gains in a high-throughput, compute-heavy research ecosystem.

## Potentially Missed Related Work
- Literature on equivalence testing (e.g., Two One-Sided Tests, TOST) for human preference studies and Likert-scale data, which would strengthen claims of methodological indistinguishability.
- Prior work on hyperparameter optimization fairness and search budget normalization (e.g., studies on tuning bias or compute-normalized evaluations), which could formally ground the Best-of-N protocol.

## Suggestions
1. **Conduct a controlled sweep on an open-ended/creative benchmark** (e.g., AlpacaEval Creative, StoryGen, or long-form dialogue evaluation) to directly test the original paper's core claims. Without evaluating the exact domain min-p was designed for, the refutation remains incomplete.
2. **Supplement non-significance with equivalence testing or power analysis:** Replace or augment t-test p-values with formal equivalence bounds (e.g., TOST with a pre-defined practical equivalence margin) and explicitly report achieved statistical power. This transforms "we failed to reject the null" into "we can confidently assert the methods are practically indistinguishable."
3. **Explicitly separate hyperparameter volume from search efficiency in the discussion:** Acknowledge that while volume control prevents cherry-picking, it does not measure practical tuning cost. Clarify the Best-of-N approach as a *diagnostic for reporting bias* rather than a comprehensive fairness metric, and discuss how future work could integrate computational budget normalization.
