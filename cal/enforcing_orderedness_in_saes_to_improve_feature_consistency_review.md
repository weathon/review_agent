# Harsh Critic Review
## Section-by-Section Critical Review

### Title & Abstract
- **Does the title accurately reflect the contribution?** Yes. It clearly signals the core mechanism (enforcing orderedness) and the intended outcome (improved feature consistency).
- **Does the abstract clearly state the problem, method, and key results?** Mostly. It concisely identifies the reproducibility gap in SAEs, introduces OSAE as an extension of Matryoshka/nested dropout, and reports theoretical and empirical gains. However, the phrase *"deterministically using every feature dimension, avoiding the sampling-based approximations"* is potentially misleading. Equation 4 still frames the loss as an expectation $\mathbb{E}_{\ell \sim p_{ND}}[\cdot]$, which in practice is implemented via stochastic sampling or mini-batch averaging. If the method actually iterates through all prefixes deterministically, this should be explicitly stated; otherwise, the claim overstates the departure from prior sampling-based methods.
- **Are any claims in the abstract unsupported by the paper?** The theoretical claim *"resolve permutation non-identifiability in settings of sparse dictionary learning where solutions are unique"* is carefully hedged and supported by Theorem 3.1. The empirical claim is supported, but the abstract omits the noted trade-off: OSAE improves consistency primarily in high-importance (early) features, often at the expense of reconstruction quality and stability in later features. Acknowledging this trade-off upfront would better calibrate reader expectations.

### Introduction & Motivation
- **Is the problem well-motivated? Is the gap in prior work clearly identified?** Yes. The instability of SAE features across seeds/hyperparameters is a recognized bottleneck for mechanistic interpretability. The gap relative to Matryoshka SAEs is clearly identified: sampling only a few group sizes leaves exchangeability within groups intact, failing to establish a strict global ordering.
- **Are the contributions clearly stated and accurate?** Yes, three clear contributions are listed: (1) the OSAE objective, (2) theoretical recovery guarantees, (3) empirical consistency improvements. All are substantiated in the text.
- **Does the introduction over-claim or under-sell?** Slight over-claim potential in how the theory is framed. Theorem 3.1 relies on strong assumptions (strict non-negativity and ground-truth ordering by activation frequency) that are rarely met in practice. The introduction does not sufficiently caveat that the theoretical guarantees are primarily motivational/idealized, which could lead readers to expect identifiability guarantees on real LLM activations.

### Method / Approach
- **Is the method clearly described and reproducible?** The mathematical formulation (Eq. 2-6) is clean, but implementation details crucial for reproducibility are sparse. The prefix distribution $p_{ND}(\ell)$ is never specified for the LLM experiments. The warmup schedule for $k$ and the "unit sweeping" mechanism are described qualitatively but lack precise scheduling formulas (e.g., exact $T$ for freezing, burn-in epochs, learning rate adjustments post-freeze). Without these, independent reproduction is difficult.
- **Are key assumptions stated and justified?** Theoretical assumptions (spark condition, non-negative codes, frequency-based ground-truth ordering) are stated in Sec 3. However, the justification for assuming ground-truth features in neural networks naturally align with a strict frequency-based order is weak. Real representations are likely partially ordered or structured by semantic hierarchy, not just activation frequency. The method does not inject a prior; it relies on the loss implicitly discovering this order. This leap is under-justified.
- **Are there logical gaps in the derivation or reasoning?** The transition from the undercomplete nested dropout setting (Sec 2.2) to the overcomplete hard Top-$m$ setting (Sec 2.6) skips a critical detail: the Top-$m$ operator is non-differentiable and introduces combinatorial routing. The theoretical proof (App A) essentially treats the code matrix as fixed or analyzes perturbations assuming the active set doesn't change, which sidesteps the core difficulty of optimizing non-smooth sparse autoencoders. The theory guarantees global minimizer properties, but provides no insight into whether gradient-based optimization can reach them given the non-convexity of Top-$m$ SAEs.
- **Are there edge cases or failure modes not discussed?** Yes. If the activation distribution is near-uniform (no strong frequency skew), forcing a total order may arbitrarily permute semantically similar features, potentially harming interpretability. The method's behavior in this regime is not analyzed.

### Experiments & Results
- **Do the experiments actually test the paper's claims?** Yes. The toy model tests exact recovery and consistency under controlled conditions. The Gemma2-2B and Pythia-70M experiments test cross-seed and cross-dataset consistency/orderedness. The SAE stitching experiment (App D) indirectly tests feature completeness.
- **Are baselines appropriate and fairly compared?** Standard baselines (Vanilla Top-$m$, Fixed/Random MSAE) are included. However, the claim in Sec 4.1 that *"we compare checkpoints with similar loss values for a fair orderedness and stability comparison"* introduces a critical experimental ambiguity. The exact target loss, whether it was achieved via early-stopping or extended training, and the final loss values compared are not reported in figures or text. Without matching training compute or explicit loss-matching criteria, the fairness of the comparison is unclear.
- **Are there missing ablations that would materially change conclusions?** Yes. The sensitivity of results to the prefix distribution $p_{ND}(\ell)$ is never ablated. Since prefix losses inherently weight earlier indices more heavily, the choice of $p_{ND}$ directly dictates the performance trade-off between early and late features. An ablation over distribution shapes (uniform, linear decay, Zipfian, etc.) would clarify whether the observed "crossover" in stability is fundamental or a hyperparameter artifact.
- **Do error bars / statistical significance reported?** Table 1 reports mean $\pm$ std. Figure 2 reports 95% CIs. This is adequate for ICLR standards. However, $N=10$ (or 36 pairs) for SAE training runs is on the lower side for high-variance LLM interpretability tasks; acknowledging the sample size limitation would be prudent.
- **Do the results support the claims made, or are they cherry-picked?** Results broadly support the claim of improved orderedness and early-feature stability. The authors are transparent about the stability degradation in later features (Fig 2b crossover) and higher reconstruction loss, which strengthens credibility. However, the claim that OSAE "captures more of the underlying structure" based on stitching (App D) only compares to BatchTopK and Random MSAE; omitting Fixed MSAE in that specific table weakens the hierarchy argument slightly.

### Writing & Clarity
- **Are there sections that are confusing or poorly explained?** The distinction between "deterministic use" (claimed in abstract/intro) and "expected loss minimization" (Eq 4) remains confusing. Sec 4.1's checkpoint matching strategy needs a precise definition. The proof of Lemma 3.1 is clear mathematically, but the connection to practical SGD dynamics is hand-waved.
- **Are figures and tables clear and informative?** Yes. Figure 1 and Figure 2 effectively visualize the ordered recovery and prefix-wise metrics. Table 1 is well-structured. The appendix figures (activation rasters, similarity matrices) are informative, though the note about per-panel normalization making scales non-comparable in vanilla models slightly complicates direct visual assessment.

### Limitations & Broader Impact
- **Do the authors acknowledge the key limitations?** Yes. Section 5 acknowledges idealized theoretical assumptions, risk of over-regularization if ordering is misspecified, higher compute cost, and sensitivity to scheduling.
- **Are there fundamental limitations they missed?** The most critical missed limitation is the *interpretability cost* of imposed ordering. In mechanistic interpretability, the meaning of a feature should be invariant to its dictionary index. By tying index to activation frequency (or an arbitrary proxy), OSAE makes later indices semantically unstable and potentially meaningless. This directly contradicts the goal of interpretability if users expect stable semantic mappings across indices. This should be explicitly discussed alongside the broader impact.
- **Are there failure modes or negative societal impacts not discussed?** Not significantly negative beyond standard SAE concerns. The computational overhead is noted. The paper responsibly limits scope to controlled evaluations rather than claiming downstream task superiority.

### Overall Assessment
This is a solid ICLR submission that addresses a genuine pain point in SAE research: feature instability across runs. The theoretical extension of nested dropout to the hard Top-$m$ sparse setting is novel, and the empirical results convincingly demonstrate improved orderedness and early-feature consistency. However, the paper falls short in bridging the gap between its idealized theory (non-negative, frequency-ordered codes) and the messy reality of LLM latent spaces, where no natural global frequency order exists. The experimental evaluation would be significantly strengthened by clarifying the "deterministic" vs "sampled expectation" discrepancy, providing exact details on checkpoint loss-matching, and ablating the prefix distribution $p_{ND}$ to prove the observed trade-offs are fundamental rather than tunable. The acknowledged degradation of stability in later features is a real concern for interpretability that warrants deeper discussion. Despite these issues, the core contribution—a structural prior that shrinks the SAE solution space toward ordered consistency—is valuable, well-motivated, and merits publication with clarifications on reproducibility and a more critical discussion of the ordering-interpretability trade-off.

# Neutral Reviewer
## Balanced Review

### Summary
This paper introduces Ordered Sparse Autoencoders (OSAEs), an extension of Matryoshka SAEs that enforces a strict, deterministic ordering over latent dimensions using a nested dropout-style prefix loss to reduce permutation non-identifiability. The authors provide theoretical guarantees for exact feature recovery under spark and non-negativity conditions, and empirically demonstrate that OSAEs achieve higher feature stability and orderedness than vanilla and Matryoshka baselines on synthetic data and LLM activations (Gemma2-2B, Pythia-70M). The method also reduces feature incompleteness in SAE stitching experiments, though it occasionally trades off reconstruction fidelity for tail features.

### Strengths
1. **Clear Targeting of a Known Bottleneck:** The paper directly addresses permutation non-identifiability and seed instability in SAEs, a well-documented obstacle to mechanistic interpretability. By treating each atom as a distinct prefix level (Sec 2.6), OSAEs deterministically break symmetry, yielding quantitatively higher stability (`Stab`) and rank correlation (`Ord`) than grouped Matryoshka sampling (Table 1, Fig 2, Fig 3).
2. **Rigorous Synthetic Validation & Metric Innovation:** The authors ground their claims in a controlled Gaussian/Zipfian toy model where ground-truth ordering and uniqueness conditions are known. The introduction of the Frequency-Invariant Feature Reconstruction (FIFR) Error (Sec B.4) is a valuable contribution, as it correctly exposes how global MSE underweights rare features and better correlates with true dictionary stability in skewed activation regimes.
3. **Practical Interpretability Relevance via Stitching:** The SAE stitching experiment (Table 2, Sec D) provides compelling downstream evidence: OSAEs drop the novel feature percentage from 73.8% (BatchTopK) to 33.8%, suggesting the learned dictionary captures a more complete and less redundant subspace. This bridges theoretical ordering constraints with tangible gains in feature coverage.

### Weaknesses
1. **Theoretical Assumptions Diverge from Real SAE Dynamics:** Theorem 3.1 requires non-negative codes and a ground-truth ordering defined strictly by support frequency. Real LLM activations exhibit signed values, and feature importance/ordering is rarely governed purely by activation frequency (often involving semantic abstraction or circuit role). The paper does not adequately discuss how the theoretical guarantees translate when these assumptions are violated, which limits the rigor of the theoretical contribution.
2. **Limited Empirical Scale and Baseline Coverage:** Evaluations are restricted to Pythia-70M and a single layer (L12) of Gemma2-2B with a 4096-dim dictionary. ICLR expectations typically involve scaling studies or at least evaluation across multiple layers/model scales. Additionally, the paper omits comparisons to other widely used stabilization techniques such as orthogonality constraints, gated SAEs, or weight-tying/decoupling methods, making it hard to isolate whether improvements stem from ordering specifically or just increased regularization.
3. **Unresolved Early-vs-Late Feature Trade-off:** Figures 2b and 3a consistently show OSAE stability degrading sharply after a certain prefix length (~128–512 features), with lower overall stability than fixed MSAE in the tail. The authors note this but attribute it vaguely to "restricted solution space" or "limited hyperparameter sweeps" (Sec 4.1, Sec 5). Without gradient magnitude analyses or ablation on prefix sampling distributions, it remains unclear whether late-feature degradation is a fundamental consequence of prefix truncation (gradient starvation) or a tunable artifact.
4. **Reproducibility & Training Protocol Gaps:** Key training details are scattered or underspecified. The "unit sweeping" schedule ("freeze one additional unit every T epochs", Sec 3.1), the exact form of the prefix distribution `p_ND(ℓ)`, warmup schedules for `k`, and optimizer/batch-size configurations are not fully detailed. The appendix adds some context but lacks a consolidated, reproducible training recipe.

### Novelty & Significance
The contribution is a well-motivated, incremental but meaningful advance in SAE architecture design. By extending Matryoshka SAEs with a deterministic, per-dimension nested dropout objective, the paper cleanly shrinks the permutation-equivalence class and offers a principled way to impose a canonical basis order. The novelty lies in the exact theoretical linkage to nested dropout and spark-based uniqueness, coupled with the practical demonstration that prefix ordering improves early-feature consistency and reduces stitching incompleteness. Significance is moderate-to-high for the mechanistic interpretability community, as feature stability is a critical prerequisite for cross-seed analysis and mechanistic discovery. However, to meet ICLR's bar, the method would need stronger empirical scaling, broader baseline comparisons, and a more rigorous treatment of the tail-feature stability trade-off.

### Suggestions for Improvement
1. **Strengthen Theory-to-Practice Bridge:** Relax or explicitly discuss the non-negativity and frequency-ordering assumptions in Theorem 3.1. Add an ablation showing how signed activations or randomized support frequencies affect exact recovery, or reframe the theory as a worst-case/idealized baseline rather than a direct guarantee for LLM SAEs.
2. **Expand Baselines and Empirical Scope:** Compare OSAE against recent consistency-focused approaches (e.g., orthogonalization penalties, Gated SAE variants, Top-K with decaying sparsity schedules). Evaluate across multiple layers and, if feasible, a larger model (e.g., Gemma2-9B/27B or LLaMA-3-8B) to demonstrate that ordering benefits scale and aren't limited to shallow/small models.
3. **Diagnose and Mitigate Tail-Feature Degradation:** Perform gradient norm analysis across prefix indices to confirm whether gradient starvation causes late-feature instability. Explore adaptive prefix distributions (e.g., heavier tails or curriculum learning that shifts weight to higher-`ℓ` prefixes) to recover reconstruction fidelity without sacrificing early-feature orderedness.
4. **Provide a Consolidated Reproducibility Package:** Add a clear table of all hyperparameters (learning rates, batch sizes, optimizer settings, `p_ND` distribution parameters, warmup schedule, and exact unit-sweeping `T` values). Release training scripts or provide a detailed appendix section mapping exactly to your configurations to ensure independent replication, which is critical for ICLR acceptance.

# Spark Finder Review
## How to Improve This Paper

### Missing Experiments (top 3-5 only)
1. **Matched compute/FLOP training:** OSAE samples more prefixes per batch, increasing per-step compute. Train all baselines and OSAE to identical FLOP budgets and wall-clock time to prove gains aren't simply artifacts of heavier training or implicit early-feature bias.
2. **Unit sweeping ablation on LLMs:** Unit sweeping is critical for synthetic success but explicitly disabled for Gemma/Pythia. Apply the exact same sweeping schedule to the LLM runs; without it, it is unclear whether the observed stability stems from the method or from unconstrained gradient starvation.
3. **Modern consistency baselines:** Compare only against Vanilla Top-K and Matryoshka variants. Include current state-of-the-art approaches for feature consistency (e.g., Archetypal SAEs, SCoPE-style post-hoc alignment, or Neuron Shapley) to show OSAE outperforms the best available alternatives, not just its immediate predecessors.
4. **Isolated same-dataset, cross-seed variance study:** Cross-dataset results conflate distribution shift with algorithmic stability. Run a large-scale experiment (e.g., N≥30 seeds) on a single static dataset to directly quantify variance reduction and prove OSAE genuinely shrinks the solution class.

### Deeper Analysis Needed (top 3-5 only)
1. **Cause of late-index stability degradation:** Stability drops sharply for high-index features. Analyze whether this is a fundamental trade-off of the strict prefix prior (systematically under-fitting the tail) versus a tuning issue, and quantify the information lost in these dimensions.
2. **Alignment of enforced order with semantic utility:** The theory orders atoms by activation frequency, but real LLM features do not guarantee a monotonic frequency-to-utility hierarchy. Empirically correlate feature index position with interpretability scores, activation sparsity, and human-annotated concept importance to justify that the enforced order captures meaningful structure.
3. **Sensitivity to the prefix sampling distribution $p_{ND}(\ell)$:** Results appear dependent on the piecewise/Zipf weighting scheme. Systematically sweep the distribution shape and report performance stability; without this, the method's robustness to hyperparameter choice is unproven.
4. **FIFR metric validation beyond synthetic recovery:** FIFR is proposed to fix MSE's bias toward frequent features. Demonstrate that FIFR correlates with human-verified feature quality or downstream mechanistic interpretability tasks in real models; otherwise, it remains an alternative norm with unclear practical advantage.

### Visualizations & Case Studies
1. **Activation frequency/sparsity heatmap by prefix index:** Plot average activation rate per index for Gemma/Pythia SAEs to reveal whether the enforced ordering matches actual feature usage or forces arbitrary rank assignments.
2. **Gradient norms or weight update trajectories per index over training:** Visualizing $\|\nabla \theta_i\|$ or parameter drift by neuron index exposes whether orderedness emerges from genuine feature specialization or simply gradient starvation of late units (which would explain reconstruction degradation).
3. **Qualitative failure cases for high-index features:** Show example prompts/activations that should trigger stable late features versus what OSAE actually learns; this directly tests if the method suppresses valid features or successfully enforces a hierarchical curriculum.

### Obvious Next Steps
1. **Strict compute-controlled experimental protocol:** Re-run all empirical claims with FLOP-equated training schedules; ICLR requires isolating architectural benefits from compute confounds.
2. **Downstream interpretability correlation:** Report auto-interp scores or human-evaluation results for the top-ordered features; consistency metrics alone are insufficient for an interpretability-focused submission.
3. **Statistical rigor for SAE stitching:** Table 2 reports single-run percentages without variance. Re-run stitching across multiple seeds with confidence intervals and proper hypothesis testing; a single number cannot support a core claim of reduced incompleteness.
4. **Address the non-negativity theory assumption gap:** Theorem 3.1 assumes non-negative codes to resolve sign ambiguity, but ReLU/SAE activations are not globally non-negative in practice. Provide a relaxed theoretical bound or empirical evidence showing how sign-flipping ambiguity affects real ordered recovery.

# Final Consolidated Review
## Summary
This paper proposes Ordered Sparse Autoencoders (OSAEs), which enforce a strict, hierarchical ordering over latent dimensions by extending Matryoshka SAEs with a nested prefix reconstruction objective. The authors provide theoretical exact-recovery guarantees under idealized conditions and empirically demonstrate that OSAEs yield higher cross-seed/cross-dataset stability for early-prefix features while reducing feature incompleteness in stitching experiments.

## Strengths
- **Clear formulation of a known SAE bottleneck:** The method directly targets permutation non-identifiability by treating each latent dimension as a distinct prefix group, effectively shrinking the SAE equivalence class toward a canonical basis. The empirical reduction in stability variance across independent seeds is well-documented.
- **Valuable evaluation metrics:** The introduction of the Orderedness metric (Spearman rank correlation over Hungarian-matched atoms) and the Frequency-Invariant Feature Reconstruction (FIFR) Error addresses measurable blind spots in standard MSE and decoder-cosine stability, particularly in skewed/Zipfian activation regimes where rare features are otherwise overlooked.
- **Strong synthetic validation & theoretical linkage:** The connection to nested dropout is clean, and the proofs correctly show that under spark and ordering assumptions, the global minimizer recovers the true dictionary in order. The FIFR analysis in Appendix B provides rigorous evidence that global MSE fails to penalize tail-feature under-reconstruction.

## Weaknesses
- **Significant theory-practice gap in core guarantees:** Theorem 3.1 relies on strict non-negativity of codes and a ground-truth ordering defined purely by activation frequency. Real LLM latent spaces exhibit signed activations, and feature importance is driven by circuit roles, semantic hierarchy, or positional information, not just frequency. The paper does not empirically or theoretically address how violations of these assumptions break the identifiability guarantees, weakening the claim that OSAEs "resolve permutation non-identifiability" in realistic settings.
- **Unexplained tail-feature degradation and missing distribution ablations:** Multiple figures (Figs 2b, 3a) show a sharp crossover where OSAE stability and reconstruction quality degrade for higher-index features. The authors attribute this vaguely to a "restricted solution space" or limited sweeps but fail to diagnose whether this is a fundamental consequence of gradient starvation inherent to prefix truncation or a fixable hyperparameter artifact. Crucially, no ablation over the prefix sampling distribution $p_{ND}(\ell)$ is provided, making it impossible to verify if the early/late trade-off is structural or tunable.
- **Conflated evaluation protocols and underpowered statistical validation:** The empirical comparison relies on matching checkpoints to "similar loss values" without controlling for total compute/FLOPs or specifying the exact matching procedure, introducing a clear confound when evaluating architectural benefits. Additionally, the SAE stitching results (Table 2) report single-run percentages without variance, seed counts, or statistical testing, which is insufficient to support the strong claim that OSAEs inherently capture a "more complete" basis.
- **Inconsistent training mechanisms and narrow empirical scope:** "Unit sweeping" is critical for synthetic recovery but explicitly disabled for LLM experiments (Sec 4.1) without justification, creating an inconsistency in the claimed protocol. Furthermore, evaluations are limited to a single layer in relatively small models (Gemma2-2B, Pythia-70M) against only two Matryoshka variants, omitting comparisons to other consistency-focused approaches (e.g., orthogonal penalties, Gated SAEs, or post-hoc alignment) that would contextualize OSAE's practical standing.

## Nice-to-Haves
- Gradient norm or weight-update trajectory analysis per prefix index to empirically verify whether late-index degradation stems from gradient starvation or capacity under-allocation.
- FIFR correlation validation against human-interpreted feature scores or downstream mechanistic interpretability benchmarks to justify its practical advantage over MSE.
- Matched FLOP/wall-clock training schedules across all methods to definitively isolate architectural gains from compute allocation artifacts.

## Novel Insights
Framing permutation invariance reduction as a strict hierarchical prefix loss is a conceptually clean mechanism that systematically shrinks the admissible hypothesis space of overcomplete dictionaries. By enforcing reconstruction across all prefixes in expectation, OSAE naturally imposes a feature curriculum that prioritizes high-signal dimensions. Coupled with FIFR Error, which correctly exposes how standard $\ell_2$ norms disproportionately reward frequent features and mask tail recovery failures, the paper introduces a more structurally faithful lens for evaluating sparse dictionary learning in skewed, real-world activation distributions.

## Potentially Missed Related Work
- None identified. The paper adequately covers Matryoshka SAEs, classical sparse dictionary learning (K-SVD, OMP), stability/post-hoc alignment work, and recent SAE consistency papers.

## Suggestions
1. **Ablate the prefix distribution $p_{ND}(\ell)$ and control compute:** Systematically sweep $p_{ND}$ shapes (uniform, heavy-tail, linear decay) and report stability/reconstruction trade-offs. Run all methods under identical FLOP/token budgets to prove gains are architectural, not compute-driven.
2. **Strengthen the theory-practice bridge:** Empirically test recovery when activations are signed or when the ground-truth ordering is semantic/random rather than frequency-based. Reframe Theorem 3.1 as an idealized baseline or provide a relaxed analysis for signed, multidimensionally-ordered features.
3. **Standardize evaluation reporting:** Report SAE stitching metrics with multiple seeds and confidence intervals. Explicitly justify the disabling of unit sweeping in LLM experiments, or adapt it with a clear schedule to ensure protocol consistency.
4. **Expand baselines & scaling scope:** Compare OSAE against at least one representative stability-focused SAE variant (e.g., orthogonal regularization or Gated SAEs) and test across multiple model scales or layers to rule out scale/depth-specific artifacts.
