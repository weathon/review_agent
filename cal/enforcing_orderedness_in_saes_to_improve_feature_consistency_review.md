# Harsh Critic Review
## Section-by-Section Critical Review

### Title & Abstract
- **Accuracy:** The title accurately reflects the core contribution: enforcing an ordered structure in SAEs to improve consistency. 
- **Clarity of Abstract:** The abstract clearly states the problem (feature instability across seeds), the method (OSAE combining Matryoshka SAEs with nested dropout to enforce strict ordering), and the key results (improved consistency on Gemma2-2B and Pythia-70M).
- **Unsupported Claims:** The abstract states OSAE "resolves permutation non-identifiability." This is theoretically supported only under the idealized assumptions detailed in Theorem 3.1 (strict spark condition, non-negative codes, and a ground-truth frequency ordering). In real LLM activations, these conditions rarely hold strictly. The abstract should qualify this claim to avoid implying the method solves permutation ambiguity universally in practical settings, rather than in the specific theoretical regime proven. Additionally, "deterministically using every feature dimension" is slightly misleading; the loss covers all prefixes in expectation via stochastic sampling, not deterministically per update.

### Introduction & Motivation
- **Motivation & Gap:** The motivation is well-grounded in recent concerns about SAE reproducibility (Song et al., Fel et al.). The gap is clearly identified: Matryoshka SAEs only partially break permutation symmetry within coarse groups, leaving residual feature swapping.
- **Contributions:** Clearly stated. The three contributions (method, theory, empirical consistency) align with the paper structure.
- **Over-claiming/Under-selling:** The introduction slightly over-claims the generality of the theoretical guarantee without emphasizing the restrictive assumptions (e.g., non-negativity, exact frequency skew). Conversely, it under-sells the SAE Stitching results (Appendix D), which provide empirical evidence that OSAE yields more "complete" feature sets. This is a strong practical validation of consistency that deserves mention in the intro.

### Method / Approach
- **Reproducibility:** The objective function (Section 2.6) is clearly formulated as an expectation over prefixes. However, there is a **critical notation conflict** that harms reproducibility: Section 2.1 defines $K$ as the dictionary dimension ($D \in \mathbb{R}^{d \times K}$). In Section 4.1, you state "dictionary size 4096... with K=80." Given the context, $K=80$ almost certainly refers to the target sparsity level (denoted as $m$ in Section 2.3/2.4). This contradiction makes it impossible to verify hyperparameters.
- **Assumptions & Justification:** The reliance on unit sweeping (Section 3.1) to combat gradient starvation in late indices is borrowed from Rippel et al. (2014), but its justification for SAEs is brief. If unit sweeping freezes early units, how does the method handle cases where early units later need to adapt? The empirical results (Section 4.1) do not use unit sweeping, creating a methodological gap between the toy model and the main LLM experiments.
- **Logical Gaps:** The nested dropout loss $\mathcal{L}_{ND}$ samples prefixes $\ell$. If $p_{ND}$ places low probability on large $\ell$, late features receive weak gradient signal. The paper mentions this but does not ablate the sensitivity to the choice of $p_{ND}$. For ICLR standards, an ablation on the prefix sampling distribution is necessary to show robustness.
- **Theoretical Claims:** Lemma 3.1 and Theorem 3.1 are standard extensions of nested dropout theory to the sparse setting. The proof strategy (perturbation on the $K$-th atom) appears sound within its scope. However, the assumption that ground-truth atoms are ordered by support frequency is a strong prior. The theorem effectively states that *if* the ground truth obeys this prior and spark holds, the loss preserves it. It does not guarantee the model will *discover* a meaningful ordering if feature frequencies in LLMs are flat or noisy.

### Experiments & Results
- **Testing Claims:** The experiments directly test consistency (Stability and Orderedness) across seeds and datasets. The prefix-wise analysis (Figures 2 & 3) is appropriate given the ordered nature of the method.
- **Baselines:** Fixed/Random MSAE and Vanilla SAE are reasonable baselines. However, the field includes stronger, widely used architectures like Gated SAEs or JumpReLU SAEs. Their omission weakens the empirical positioning. Appendix D compares against BatchTopK, which should ideally be in the main text.
- **Missing Ablations:** 
  1. **Prefix distribution $p_{ND}$:** No results show how changing the sampling weights affects the orderedness/stability trade-off.
  2. **Unit Sweeping:** Used in the toy model but not in LLM experiments. An ablation showing its impact (or lack thereof) on LLM stability is needed to bridge the theory/experiment gap.
  3. **Sparsity level ($m$):** The paper fixates on one sparsity regime ($K=80$ typo noted above). Does OSAE break down as $m \to K$ or $m \to 1$? 
- **Statistical Significance:** Table 1 reports mean $\pm$ std over seed pairs, and figures include 95% confidence intervals. This meets ICLR standards for rigor.
- **Cherry-Picking / Metric Validity Concerns:** This is a major concern. Section 2.7 defines Stability using the Hungarian algorithm ($\max_P$). If OSAE successfully enforces a strict deterministic ordering, the permutation matrix $P$ *should be the identity* when comparing two OSAE runs. By using Hungarian matching, you explicitly **allow** permutation in the stability metric. This conflates feature quality with ordering consistency. You must report stability with $P=I$ (i.e., cosine similarity of matched indices) to truly validate that the ordering is consistent across seeds. Currently, `Stab` measures feature consistency *despite* permutation, which undermines the paper's core claim of fixing permutation ambiguity.
- **Results vs. Claims:** Figure 2 shows that while orderedness is high for early prefixes, stability of OSAE drops sharply after prefix $\approx 128$, becoming lower than MSAE baselines. The text mentions this crossover but frames OSAE as an overall improvement. The trade-off is significant: enforcing order seems to crowd out flexibility for later features, making them less stable. This needs a more thorough discussion rather than being relegated to a single sentence in Section 4.1.

### Writing & Clarity
- **Confusing Sections:** The notation clash regarding $K$ vs. dictionary size vs. sparsity ($m$) in Sections 2 and 4 is the primary clarity issue. It must be resolved globally (use $D$ for dictionary size, $k$ for active features).
- **Figures/Tables:** Figures 2 and 3 clearly communicate the prefix-dependent trends. Figure 1/4 effectively visualize the toy model recovery. Table 1 is clear but limited by the notation issues in the setup description. The SAE stitching table (Table 2) is valuable but buried in the appendix; its relevance to "consistency/completeness" should be highlighted earlier.

### Limitations & Broader Impact
- **Acknowledged Limitations:** Section 5 correctly identifies theoretical idealizations, computational overhead, and sensitivity to the ordering prior.
- **Missed Limitations:** 
  1. **Frequency Ties:** The ordering prior relies on strict frequency differences. If two semantically distinct features fire with equal probability, OSAE has no inductive bias to order them consistently, and permutation between tied features can still occur. This should be discussed.
  2. **Metric Limitation:** As noted above, the reliance on Hungarian matching for Stability evaluation is a methodological limitation. The current metric cannot disentangle whether consistency stems from the ordering constraint or just shared feature discovery.
  3. **Interpretability of Order:** The paper assumes ordering by frequency is desirable. However, in mechanistic interpretability, rare features can be highly semantically important. The method may push these to unstable tail indices, potentially making them harder for practitioners to find. 
- **Societal Impact:** Not a significant concern here. Standard interpretability caveats apply.

### Overall Assessment
This paper addresses a timely and important problem in mechanistic interpretability: the lack of reproducibility across SAE training runs. The proposal to extend Matryoshka SAEs with nested dropout-style prefix sampling is well-motivated, and the theoretical extension to the sparse regime is sound under clearly stated assumptions. The empirical results demonstrate that the method successfully induces a consistent ordering of high-frequency features. 

However, the contribution is currently hampered by three critical issues that must be addressed for acceptance: (1) **Notation and Reproducibility:** The conflicting definitions of $K$ and $m$ in the experimental setup make it difficult to verify the results. (2) **Evaluation Methodology:** Using Hungarian matching for the primary Stability metric undermines the claim that OSAE resolves permutation ambiguity; the authors must report identity-aligned stability to validate the ordering constraint. (3) **Trade-off Disclosure:** The method clearly stabilizes early features at the cost of later feature stability (as seen in Figure 2). This trade-off is fundamental to forcing an ordered structure and deserves deeper analysis and ablation rather than brief mention. 

With a rigorous correction of the evaluation metrics, resolution of notation conflicts, and an honest discussion of the stability trade-off and frequency-tie failure modes, this work represents a solid step toward canonical basis discovery in sparse coding and meets ICLR standards. As is, it requires these clarifications to ensure the community can reliably adopt and interpret the method.

# Neutral Reviewer
## Balanced Review

### Summary
This paper proposes Ordered Sparse Autoencoders (OSAEs), extending Matryoshka SAEs by enforcing a strict, deterministic ordering of latent dimensions through a full-prefix reconstruction loss adapted from nested dropout. The authors provide theoretical guarantees showing that, under classical sparse dictionary learning assumptions (spark condition, non-negativity, and frequency-ordered ground-truth codes), minimizing the prefix loss uniquely recovers the true dictionary and its ordering. Empirically, OSAEs demonstrate improved feature consistency and orderedness across random seeds on a synthetic Gaussian model, Gemma2-2B, and Pythia-70M, while an SAE stitching experiment suggests they recover a more complete feature set than standard Top-k baselines.

### Strengths
1. **Clear mechanism with strong theoretical grounding:** The transition from sampling a few Matryoshka groups to deterministic full-prefix coverage directly targets permutation non-identifiability. Lemma 3.1 and Theorem 3.1 rigorously show how covering all prefixes shrinks the admissible solution space from a permutation-invariant subspace to a unique ordered basis under standard dictionary learning conditions.
2. **Practical validation of improved feature completeness:** Table 2's SAE stitching experiment provides compelling empirical evidence for OSAE's utility. Reducing the "novel feature" rate from 73.8% (BatchTopK) to 33.8% (OSAE) strongly suggests that ordered training encourages the SAE to capture more of the model's underlying representational structure upfront, addressing a known incompleteness critique of SAEs.
3. **Thoughtful metric design and analysis:** The introduction of the Frequency-Invariant Feature Reconstruction (FIFR) Error (Appendix B.4) directly addresses a known flaw in MSE evaluation for Zipfian/skewed features. Coupled with the clear definitions of Stable (Hungarian-matched cosine) and Orderedness (Spearman rank correlation), the evaluation framework is robust and well-motivated.
4. **Honest limitation reporting:** The authors transparently discuss the trade-off in later-feature stability, potential over-regularization if the order prior is misspecified, and the computational overhead of prefix coverage. This balanced presentation aligns well with rigorous scientific standards.

### Weaknesses
1. **Theory relies on assumptions that rarely hold in practice:** Theorem 3.1 assumes non-negative codes and a ground-truth frequency ordering. Transformer hidden states and SAE latents often contain signed activations, and the "natural" ordering of features is rarely known or purely frequency-based prior to training. While the synthetic experiments satisfy these conditions, the gap between the idealized proof and real LLM activations weakens the claim that exact ordered recovery is guaranteed in practice.
2. **Trade-off in late-feature stability and reconstruction loss:** Figures 2 and 3 consistently show that while OSAE achieves high early-feature stability, stability drops sharply for later prefixes (e.g., >1024), and reconstruction loss is often higher than baselines. The paper notes this but does not deeply investigate whether the deterministic prefix loss inherently starves late features of gradient signal, or if the ordering prior actively suppresses valid alternative bases. For interpretability, losing consistency in lower-ranked features may offset gains in early features.
3. **Insufficient experimental detail for reproduction:** Key training configurations are missing. The exact SAE architecture (e.g., TopLA vs standard linear), optimizer, learning rate schedule, $L_1$ coefficient weight, batch size, warmup schedule formula, and compute budget are not fully specified. The "unit sweeping" heuristic mentions freezing units every $T$ epochs but omits how $T$ is chosen or scaled with $K$. Without these details, reproducing the exact stability/loss trade-offs is challenging.
4. **Orderedness gains may conflate with initialization bias:** In cross-dataset same-seed comparisons, the paper reports near-1.0 orderedness, suggesting strong consistency. However, the text also notes "minimal full-prefix changes in orderedness when comparing trained models against their initialization state." This raises the possibility that high orderedness stems primarily from the shared initialization rather than the data-driven learning of an ordered basis. The evaluation does not sufficiently disentangle learned ordering from initialization imprinting.
5. **Scalability to standard interpretability dictionary sizes is unclear:** Recent SAE benchmarks commonly use $K \in [32\text{k}, 131\text{k}]$. Deterministically computing prefixes up to $K$ significantly increases compute and memory overhead compared to Matryoshka sampling. The paper lacks ablations or discussion on whether OSAE remains computationally viable or requires aggressive approximations at these scales.

### Novelty & Significance
**Novelty: Moderate.** OSAE is a well-justified and logical synthesis of Matryoshka SAE hierarchy with nested dropout, extended to the hard Top-m sparse regime. The theoretical extension to overcomplete dictionary learning under spark conditions is non-trivial, but the core mechanism (deterministic prefix coverage to break permutation symmetry) is an incremental rather than transformative advance over prior ordered/curriculum latent methods.
**Clarity: Good.** The paper is logically structured, moving cleanly from motivation to theory, synthetic validation, and empirical LLM experiments. Notation is mostly consistent, and figures effectively communicate the trade-offs between methods. Minor textual inconsistencies and parser artifacts do not detract from comprehension.
**Reproducibility: Fair.** While models, datasets, and evaluation metrics are named, the absence of a complete hyperparameter table, explicit training recipes, and a statement regarding code availability falls short of current ICLR reproducibility expectations. Independent replication of the stability curves would be difficult without more granular configuration details.
**Significance: Moderate-High.** Feature consistency across seeds and hyperparameters is a critical bottleneck for mechanistic interpretability. OSAE provides a practically useful inductive bias that improves early-feature stability and reduces feature incompleteness, as evidenced by the stitching results. However, the late-feature stability drop, reconstruction penalty, and unproven scalability to large $K$ temper its immediate adoption. The method will likely be a valuable tool in the interpretability toolkit and a strong benchmark for future consistency-focused SAE variants.

### Suggestions for Improvement
1. **Ablate the late-feature stability decline:** Systematically test whether the sharp drop in late-prefix stability is caused by gradient starvation, lack of representation in early training epochs, or inherent bias of the ordering prior. Experiment with re-weighting the prefix distribution $p_{\text{ND}}(\ell)$, adaptive learning rates for later indices, or hybrid objectives (e.g., prefix loss + mild reconstruction penalty on full code) to recover late-feature stability without sacrificing orderedness.
2. **Provide a complete reproducibility appendix:** Include a table detailing the SAE architecture variant, optimizer, learning rate and schedule, $L_1$ sparsity coefficient, batch sizes, total training steps/tokens, warmup schedule formula, and exact unit sweeping parameters ($T$, burn-in epochs, scheduler). If code is not public, provide pseudo-code or explicit algorithmic steps for the training loop and metric computation.
3. **Disentangle learned ordering from initialization bias:** Add experiments comparing OSAE runs from different random initializations against a "random initialization shuffled" baseline. Report the *relative* improvement in orderedness over initialization rather than absolute scores, or explicitly measure the cosine drift of early vs late features from initialization to convergence to quantify how much structure is learned versus retained.
4. **Bridge the theory-practice gap in assumptions:** Add an empirical analysis measuring how closely real SAE activations satisfy the non-negativity and frequency-ordering assumptions during training. Discuss theoretically or empirically how violations (e.g., signed latents, non-Zipfian feature usage) impact the gradient geometry and final solution stability, offering practical recommendations for when OSAE is most appropriate.
5. **Address large-scale applicability:** Include a discussion or experiment on OSAE's behavior at $K \ge 16,384$. If deterministic full-prefix coverage becomes prohibitive, propose a principled approximation (e.g., strided prefixes, adaptive curriculum, or stochastic full-prefix sampling with higher variance) and analyze the compute-consistency trade-off, ensuring the method's relevance to modern large-dictionary SAE research.

# Spark Finder Review
## How to Improve This Paper

### Missing Experiments (top 3 only)
1. Add direct comparisons against standard Top-K/TopK SAE baselines in all LLM experiments (Gemma/Pythia). Without this, it is unclear whether OSAE offers genuine consistency improvements over standard practice or is simply a suboptimal reconstruction model trading signal for artificial structure.
2. Provide a rigorous ablation on the prefix sampling distribution ($p_{\text{ND}}$) and unit sweeping schedule. The paper notes sensitivity to these choices but provides no sweep; showing performance variance across these hyperparameters is essential to prove the method's robustness and generalizability.
3. Report the computational overhead (training FLOPs and wall-clock time) of full-prefix sampling compared to $L$-group Matryoshka SAEs. If OSAE requires significantly more compute for marginal gains in consistency (which degrade at long prefixes), the method lacks practical utility for typical SAE training pipelines.

### Deeper Analysis Needed (top 3 only)
1. Analyze whether the learned "order" correlates with meaningful feature hierarchies (e.g., low-level syntax vs. high-level semantics) or merely encodes token frequency. If the ordering just reflects statistical frequency without semantic progression, the claimed improvement in mechanistic interpretability is unsubstantiated.
2. Investigate the sharp stability drop for prefix lengths >128 by examining gradient norms, activation sparsity, and feature utilization for tail dimensions. The current results suggest feature starvation; quantifying this phenomenon is needed to determine if OSAE sacrifices completeness to force early indices to converge.
3. Address the sign ambiguity gap between Theorem 3.1 and practice. The theoretical guarantee relies on non-negative codes to resolve sign flips, yet real LLM activations are bipolar; without analyzing how OSAE handles sign flips empirically, the uniqueness claim is weakened and the "canonical basis" assertion is questionable.

### Visualizations & Case Studies (top 3 only)
1. Show top-activating text sequences and activation histograms for features at different prefix indices (e.g., 1–10 vs. 4000–4050). This qualitative evidence is required to verify that OSAE creates an interpretable feature curriculum rather than just numerically sorting uninterpretable latents by usage rate.
2. Plot the trajectory of feature similarity matrices or Hungarian assignment indices over training checkpoints. This would expose whether OSAE truly converges to a stable canonical ordering or if late indices remain highly volatile, directly testing the "strict ordering" claim in practice.
3. Visualize the activation distributions of "ordered" features versus standard SAE features using dimensionality reduction (e.g., PCA/t-SNE of decoder weights). This would reveal whether OSAE features exhibit better disentanglement/orthogonality or merely cluster around dominant training data modes.

### Obvious Next Steps (top 2 only)
1. Include downstream interpretability benchmarks (e.g., linear probing accuracy for specific concepts, or automated circuit discovery scores). Demonstrating that OSAE features yield higher downstream utility or more faithful steering than baselines is essential to prove that the "orderedness" metric translates to real-world interpretability gains.
2. Conduct cross-model transfer experiments where an OSAE trained on Pythia is evaluated on Gemma activations (or vice versa) to test if the enforced order generalizes across architectures. This would validate whether OSAE discovers a model-agnostic canonical basis as the theoretical framing implies.

# Final Consolidated Review
## Summary
This paper introduces Ordered Sparse Autoencoders (OSAE), which extend Matryoshka SAEs by enforcing a strict latent dimension ordering via a full-prefix reconstruction objective adapted from nested dropout. Under standard sparse dictionary learning assumptions (spark condition, non-negativity, and frequency-ordered codes), the authors prove exact recovery of an ordered canonical basis. Empirically, OSAEs demonstrate improved index-level consistency across seeds and datasets on Gemma-2 2B and Pythia-70M, with stitching experiments indicating higher feature completeness compared to baseline SAEs.

## Strengths
- **Elegant theoretical extension to the hard-sparse regime:** The formulation cleanly generalizes nested dropout to the top-$m$ sparse setting. Lemma 3.1 and Theorem 3.1 rigorously demonstrate how deterministic prefix coverage collapses the permutation-invariant solution space into a unique ordered basis under well-defined identifiability conditions.
- **Strong empirical evidence for feature completeness:** The SAE stitching experiment (Appendix D) is a compelling practical validation. Reducing the novel feature injection rate from 73.8% (BatchTopK) to 33.8% (OSAE) directly addresses a major critique in mechanistic interpretability: that smaller SAEs incompletely capture a model's representational structure.
- **Methodologically sound evaluation framework:** The introduction of the Frequency-Invariant Feature Reconstruction (FIFR) Error correctly identifies and corrects a known flaw in global MSE evaluation for skewed/Zipfian feature distributions. Coupled with the prefix-wise Spearman orderedness metric, the evaluation suite is well-calibrated to the paper's inductive bias.

## Weaknesses
- **Primary stability metric contradicts the core claim:** The stability metric (Section 2.7) computes maximum cosine similarity after optimal Hungarian re-indexing ($\max_P$). If OSAE successfully enforces a strict deterministic ordering, permutation should not be optimized away; consistency should be measurable at fixed indices ($P=I$). Reporting stability after optimal matching conflates general feature rediscovery with the specific index-level consistency the method claims to induce, making it impossible to verify whether the ordering constraint actually yields a canonical basis across independent runs.
- **High orderedness appears driven by initialization rather than data-driven learning:** In cross-dataset, same-seed experiments, OSAE achieves near-perfect orderedness, and the authors explicitly note "minimal full-prefix changes in orderedness when comparing trained models against their initialization state." This strongly suggests the observed consistency largely reflects inherited weight initialization structure rather than learning a meaningful hierarchical ordering from activation statistics. Without ablations using randomized initial index assignments, the claim that OSAE *learns* a canonical basis remains unverified.
- **Late-feature stability collapse is under-analyzed:** Figures 2 and 3 consistently show that stability gains for early prefixes (≤128) come with a sharp degradation for later indices, alongside higher global reconstruction loss. While the limitation is acknowledged, the paper lacks a mechanistic investigation (e.g., gradient norm tracking across prefixes, activation sparsity analysis, or $p_{ND}$ distribution ablations) to determine whether this trade-off is an inherent property of the ordered prior, a consequence of gradient starvation, or a fixable training artifact.
- **Missing reproducibility specifications:** Key training configurations necessary to reproduce the stability curves and FIFR results are absent from the manuscript. The exact SAE architecture variant, optimizer, learning rate schedule, $L_1$ sparsity coefficient, batch sizes, warmup formula, and unit sweeping schedule ($T$) are not provided. Given the method's sensitivity to curriculum dynamics, opaque hyperparameter reporting severely limits independent verification and adoption.

## Nice-to-Haves
- Compare directly against stronger contemporary SAE baselines (e.g., Gated SAEs, JumpReLU) to contextualize OSAE's consistency gains against current mechanistic interpretability standards.
- Provide a qualitative analysis of whether the enforced ordering correlates with meaningful semantic hierarchies (e.g., low-level syntax $\to$ high-level semantics) or strictly ranks features by firing frequency.
- Discuss or empirically analyze how OSAE handles sign ambiguity in real LLM activations, given the theoretical proof's reliance on non-negative codes to resolve scaling/sign flips.
- Analyze the compute-time and FLOP overhead of the full-prefix expectation relative to $L$-group Matryoshka sampling, and discuss principled approximations (e.g., strided prefixes or adaptive sampling) for scaling to $K \ge 32\text{k}$.

## Novel Insights
The paper's most revealing contribution is exposing a fundamental trade-off in consistency-focused SAE training: enforcing a strict prefix curriculum acts as a powerful regularizer against permutation collapse in high-frequency features, but this structural constraint inherently starves tail indices of gradient signal, leading to representational degradation in the dictionary tail. The empirical evidence further suggests that in practice, "orderedness" metrics may heavily conflate architectural weight inheritance with genuine data-driven hierarchy learning, particularly when activation frequencies do not strictly obey the Zipfian prior assumed by the theory. The introduction of the FIFR error is a critical corrective for the field, demonstrating that global reconstruction loss systematically masks rare-feature underfitting in consistency benchmarks, and future SAE evaluations should adopt frequency-invariant metrics to avoid conflating overall MSE with true representational completeness.

## Suggestions
- Report identity-aligned stability (fixed-index cosine similarity without Hungarian matching) as the primary metric for ordered consistency, relegating permutation-invariant stability to a secondary baseline comparison. This will rigorously test whether OSAE actually fixes index ambiguity or merely improves feature quality.
- Add an ablation that randomizes or shuffles the initial dictionary column ordering before training. Quantifying the divergence between initialized and learned ordering will isolate how much of the reported consistency is genuinely data-driven versus initialization-dependent.
- Publish a complete reproducibility appendix detailing all architectural choices, optimizer settings, learning rate/warmup schedules, and the exact unit sweeping heuristic. Include a profile of training FLOPs/wall-clock time to contextualize the overhead of full-prefix sampling.
- Investigate the late-feature stability drop by logging prefix-wise gradient norms, activation utilization rates, and reconstruction residuals throughout training. This will clarify whether the tail degradation stems from optimization dynamics (fixable via re-weighted $p_{ND}$ or adaptive scheduling) or is an inherent limitation of the hard ordering constraint.
