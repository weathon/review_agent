# Harsh Critic Review
## Section-by-Section Critical Review

### Title & Abstract
- **Does the title accurately reflect the contribution?** Yes, "Map as a Prompt" correctly highlights the geographic conditioning mechanism, and "multi-modal spatial-signal foundation model" reflects the CSI + 3D map fusion.
- **Does the abstract clearly state the problem, method, and key results?** The problem (cross-scenario wireless localization, NLoS sensitivity, poor generalization) is clearly stated. The method (cycle-adaptive masking, map-as-prompt via soft tokens) is outlined. Results claim SOTA and zero-shot generalization.
- **Are any claims in the abstract unsupported by the paper?** Yes. The abstract claims "strong zero-shot generalization in unseen environments," but Section 4.5 explicitly states that downstream heads are fine-tuned on ~100 samples per scenario. This is few-shot, not zero-shot. The claim should be corrected to match the experimental protocol, and the distinction must be clarified throughout the paper.

### Introduction & Motivation
- **Is the problem well-motivated? Is the gap in prior work clearly identified?** The motivation is solid. Classical methods fail in rich multipath/NLoS, supervised DL lacks generalization, and existing SSL approaches ignore CSI periodicity and shallowly integrate geography. The two gaps (periodicity handling, geographic integration) are well-articulated.
- **Are the contributions clearly stated and accurate?** Contributions are clearly listed: cycle-adaptive masked modeling, map-conditioned prompt tuning, and parameter-efficient generalization. They align with the methodology and experiments, though the "interpretable fusion" claim (Intro) is not substantiated experimentally (see Limitations).
- **Does the introduction over-claim or under-sell?** The introduction appropriately frames prior work but slightly conflates LLM-based wireless reasoning (WirelessLLM) with physical-layer localization, which is fair but could more sharply distinguish symbolic vs. signal-level reasoning. The over-claim on zero-shot generalization persists and permeates the framing.

### Method / Approach
- **Is the method clearly described and reproducible?** Partially. The overall pipeline (pre-training → prompt tuning) is logical, but critical implementation details are missing or inconsistent. Equation 5 states the input is real/imag component stacking `[Re(H), Im(H)]`, but Appendix B.2 states it is magnitude/phase `[|H_s|, ∠H_s]`. This directly impacts the cross-correlation step (Sec 3.3) for periodicity detection, as phase wrapping introduces artificial discontinuities that cross-correlation handles poorly. The choice must be explicit.
- **Are key assumptions stated and justified?** The assumption that 3D building meshes are available and can be directly converted into a heterogeneous graph is unrealistic for real deployments. Section 3.4 states the node set is the *union of mesh vertices and BS positions*, with edges via Delaunay triangulation. Real urban meshes contain 10^5–10^6 vertices; running even 2 GCN layers over this graph is computationally intractable without explicit vertex simplification, clustering, or subsampling. The paper does not address how graph complexity is managed.
- **Are there logical gaps in the derivation or reasoning?** Yes, a significant gap exists in Section 4.2, where Equation 11 introduces an "NLoS-aware attention mechanism" (`α_i = exp(...) / ...`) to differentiate direct and reflected paths. This mechanism is never described in Section 3 (Methodology), nor is it clear how it integrates with the existing multi-BS attention head (Eq 9-10) or the geographic prompt. It appears abruptly in the results section without architectural context.
- **Are there edge cases or failure modes not discussed?** The method assumes the geographic prompt can be prepended to a fixed sequence length. If CSI samples vary in antenna/subcarrier configuration across environments, how is sequence alignment handled before attention? Additionally, if a test environment lacks height/facade data (e.g., 2D footprints only), the GCN operates on a different topological space than pre-training; robustness to such distribution shifts is claimed in Sec 4.4 but not mechanistically explained.
- **For theoretical claims: are proofs correct and complete?** The paper makes primarily empirical/algorithmic claims rather than formal theoretical ones. The physical grounding (Sec 2.1) is standard and correctly references channel modeling. No formal convergence or generalization bounds are provided, which is acceptable for a foundation model paper, but the "interpretable fusion" claim lacks analytical justification.

### Experiments & Results
- **Do the experiments actually test the paper's claims?** The experiments test accuracy, masking efficacy, map ablation, and cross-domain transfer, which align with the claims. However, the zero-shot claim is not tested, as all generalization experiments use ~100 fine-tuning samples.
- **Are baselines appropriate and fairly compared?** Baselines include classical (OMP), supervised (CNN), and recent SSL/foundation models (SWiT, LWLM). Fairness is unclear: were baselines trained on identical CSI splits, with identical data augmentation, and same parameter budgets? For OMP and CNN, it's unclear if they received map inputs. If not, comparisons in Tables 1 & 2 are confounded by modality differences rather than architecture. This must be clarified or matched with map-aware baselines.
- **Are there missing ablations that would materially change conclusions?** Key missing ablations: (1) Prompt dimensionality sensitivity, (2) GCN depth vs. prompt performance, (3) Ablation on the cross-correlation step vs. random periodic masks, (4) Comparison to standard PEFT methods (LoRA, Adapters) using the same backbone. Without comparing to LoRA/Adapters, the claim that map-conditioned GCN prompts offer unique benefits over generic soft tokens is unproven.
- **Are error bars / statistical significance reported?** No. Tables 1-5 report point estimates averaged over 5 runs, but standard deviations or confidence intervals are omitted. ICLR expects statistical rigor to confirm that improvements (e.g., 1.564m vs 2.382m MAE) are statistically significant and not due to hyperparameter tuning or seed variance.
- **Do the results support the claims made, or are they cherry-picked?** Results consistently favor the proposed method across metrics. Table 3 shows the baseline methods clearly, but the NLoS attention (Eq 11) appears to be selectively reported without explaining its implementation overhead or integration point. The 34.4% MAE improvement in Single-BS is substantial, but without baseline training details or variance, it's difficult to fully validate the magnitude.
- **Are datasets and evaluation metrics appropriate?** DeepMIMO and WAIR-D are standard ray-tracing/generated benchmarks for mmWave/massive MIMO. Metrics (MAE, RMSE, CDF@1m) are standard for localization. However, DeepMIMO O1_p5 is synthetic; performance in real-world hardware impairments (CFO, phase noise, hardware asymmetry) is not discussed, limiting external validity.

### Writing & Clarity
- **Are there sections that are confusing or poorly explained?** Section 3.4's graph construction is computationally ambiguous as noted. Section 4.2's sudden introduction of Equation 11 disrupts methodological flow. The transition from Eq 5 (real/imag) to B.2 (magnitude/phase) creates confusion about signal representation. The description of the cycle-adaptive mask (Eq 6) lacks clear definitions of how $d_{final}$ is estimated from cross-correlation peaks, making reproduction difficult without reading Appendix B.4 carefully.
- **Are figures and tables clear and informative?** Tables are cleanly formatted and present results logically. Figures 2, 3, 4, and 5 effectively illustrate architecture, masking, prompt pipeline, and performance. However, Figure 1's bottom-right inset is referenced but the 3D-to-prompt alignment is not visually demonstrated. CDF plots in Appendix are clear, but main-text tables should ideally include ±std for quick assessment.

### Limitations & Broader Impact
- **Do the authors acknowledge the key limitations?** The paper mentions reliance on 3D maps as a future challenge and notes that 2D maps retain most performance, but does not formally list limitations in a dedicated section. The computational cost of ray-tracing and graph generation for prompt creation at test time is not acknowledged.
- **Are there fundamental limitations they missed?** Yes. (1) Real-time deployment latency of graph construction and forward pass on dynamic environments. (2) Sensitivity to map inaccuracies or outdated GIS data, which are common in practice. (3) Hardware-specific CSI characteristics (calibration, array geometry mismatches) that break the assumption of domain-agnostic periodic masking.
- **Are there failure modes or negative societal impacts not discussed?** Failure modes include degraded performance in environments with highly dynamic obstacles (vehicles, crowds) that are not in the static 3D map. Societal impacts: while wireless localization enables positive applications (emergency response, smart cities), it also enables tracking and surveillance. The paper does not address privacy-preserving localization or data security, which ICLR increasingly expects for localization/positioning systems.

### Overall Assessment
This paper proposes a promising integration of geographic context and self-supervised representation learning for wireless localization, with a well-motivated cycle-adaptive masking strategy that directly addresses periodic shortcut learning in CSI. The "map-as-prompt" concept is novel in the wireless domain and demonstrates strong empirical gains with low parameter overhead. However, for ICLR's acceptance bar, several critical issues must be resolved: (1) The contradiction between the claimed "zero-shot" generalization and the actual few-shot fine-tuning protocol must be corrected and the contribution accurately framed; (2) A major methodological gap exists with the abrupt appearance of the NLoS-aware attention (Eq 11) in the results section without architectural definition; (3) The computational feasibility of constructing a GCN over raw 3D mesh vertices requires urgent clarification (subsampling/simplification strategy must be detailed); (4) Baseline experimental conditions must be explicitly matched for fairness, and statistical significance (error bars/tests) should be reported across all tables; (5) The inconsistency between real/imag and magnitude/phase preprocessing must be unified. If these issues are addressed with rigorous ablations, comparative PEFT baselines, and clarified implementation details, the paper would make a strong, reproducible contribution to the intersection of foundation models and wireless signal processing. As it stands, the conceptual contribution is solid, but methodological clarity and experimental rigor fall short of ICLR standards.

# Neutral Reviewer
## Balanced Review

### Summary
The paper introduces SigMap, a foundation model for wireless localization that combines a cycle-adaptive masked autoencoder to learn robust Channel State Information (CSI) representations with a graph-based "map-as-prompt" mechanism for parameter-efficient fine-tuning. By dynamically adjusting masking patterns to disrupt CSI periodicity and injecting 3D geographic context via soft prompts, the model achieves state-of-the-art localization accuracy while demonstrating strong few-shot generalization to unseen environments with minimal trainable parameters.

### Strengths
1. **Well-Motivated Pre-training Objective:** The cycle-adaptive masking strategy directly addresses a known failure mode in SSL for wireless signals: models exploiting periodic hardware-induced patterns as shortcuts. The ablation in Table 3 empirically validates that disrupting these periodic shifts yields consistent gains in CDF@1m and MAE over naive grid/strip masking.
2. **Effective Parameter-Efficient Adaptation:** The map-conditioned prompt design leverages a GNN to encode 3D spatial topology into frozen Transformer inputs, updating only ~0.7% of parameters during fine-tuning. This yields rapid adaptation (30 min for 1000 epochs) and strong cross-scenario generalization, as demonstrated on DeepMIMO O2 and WAIR-D (Table in Sec 4.5).
3. **Strong Empirical Rigor & Reproducibility:** The paper provides detailed dataset configurations, hyperparameters, compute specifications, and a public pipeline repository. The evaluation covers single-BS and multi-BS settings, includes appropriate baselines, and reports standard metrics averaged over 5 runs, aligning well with ICLR's expectations for thorough experimental design.

### Weaknesses
1. **Architectural Inconsistency in Methodology:** Equation 11 introduces an "NLoS-aware attention mechanism" directly in the Main Results section (4.2) without prior definition or integration into the Methodology (Sec 3). This breaks the logical flow and leaves the reader uncertain about how this component interacts with the base Transformer, prompt tokens, or standard self-attention.
2. **Limited Pre-training Scope for "Foundation Model" Claims:** The self-supervised pre-training relies exclusively on a single scenario (DeepMIMO O1_3p5) with fixed frequency bands and bandwidths. While fine-tuning generalizes well, the pre-training phase lacks cross-domain or multi-frequency diversity typically expected of foundation models, raising questions about the robustness of the learned representations under distribution shifts in carrier frequency or antenna configurations.
3. **Scalability of Map Prompt Generation:** The prompt pipeline constructs a GCN over a Delaunay triangulation of all 3D mesh vertices and base stations. For large-scale urban environments, this graph can become extremely dense, yet the paper provides no analysis of prompt generation latency, memory overhead, or graph-size scaling, which is critical for real-world deployment.
4. **Baseline Selection Gaps:** While comparisons to SSL methods like LWLM and SWiT are included, the evaluation omits stronger recent foundation-model or cross-domain SSL baselines in wireless/signal processing. Additionally, the comparison focuses heavily on downstream localization metrics without analyzing representation quality (e.g., linear probing, feature alignment, or transfer learning curves).

### Novelty & Significance
The methodological novelty is incremental but well-executed from an applied ML perspective. Cycle-adaptive masking and prompt-based spatial conditioning are established paradigms in vision and NLP, but their co-adaptation to the unique spatial-spectral structure of CSI and 3D geographic constraints represents a meaningful contribution to scientific foundation models. The work is significant for 6G network design, offering a practical, parameter-efficient pipeline that bridges raw signal data and environmental semantics while demonstrating strong empirical generalization. For ICLR, the paper is competitive in empirical engineering and cross-domain transfer but would benefit from deeper representation analysis and clearer methodological rigor to match the venue's bar for foundational ML contributions.

### Suggestions for Improvement
1. **Relocate and Formalize NLoS Attention:** Move the description of the NLoS-aware attention mechanism (Equation 11) to the Methodology section, explicitly defining its inputs, outputs, and how it integrates with the multi-BS fusion head or prompt-conditioned tokens.
2. **Strengthen the Foundation Model Framing:** Either expand the pre-training phase to include multiple scenarios/frequencies to better justify the "foundation model" terminology, or explicitly scope the claims to "scenario-agnostic pre-trained models." Include an analysis of representation transfer (e.g., frozen feature linear probes or cosine similarity of embeddings across domains) to quantify pre-training quality.
3. **Analyze Prompt Generation Scalability:** Add a brief computational analysis of the map-prompt pipeline, reporting graph node counts, prompt generation latency, and memory usage across different environment scales. Consider discussing hierarchical pooling or point-cloud downsampling as future mitigation strategies.
4. **Deepen Ablation & Analysis:** Beyond masking strategies, include an analysis of prompt token dimensionality, GCN depth, and the impact of varying map fidelity (e.g., comparing vertex-level vs. building-footprint-level graphs). Visualizing attention maps between prompt tokens and CSI embeddings would also improve interpretability and strengthen the claims about LoS/NLoS disentanglement.

## How to Improve This Paper

### Missing Experiments (top 3-5 only)
1. Add a true zero-shot evaluation alongside systematic few-shot curves (1, 10, 50, 100 samples) because the abstract explicitly claims "zero-shot generalization" but Section 4.5 trains on ~100 labeled instances, making the core adaptation claim demonstrably inaccurate.
2. Add a direct comparison of prompt-only tuning versus full backbone fine-tuning on unseen domains because the parameter-efficiency claim is unsubstantiated if updating all parameters yields comparable accuracy within reasonable compute bounds.
3. Add robustness evaluations with injected CSI impairments (phase noise, carrier frequency offsets, calibration drift) because evaluating solely on idealized ray-traced data (DeepMIMO/WAIR-D) fails to validate cross-scenario robustness for any real-world wireless deployment.

### Deeper Analysis Needed (top 3-5 only)
1. Provide mechanistic analysis (e.g., feature probing or representation similarity metrics) showing how the geographic prompt conditions the backbone because without it, the prompt likely functions as a superficial dataset ID rather than a geometric constraint, undermining the "map-as-prompt" claim.
2. Rigorously define and integrate the "NLoS-aware attention mechanism" (Equation 11) into the methodology because introducing an undefined, mathematically garbled component during results presentation destroys methodological transparency and prevents reproducing the reported single-BS gains.
3. Quantify the feature space impact of cycle-adaptive masking using latent visualization (PCA/t-SNE) or periodicity-ablation tasks because marginal MAE differences in Table 3 lack statistical significance and do not prove the model actually abandons periodic shortcuts for global representations.

### Visualizations & Case Studies
1. Cross-attention heatmaps between the geographic prompt token and CSI/antenna subsequences in both LoS and NLoS conditions would expose whether the model genuinely leverages 3D topology to multipath disambiguation or ignores the prompt entirely.
2. Direct overlay of the generated cycle-adaptive masks on raw CSI amplitude/phase channels is required to verify that the masking strategy physically disrupts antenna/subcarrier periodicity rather than applying trivial random or block patterns.
3. Error distribution plots for geometrically constrained WAIR-D sub-regions (sharp urban canyons, severe occlusions) with and without map conditioning would reveal whether geographic integration actually resolves structural localization failures or merely shifts aggregate error distributions.

### Obvious Next Steps
1. Reconcile the contradiction between the claimed 30-minute fine-tuning (Section 4.6) and the 1000-epoch training schedule cited alongside inconsistent hyperparameters in Appendix B because unexplained training instability directly invalidates the stated parameter and time efficiency.
2. Specify vertex downsampling, spatial hashing, or subgraph extraction protocols for the GNN map encoder because running graph convolutions over raw 3D city meshes (often >10k vertices) is computationally intractable and contradicts the lightweight prompt claim.
3. Standardize optimizer settings, learning rate schedules, and epoch counts across all reported scenarios because inconsistent training configurations across pre-training, fine-tuning, and transfer datasets prevent meaningful baseline comparisons and independent reproduction.

# Final Consolidated Review
## Summary
This paper proposes SigMap, a multimodal foundation model for wireless localization that combines a cycle-adaptive masked autoencoder for robust Channel State Information (CSI) representation learning with a "map-as-prompt" fine-tuning framework. The method leverages graph neural networks to encode 3D geographic topology into soft prompts that condition a frozen Transformer backbone. Experiments on ray-traced datasets demonstrate state-of-the-art localization accuracy across single- and multi-base station scenarios, alongside parameter-efficient cross-environment transfer.

## Strengths
- **Domain-Aware Self-Supervised Pre-training:** The cycle-adaptive masking strategy directly addresses a critical failure mode in wireless SSL: models exploiting hardware-induced periodic patterns as reconstruction shortcuts. Table 3 empirically validates that disrupting these periodic shifts yields consistent gains in localization accuracy over naive grid/strip masking.
- **Highly Parameter-Efficient Adaptation:** Translating 3D spatial topology into prompt tokens that condition frozen attention heads updates only ~0.7% of parameters. This design enables rapid adaptation (~30 min fine-tuning) and strong few-shot transfer to entirely new environments, aligning well with practical deployment constraints.
- **Rigorous Empirical Validation & Reproducibility:** The evaluation covers challenging NLoS conditions, uses standard localization metrics, and provides detailed hyperparameters, compute specifications, and an open pipeline, facilitating independent reproduction.

## Weaknesses
- **Contradictory Generalization Claims:** The abstract and introduction explicitly claim "strong zero-shot generalization in unseen environments," yet Section 4.5 clearly states that downstream heads are fine-tuned on ~100 labeled samples per scenario. This constitutes few-shot adaptation, not zero-shot. The mismatch misrepresents the model's capability and must be corrected throughout the manuscript.
- **Undescribed Architectural Component:** Equation 11 introduces an "NLoS-aware attention mechanism" in the Main Results section without any prior definition or integration details in the Methodology. Its inputs, training dynamics, and relationship to the base Transformer, prompt tokens, or multi-BS fusion head are entirely undefined, severely breaking methodological transparency and reproducibility.
- **Overextended "Foundation Model" Framing:** The self-supervised pre-training relies exclusively on a single simulated scenario (DeepMIMO O1_3p5) with fixed frequency bands and antenna configurations. True foundation models typically require multi-domain, heterogeneous pre-training data to learn distribution-agnostic representations. Pre-training on a single synthetic environment limits the claim of robust, scenario-agnostic representation learning and raises questions about representation transfer under distribution shifts (e.g., carrier frequency, array geometry).
- **Graph Prompt Scalability and Computational Intractability:** The geographic prompt generation constructs a GCN over a Delaunay triangulation of all 3D mesh vertices and base station positions (Sec 3.4). Real urban meshes routinely contain 10^4–10^6 vertices. The paper provides no vertex subsampling, spatial hashing, or computational scaling analysis, rendering the prompt generation step computationally ambiguous and likely infeasible for large-scale or real-time deployments.

## Nice-to-Haves
- Report standard deviations or confidence intervals across the 5 independent runs to establish the statistical significance of the reported accuracy improvements.
- Reconcile the input representation discrepancy between Eq. 5 (real/imaginary stacking) and Appendix B.2 (magnitude/phase decomposition) to ensure exact reproducibility of the cross-correlation periodicity detection.
- Include a direct comparison against standard Parameter-Efficient Fine-Tuning (PEFT) baselines (e.g., LoRA, Adapters) to isolate the unique contribution of GCN-based geographic prompts versus generic soft prompts.

## Novel Insights
The work demonstrates that the spatial-spectral-temporal structure of wireless channels fundamentally resists naive transposition of vision or NLP architectures. By showing that physical priors (building layouts, base station coordinates) can be distilled into minimal GCN-generated prompt tokens which condition a frozen signal encoder, the authors reveal a highly efficient paradigm for multimodal conditioning. This approach successfully leverages environmental topology to disambiguate complex multipath propagation, significantly reducing the labeled data burden while preserving geometric reasoning capabilities that pure signal encoders lack.

## Potentially Missed Related Work
- *Physics-Informed Neural Networks (PINNs) for Channel Estimation/Ray-Tracing:* Incorporating geometric optics or Maxwell-equation constraints directly into neural architectures could offer stronger theoretical grounding for the claimed LoS/NLoS disentanglement and improve generalization beyond pure data-driven graph encoding.

## Suggestions
1. Relocate and formally define the NLoS-aware attention mechanism (Eq. 11) in Section 3, explicitly detailing its forward pass, parameterization, and how it integrates with the prompt-conditioned token sequence.
2. Correct the "zero-shot" terminology to "few-shot" throughout the paper and add a sample-efficiency curve (e.g., 1, 10, 50, 100 tuning samples) to precisely characterize the adaptation boundary and validate the parameter-efficiency claim.
3. Introduce and evaluate a vertex simplification strategy (e.g., mesh quadric decimation, building-level centroid pooling, or spatial clustering) for the geographic graph encoder, and report the resulting node counts, prompt generation latency, and memory footprint to address real-world scalability.
