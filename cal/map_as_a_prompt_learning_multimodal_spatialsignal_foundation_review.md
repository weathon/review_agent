# Harsh Critic Review
## Section-by-Section Critical Review

**Title & Abstract**
The title accurately reflects the core technical components. However, the abstract claims "strong zero-shot generalization in unseen environments," which directly contradicts Section 4.5, where you explicitly state: *"only the downstream task heads are fine-tuned using limited target samples (approximately 100 instances per scenario)... This few-shot learning setup"*. ICLR reviewers are highly sensitive to terminology precision; zero-shot (no target-domain labels) and few-shot (minimal target-domain labels) are fundamentally different evaluation protocols. Please align the abstract with the actual experimental design or clearly separate zero-shot (if tested) from few-shot results.

**1. Introduction & Motivation**
The motivation for moving beyond supervised CSI-to-position mapping is well-articulated. The two identified gaps (signal periodicity shortcuts, superficial geographic integration) logically justify the proposed method. However, the positioning of LLMs in the literature review (Sec 1, paragraph 4) feels slightly misaligned. LLMs are indeed poor at low-level signal regression, but they are rarely positioned as direct competitors to physical-layer localization models. The real methodological gap lies more squarely between generic SSL/foundation models (e.g., LWM, WirelessGPT) and specialized localization models. Tightening the related work to focus on *modality-specific foundation models* rather than LLMs would strengthen the motivation.

**2 & 3. Preliminaries & Methodology**
The physical modeling and two-stage paradigm are conceptually sound, but several methodological details lack the precision required for reproducibility:
- **Cycle-Adaptive Masking (Sec 3.3, Eq 6):** The core claim is that the strategy "dynamically adjusts masking patterns based on channel periodicity characteristics." However, the mechanism for computing $d_{final}$ from cross-correlation is not specified in the main text. Appendix B.4 describes a shift generation algorithm with hand-tuned/sampled parameters ($d, N_a, N_s$), but it reads more like a structured data augmentation than a sample-adaptive mechanism. Is $d$ estimated *per-sample* from the input CSI? If so, how is the cross-correlation computed efficiently in the preprocessing pipeline? If it is randomly sampled during training to simulate periodic disruption, "adaptive" is misleading and should be renamed to "periodicity-aware structured masking." The exact definition of $d_{final}$ and its dynamic computation pathway must be clarified.
- **Geographic Prompt Generation (Sec 3.4, Alg 1):** The method constructs a Delaunay triangulation graph over all building vertices and BS positions, applies 2 GCN layers, and uses `GlobalMeanPool` to produce a *single* prompt vector $\mathbf{g}_{\text{prompt}}$. Urban 3D meshes contain tens of thousands of vertices. Collapsing this high-dimensional, spatially heterogeneous structure into a single $D$-dimensional token is a severe representational bottleneck. How does a single prompt token capture localized multipath interactions, occlusion patterns, and varying LoS/NLoS conditions across different user positions? A spatially distributed prompt mechanism (e.g., region-wise prompts or cross-attention over a pruned graph) would be more principled. This architectural choice needs justification or ablation.
- **Undefined Equation (Eq 11):** In Section 4.2, an "NLoS-aware attention mechanism" is introduced with Eq 11, featuring undefined terms $\phi$ and $\mathbf{o}_{s_i}$. This equation does not appear in the Methodology section, and its variables are never defined. If this is part of the multi-BS fusion head (Sec 3.5), it must be moved there and fully specified. If it's a post-hoc analysis or typo, it should be removed.

**4. Experiments & Results**
The experimental design addresses the right questions, but several claims are undermined by inconsistencies and missing context:
- **Baseline Coverage:** The main tables compare against OMP, CNN, SWiT, and LWLM. However, the introduction explicitly discusses CrowdBERT, signal-guided MAE, and other SSL frameworks. Why are these omitted from the quantitative comparison? ICLR expects baselines that match the method's paradigm (self-supervised/foundation). Omitting recent SSL localization models weakens the SOTA claim.
- **Numerical Contradictions (Sec 4.5 vs. Table):** The text states SIGMAP reaches "1.026 m MAE on DeepMIMO O2 and 1.580 m on WAIR-D Scenario-2," but the accompanying table lists WAIR-D MAE as **1.880 m**. Which value is correct? Furthermore, Table 3 shows Strip-masking achieving lower RMSE (0.972) than Adaptive masking (1.099), yet the text only claims "best trade-off" without analyzing the error distribution shift. RMSE is highly sensitive to outliers; a thorough analysis of tail errors (e.g., 95th percentile) would clarify whether adaptive masking truly improves robustness or just shifts bias.
- **Map Realism in Cross-Domain Tests (WAIR-D):** The WAIR-D dataset is built from OpenStreetMap, which provides 2D footprints and often lacks accurate 3D building heights or facade normals. How is the 3D mesh $M$ constructed for the prompt generator in this scenario? If heights are imputed or set to constants, the prompt encodes synthetic geometry rather than true environmental constraints. This is a critical practical limitation that must be disclosed, as it directly impacts the validity of the "cross-scenario generalization" claim.
- **Statistical Reporting:** Tables show single-point metrics. Given the inherent variance in wireless channel simulations and neural network training, reporting standard deviations or confidence intervals across the 5 runs mentioned in Sec 4.1 is necessary to confirm that the ~0.7m differences are statistically significant.

**5. Writing & Clarity / Reproducibility**
The paper is generally well-structured. The reproducibility statement is commendable, and the anonymous GitHub link is appropriate. However, the discrepancy between Eq 11 and Sec 3.5, the ambiguous masking adaptation mechanism, and the prompt pooling bottleneck currently hinder exact replication. The appendix correctly details hyperparameters, but the data preprocessing pipeline for graph construction and periodicity detection remains under-specified.

**6. Limitations & Broader Impact**
The paper briefly mentions future work (visual modalities, general wireless tasks) but lacks a dedicated limitations section. Key practical constraints are unaddressed:
- Dependence on accurate 3D maps (unavailable in many real-world deployments)
- Computational overhead of Delaunay triangulation for large-scale meshes
- Performance degradation under severe mobility (Doppler spread not modeled in static ray-tracing data)
A concise limitations paragraph would significantly strengthen the paper's maturity and align with ICLR's emphasis on honest self-assessment.

### Overall Assessment
This paper tackles an important problem in wireless localization with a clear two-stage foundation model design, combining structured masking with geographic prompt tuning. The empirical results are promising, showing significant gains over traditional and recent learning-based methods, particularly in parameter-efficient adaptation. However, the contribution is currently undermined by several critical issues that ICLR reviewers will prioritize: (1) contradictory claims around zero-shot vs. few-shot generalization; (2) an unclear "cycle-adaptive" masking mechanism that reads more like a fixed augmentation in practice; (3) a representational bottleneck in collapsing complex 3D environment graphs into a single prompt token; (4) numerical inconsistencies and undefined equations; and (5) missing comparisons against state-of-the-art SSL baselines and unaddressed map realism assumptions in cross-domain tests. With rigorous clarifications of the adaptive masking algorithm, justification or redesign of the prompt encoding mechanism, correction of numerical/reporting inconsistencies, and a more comprehensive baseline comparison, this work has strong potential for acceptance. In its current form, the empirical claims outpace the methodological transparency, requiring a major revision before meeting ICLR's standards.

# Neutral Reviewer
## Balanced Review

### Summary
The paper introduces SigMap, a foundation model for wireless localization that combines cycle-adaptive masked autoencoding for Channel State Information (CSI) pre-training with a parameter-efficient "map-as-prompt" fine-tuning mechanism. By dynamically masking periodic CSI patterns to prevent shortcut learning and injecting 3D geographic constraints via GNN-generated soft prompts, the approach achieves state-of-the-art localization accuracy and demonstrates strong few-shot adaptability across unseen environments.

### Strengths
1. **Domain-Aware Pre-Training Objective:** The cycle-adaptive masking strategy explicitly accounts for the intrinsic periodic correlations in CSI across antennas and subcarriers, directly addressing a known weakness of standard MAE in wireless domains. Table 3 provides clear empirical validation, showing that adaptive masking outperforms fixed grid/strip baselines in both MAE and CDF@1m.
2. **Parameter-Efficient Cross-Scenario Adaptation:** The geographic prompt tuning mechanism effectively bridges 3D environmental topology and signal representations without full backbone retraining. By updating only ~0.7% of parameters, fine-tuning completes in 30 minutes (Table 5), and the model maintains robust performance when transferred to entirely unseen DeepMIMO and WAIR-D scenarios (Table 4.5).
3. **Comprehensive Empirical Validation:** The paper includes well-structured ablation studies isolating the impact of map granularity (3D vs. 2D vs. no map in Table 4) and masking strategies. The evaluation spans single-BS, multi-BS, and cross-domain settings, providing a thorough assessment of accuracy, robustness, and computational trade-offs.

### Weaknesses
1. **Inaccurate Generalization Terminology:** The abstract and Section 1.2 claim "zero-shot generalization," yet Section 4.5 explicitly states that downstream task heads are fine-tuned on ~100 target samples per unseen scenario. In the ICLR context, conflating parameter-efficient few-shot adaptation with true zero-shot inference misrepresents the experimental setup and undermines reproducibility.
2. **Under-Specified Methodology and Displaced Components:** The computation of the periodicity shift `d_final` in Equation 6 is mentioned but not formally derived or algorithmically detailed in Section 3.3. Furthermore, the "NLoS-aware attention mechanism" (Equation 11) appears abruptly in the Results (Section 4.2) without prior definition in the Methodology, disrupting the logical flow and hindering independent reproduction.
3. **Limited Positioning Relative to Broader ML Literature:** While the approach is innovative for wireless localization, the prompt mechanism (GNN-encoded vectors prepended to a frozen Transformer) closely parallels existing soft prompt tuning and structure-aware adapters used in vision-language and graph-ML. The paper lacks comparisons to recent multimodal prompt-tuning or adapter baselines, making it difficult to isolate how much of the gain stems from the geographic prompting versus the domain-specific pre-training.

### Novelty & Significance
The paper makes a solid applied contribution by demonstrating how domain-specific inductive biases (channel periodicity and 3D spatial topology) can be integrated into foundation model design. The cycle-adaptive masking is a thoughtful adaptation of self-supervised objectives to physical signal structures, and the geographic prompt mechanism offers a practical pathway for rapid cross-environment deployment in 6G infrastructure. For ICLR, however, the methodological novelty leans toward effective engineering and system integration rather than fundamental advances in representation learning. The significance is high for the wireless/telecommunications community and moderately high for ML practitioners working on parameter-efficient multimodal adaptation of physical data. Broadening the framing to highlight transferable design principles for structured, spatiotemporal signals would better align with ICLR's core focus.

### Suggestions for Improvement
1. **Correct Terminology and Experimental Protocol:** Replace all instances of "zero-shot" with "few-shot" or "parameter-efficient adaptation." Clarify whether true zero-shot performance is possible (e.g., by evaluating the frozen backbone with prompts only) and add it as a baseline to strictly delineate generalization capabilities.
2. **Reorganize and Complete Methodology:** Move Equation 11 to Section 3.4 or 3.5, define its integration with the frozen Transformer attention, and provide a brief ablation on whether the NLoS attention or map prompts drive the main gains. Add explicit pseudocode or a mathematical definition for computing `d_final` via cross-correlation in Section 3.3/Appendix B.4.
3. **Strengthen Baselines and ML Context:** Compare the map-prompt approach against recent prompt-tuning/adapters from the vision/multimodal literature (e.g., MaPLe, Vor-Adapter, or standard continuous prompts) adapted to this setting. Discuss whether the geographic GNN encodes structural priors that are learnable or strictly geometric, and provide a sensitivity analysis on graph resolution/mesh simplification.
4. **Fix Referencing and Presentation Inconsistencies:** Section 4.4 incorrectly cites "Figure 1" when discussing map ablation visuals; update to the correct figure number. Standardize the formatting of Table 4.5, clarify the 2-D vs 3-D prompt generation pipeline, and ensure all hyperparameters (e.g., GNN layers, prompt token dimension `D_p`) are explicitly stated in the main text or Appendix B.2.

### Visualizations & Case Studies

3. Show side-by-side reconstruction heatmaps for samples where cycle-adaptive masking succeeds vs. fails, to reveal whether the mask dynamically targets physically meaningful high-frequency multipath components or merely alters data density.

### Obvious Next Steps
1. Report end-to-end inference latency and memory usage against CNN, SWiT, and LWLM on identical hardware, because parameter efficiency during fine-tuning does not equate to deployability if the transformer backbone or prompt generation introduces prohibitive runtime overhead.

# Final Consolidated Review
## Summary
SigMap proposes a two-stage foundation model for wireless localization, combining a cycle-adaptive masked autoencoder to learn robust Channel State Information (CSI) representations with a parameter-efficient "map-as-prompt" fine-tuning mechanism. By injecting 3D geographic topology via GNN-encoded soft prompts into a frozen Transformer backbone, the method achieves state-of-the-art positioning accuracy with minimal labeled data and demonstrates rapid adaptation to previously unseen environments.

## Strengths
- **Domain-Aware Pre-Training Objective:** The cycle-adaptive masking strategy explicitly targets the intrinsic periodic correlations in multi-antenna OFDM signals, directly mitigating the shortcut learning typical of standard random or grid masking. Table 3 provides clear empirical validation, showing that structured periodic disruption improves both mean localization error and precision (CDF@1m) over fixed baselines.
- **Highly Parameter-Efficient Cross-Scenario Adaptation:** The geographic prompt mechanism successfully bridges environmental topology and signal representations without full backbone retraining. By updating only ~0.7% of parameters, fine-tuning completes in ~30 minutes while maintaining robust transfer performance across entirely different DeepMIMO and WAIR-D scenarios, offering a practical pathway for rapid deployment.

## Weaknesses
- **Terminology vs. Experimental Protocol Mismatch:** The abstract and introduction claim "strong zero-shot generalization," yet Section 4.5 explicitly describes fine-tuning downstream task heads on ~100 labeled samples per unseen scenario. In machine learning, parameter-efficient few-shot adaptation and true zero-shot inference are distinct evaluation protocols. Conflating them misrepresents the model's actual zero-sample capabilities and risks overstating its generalization claims.
- **Under-Specified Methodology & Representational Bottleneck:** The "cycle-adaptive" masking mechanism lacks precise algorithmic clarity. While Section 3.3 claims dynamic adjustment based on per-sample cross-correlation, Appendix B.4 describes a shift-generation routine with sampled parameters that reads closer to structured data augmentation than genuine input-driven adaptation. Furthermore, the geographic prompt encodes complex urban meshes (tens of thousands of vertices) into a *single* prompt token via global mean pooling. Collapsing highly heterogeneous spatial-topological data into one low-dimensional vector creates a severe representational bottleneck, yet the paper provides no ablation or justification for why a single token suffices to resolve localized multipath interactions.
- **Presentation Inconsistencies & Missing Granular Analysis:** Equation 11 introduces an "NLoS-aware attention mechanism" abruptly in Section 4.2 without defining its variables ($\phi$, $\mathbf{o}_{s_i}$) or explaining its integration into the forward pass. Additionally, the text claims the multi-BS attention dynamically prioritizes stations based on signal quality, but provides no quantitative correlation to empirical metrics (e.g., path loss or received power) to verify this. All tables aggregate LoS and NLoS performance; disaggregated metrics are necessary to substantiate the paper's central motivation of overcoming NLoS ambiguity. Finally, Section 4.5 states a WAIR-D MAE of 1.580m in the text, while Table 4.5 reports 1.880m.

## Nice-to-Haves
- Reporting standard deviations or confidence intervals across the 5 independent training runs mentioned in Section 4.1 to contextualize whether reported marginal gains are statistically consistent.
- Adding a true zero-shot baseline (frozen backbone + geographic prompts, zero target-domain fine-tuning) to clearly separate inherent generalization capacity from few-shot parameter adaptation.
- Visualizing cross-attention maps between the geographic prompt token and CSI spectral-spatial patches to empirically verify that environmental constraints actively modulate channel feature extraction.
- Evaluating robustness to imperfect map registration (e.g., simulated GPS drift or missing building heights) to assess practical viability beyond idealized ray-tracing alignments.

## Novel Insights
The paper effectively bridges wireless propagation physics with modern foundation model design by recognizing that CSI cannot be treated as generic time-series or image data. The core insight is that self-supervised learning for RF signals must explicitly disrupt hardware-induced periodic shortcuts to force meaningful multipath reasoning, and that environmental topology can be distilled into a lightweight prompt that guides a frozen backbone to resolve spatial ambiguities without re-learning signal propagation laws. This demonstrates that structured, physics-informed inductive biases—applied during both pre-training and domain adaptation—are critical for sample-efficient, cross-environment deployment in physical-layer machine learning.

If it relies on pre-sampled or fixed parameters rather than true per-sample cross-correlation, rename the strategy (e.g., to "periodicity-aware structured masking") and adjust claims to reflect implemented behavior.
- **Define and Relocate Equation 11:** Move the attention formulation to Section 3.5, fully define all variables, and explicitly detail its role in the multi-BS fusion head. If it is a conceptual illustration rather than an implemented module, remove it or replace it with a clear architectural diagram.
