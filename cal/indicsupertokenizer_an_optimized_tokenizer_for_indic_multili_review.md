# Harsh Critic Review
## Section-by-Section Critical Review

### Title & Abstract
- **Title accuracy:** Accurately reflects the paper's scope (Indic multilingual LLM tokenization) and core contribution (an optimized, linguistically aware tokenizer).
- **Abstract clarity:** Clearly states the problem (morphological fragmentation in low/mid-resource languages), method (2-stage subword-superword learning + script-aware pre-tokenization), and key results (fertility improvements, throughput gain, maintained benchmark performance).
- **Unsupported/underspecified claims:** The abstract claims a `39.5%` improvement in average fertility over LLaMA-4 and an `18%` improvement over Sutra, but does not specify how these averages are computed (micro vs. macro, geometric mean, weighted by corpus frequency). Similarly, the `44%` inference throughput improvement claims lack context regarding batch size, sequence length distribution, and serving configuration. Table 5 shows Output Throughput (OTPT) increasing from ~118 to ~169 tokens/s, which aligns with the claim, but throughput is highly sensitive to KV-cache utilization and prompt composition. Without specifying the evaluation protocol, the absolute gain claim is difficult to independently verify.

### Introduction & Motivation
- **Problem motivation & gap:** Well-motivated. The fragmentation of Indic scripts under English-optimized BPE (e.g., LLaMA-4's 10.5 fertility for Odia in Table 3) is a legitimate efficiency and fairness bottleneck. The gap in systematic, multilingual tokenizer design for morphologically rich languages is clearly identified.
- **Contributions:** Explicitly listed. The 5 research questions effectively frame the ablation trajectory.
- **Over-claim/Under-sell:** The claim that this is `"[To] the best of our knowledge, we are the first to carry out a comprehensive benchmarking of a tokenizer across multiple intrinsic quality measures, as well as to study its downstream impact on task performance and LLM inference efficiency in both pretraining from scratch as well as continual pretraining settings"` (Section 1) is overreaching. Prior works (e.g., Dagan et al., 2024; Petrov et al., 2023; Schmidt et al., 2024) have performed similar multi-faceted evaluations. The novelty lies in the specific Indic focus, the two-stage curriculum adapted with sentence-level constraints, and the extensive data/vocab ablations. I recommend tempering the "first" claim to avoid unnecessary reviewer friction.

### Method / Approach
- **Clarity & reproducibility:** The two-stage training pipeline (Section 3.1) is conceptually clear and builds transparently on SuperBPE (Liu et al., 2025a). However, implementation details crucial for reproducibility are vague. Specifically, how is the `sentence-level boundary constraint` (Section 3.2) enforced during BPE merging? Standard HuggingFace/SentencePiece implementations do not natively pause merging at arbitrary delimiters. Did you artificially inject a special boundary token (`<eos>` or `<sep>`) into the training corpus, or did you modify the BPE priority queue to skip cross-sentence merges? This requires explicit clarification.
- **Assumptions & justification:** The assumption that 10GB of high-quality multilingual data is sufficient is supported by the plateau observed in Table 13. The transition point `t` is initially described as a token count but later treated as a percentage of vocabulary size (`90% t`). This shift in terminology (Section 3.1 vs. Section 5) creates minor confusion. Clarify whether `t` is a fixed merge count or a dynamic percentage of the target vocabulary budget.
- **Logical gaps / Edge cases:** The method relies heavily on frequent multi-word expressions ("superwords") to compress text. While effective for standard prose, this design may struggle with domain-shift data (e.g., formal mathematics, code-heavy prompts, or highly synthetic/templated text) where token co-occurrence distributions diverge from the training corpus. Additionally, how does the sentence-boundary constraint behave on poorly punctuated web text or conversational data? The paper does not address degradation modes when sentence boundaries are missing or noisy.

### Experiments & Results
- **Do experiments test claims?** Yes. Intrinsic metrics (Tables 3, 4, 6, 7) strongly support the fertility/efficiency claims. The latency/throughput experiment (Table 5) supports the deployment efficiency claim. Downstream tables (8, 11) test generalization.
- **Baselines & fairness:** Baselines are appropriate and span Indic-focused (Sutra, Sarvam) and general-purpose (Gemma-3, LLaMA-4, Qwen-3) tokenizers. Iso-compute pretraining (Appendix B.2) ensures fair comparison. However, the vocabulary sizes vary significantly (e.g., Sarvam at 256K vs. IST at 200K), which can intrinsically affect fertility and entropy. The paper controls for this via corpus-driven alignment, but explicitly acknowledging size-vs-efficiency trade-offs in the main text would strengthen fairness.
- **Missing ablations / Statistical rigor:** 
  - **Statistical significance:** Table 8 and Table 11 report downstream benchmark averages differing by ≤1%. In LLM evaluation literature, differences <1% are frequently within the noise of a single random seed. No error bars, standard deviations, or multi-seed evaluations are provided. ICLR standards typically require statistical validation when claiming "comparable" or "competitive" performance, especially for marginal gains (e.g., Indic average 0.394 vs 0.388).
  - **Throughput measurement:** Table 5 reports TTFT and OTPT but does not specify batch size, decode strategy (greedy vs. sampling), or KV-cache management. Throughput (`tokens/s`) is heavily influenced by these factors. A single averaged value across 200 samples obscures variance. Reporting a distribution or explicitly controlling for sequence length (since IST sequences are ~50% shorter per Table 21) is necessary.
  - **Task-specific impact of superwords:** Superwords compress common phrases but may hurt tasks requiring fine-grained subword reasoning (e.g., spelling, code syntax, or mathematical notation). Tables 8/11 only report macro averages. Granular results for Code/Math benchmarks would clarify whether the two-stage approach introduces hidden degradation.
- **Results vs. claims:** Results largely support claims, except for the statistical significance of downstream benchmarks. The "glitch token" analysis (Section 4.5, Figure 5) is a novel diagnostic but is somewhat tangential to the core tokenizer contribution.

### Writing & Clarity
- **Structure & Explanation:** The paper is well-organized and logically flows from motivation → method → intrinsic/extrinsic evaluation → ablations. The distinction between Stage 1 (subword) and Stage 2 (superword) is clearly explained.
- **Figures & Tables:** Tables 3, 4, 6, 7, and 13-16 are highly informative and effectively communicate intrinsic gains. Appendix Table 21 nicely contrasts input/output token distributions. However, the reference to `Figure 1` in Section 1 claims it shows "IST captures superwords... avoids fragmenting", but without a visible schematic or concrete tokenization traces in the figure, the visual claim is lost. Providing explicit tokenization boundary comparisons (e.g., `Tokenized by LLaMA-4: [वाक्य, वि, द्या]` vs `Tokenized by IST: [वाक्यविद्या]`) within the text would drastically improve clarity.

### Limitations & Broader Impact
- **Acknowledged limitations:** The paper explicitly addresses the latency overhead of morphological analyzers (Appendix C.2) and the mismatch between training loss and downstream accuracy (Appendix C.1). This shows good self-awareness.
- **Missed fundamental limitations:** 
  1. **Domain generalization:** Superwords are corpus-specific. If deployed on scientific, medical, or highly informal social media text, the fixed superword vocabulary may become irrelevant or cause segmentation inconsistencies.
  2. **Script/dialect coverage:** The 10GB training corpus and proportional vocab allocation (Table 10) still heavily skew toward high-resource scripts (Devanagari 23.25%, English 37.94%). Extremely low-resource dialects or code-switched text (Hinglish/Tanglish) may still suffer from under-allocation or unnatural fragmentation.
  3. **Continual Pretraining constraint:** Section 4.6 only retrains embeddings/LM heads. Full model finetuning or LoRA adaptation with IST could yield different performance curves, but this is unexplored.
- **Broader impact/failure modes:** The paper focuses on efficiency and fairness for low-resource languages but does not discuss potential negative impacts of fixed vocabulary distribution (e.g., if downstream developers fine-tune the model on code or mathematical text, the superword-heavy tail may become inefficient or degrade gradient flows).

### Overall Assessment
IndicSuperTokenizer presents a rigorous, well-executed study on optimizing subword tokenization for Indic languages. The combination of regex-aware pre-tokenization, NFKC normalization, and a two-stage subword-to-superword curriculum yields consistent, measurable gains in fertility, byte efficiency, and inference throughput across 22 languages. The extensive ablations on data size, transition points, vocabulary scaling, and merging strategies provide actionable insights for the broader multilingual modeling community. However, the contribution is incremental, building transparently on SuperBPE (Liu et al., 2025) rather than introducing a fundamentally novel algorithmic paradigm. The primary concerns preventing a stronger endorsement are: (1) the lack of statistical validation (multiple seeds/error bars) for downstream benchmark claims where margins are <1%, (2) ambiguous implementation details for the sentence-boundary constraint during BPE training, and (3) insufficient discussion of failure modes when the superword vocabulary encounters domain-shift or poorly punctuated text. If the authors can clarify the measurement protocols for throughput/latency, provide statistical rigor for extrinsic results, and explicitly detail the cross-sentence merge enforcement, this paper will meet ICLR's standards for sound, empirically validated resource contributions.

# Neutral Reviewer
## Balanced Review

### Summary
The paper introduces IndicSuperTokenizer (IST), a two-stage BPE-based tokenizer that sequentially learns subword units within word boundaries and cross-word "superwords," paired with Indic-aware regex pre-tokenization and NFKC normalization. Through extensive intrinsic metrics and extrinsic downstream evaluations, the authors demonstrate that IST achieves state-of-the-art fertility scores across 22 Indic languages and English, translating to ~44% higher inference throughput while maintaining competitive performance on standard benchmarks. The work provides systematic ablations on vocabulary allocation, transition points, and corpus size, offering a practical recipe for efficient multilingual tokenization.

### Strengths
1. **Comprehensive Benchmarking & Practical Evaluation:** The paper evaluates IST against 9 strong baselines using four distinct intrinsic metrics (fertility, NSL, Rényi efficiency, bytes-per-token) and complements this with extrinsic model training (from-scratch and continual pretraining) plus real-world latency/throughput measurements on H100 GPUs. This multi-faceted evaluation strongly aligns with ICLR's emphasis on empirical rigor and deployment relevance.
2. **Thorough Design Ablations:** The authors systematically isolate the impact of key hyperparameters and architectural choices, including one-stage vs. two-stage merging (vs. BoundlessBPE), corpus size scaling, subword/superword transition point, vocabulary scaling, and Unicode normalization. These ablations yield clear, actionable insights (e.g., 90% transition point and ~200K vocab are optimal, diminishing returns past 10GB data).
3. **Tangible Efficiency Gains Without Quality Degradation:** The reported ~44% improvement in output throughput (169.42 vs. 117.99 tokens/s) for a 1B model, alongside comparable downstream performance across English and Indic benchmarks, demonstrates a highly compelling accuracy-efficiency trade-off. This directly addresses a critical bottleneck in deploying multilingual LLMs for resource-constrained settings.
4. **Commitment to Reproducibility and Open Resources:** The explicit commitment to release the tokenizer, evaluation dataset, and modular benchmarking framework is a strong positive. Appendices provide detailed metric definitions, hyperparameters, and extended results, facilitating independent verification and future research.

### Weaknesses
1. **Incremental Algorithmic Novelty:** The core methodology builds directly on the two-stage "subword-then-superword" curriculum popularized by SuperBPE (Liu et al., 2025) and recent relaxations of pre-tokenization boundaries (BoundlessBPE). The primary contribution is domain-specific adaptation (Indic-aware regex, NFKC, curated data mix) rather than a novel tokenization algorithm. For ICLR, this places the paper more in the applied/empirical track, relying heavily on the strength of the evaluation to compensate.
2. **Limited Model Scale for Downstream Validation:** All extrinsic evaluation is confined to a 1B-parameter architecture. Tokenizer effects on KV-cache utilization, attention sparsity, and long-context retention often scale non-linearly. Claims about broad LLM inference efficiency would be substantially stronger if validated on at least one mid-scale model (3B–7B), as is now standard in tokenization/LLM papers.
3. **Ambiguity in Word Boundary Definition for Fertility:** Fertility (token-to-word ratio) is highly sensitive to how "words" are segmented, especially for languages with complex compounding, clitics, and varying whitespace conventions. The paper does not specify the ground-truth word segmentation tool or script-specific rules used during evaluation, which introduces uncertainty in cross-tokenizer fairness and reproducibility.
4. **Omission of Morphology-Driven Baselines:** While Appendix C.2 acknowledges morphological analyzers, the paper does not benchmark against or deeply compare with dedicated morphology-aware tokenizers for Indic languages (e.g., Morphtok, cited in references). A quantitative accuracy/latency Pareto comparison would better justify the design choice to avoid explicit morphological segmentation.

### Novelty & Significance
**Novelty:** Moderate. The method is an effective engineering synthesis and domain-specific optimization of existing two-stage BPE and regex pre-tokenization techniques. The novelty lies in the systematic adaptation to Indic linguistic structures and the comprehensive empirical validation rather than algorithmic invention.
**Significance:** High for the multilingual/low-resource NLP community. Efficient tokenization is a recognized bottleneck for equitable LLM deployment in Indic languages. Demonstrating substantial throughput gains with maintained task performance directly addresses cost and latency barriers, making the work highly relevant for both academic research and open-source ecosystem development.
**Clarity & Reproducibility:** High. The paper is well-organized, metrics are clearly defined, and results are presented in detailed tables. The methodology is straightforward to implement with existing libraries, and the promised artifact release will further solidify reproducibility. Minor clarity gaps exist regarding data curation details and table typos, but these do not obscure the core contributions.

### Suggestions for Improvement
1. **Expand Downstream Model Scale:** Include training and evaluation on at least one 3B-parameter model to verify that tokenizer efficiency gains and downstream performance hold at a scale where memory bandwidth and KV-cache limitations become more pronounced.
2. **Explicitly Define Word Segmentation for Fertility:** Document the exact tool, rules, or script-specific conventions used to compute ground-truth word counts in the evaluation corpus. If language-dependent segmenters were used, report how they impact cross-baseline fertility comparisons.
3. **Contextualize Against Morphology-Aware Tokenization:** Add a direct comparison or a detailed Pareto-analysis against morphology-grounded tokenizers (e.g., Morphtok). Quantify the trade-off between morphological fidelity, inference latency, and downstream accuracy to justify the chosen design space.
4. **Increase Data Curation Transparency:** Provide a more granular breakdown of the 10GB training corpus, including filtering criteria, deduplication methodology, language balancing strategy, and quality metrics. Tokenizer training is highly data-sensitive, and this transparency is critical for reproducibility.
5. **Address Minor Presentation Issues:** Correct the typo in Table 25 (`IST-Stage-1 / IST-Stage-1` should likely show Stage-2 in the second column). Standardize baseline nomenclature across tables (e.g., `GPT-oss` vs `GPT-OSS`). In the abstract, clarify how the claimed 39.5% and 18% improvements are computed (e.g., micro-average across all 24 languages, or weighted by corpus size?).

# Spark Finder Review
## How to Improve This Paper

### Missing Experiments (top 3-5 only)
1. Extrinsically evaluate downstream performance against Sutra and Gemma-3 under matched compute, not just LLaMA-4. The "comparable performance" claim is only validated against one baseline, so we cannot tell if IST's lower fertility sacrifices semantic representation quality compared to the actual best Indic baseline (Sutra).
2. Report training throughput and total FLOP efficiency, not just inference latency. The abstract explicitly claims "cheaper LLM training" and "training efficiency," but Section 4.3 only measures inference TTFT/OTPT; without wall-clock training speed or compute-per-token, the core efficiency value proposition is unverified.
3. Evaluate context extension or KV-cache savings enabled by the compression. Since IST reduces sequence length by ~55%, the primary architectural lever is fitting 2x context into the same memory; omitting long-context or memory-bandwidth benchmarks fails to prove the compression translates to practical system benefits.

### Deeper Analysis Needed (top 3-5 only)
1. Quantify cross-script vocabulary interference and low-resource coverage trade-offs. The corpus-driven allocation assumes no negative transfer, but without measuring shared byte collisions, token overlap rates, or performance degradation in low-resource scripts (e.g., Bodo, Santali), the fairness and robustness claims are speculative.
2. Rigorously validate the "higher loss, better accuracy" hypothesis. Section C.1 handwaves that superwords increase entropy and inflate per-token loss while preserving task performance, but lacks per-token perplexity breakdowns, calibration metrics, or attention analysis to prove this isn't merely optimization instability or noisy gradient signals.

### Visualizations & Case Studies
1. Provide token boundary overlays on code-switched (Hinglish) and noisy social media text. Clean examples in Figure 1 hide real-world failure modes; visualizing where the LLaMA-4 regex or superword merges incorrectly across languages, transliterations, or abbreviations directly exposes the practical limits of the approach.
2. Visualize the embedding distribution at the 90% transition boundary (subwords vs. superwords). A similarity heatmap or PCA projection would immediately reveal whether the two-stage curriculum learns coherent hierarchical semantic structures or merely clusters high-frequency statistical co-occurrences.
3. Plot fertility/vocabulary size ablations with confidence intervals across multiple random seeds or data subsamples. The current tables show single-point estimates; without variance, we cannot verify that the 10G data plateau and 200K vocab saturation are robust properties rather than artifacts of a specific data split.

### Obvious Next Steps
1. Release a unified, reproducible training pipeline for both stages. The appendix admits relying on fragmented HuggingFace and custom scripts due to framework limitations; without a clean, end-to-end implementation, the community cannot adopt the two-stage curriculum or validate the ablation recipes.
2. Validate the tokenizer and efficiency gains on a mid-scale model (≥3B parameters). A 1B model is widely considered too shallow at ICLR to generalize tokenizer impacts to complex multilingual semantics, reasoning, and scaling laws; skipping this leaves the findings confined to toy-scale settings.

# Final Consolidated Review
## Summary
This paper introduces IndicSuperTokenizer (IST), a BPE-based tokenizer designed for 22 Indic languages, English, and code. It employs a two-stage training curriculum that first learns subword units within whitespace constraints, then learns cross-word "superwords" while enforcing sentence-level boundary constraints, augmented by script-aware regex pre-tokenization and NFKC normalization. The authors claim state-of-the-art fertility scores, translating to ~44% higher inference throughput on a 1B model while maintaining downstream benchmark performance. The work provides extensive intrinsic metric evaluations and systematic ablations on vocabulary allocation, transition points, data size, and merging strategies.

## Strengths
- **Rigorous, multi-faceted empirical evaluation:** The paper evaluates IST against 9 strong baselines using four complementary intrinsic metrics (fertility, NSL, Rényi efficiency, bytes-per-token) and complements this with extrinsic validation via from-scratch pretraining and continual pretraining under iso-compute conditions. This aligns well with empirical standards for tokenizer research.
- **Systematic and actionable ablations:** The study cleanly isolates the impact of key design choices, including corpus-driven vs. explicit script merging, transition point scheduling, vocabulary scaling, and Unicode normalization. The findings (e.g., 90% transition point optimality, ~10GB data plateau, diminishing returns beyond 200K vocab) provide a clear, reproducible recipe for multilingual tokenizer development.
- **Tangible deployment relevance:** Demonstrating a ~44% improvement in output throughput (169.42 vs 117.99 tokens/s) on an H100 cluster without degrading downstream accuracy directly addresses critical latency and compute cost barriers for deploying Indic LLMs. The commitment to release the tokenizer, evaluation dataset, and modular benchmarking framework further strengthens community utility.

## Weaknesses
- **Unsubstantiated training efficiency claims & limited extrinsic baselines:** The introduction and motivation repeatedly frame efficiency gains in terms of "cheaper LLM training and inference," yet the paper only reports inference latency/throughput (Section 4.3). Wall-clock training time or total FLOP comparisons for the 50B token pretraining runs are absent, leaving the core training efficiency claim unverified. Additionally, extrinsic downstream results are only compared against LLaMA-4. Despite claiming SOTA fertility over Indic-focused tokenizers like Sutra and Sarvam, the paper does not benchmark downstream accuracy against them. This obscures whether IST's aggressive compression sacrifices semantic representation quality relative to the strongest actual Indics baselines.
- **Reproducibility gaps in implementation and metric definitions:** The mechanism for enforcing "sentence-level boundary constraints" during Stage 2 cross-word merging is unspecified. Standard BPE implementations do not naturally respect sentence boundaries without explicit masking or injected tokens; omitting this detail hinders independent reproduction. Furthermore, the fertility metric relies on a word-count denominator, but the paper never defines the ground-truth word segmentation tool or script-specific rules used for evaluation. For morphologically rich and agglutinative Indic languages, word boundaries are highly ambiguous, making cross-tokenizer fairness difficult to assess without explicit segmentation protocols.
- **Scale limitations and algorithmic incrementality:** All extrinsic evaluations are confined to a 1B parameter architecture. Tokenizer effects on attention sparsity, KV-cache memory footprint, and long-context retention often scale non-linearly, so findings may not generalize to the 3B–7B+ models now standard in the community. Algorithmically, IST is a careful engineering synthesis and domain-specific adaptation of the SuperBPE/BoundlessBPE curriculum rather than a novel algorithmic contribution; its significance rests entirely on empirical execution rather than methodological invention.

## Nice-to-Haves
- Provide statistical validation (multiple seeds or error bars) for downstream benchmark differences, particularly for the <1% average margin improvements.
- Include confidence intervals or variance estimates on ablation tables (e.g., data size scaling) to verify robustness against specific data subsamples.
- Visualize failure modes on code-switched (e.g., Hinglish) or poorly punctuated web text to assess how superword vocabularies degrade under domain shift or missing sentence delimiters.
- Fix the header typo in Table 25 (`IST-Stage-1 / IST-Stage-1` should read `IST-Stage-2`) and standardize baseline capitalization (e.g., `GPT-oss` vs `GPT-OSS`) across tables.

## Novel Insights
Beyond the paper's direct contributions, the analysis reveals a valuable structural insight: a two-stage curriculum that deliberately reserves the vocabulary tail for high-frequency multiword expressions naturally mitigates the "glitch token" problem that plagues long-tail subword learning. By anchoring morphemes in Stage 1 and allowing cross-word co-occurrences to fill the residual budget in Stage 2, the tokenizer avoids overfitting to rare, noisy character combinations while maintaining open-vocabulary coverage. Additionally, the empirical demonstration that corpus-driven vocabulary allocation consistently outperforms explicit, rule-stacked script merging challenges the modularity assumption in multilingual tokenizer design, suggesting that distributional alignment naturally resolves cross-script interference better than manual budget partitioning.

## Suggestions
1. Clarify the exact technical implementation of the sentence-boundary constraint during Stage 2 training (e.g., corpus-level document splitting, injected delimiter tokens, or priority-queue masking in the BPE merging step).
2. Explicitly define the word segmentation methodology used to compute fertility scores, particularly for scripts with complex compounding or variable whitespace conventions.
3. Report training wall-clock duration or compute-normalized throughput (FLOPs/token) to substantiate "training efficiency" claims, and add extrinsic downstream comparisons against at least one strong Indic baseline (e.g., Sutra or Gemma-3) under matched compute.
4. If feasible, validate the throughput gains and downstream performance on a mid-scale model (3B+) to demonstrate that compression benefits and vocabulary trade-offs hold where memory bandwidth and KV-cache constraints become pronounced.
