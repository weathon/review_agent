# Harsh Critic Review
## Section-by-Section Critical Review

### Title & Abstract
- **Accuracy & Clarity:** The title accurately captures the core methodological shift. The abstract clearly outlines the problem (spectral dynamics vs. temporal collapse), the proposed solution (STFT → video tensor → video diffusion), and the empirical outcome (SOTA on unconditional benchmarks).
- **Unsupported Claims:** The abstract claims the framework "establishes a new state-of-the-art in unconditional time series generation." While largely supported by Table 1, this claim is partially tempered by the substantial number of missing results (`–`) for the direct image-based competitor (ImagenTime). The abstract also states the paradigm has potential for "a broad spectrum of sequence modeling tasks beyond unconditional time-series generation"; while reasonable, this remains speculative without conditional experiments (e.g., forecasting/imputation), which are only mentioned as future work.

### Introduction & Motivation
- **Motivation & Gap Identification:** The dichotomy drawn between time-domain diffusion (lacking spectral inductive bias) and image-based diffusion (collapsing the temporal axis) is well-motivated and logically sound. The core question of preserving explicit time while leveraging frequency structure is clearly articulated.
- **Contributions:** The three contributions are accurate and match the paper's content. However, the positioning under-acknowledges the audio/speech generation literature. Mel-spectrogram diffusion (e.g., DiffWave, AudioLDM, WaveGrad) has successfully used spectro-temporal diffusion for waveform generation for several years. While multivariate time series differ fundamentally from audio, framing this as a completely novel paradigm without discussing why audio-style spectro-temporal diffusion cannot be directly adapted or why it fails for multivariate TS dilutes the novelty contribution for ICLR reviewers familiar with generative audio.
- **Over/Under-claiming:** Balanced. The authors appropriately limit claims to unconditional generation. The "paradigm shift" language is strong but acceptable given the structural re-framing.

### Method / Approach
- **Description & Reproducibility:** The pipeline is conceptually clear, but several implementation details critical for reproducibility and theoretical soundness are missing:
  1. **Auxiliary Loss Integration:** Section 5 introduces a "cross-covariance loss applied directly to the STFT magnitudes" to align generated spectra. The exact formulation, where it is applied in the denoising step, and the weighting coefficient $\lambda$ relative to the standard DDPM MSE loss are omitted. This makes it impossible to reproduce or understand its contribution.
  2. **Phase Consistency in iSTFT:** The model predicts real and imaginary STFT coefficients as independent channels in a standard diffusion process. Diffusion noise corruption is applied isotropically per channel, which can break the strict mathematical relationship between magnitude and phase. Predicting them independently via MSE may yield valid marginal distributions but introduce phase inconsistencies that cause iSTFT artifacts (phase wrapping errors, temporal blur). The authors should discuss phase constraints or justify why independent channel prediction does not degrade reconstruction.
  3. **Trend Resampling:** Section 4.1 states the trend (originally length $L$) is "resampled to match the temporal dimension $T$" for the video tensor, and Section 4.2 adds the generated trend back after iSTFT. The resampling method (linear interpolation? spline?) is unspecified. If $T \ll L$ (as seen in Table 4, e.g., $L=24 \rightarrow T=5$ for Sines), predicting the trend at coarse $T$ and upsampling back to $L$ inherently filters high-frequency trend variations and may misalign temporally with the residual component upon addition.
- **Assumptions & Edge Cases:** The EMA-based detrending assumes residuals become quasi-stationary enough for STFT. This is reasonable for ETTh/Energy but questionable for highly non-stationary regimes (e.g., financial crashes in Stocks or regime shifts in fMRI). No analysis of failure cases under abrupt non-stationarity is provided.
- **Logical Gaps:** The STFT uses 75% window overlap, meaning adjacent video frames are ~75% identical. Standard video diffusion architectures (factorized space-time attention) may degenerate into learning temporal identity mappings rather than meaningful spectral evolution, wasting capacity. The paper does not discuss how the attention mechanism or temporal embeddings are adjusted to handle this extreme inter-frame redundancy.

### Experiments & Results
- **Testing Claims & Baseline Fairness:** The experiments broadly test unconditional generation, but a major fairness issue exists in Table 1. ImagenTime is the primary competitor representing the "static image" baseline, yet it has missing results (`–`) for Context-FID, Correlational, and Predictive scores across almost all datasets. Comparing against baselines reported solely from their original papers without matching training protocols, evaluation scripts, or random seeds introduces confounding variance in diffusion benchmarks. ICLR expects either reproduced baselines on a unified codebase or a clear justification for why certain metrics could not be computed.
- **Missing Ablations:** Strong ablation studies are missing to validate the architectural claims:
  - **Video vs. Image vs. Time:** Is performance gain driven by the *video* structure itself, or by the auxiliary cross-covariance loss/bias matrices? An ablation removing the video structure (e.g., flattening $T$ into channels or treating $(F \times K)$ as static images) is essential.
  - **Component-wise:** Impact of $B_C$, $B_F$, anisotropic patching, and the EMA trend extraction. Without these, the "time-series-as-video" claim cannot be isolated from careful inductive bias engineering.
- **Statistical Significance & Error Bars:** Error bars are reported and appropriate. However, on the Sines dataset (Table 1), ST-Diff's Predictive Score (0.186) is significantly worse than Diffusion-TS (0.093). The paper does not discuss why the method struggles on this simple periodic benchmark despite excelling on complex datasets.
- **Scalability & Dataset Scope:** Long-sequence evaluation is restricted solely to ETTh. While results are strong (Table 2), claiming general scalability across high-dimensional or non-periodic data (e.g., Stocks, fMRI) at $L>24$ is unsupported. The compute scaling with $L$ is not reported (FLOPs, parameters, inference time per sample), which is crucial for ICLR given the known memory intensity of video diffusion.
- **Metrics:** The metric suite is standard and well-justified. The Context-FID uses pre-trained TS2Vec, which is appropriate, though the authors should confirm the embedding model is frozen during evaluation to avoid distributional shifts.

### Writing & Clarity
- **Clarity of Sections:** Sections 1–3 are well-written. Section 4.1's tensor construction and Section 5's implementation details lack precision regarding the trend resampling, loss weighting, and phase handling.
- **Figures & Tables:** Table 1 is comprehensive but hampered by missing ImagenTime data. Figure 4 (ACF/PSD) and Appendix Figures 6–7 effectively demonstrate spectral/temporal fidelity. The t-SNE/KDE plots (Fig 3) are informative but could benefit from a brief caption explaining what specific projection metric was used (e.g., standard Euclidean on raw values vs. learned embeddings).

### Limitations & Broader Impact
- **Acknowledged Limitations:** The authors correctly identify higher computational/memory costs and suggest latent diffusion or distillation as mitigation.
- **Missed Limitations:**
  1. **Irregular Sampling:** The STFT framework inherently assumes uniform sampling. Real-world TS (medical, industrial sensors) is often irregularly sampled. The method does not address interpolation artifacts prior to STFT.
  2. **Conditional Tasks:** The paper focuses on unconditional generation but notes forecasting/imputation as future work. Given the explicit temporal axis, the framework is theoretically well-suited for conditional generation via classifier guidance or partial input masking. Not exploring even a basic conditional variant weakens the demonstration of the "explicit temporal axis" advantage.
  3. **Phase/Temporal Artifacts:** As noted in Method, iSTFT phase sensitivity is a recognized limitation in spectrogram inversion that warrants discussion.
- **Societal Impact:** Minimal discussion on downstream risks. While not the focus, a brief note on misrepresentation in time-sensitive domains (e.g., financial simulation, medical synthetic data) would align with ICLR's broader impact expectations.

### Overall Assessment
This paper presents a compelling conceptual shift by treating multivariate time series as spectro-temporal videos to leverage spatiotemporal diffusion architectures. The method is intuitively motivated, and the empirical results on short-sequence unconditional generation are strong across multiple datasets, with convincing qualitative ACF/PSD evidence. However, the paper currently falls short of the ICLR acceptance bar due to significant methodological ambiguities and empirical gaps. Critical missing details include the formulation/weighting of the auxiliary covariance loss, the handling of STFT phase consistency, and the trend resampling protocol. The experimental section suffers from missing metrics for the key image-based baseline (ImagenTime) and lacks ablations to isolate whether gains stem from the video paradigm or the specific architectural biases/auxiliary losses. Additionally, the long-sequence scalability claim is only validated on a single dataset (ETTh), and compute/inference costs are not quantified against baselines. The contribution is promising and addresses a real gap in generative time series modeling, but acceptance requires rigorous ablation studies, clarification of the missing implementation details, fair reproduction/completion of baseline comparisons, and a more thorough discussion of phase consistency and temporal redundancy in the STFT video tensor.

# Neutral Reviewer
## Balanced Review

### Summary
This paper introduces Spectro-Temporal Diffusion (ST-Diff), a framework that reframes multivariate time series generation as a video generation task by converting sequences into 3D time-frequency video tensors via the Short-Time Fourier Transform (STFT). The authors design a custom spatiotemporal diffusion transformer with tri-axial attention and empirically initialized bias matrices to model the joint evolution of spectral content and cross-covariate dependencies over time. Extensive experiments across six benchmarks and sequence lengths up to 256 demonstrate that ST-Diff establishes a new state-of-the-art in unconditional generation, significantly outperforming existing time-domain and image-based diffusion models.

### Strengths
1. **Well-Motivated Representation Paradigm**: The paper clearly identifies a limitation in prior work: time-domain models often miss complex spectral dynamics, while image-based transforms collapse the explicit temporal axis. Mapping sequences to STFT-derived video tensors elegantly preserves both temporal continuity and frequency structure, enabling the direct use of modern spatiotemporal generative architectures.
2. **Strong and Comprehensive Empirical Validation**: ST-Diff achieves top performance on 21 of 24 metric–dataset combinations in Table 1, with particularly large margins on high-dimensional datasets (fMRI, Energy, MuJoCo). The inclusion of long-sequence experiments (L=64, 128, 256) in Table 2 shows robust scalability and stable Discriminative Scores, addressing a common weakness of sequence models that degrade over longer horizons.
3. **Thoughtful Architectural Biases**: The design choices are well-aligned with the data structure. Anisotropic patching preserves unit granularity along the unordered covariate axis; empirically initialized covariate and frequency bias matrices inject meaningful priors; and the separation of RoPE (for ordered time/frequency) vs. learnable embeddings (for unordered covariates) demonstrates careful consideration of inductive biases. Qualitative analyses (ACF, PSD, t-SNE, KDE) consistently validate the quantitative metrics.

### Weaknesses
1. **Absence of Critical Ablation Studies**: The paper does not isolate the source of performance gains. It is unclear how much improvement stems from the video representation itself versus the custom attention design, the initialized bias matrices, trend-residual decomposition, or the auxiliary cross-covariance loss. Comparisons against a standard off-the-shelf video DiT or a 2D image diffusion model trained on the same STFT tensors are missing.
2. **Under-Specified Auxiliary Loss**: The cross-covariance loss on STFT magnitudes is mentioned in Section 5 ("Implementation Details") but lacks a formal mathematical definition, weighting scheme, or gradient formulation. Without this, the contribution of the loss cannot be verified or reproduced, and its necessity remains unproven.
3. **Unexplained Performance Degradation on Simple Data**: On the synthetic Sines dataset, ST-Diff achieves a Predictive Score of 0.186 ± 0.004, which is nearly double that of all baselines (~0.093). Since Sines contains simple, highly periodic signals, this anomaly suggests potential issues with trend decomposition, STFT windowing, or model over-parameterization that are neither investigated nor discussed.
4. **Lack of Computational Efficiency Metrics**: While the conclusion acknowledges higher computational costs, the paper provides no quantitative comparison of parameter counts, training time, memory footprint, or inference latency relative to baselines. ICLR increasingly expects efficiency analysis to contextualize SOTA gains and assess practical viability.
5. **Incomplete Baseline Comparisons**: ImagenTime reports missing values ("–") across all metrics in Table 1. Relying partially on results from original publications without reproducing them under identical data splits, preprocessing, and evaluation pipelines weakens the fairness of the comparison, particularly given the sensitivity of generative metrics to evaluation setup.

### Novelty & Significance
The core novelty lies in systematically adapting video diffusion models to general multivariate time series via an explicit time-frequency tensor representation. While STFT-based generation is mature in audio processing, its application to general multivariate sequences with a dedicated spatiotemporal diffusion backbone, covariate-aware attention, and empirical bias initialization is a meaningful and timely contribution. The work effectively bridges classical signal processing with modern generative modeling, offering a scalable alternative to both time-domain and static-image paradigms. Significance is moderate-to-high for the time series community, particularly for domains where spectral-temporal co-evolution is critical. However, the conceptual step is incremental relative to spectrogram-based audio diffusion and recent pure frequency-domain generative models (e.g., Crabbe et al., 2024), and the paper would benefit from sharper positioning against these methods.

### Suggestions for Improvement
1. **Conduct Targeted Ablations**: Quantify the individual and combined contributions of: (a) the STFT video representation vs. raw time-series or static-image baselines run with the same architecture; (b) the empirically initialized bias matrices vs. standard zero-initialized attention; (c) trend-residual decomposition; and (d) the cross-covariance auxiliary loss.
2. **Formalize the Auxiliary Loss**: Provide the exact mathematical definition, loss weighting coefficient, optimization schedule, and an ablation study demonstrating its impact on spectral (PSD) and correlational metrics.
3. **Investigate the Sines Anomaly**: Diagnose why predictive performance degrades on highly periodic data. Test whether alternative decomposition strategies, STFT hyperparameters, or simplified architectures recover baseline-level performance, and report findings transparently.
4. **Report Computational Trade-offs**: Add a table comparing parameter counts, FLOPs, training hours, GPU memory usage, and inference time per sample across ST-Diff and baselines. Discuss whether the fidelity gains justify the computational overhead for typical use cases.
5. **Ensure Baseline Fairness**: Re-run key baselines (especially ImagenTime and Diffusion-TS) using the exact same data splits, preprocessing, and evaluation scripts. If reproduction is infeasible, clearly document the protocol differences and justify how comparisons remain valid.
6. **Clarify Positioning vs. Frequency-Domain Methods**: Expand Section 2 to explicitly contrast ST-Diff with full frequency-domain diffusion approaches. Clarify why joint time-frequency modeling (video) is theoretically and empirically preferable to modeling frequency coefficients alone, particularly for non-stationary or multi-scale signals.

# Spark Finder Review
## How to Improve This Paper

### Missing Experiments (top 3-5 only)
1. **Direct comparison to Image-Diffusion on identical metrics:** The paper's core claim is that explicit temporal preservation in a video tensor outperforms static image representations, yet Table 1 reports “–” for ImagenTime across all short-sequence metrics. You must include ImagenTime results (trained and evaluated under identical conditions) to isolate whether gains come from the video paradigm or unrelated architectural/regularization differences.
2. **Systematic architectural ablations:** The specific contributions of the EMA trend channel, anisotropic frequency-only patching, learnable covariate/frequency bias matrices, and the auxiliary cross-covariance loss are unverified. Disable or replace each module with standard video diffusion components to prove the SOTA results actually stem from your design rather than the backbone alone.
3. **Quantitative compute and memory benchmarking:** The conclusion acknowledges higher cost but provides zero empirical measurements. Report parameters, peak VRAM, training FLOPs, and per-step inference time versus Diffusion-TS and ImagenTime to prove the marginal fidelity improvements justify adopting a computationally heavy video diffusion framework.

### Deeper Analysis Needed (top 3-5 only)
1. **Phase consistency and iSTFT reconstruction error:** Diffusion models predicting independent real and imaginary channels frequently violate STFT conjugate symmetry, introducing temporal artifacts upon inversion. Quantify the iSTFT reconstruction error or phase mismatch against ground-truth spectrograms to verify your pipeline doesn't mask temporal degradation that average ACF/PSD plots hide.
2. **Normalization strategy and data leakage audit:** The EMA trend extraction and channel normalization pipeline is underspecified, raising leakage concerns if statistics are computed across train/validation/test splits or globally per dataset. Explicitly document the split-aware normalization procedure; inadvertent leakage into the EMA baseline or scaling factors would artificially suppress discriminative and predictive scores.
3. **Statistical significance across random seeds:** Standard improvements over Diffusion-TS (e.g., Correlational Score on fMRI, Predictive Score on Sines) overlap heavily with reported standard deviations. Perform paired significance testing (e.g., paired t-test or Wilcoxon over 5+ seeds) to confirm the claimed SOTA status isn't a high-variance artifact of the stochastic diffusion sampler.

### Visualizations & Case Studies
1. **Individual raw trajectory comparisons:** Aggregate metrics like ACF and PSD average out phase jitter, blurring, and discontinuities. Plot 5-10 aligned real and generated multivariate trajectories per dataset to visually confirm the iSTFT inversion yields sharp, physically coherent temporal transitions rather than smoothed or artifact-heavy samples.
2. **Cross-axis attention weight visualization:** To validate that the custom architecture genuinely learns spectro-temporal dynamics, visualize attention probability distributions between temporal patches, frequency bins, and covariate tokens during generation. Flat or collapsed attention patterns would indicate the model ignores the proposed inductive biases and relies on generic denoising.
3. **Failure-case STFT spectrogram analysis:** Select generated samples with the highest discriminative classifier scores and plot their raw time-frequency tensors. Highlighting systematic spectral smearing or temporal misalignment in failure cases would transparently expose the current limits of the video diffusion paradigm.

### Obvious Next Steps
1. **Extend long-sequence evaluation beyond ETTh:** Claiming "superior scalability" based on a single 7-covariate dataset is insufficient for ICLR standards. Evaluate sequence lengths 64–256 on at least one high-dimensional dataset (MuJoCo or fMRI) to prove the video representation does not suffer exponential context-window collapse or memory bottlenecks.
2. **Demonstrate conditional generation capability:** The introduction positions the paradigm as foundational for forecasting, imputation, and anomaly detection, but the paper stops at unconditional generation. Implement and benchmark a minimal masking-based imputation or short-horizon forecasting task to prove conditioning on partial STFT-video inputs remains computationally and empirically viable.

# Final Consolidated Review
## Summary
This paper introduces ST-Diff, a framework that reframes multivariate time series generation as a video generation task. By applying the Short-Time Fourier Transform (STFT) and trend-residual decomposition, time series are mapped into 3D spectro-temporal tensors (time, frequency, covariates). A custom video diffusion transformer with domain-specific inductive biases (anisotropic patching, empirically initialized attention biases, axis-specific positional embeddings) is trained to generate these tensors, which are subsequently inverted via iSTFT. The authors report state-of-the-art unconditional generation performance across standard short-sequence benchmarks and demonstrate stability on longer sequences for the ETTh dataset.

## Strengths
- **Conceptually Sound & Well-Motivated Paradigm**: The reframing directly addresses a structural gap in prior work: time-domain diffusion models lack explicit spectral inductive biases, while static image transforms collapse the temporal axis. Preserving explicit temporal evolution while exposing joint frequency-covariate structure provides a strong theoretical foundation for modeling non-stationary, multi-scale dynamics.
- **Strong Empirical Performance on Complex Data**: ST-Diff consistently outperforms established baselines across six datasets, achieving superior scores on 21 of 24 metric-dataset combinations. Gains are particularly pronounced on high-dimensional, real-world datasets (fMRI, Energy, MuJoCo), and the model maintains stable, low discriminative scores as sequence length increases (tested up to $L=256$ on ETTh).
- **Thoughtful Architectural Inductive Biases**: Rather than naively applying off-the-shelf video diffusion, the authors design components aligned with time series structure. Anisotropic patching prevents artificial spatial correlations across variables, RoPE is correctly applied to the ordered time/frequency axes while unordered covariates receive learnable embeddings, and empirically initialized bias matrices inject meaningful priors over spectral and cross-covariate dependencies.

## Weaknesses
- **Complete Lack of Isolating Ablations**: The paper conflates the proposed "time-series-as-video" representation with extensive architectural engineering. Without ablations, it is impossible to determine whether performance gains stem from the video tensor structure itself, the custom attention factorization, the initialized bias matrices, the EMA trend decomposition, or the auxiliary cross-covariance loss. A direct comparison against a standard Video DiT or a 2D image diffusion model operating on the exact same STFT tensors is critically missing.
- **Incomplete Baseline Comparisons & Unsubstantiated Scalability Claims**: Table 1 reports missing results (`–`) for ImagenTime across Context-FID, Correlational, and Predictive scores. Since ImagenTime is the primary representative of the static-image paradigm that ST-Diff aims to surpass, these gaps fundamentally undermine the core empirical argument. Furthermore, long-sequence scalability ($L=64$ to $256$) is exclusively validated on ETTh; extrapolating these results to high-dimensional or non-periodic series is unsupported.
- **Methodological Ambiguities & Potential iSTFT Artifacts**: The framework predicts real and imaginary STFT coefficients as independent channels under isotropic Gaussian noise. This approach disregards the strict mathematical phase-magnitude coupling inherent in time-frequency representations, risking phase inconsistencies that manifest as temporal blurring or discontinuities upon iSTFT reconstruction. Additionally, the auxiliary cross-covariance loss introduced in Section 5 lacks a mathematical formulation, loss weighting coefficient ($\lambda$), or optimization schedule, blocking reproducibility.
- **Unexplained Regressions & Missing Efficiency Analysis**: On the simple, highly periodic Sines dataset, ST-Diff's Predictive Score (0.186) is nearly double that of all baselines ($\sim$0.093), indicating potential over-parameterization, trend-misalignment, or spectral windowing failures that are neither diagnosed nor discussed. While the conclusion acknowledges higher computational costs, the paper provides zero quantitative metrics (FLOPs, peak VRAM, training hours, or inference latency) to contextualize whether the marginal fidelity gains justify the heavy overhead of video diffusion architectures.

## Nice-to-Haves
- Diagnostic analysis of the Sines dataset anomaly to determine if performance degradation stems from EMA trend artifacts or STFT windowing mismatches on pure sinusoids.
- Extension of long-sequence evaluation to at least one high-dimensional dataset (e.g., MuJoCo or fMRI) to validate memory/compute behavior beyond ETTh.
- Visualization of learned attention weights across the temporal, frequency, and covariate axes to empirically verify that the model leverages the proposed inductive biases rather than relying on generic denoising capacity.

## Novel Insights
The paper's most compelling observation is that multivariate time series are structurally isomorphic to video data when observed through a joint time-frequency lens. Unlike univariate audio generation where spectrograms are treated as single-channel spatial maps, multivariate series naturally map covariates to spatial channels that co-evolve non-linearly in frequency space over time. By rigorously decoupling the unordered covariate axis from the ordered time-frequency axes, ST-Diff demonstrates that generative modeling of temporal dynamics benefits fundamentally from disentangling these structural priors. The success of empirically initialized attention biases further indicates that cross-variable and spectral correlations in real-world time series are highly structured, making them amenable to static prior injection rather than purely data-driven discovery.

## Potentially Missed Related Work
- **Modern Spectrogram-Based Diffusion Models (e.g., DiffWave, AudioLDM)**: The paper briefly cites Shen et al. (2018) but does not deeply engage with contemporary audio generation literature that already heavily relies on mel-spectrogram/video diffusion pipelines. The novelty of applying this to general multivariate time series is diminished without explicitly detailing why standard audio diffusion frameworks fail or require substantial modification (e.g., lack of uniform sampling rates, absence of phonetic priors, complex cross-channel dependencies).

## Suggestions
1. **Implement Rigorous Component-Wise Ablations**: Systematically ablate the empirical bias matrices, anisotropic patching, trend decomposition, and auxiliary loss. Crucially, benchmark against a standard Video DiT or a flattened 2D diffusion model trained on identical STFT tensors to isolate the true contribution of the "video" representation.
2. **Reproduce Baselines and Complete Evaluation Tables**: Re-run ImagenTime (and other baselines) using identical data splits, preprocessing, and evaluation scripts to fill the missing entries in Table 1. This is non-negotiable for claiming a paradigm shift over static-image methods.
3. **Formalize Methodology & Provide Compute Benchmarks**: Explicitly define the auxiliary cross-covariance loss formulation and its weighting. Address phase consistency constraints for iSTFT or quantify reconstruction error. Add a table comparing parameter counts, peak VRAM, and inference latency against baselines to justify the computational trade-offs.
