# Harsh Critic Review
## Section-by-Section Critical Review

### Title & Abstract
- **Does the title accurately reflect the contribution?** Yes. It clearly highlights the core mechanism (scores learning geometry) and the theoretical tool (rate separations under the manifold hypothesis).
- **Does the abstract clearly state the problem, method, and key results?** Yes. It identifies the difficulty of low-$\sigma$ score estimation, proposes the scale separation insight, introduces the Tempered Score (TS) modification, and states three concrete theoretical consequences with preliminary empirical validation.
- **Are any claims in the abstract unsupported by the paper?** The claim that the framework "provably tolerates $O(\sigma^{-2})$ larger errors" is well-supported by Theorems 4.1 and 5.2. The mention of "large-scale models, including Stable Diffusion" is accurate but represents a proof-of-concept rather than a comprehensive large-scale evaluation, which the authors transparently label as "preliminary." No unsupported claims detected.

### Introduction & Motivation
- **Is the problem well-motivated? Is the gap in prior work clearly identified?** Strongly motivated. The introduction correctly pinpoints the tension between the theoretical difficulty of accurate small-$\sigma$ score estimation and the empirical success of diffusion models. The gap is clearly delineated: prior work identifies manifold concentration but fails to quantify the exact rate separation between geometric recovery ($\Theta(\sigma^{-2})$) and density recovery ($\Theta(1)$).
- **Are the contributions clearly stated and accurate?** Yes. The three numbered contributions map directly to Sections 4, 5, and 6. The distinction between recovering *any* distribution on the manifold vs. the *uniform* distribution is sharply drawn and theoretically grounded.
- **Does the introduction over-claim or under-sell?** The rhetoric "paradigm shift" is standard for framing; the technical claims are carefully bounded by explicit assumptions ($L_\infty$ error, compact $C^4$ manifold, etc.). The introduction accurately represents the scope of the results.

### Method / Approach
- **Is the method clearly described and reproducible?** Yes. Equation (8) defines TS Langevin dynamics in one line: scaling the score by $\sigma^\alpha$. Algorithmic application to Predictor-Corrector samplers (Section 6, Section 7.2) is explicit and easily reproducible.
- **Are key assumptions stated and justified?** Assumptions are clearly stated: Manifold Hypothesis (2.1), density regularity (2.2), compactness/path-connectedness of support (4.1), and $L_\infty$ score error. The justification for these is standard in geometric ML theory.
- **Are there logical gaps in the derivation or reasoning?** A notable theoretical leap occurs in **Theorem 5.2** (General Non-Gradient Score Oracle) and **Assumption B.2**. The proof assumes the stationary distribution of the non-reversible SDE admits a local WKB expansion and that the SDE admits a *unique* stationary distribution. While standard in asymptotic SDE analysis, these are non-trivial assumptions for drift fields parameterized by neural networks, which do not generally guarantee gradient structure or uniqueness of invariant measures. The derivation in Appendix B.4 relies heavily on balancing orders of $\theta$ in the Fokker-Planck equation and applying the strong maximum principle to a parabolic PDE on $M$ (Eq. 16–17). The mathematical steps are rigorous under the ansatz, but the validity of the WKB ansatz itself for high-dimensional, highly expressive neural network scores remains an open assumption rather than a proven property.
- **Are there edge cases or failure modes not discussed?** The theory is asymptotic ($\sigma \to 0$). Practical diffusion models operate at a minimum noise level $\sigma_{\min} > 0$. While the corrector step operates at small $\sigma$, the paper does not explicitly discuss how the $o(\sigma^{-2})$ guarantees degrade or translate when the schedule stops at fixed $\sigma_{\min}$. Additionally, the analysis assumes the score error dominates discretization error, but real-world sampling combines continuous-time SDE limits with discrete Euler-Maruyama steps.

### Experiments & Results
- **Do the experiments actually test the paper's claims?** The synthetic ellipse/circle experiments (Fig. 2, Fig. 4) directly validate the central claim: standard Langevin with $O(1)$ error recovers arbitrary densities, while TS with the same error reliably recovers the uniform distribution. This is strong evidence for the theory. The Stable Diffusion experiments test whether TS improves diversity/quality, which aligns with the geometric learning hypothesis.
- **Are baselines appropriate and fairly compared?** Yes. Comparisons against DDPM and the standard Predictor-Corrector (PC) sampler are appropriate. Hyperparameter tuning for baselines is mentioned to ensure fairness.
- **Are there missing ablations that would materially change conclusions?** The paper lacks an ablation on $\sigma_{\min}$ or different noise schedules, which would bridge the $\sigma \to 0$ theory to practical finite-noise implementations. Additionally, comparing against other uniform sampling baselines (e.g., De Santi et al., 2025 fine-tuning approach) would contextualize the performance gains.
- **Are error bars / statistical significance reported?** No. Tables 1–4 report single deterministic values across 512 samples. Given the modest effect sizes (e.g., P-sim improvements of $\sim 0.3$–$1.0$, I-sim reductions of $\sim 0.2$–$0.5$), reporting variance or confidence intervals is critical to establish that the gains are not statistical noise.
- **Do the results support the claims made, or are they cherry-picked?** Results are reported across multiple prompts, corrector steps, and $\alpha$ values, suggesting robustness. However, the use of CLIP-based proxies (P-sim, I-sim) instead of standard distributional metrics (FID, Precision/Recall, or human evaluation) weakens the empirical claim of improved sample quality and diversity. CLIP scores correlate poorly with human-perceived realism and can be biased by prompt phrasing.
- **Are datasets and evaluation metrics appropriate?** Synthetic manifolds are perfectly appropriate for theory validation. For image generation, CLIP similarity is a weak proxy for the manifold geometry claims. Without FID or manifold-aware metrics, it is difficult to verify that the model is actually sampling more uniformly from the underlying data manifold rather than simply escaping mode collapse or exploiting CLIP feature geometry.

### Writing & Clarity
- **Are there sections that are confusing or poorly explained?** The main text is exceptionally well-organized. However, **Appendix B.4** is highly dense. The transition from the $\theta^1$ order equation to the local coordinate PDE (Eq. 16) and the subsequent application of the strong maximum principle to conclude $\tilde{c}_0$ is constant requires careful reading. A brief intuitive bridge in the main text explaining *why* the transport equation forces constancy along gradient flows, leaving normal directions to be resolved by the next-order elliptic/parabolic PDE, would significantly improve accessibility without sacrificing rigor.
- **Are figures and tables clear and informative?** Yes. Figure 1 provides a clear conceptual illustration. Figure 2 and 4 effectively show density concentration vs. uniformity. Tables 1–4 are cleanly formatted. The only minor issue is that Figure 3 lacks quantitative captions linking specific hyperparameters to the visualized outputs, but this is acceptable for qualitative illustration.

### Limitations & Broader Impact
- **Do the authors acknowledge the key limitations?** Yes. The paper explicitly lists: (a) lack of cumulative error tracking along trajectories, (b) mismatch between theoretical $L_\infty$ bounds and practical $L_2$ DSM objectives, (c) lack of statistical sample complexity bounds, (d) continuous-time vs. discretization gap, and (e) preliminary experimental scale.
- **Are there fundamental limitations they missed?** The reliance on the assumption of a unique stationary distribution for non-reversible dynamics with NN drifts is a theoretical constraint that should be elevated in the main limitations. Additionally, the computational cost of TS Langevin: scaling the score changes the spectral properties of the drift, which could affect stiffness. While Appendix D suggests mixing can improve, practical step-size constraints for stability in the small-$\sigma$ regime are not discussed.
- **Are there failure modes or negative societal impacts not discussed?** Standard for theoretical work. No direct negative impacts highlighted. A brief note on the computational overhead or potential sampling slowdown at very high $\alpha$ would be practical.

### Overall Assessment
This is a theoretically rigorous and insightful paper that makes a compelling case for reframing score-based generative modeling as a geometric learning problem first, and a distributional learning problem second. The identification of the $\sigma^{-2}$ vs. $\Theta(1)$ scale separation (Eq. 6) is a clean, non-trivial contribution that elegantly explains empirical phenomena. The proposed Tempered Score Langevin dynamics (Eq. 8) is simple, theoretically grounded, and empirically promising. The primary concerns lie in the empirical validation and theoretical assumptions: the reliance on the WKB ansatz and uniqueness of stationary distributions for non-gradient neural network drifts is a non-trivial mathematical assumption that requires careful contextualization. Furthermore, the experimental section, while directionally supportive, lacks statistical rigor (no error bars, modest effect sizes) and relies on CLIP proxy metrics rather than standard generative benchmarks (FID, human eval). The theory is asymptotic ($\sigma \to 0$), while practical implementations stop at $\sigma_{\min} > 0$; bridging this gap analytically or empirically would strengthen the work. Despite these points, the mathematical novelty, clear theoretical contributions, and practical simplicity of the proposed method align well with ICLR's appreciation for rigorous, insightful theory that informs practice. The paper is strong and warrants acceptance, provided the authors address the statistical reporting of experiments and clarify the scope/validity of the WKB/stationarity assumptions in a rebuttal.

# Neutral Reviewer
## Balanced Review

### Summary
This paper establishes a sharp rate separation in score-based models under the manifold hypothesis, demonstrating that geometric information (distance to the data manifold) scales as $\Theta(\sigma^{-2})$ while distributional density information scales as $\Theta(1)$. Leveraging this insight, the authors propose Tempered Score (TS) Langevin dynamics, a simple one-line algorithmic modification that provably recovers the uniform distribution on the manifold under a relaxed $o(\sigma^{-2})$ score error condition. The framework is used to explain the empirical success of diffusion models, improve sampling diversity, and show that uniform priors in Bayesian inverse problems are significantly more robust to score approximation errors.

### Strengths
1. **Rigorous Quantification of Scale Separation:** The core theoretical insight (Theorem 3.1 and Section 4) cleanly separates geometric and density learning by explicitly quantifying their asymptotic orders in $\sigma$. This provides a formal, quantitative explanation for why diffusion models successfully capture data geometry even with imperfect scores, advancing beyond prior qualitative or restricted subspace analyses cited in Section 1.1.
2. **Elegant & Mathematically Non-Trivial Algorithm:** The proposed TS dynamics (Equation 8) is remarkably simple to implement yet supported by substantial theoretical machinery (WKB expansion, Fokker-Planck analysis, and the strong maximum principle in Appendix B.4). Proving convergence to the Riemannian uniform measure under relaxed error bounds (Theorems 5.1 and 5.2) is a strong contribution with immediate practical applicability.
3. **Broad Theoretical & Practical Applicability:** The rate separation is successfully leveraged across multiple domains: explaining generative modeling behavior, enabling robust uniform sampling for geometric tasks, and proving that maximum entropy priors in Bayesian inverse problems require only $o(\sigma^{-2})$ accuracy (Theorem 6.1). The Appendix D analysis of Poincaré constants and mixing times further demonstrates that TS Langevin can provably converge faster than standard Langevin in multimodal settings.

### Weaknesses
1. **Mismatch Between Theoretical Error Norms and Practical Training:** The main guarantees rely on worst-case $L_\infty$ score error bounds (e.g., Assumption 4.1, Theorem 4.1/5.2), whereas practical diffusion training optimizes Fisher divergence ($L_2$-type error). As noted in the limitations, extending the theory to high-probability or $L_2$ guarantees remains open. This gap is significant for ICLR, as pointwise uniform control of neural network scores in high dimensions is notoriously difficult to verify empirically.
2. **Preliminary & Dated Empirical Validation:** The real-world experiments are limited to Stable Diffusion 1.5 and rely on CLIP-based proxy metrics (P-sim, I-sim) rather than standard generative benchmarks (FID, Precision-Recall, or human evaluation). For a venue like ICLR, validation on modern architectures (e.g., DiT or SDXL) with widely accepted quantitative metrics is expected to substantiate claims of improved sample quality and diversity.
3. **Continuous-Time Analysis Ignores Discretization & Cumulative Error:** The theoretical analysis focuses on the stationary distribution of continuous-time SDEs. In practice, diffusion samplers operate with discrete schedules and accumulate errors over reverse-time trajectories. The paper acknowledges this limitation but does not analyze how the $\sigma^\alpha$ tempering interacts with Euler-Maruyama discretization or multi-step predictor-corrector loops, leaving a gap between the asymptotic theory and finite-step practice.
4. **Ambiguity Around Desirability of Uniform Sampling:** While uniform sampling on the manifold is mathematically elegant and useful for geometric/operator learning, it may not align with desiderata in all generative tasks (e.g., semantically meaningful data often follows non-uniform cluster distributions). The paper does not sufficiently clarify when uniformity is strictly beneficial versus potentially undesirable (e.g., mode over-exploration in downstream applications).

### Novelty & Significance
**Novelty:** High. The explicit asymptotic separation between $\Theta(\sigma^{-2})$ geometric and $\Theta(1)$ density terms is a novel theoretical contribution. Coupling this scale separation with a provably correct, one-line sampling modification (TS dynamics) and demonstrating its utility in inverse problems represents a substantial conceptual and algorithmic advance over prior work that either treated geometry/density jointly or focused only on linear/simplified manifolds.
**Significance:** Moderate to High. The paper successfully advocates for a paradigm shift from distributional to geometric learning, which has strong implications for theoretical ML and scientific computing. The TS method is promising and could see adoption if validated on modern models. However, the significance is somewhat tempered by the $L_\infty$ vs $L_2$ theoretical gap, the lack of discretization analysis, and the preliminary nature of the empirical results relative to current ICLR standards.

### Suggestions for Improvement
1. **Bridge the Theoretical-Practical Error Gap:** Provide at least a high-probability relaxation of the $L_\infty$ assumptions, or empirically measure the distribution of pointwise score errors across noise levels to demonstrate that practical $L_2$-trained models satisfy the required uniform bounds in high-probability regions of interest.
2. **Strengthen Empirical Rigor & Modern Relevance:** Evaluate the TS method on a contemporary architecture (e.g., a DiT-based model or SDXL) using standard datasets (e.g., COCO or ImageNet subsets) and report established metrics like FID and Precision-Recall curves. This will solidify the practical impact of the proposed modification.
3. **Analyze Discretization & Schedule Interaction:** Extend the theoretical discussion or add an ablation studying how the $\sigma^\alpha$ tempering interacts with finite discretization steps. Derive or empirically test whether tempering at each corrector step mitigates or amplifies cumulative discretization error compared to standard PC sampling.
4. **Clarify Use-Cases for Uniform Distribution:** Explicitly delineate scenarios where targeting the uniform manifold measure is advantageous (e.g., data augmentation, manifold exploration, debiasing, scientific discovery) versus cases where preserving the original non-uniform $\mu_{\text{data}}$ is critical. Positioning the TS method as a specialized tool for geometric/inverse tasks rather than a universal generative replacement will strengthen the paper's claims.

## How to Improve This Paper

### Missing Experiments (top 3-5 only)
1. **Empirical measurement of score errors vs. theoretical thresholds:** Plot the empirical score mismatch (ideally `L∞` and `L2`) against noise levels `σ` for real pre-trained models, explicitly overlaying the `o(σ^[−]2)` and `o(1)` bounds, to prove that practical networks actually operate within the error regimes your theory describes.
2. **Rigorous distributional metrics instead of CLIP proxies:** CLIP scores cannot verify manifold uniformity; you must use FID/KID, Precision-Recall for Distributions, or local intrinsic dimension estimation to credibly demonstrate that TS Langevin recovers the Riemannian volume measure rather than a different high-diversity non-uniform distribution.
3. **High-`σ` behavior and schedule integration:** The tempering is theoretically justified only as `σ → 0`, yet it is applied across the full diffusion noise schedule; experiments must ablate performance when `σ` is large and demonstrate that early-stage tempering does not destabilize generation or require dynamic `α` scheduling.

### Deeper Analysis Needed (top 3-5 only)
1. **Translation from `L^2` training loss to `L^∞` theoretical guarantees:** Your proofs strictly require uniform `L^∞` score error, but diffusion models optimize `L^2` denoising score matching; without bridging this gap, the claimed `o(σ^[−]2)` error tolerance is mathematically disconnected from how models are actually trained.
2. **Discretization bias in the Euler-Maruyama scheme:** The stationary distribution analysis is purely continuous, ignoring that practical samplers use discrete steps that accumulate bias; you must analyze or empirically bound how discretization error interacts with the `σ^α` scaling, as naive step sizes could easily overwhelm the theoretical uniformity guarantee.
3. **Theoretical impact of asymmetric tempering in Classifier-Free Guidance:** You temper only the unconditional score while leaving the conditional guidance increment unchanged; the analysis must explain why this selective modification preserves posterior concentration, as mismatched drift scaling could theoretically break the manifold projection properties for conditional generation.

### Visualizations & Case Studies
1. **Latent-space density projections for uniformity verification:** Provide 2D embeddings (e.g., PCA/t-SNE) of latent representations from synthetic or real data, directly contrasting the flat, uniform coverage of TS Langevin against the peaked distributions of baselines to visually substantiate the geometric learning claim.
2. **Step-size sensitivity and failure-mode illustrations:** Show concrete failure cases where TS Langevin produces off-manifold artifacts or mode collapse when discretization steps violate stability limits or `α` exceeds theoretical bounds, proving the practical necessity of your mathematical conditions rather than just their sufficiency.

### Obvious Next Steps
1. **Derive an explicit stability condition/adaptive step-size rule for discrete TS dynamics:** The `σ^α` scaling drastically alters the drift's local Lipschitz constant near the manifold; without a step-size constraint tied to `σ` and `α`, the method cannot be safely deployed in standard diffusion samplers.
2. **Formalize the uniform prior's advantage over realistic Plug-and-Play priors in BIPs:** Theorem 6.1 assumes a perfectly uniform manifold prior, but inverse problems typically use smoothed empirical priors; extending the analysis to quantify robustness gains over non-uniform, data-driven priors is necessary to justify the BIP application.

# Final Consolidated Review
## Summary
This paper establishes a rigorous scale separation in score-based models under the manifold hypothesis, demonstrating that geometric information (distance to the data manifold) emerges at $\Theta(\sigma^{-2})$ while distributional density information appears only at $\Theta(1)$. Leveraging this insight, the authors introduce Tempered Score (TS) Langevin dynamics, a simple one-line inference-time modification that provably recovers the uniform manifold distribution under relaxed $o(\sigma^{-2})$ score error bounds. The framework is applied to explain diffusion model behavior, improve sampling diversity, and demonstrate enhanced robustness of uniform priors in Bayesian inverse problems.

## Strengths
- **Sharp, non-trivial theoretical characterization:** The asymptotic expansion of $\log p_\sigma$ cleanly isolates geometric and density components, providing the first rigorous quantification of the $\sigma^{-2}$ vs. $\Theta(1)$ error tolerance gap. This formally explains why diffusion models capture data support robustly despite imperfect low-noise scores, advancing beyond prior qualitative or restricted subspace analyses.
- **Elegant, theoretically-grounded algorithm:** The proposed TS Langevin dynamics (Eq. 8) is trivial to implement yet supported by sophisticated mathematical machinery, including WKB expansions, Fokker-Planck analysis, and the strong maximum principle. Proving convergence to the Riemannian uniform measure under relaxed error assumptions (Thms 5.1–5.2) in a general non-gradient setting is a substantial technical contribution.
- **Broad applicability and refined mixing analysis:** The rate separation insight is successfully leveraged across generative modeling, geometric sampling, and Bayesian inverse problems (Thm 6.1). Furthermore, Appendix D provides a careful Poincaré constant analysis demonstrating that TS dynamics can provably mix faster than standard Langevin in multimodal settings, directly addressing concerns about computational overhead.

## Weaknesses
- **Empirical validation does not directly verify the core geometric claim:** While synthetic experiments strongly support the theory, the real-world evaluation relies on Stable Diffusion 1.5 and uses CLIP-based similarity proxies (P-sim, I-sim). These metrics correlate poorly with true manifold uniformity, local intrinsic dimension, or standard distributional alignment. The reported improvements are modest and lack statistical variance reporting, making it difficult to confirm whether the method genuinely recovers uniform geometric coverage or simply shifts the sampling distribution in latent space.

## Nice-to-Haves
- Empirically measuring pointwise or high-probability score errors across noise levels to characterize how closely practical $L_2$-trained models align with the theoretical $o(\sigma^{-2})$ threshold.
- Extending the analysis or ablation to discrete Euler-Maruyama steps to quantify how $\sigma^\alpha$ tempering interacts with cumulative discretization error over a full reverse trajectory.
- Clarifying practical use-cases where targeting the uniform manifold measure is strictly advantageous (e.g., scientific discovery, manifold exploration, debiasing) versus scenarios where preserving the original non-uniform $\mu_{\text{data}}$ density remains critical.

## Novel Insights
The paper’s central contribution reframes score-based learning as a two-stage process where geometry is learned orders of magnitude faster than density. This separation not only demystifies the empirical robustness of diffusion models in the low-noise regime but also suggests that forcing exact distributional recovery at small $\sigma$ may be mathematically misaligned with the inherent structure of high-dimensional data. By targeting uniform manifold sampling instead of exact density matching, practitioners can operate in a weaker, more achievable error regime. This geometric-first perspective elegantly bridges theoretical asymptotics with practical sampling design, offering a principled, inference-only alternative to fine-tuning or heuristic stabilization for manifold-aware generation and inverse problems.

## Suggestions
- In the revision, supplement CLIP scores with manifold-aware diagnostic metrics (e.g., local intrinsic dimension estimation, uniformity/coverage tests, or Precision-Recall distributions) and report error bars across multiple random seeds to rigorously validate the geometric learning hypothesis.
- Provide a high-probability relaxation or empirical characterization of the $L_\infty$ score error assumption, demonstrating how standard denoising score matching ($L_2$) objectives implicitly satisfy the required uniform bounds in high-density regions of the data manifold.
- Explicitly discuss the scope of the WKB ansatz and unique stationary distribution assumption for neural network drifts in the main text, and frame non-asymptotic/discrete-time guarantees (where $\sigma \geq \sigma_{\min} > 0$) as a clear direction for foundational future work.
