# Harsh Critic Review
## Section-by-Section Critical Review

### Title & Abstract
- **Does the title accurately reflect the contribution?** Yes. The title succinctly captures the core theoretical finding: that in the small-$\sigma$ regime, score learning prioritizes geometric recovery (manifold structure) over precise density reconstruction.
- **Does the abstract clearly state the problem, method, and key results?** Mostly. It clearly states the problem (difficulty of low-noise score learning), the perspective shift (geometry vs. distribution), the rate separation result, and the three theoretical consequences. The proposed method (Tempered Score Langevin) and its application to Bayesian inverse problems are summarized.
- **Are any claims in the abstract unsupported by the paper?** The phrase "provably tolerates $O(\sigma^{-2})$ larger errors" is slightly imprecise. The theory (Theorem 4.1 vs. 5.1/5.2) establishes a separation in the *required error tolerance*: $o(\sigma^{-2})$ suffices for uniform/geometric recovery, whereas $o(1)$ is needed for exact density recovery. The abstract's wording could be misread as stating that the algorithm's *own* error scales as $O(\sigma^{-2})$, rather than it being *robust* to errors of that magnitude. Clarifying that "larger errors" refers to the *allowable approximation error bound*, not the algorithm's output error, would improve precision.

### Introduction & Motivation
- **Is the problem well-motivated? Is the gap in prior work clearly identified?** Yes. The introduction effectively motivates the difficulty of learning scores in the low-temperature limit and connects it to known empirical behaviors of diffusion models. The gap is clearly identified: prior work either analyzes geometry/density jointly or under restrictive settings (linear manifolds, empirical observations), without quantifying how approximation errors scale differently for geometric vs. distributional recovery as $\sigma \to 0$.
- **Are the contributions clearly stated and accurate?** The three main contributions (Theorem 4.1, Theorems 5.1–5.2, Theorem 6.1) are accurately summarized and align with the body. The "paradigm shift" framing is rhetorically strong but scientifically justified by the derived rate separations.
- **Does the introduction over-claim or under-sell?** The claim that learning the uniform distribution is "$O(\sigma^{-2})$ easier" could be misinterpreted as lower computational complexity. In context, it clearly refers to error tolerance relaxations, but explicitly distinguishing *statistical/approximation robustness* from *algorithmic complexity* would prevent confusion. Otherwise, claims are well-calibrated to the theoretical results.

### Method / Approach
- **Is the method clearly described and reproducible?** The TS Langevin update (Eq. 8) is simple and reproducible. The algorithmic modification to standard Predictor-Corrector samplers (scaling the unconditional score by $\sigma_t^\alpha$) is clearly explained in Section 7.2 and Appendix C.2.
- **Are key assumptions stated and justified?** Key assumptions are stated (Assumption 2.1: $C^4$ compact boundaryless manifold; Assumption 2.2: strictly positive $C^1$ $p_{\text{data}}$; Assumption 4.1: compact concentration and path-connectivity; Assumption B.2: local WKB ansatz). However, two assumptions warrant scrutiny:
  1. **$L_\infty$ Score Error:** The theory relies on $\|s - s^*\|_{L^\infty(K)}$ bounds (Eq. 9). In practice, score networks are trained via $L_2$-based objectives (Denoising Score Matching / Fisher divergence). Bridging $L_2$ training guarantees to $L_\infty$ error bounds is non-trivial and generally requires strong concentration or Sobolev embedding arguments not provided here.
  2. **WKB Ansatz (Assumption B.2):** The assumption that the stationary density takes the form $\exp(-V/\theta)c_\theta(x)$ is standard in asymptotic physics but is *assumed* rather than derived for non-reversible SDEs with neural score fields. While common in matched asymptotic analysis, ICLR's theoretical standards typically expect existence/uniqueness justification or at least a discussion of when this ansatz fails for parameterized drifts.
- **Are there logical gaps in the derivation or reasoning?** The Laplace expansion (Theorem 3.1/Appendix B.2) is mathematically sound under the stated regularity conditions. The WKB matching in Appendix B.4 is logically consistent, but the transition from the transport equation (Eq. 15) to the manifold PDE (Eq. 17) and application of the strong maximum principle to conclude $\tilde{c}_0$ is constant relies on careful boundary/normal coordinate elimination. The derivation is sketched rather than fully expanded, leaving room for subtle gaps in coordinate transformation details.
- **Are there edge cases or failure modes not discussed?** 
  - The theory breaks down if $\alpha \ge 2$ or $\beta \le -2$. What happens at these boundaries?
  - Manifolds with boundaries or singularities violate Assumption 2.1, which is common for real-world data (e.g., image manifolds with clipping/artifacts).
  - The analysis assumes a single-chart approximation globally; handling nontrivial topologies (multiple charts, non-orientable manifolds) requires careful stitching, briefly mentioned but not theoretically integrated into the PDE step.
- **For theoretical claims: are proofs correct and complete?** The proofs are largely complete and follow rigorous asymptotic analysis. The reliance on prior asymptotic physics literature (WKB, Holley-Stroock, Lyapunov functions) is appropriate, but the paper would benefit from explicitly stating the regularity conditions under which the non-reversible Fokker-Planck equation admits the WKB expansion for learned neural drifts.

### Experiments & Results
- **Do the experiments actually test the paper's claims?** The synthetic experiments (Fig. 2, App. C.3) directly test the geometric recovery vs. arbitrary distribution claims under controlled $O(1)$ score errors, strongly supporting Theorems 4.1 and 5.1. The Stable Diffusion experiments test downstream effects (diversity/quality) but do not directly measure geometric recovery or rate separation, which is expected given real-data manifold inaccessibility.
- **Are baselines appropriate and fairly compared?** Yes. DDPM, standard Predictor-Corrector (PC), and tuned hyperparameters are used as baselines. Grid searches for $\alpha$ and corrector steps are reported fairly (Tables 1–4).
- **Are there missing ablations that would materially change conclusions?** 
  - The connection between the asymptotic $\sigma \to 0$ theory and the *finite, discrete* diffusion schedule (e.g., VP noise schedule) is not empirically bridged. An ablation showing how performance varies as $\alpha$ interacts with the *minimum* $\sigma$ in the schedule would strengthen the theory-practice link.
  - No computational overhead or step-stability analysis is provided. Tempering the drift may implicitly change the effective Lipschitz constant, requiring different step sizes $\delta_i$ for stable integration.
- **Are error bars / statistical significance reported?** No. Tables 1–4 report single-run CLIP scores. Given the small absolute differences (e.g., I-sim changes of $0.02$ to $0.3$), reporting mean $\pm$ std over multiple seeds or significance tests is necessary to claim "consistent improvements."
- **Do the results support the claims made, or are they cherry-picked?** Results are not cherry-picked (multiple seeds/prompts reported in Tables 1–4), but the claimed improvements are marginal. The paper states TS "enhances both the quality and diversity," but Table 1 shows I-sim improvements of $0.01$–$0.2$ points and P-sim improvements of $0.1$–$0.6$ points. These are within typical CLIP score variance. The claims should be softened to "modest, consistent trends consistent with theoretical predictions" rather than definitive empirical validation.
- **Are datasets and evaluation metrics appropriate?** Synthetic manifolds are appropriate for theoretical validation. For real images, CLIP P-sim/I-sim are standard but noisy proxies for geometry/diversity. Lacking FID, IS, or human evaluation limits the strength of the empirical contribution for ICLR standards.

### Writing & Clarity
- **Are there sections that are confusing or poorly explained?** Appendix B.4 (WKB analysis) is dense. The transition from Eq. (16) to Eq. (17) involves dropping/transforming terms involving normal coordinates without explicit Jacobian/chain-rule expansion, which may confuse readers unfamiliar with differential geometry or matched asymptotics. A schematic diagram or more explicit intermediate steps in the main text would improve accessibility.
- **Are figures and tables clear and informative?** Figure 2 clearly shows uniform vs. biased concentration. Figure 3's qualitative comparison is legible but small; higher resolution would help assess "richer details" claims. Tables 1–4 are well-formatted but lack statistical uncertainty metrics, making it hard to assess practical significance.

### Limitations & Broader Impact
- **Do the authors acknowledge the key limitations?** Yes, Section 8 honestly lists five major limitations: cumulative trajectory error ignored, $L_\infty$ vs $L_2$ mismatch, lack of sample complexity results, unaddressed discretization error, and preliminary experimental scale.
- **Are there fundamental limitations they missed?** 
  - **Manifold Regularity:** Real high-dimensional data (e.g., natural images) may not form $C^4$ compact boundaryless manifolds. Sharp edges, discrete variables, or varying intrinsic dimensions violate core assumptions. The robustness of the $\sigma^{-2}$ separation to such irregularities is unknown.
  - **Algorithmic Stability:** Tempering the drift by $\sigma^\alpha$ changes the SDE's stiffness profile. The paper argues mixing may be faster (Appendix D), but in practice, this can cause numerical instability in the corrector step unless step sizes are aggressively rescaled. This practical limitation isn't discussed.
- **Are there failure modes or negative societal impacts not discussed?** No specific negative societal impacts are expected beyond standard generative model concerns. Failure modes include potential degradation if $\alpha$ is poorly tuned relative to the minimum $\sigma$ in a schedule, or if the learned score lacks sufficient regularity for the WKB expansion to hold locally.

### Overall Assessment
This paper presents a theoretically grounded and conceptually elegant analysis of score-based generative models under the manifold hypothesis. The core insight—that geometric information ($\Theta(\sigma^{-2})$) dominates distributional information ($\Theta(1)$) in the low-noise limit—is rigorously derived and provides a compelling explanation for the empirical robustness of diffusion models. The introduction of Tempered Score Langevin dynamics and its analysis via WKB expansions and maximum principles is a novel contribution that bridges asymptotic physics techniques with ML theory. However, the work has notable gaps relative to ICLR's empirical standards: the reliance on $L_\infty$ error bounds creates a disconnect with standard $L_2$ training objectives, the WKB ansatz is assumed rather than derived for learned drifts, and the experimental validation, while honest, shows marginal quantitative gains without statistical reporting or standard generative metrics (FID/IS). If the authors can explicitly address the $L_2 \to L_\infty$ translation gap, clarify the finite-$\sigma$ vs. asymptotic regime connection, and strengthen the empirical validation with uncertainty estimates, this would be a strong contribution. As currently written, the theoretical novelty justifies acceptance, but the empirical component remains preliminary.

# Neutral Reviewer
## Balanced Review

### Summary
This paper establishes a fundamental rate separation in score-based generative modeling, proving that under the manifold hypothesis, geometric information (manifold structure) emerges at scale $\Theta(\sigma^{-2})$ while density information appears only at $\Theta(1)$ in the low-noise limit. This insight motivates "Tempered Score" (TS) Langevin dynamics, a simple one-line corrector modification that provably recovers the uniform distribution on the data manifold under significantly relaxed $o(\sigma^{-2})$ score accuracy requirements, compared to the stringent $o(1)$ needed for exact distributional recovery. Theoretical findings are supported by controlled synthetic experiments and a preliminary application to Stable Diffusion 1.5, demonstrating consistent improvements in sample diversity without sacrificing fidelity.

### Strengths
1. **Novel Theoretical Lens:** The explicit asymptotic separation between geometry and density learning (Theorem 3.1) provides a fresh, rigorous explanation for why diffusion models often produce realistic samples despite imperfect score estimates. This reframes the learning objective from full distribution recovery to geometric support recovery, which is highly insightful for the generative modeling community.
2. **Mathematical Rigor and Technical Depth:** The paper correctly employs advanced asymptotic and geometric tools, including Laplace’s method for integrals, WKB approximations for the Fokker–Planck equation, the strong maximum principle to handle non-gradient scores (Theorem 5.2), and Poincaré constant analysis for mixing times (Appendix D). The derivations are thorough and well-structured across the main text and appendices.
3. **Practical, Low-Overhead Algorithm:** The proposed TS Langevin dynamics requires only a single scaling factor $\sigma^\alpha$ applied to the unconditional score in the corrector step. It needs no retraining, integrates seamlessly into existing Predictor-Corrector pipelines, and empirically improves diversity (lower CLIP I-sim) while maintaining quality (Table 1 & 2).
4. **Transparent Reporting of Limitations:** The authors clearly delineate the boundaries of their analysis in Section 8, acknowledging gaps such as the lack of cumulative error tracking along noise schedules, $L_\infty$ vs. $L_2$ training objectives, and discretization effects. This honesty aligns well with ICLR's emphasis on rigorous and self-aware theoretical work.

### Weaknesses
1. **Strict $\sigma \to 0$ Regime:** The analysis focuses entirely on the low-temperature limit, whereas practical diffusion models rely on a full noise schedule $\sigma(t)$. The paper does not track how errors accumulate or propagate across time steps ( acknowledged in limitations but limits direct practical applicability).
2. **Strong Assumptions on Score Error & Geometry:** The core theorems rely on $L_\infty$ score approximation bounds and a compact, boundaryless $C^4$ manifold. Real-world score matching is trained via $L_2$/Fisher divergence objectives, and real data manifolds often exhibit boundaries or singularities. The gap between these assumptions and practical training is non-trivial.
3. **WKB Ansatz for Neural Scores:** Theorem 5.2's extension to non-gradient scores hinges on Assumption B.2 (local WKB ansatz for the stationary density). While standard in singular perturbation theory, justifying this ansatz for highly parameterized neural network drift fields remains an open heuristic step. The paper does not empirically or theoretically verify how well NN stationary distributions conform to this form.
4. **Limited Experimental Scope:** Empirical validation is restricted to low-dimensional synthetic manifolds and Stable Diffusion 1.5 using CLIP-based proxy metrics. ICLR typically expects broader validation, such as testing on modern architectures (SDXL, DiT, or flow-matching baselines), quantitative evaluation on downstream geometric tasks, or standard generative benchmarks (FID, precision/recall).

### Novelty & Significance
**High.** The explicit characterization of the $\Theta(\sigma^{-2})$ vs. $\Theta(1)$ scale separation is a novel contribution that bridges differential geometry, asymptotic analysis, and generative modeling. It offers a principled theoretical foundation for observed phenomena in diffusion models and introduces a practically useful inference-time modification. The work is highly relevant to ICLR's focus on deep learning theory, score-based methods, and principled generative modeling. While the strict asymptotic setting limits immediate widespread adoption, the theoretical insights are substantial and the algorithmic modification is immediately deployable.

### Suggestions for Improvement
1. **Bridge the $\sigma \to 0$ Gap to Discrete Schedules:** Provide either a multi-step error propagation bound or empirical analysis showing how the TS modification interacts with standard noise schedules (e.g., VP/VESDE trajectories). Even a heuristic or numerical cumulative error analysis would strengthen the practical relevance.
2. **Relax Error Norm Assumptions:** Explore extending the rate separation results to $L_2$ or Fisher divergence error bounds, which directly align with denoising score matching objectives. This would make the theoretical guarantees more compatible with how models are actually trained.
3. **Interpolation/Trade-off Mechanism:** Since the TS method targets the uniform manifold distribution, it intentionally discards the true data density $p_{data}$. Propose or analyze a tunable interpolation scheme (e.g., $s_{hybrid} = \lambda s + (1-\lambda)\sigma^\alpha s$) that allows practitioners to control the trade-off between uniform geometric coverage and high-density mode matching.
4. **Broaden Empirical Validation:** Evaluate TS on at least one modern, state-of-the-art architecture or a downstream task where manifold coverage is critical (e.g., molecular conformation generation, inverse physical problems, or precision/recall metrics on image benchmarks). Additionally, include a brief ablation on how $\alpha$ affects the mode-density vs. diversity trade-off to guide practical hyperparameter selection.

### Obvious Next Steps
1. Quantitize the computational overhead (NFE, wall-clock time) against the marginal CLIP metric gains to prove the method's efficiency; adding corrector steps for ~0.3% metric improvements without an efficiency trade-off analysis makes the "one-line modification" practically unappealing for ICLR standards.
2. Extend the rate separation theory to finite-sample score estimation and explicit discretization error bounds, as the continuous-time, oracle-based analysis cannot be trusted for actual deployment where both training data and numerical solvers are bounded and discrete.

# Final Consolidated Review
## Summary
This paper establishes a sharp asymptotic rate separation in score-based generative modeling under the manifold hypothesis, proving that geometric information (manifold structure) scales as $\Theta(\sigma^{-2})$ while density information appears only at $\Theta(1)$ in the low-noise limit. Leveraging this insight, the authors introduce Tempered Score (TS) Langevin dynamics—a one-line inference-time modification that provably recovers the uniform manifold distribution under relaxed $o(\sigma^{-2})$ score error, compared to the stringent $o(1)$ required for exact density recovery. The work extends this principle to Bayesian inverse problems and provides preliminary synthetic and image-generation experiments.

## Strengths
- **Fundamental theoretical insight:** The explicit $\Theta(\sigma^{-2})$ vs. $\Theta(1)$ scale separation rigorously formalizes why diffusion models produce realistic samples despite imperfect low-noise score estimates. This reframes score learning from a distribution-matching problem to a geometric-recovery problem, offering a compelling new lens for the community.
- **Sophisticated mathematical machinery:** The derivation correctly bridges differential geometry, Laplace asymptotics, and singular perturbation theory. Notably, using the WKB ansatz with a transport equation and invoking the strong maximum principle to handle non-reversible, non-gradient neural drifts (Theorems 5.1–5.2) is technically elegant and avoids common restrictive assumptions seen in prior SDE analyses.
- **Practical, zero-training algorithmic contribution:** TS Langevin requires only scaling the unconditional score by $\sigma^\alpha$ during corrector steps. It integrates seamlessly into existing Predictor-Corrector pipelines without retraining, aligning well with the paper’s claim of a low-overhead inference modification.
- **Transparent scope and self-critique:** Section 8 explicitly acknowledges major limitations (cumulative trajectory errors, $L_\infty$ vs. $L_2$ mismatch, discretization effects), which prevents overclaiming and clarifies the boundary between asymptotic theory and practical deployment.

## Weaknesses
- **Disconnect between $L_\infty$ theory and $L_2$ practice:** The core theorems fundamentally rely on uniform $L_\infty$ score approximation bounds. However, practical score matching minimizes $L_2$ Fisher divergence. The authors acknowledge this gap but do not bridge it, leaving the claimed error tolerances practically unverifiable. Without an $L_2 \to L_\infty$ translation or empirical measurement of actual model $L_\infty$ errors, the theoretical guarantees remain heuristic for real networks.
- **Asymptotic isolation ignores full-schedule dynamics:** The analysis is strictly $\sigma \to 0$ and assumes access to the final distribution’s score error. Real diffusion sampling integrates over a full noise schedule where early-step inaccuracies and discretization errors accumulate and propagate. Ignoring cumulative trajectory error significantly weakens the claim that TS robustness translates to end-to-end sampling improvements.
- **Preliminary and statistically unverified experiments:** The empirical validation is insufficient for ICLR standards. CLIP metric improvements are marginal (e.g., I-sim shifts of 0.01–0.3) and lack standard deviation or significance testing across seeds. Crucially, standard generative benchmarks (FID, Precision/Recall) are absent, and the Bayesian inverse problem claims (Theorem 6.1) are entirely untested. The experiments read as proof-of-concept rather than validation of the theoretical magnitude.
- **Theoretical mismatch in CFG implementation:** The paper empirically scales only the unconditional score in Classifier-Free Guidance while leaving the conditional drift unchanged. This asymmetric modification breaks the theoretical uniformity guarantee derived for a single score oracle, introducing an uncontrolled bias that is neither analyzed nor bounded.

## Nice-to-Haves
- Provide a formal or heuristic analysis of how TS tempering interacts with standard discrete noise schedules (e.g., variance-preserving schedules) and corrector step-sizes to address potential stiffness or numerical instability.
- Quantify the computational overhead (NFE, wall-clock time) against the metric gains to clarify the practical cost-benefit of additional corrector steps.
- Explore a tunable interpolation between raw and tempered scores to allow practitioners to trade off uniform geometric coverage against high-density mode fidelity.

## Novel Insights
The paper’s most compelling contribution is the formalization of a "geometry-first" learning paradigm in score-based models. By isolating the $\sigma^{-2}$ singular term in the log-density expansion, the authors demonstrate that manifold concentration is structurally insulated from density inaccuracies, explaining the empirical robustness of diffusion samplers. The insight that a simple temperature scaling of the score drift forces the stationary distribution to collapse onto the intrinsic Riemannian volume measure—via a clever application of the strong maximum principle to non-reversible SDEs—provides a mathematically elegant tool for geometry recovery that bypasses explicit density estimation.

## Potentially Missed Related Work
- None identified. The paper thoroughly situates itself against recent work on manifold diffusion (Lu et al., 2023; Lyu et al., 2025), non-reversible sampling (Bouchet & Reygner, 2016), and geometric manifold learning (Coifman & Lafon, 2006), and correctly distinguishes its contributions.

## Suggestions
- **Bridge the norm gap:** Either extend the rate separation theorems to $L_2$/Fisher divergence bounds or empirically measure the actual $L_\infty$ score error of the Stable Diffusion models used to verify they operate within the $o(\sigma^{-2})$ tolerance regime.
- **Strengthen empirical validation:** Report mean ± standard deviation over multiple random seeds for all CLIP metrics, and include standard generative benchmarks (e.g., FID, Precision-Recall curves) or a downstream geometric task. Add empirical validation for the Bayesian inverse problem setting (Theorem 6.1) to match theoretical breadth.
- **Analyze CFG interaction:** Provide a theoretical bound or ablation explaining how the asymmetric tempering of the unconditional score affects the conditional posterior, or formally adapt the theory to handle guided drifts.
- **Clarify finite-$\sigma$ bridging:** Include a multi-step error propagation analysis or a numerical study showing how $\sigma^\alpha$ tempering behaves across the full reverse trajectory, explicitly linking the asymptotic $\sigma \to 0$ guarantees to finite-step sampler performance.
