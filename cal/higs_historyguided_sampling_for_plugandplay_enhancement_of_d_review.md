# Harsh Critic Review
## Section-by-Section Critical Review

**Title & Abstract**
The title accurately reflects the method and its plug-and-play nature. The abstract claims "practically no additional computation" and a "new state-of-the-art FID of 1.61 for unguided ImageNet generation... with only 30 sampling steps." Both claims are subsequently addressed in the text (Appendix D for runtime, Table 3 for FID). However, the abstract omits that the method relies on three critical empirical stabilizers (weight scheduling, orthogonal projection, and DCT high-pass filtering) to prevent artifacts. Framing HiGS purely as a momentum-based step without acknowledging the necessary post-hoc corrections in the abstract slightly oversells the theoretical simplicity of the approach.

**Introduction & Motivation**
The motivation is clear: diffusion sampling suffers from quality degradation at low NFEs or low CFG scales, while high CFG scales incur double computation and cause oversaturation. The gap in the literature (training-free improvement for low-CFG/low-NFE regimes) is well-stated. The inspiration from STORM (variance-reduced optimization) is interesting but conceptually stretched in Section 1. STORM operates on gradients of an objective function w.r.t. optimization variables, whereas HiGS operates directly in the *output space* of a neural denoiser. The connection between "gradient variance reduction in parameter optimization" and "output space history averaging in reverse-time sampling" is not rigorously established, making the optimization analogy feel more like a useful heuristic than a grounded theoretical bridge. The authors should temper this claim or explicitly frame it as an empirical inspiration rather than a direct mathematical equivalence.

**Method / Approach (Section 4, Appendices A & B)**
This is where the most significant concerns lie:
1. **Theory-Practice Mismatch (Appendix B vs Section 4.2):** Theorem B.1 claims that incorporating history reduces the local truncation error from $O(h^2)$ to $O(h^3)$ by setting the weight $w_k = 2h_k/h_{k-1}$. However, the actual implementation uses a completely different scheduling rule: a square-root schedule bounded by $t_{\min}$ and $t_{\max}$ (Eq. 6). The theoretical weight is a precise function of step sizes designed to cancel Taylor expansion terms, while the practical weight is a bounded, time-dependent heuristic. This disconnect means the error analysis does not rigorously justify the method as implemented. If the theoretical weighting is not used in practice, the convergence improvement claim is largely aspirational for real-world diffusion models.
2. **Stability & Empirical Stabilizers:** The method introduces $\Delta D_{t_k} = D_{CFG}(z_{t_k}) - g(H_k)$ as the guidance direction. However, Section 4.2 and Appendix E candidly admit that this raw difference causes oversaturation, color shifts, and noisy artifacts unless three additional mechanisms are applied: (i) orthogonal projection (Eq. 7), (ii) a time-dependent weight schedule (Eq. 6), and (iii) DCT-based high-pass filtering (Eq. 8). While these are common and acceptable in diffusion sampling research, their necessity indicates that the core history term is fundamentally misaligned with the sampling ODE's geometry. The paper would benefit from an analysis of *why* the raw history term fails (e.g., does it introduce low-frequency bias that misaligns with the score function?) rather than presenting these as minor design choices.
3. **Unguided Sampling Protocol:** The abstract and Table 3 report strong results for *unguided* (CFG=1) sampling. The method states that when CFG is disabled, the history buffer can track conditional outputs $D_c(z_{t_k})$. However, the pipeline in Algorithm 1 explicitly computes CFG outputs (Line 4) and uses them for history (Line 5). The exact adaptation for the truly unguided case is not explicitly detailed in the main algorithm or pseudocode, leaving a reproducibility gap for the most prominent numerical claim (FID 1.61).

**Experiments & Results (Section 5, Appendices D & E)**
1. **Baseline Comparisons:** The paper compares HiGS against standard CFG sampling with default solvers (Euler, DDIM, etc.). This is fair for establishing baseline utility, but the low-NFE/low-CFG space is heavily researched. The paper lacks direct quantitative comparison against state-of-the-art few-step samplers (e.g., DPM-Solver++, UniPC, PNDM) or modern guidance-scheduling techniques that explicitly target the same regime. Table 6 shows compatibility with DPM++ and UniPC, but reports results at a fixed 15 steps without ablation on how HiGS stacks against those solvers' native few-step capabilities. Without this, it's unclear whether HiGS provides marginal gains or fundamentally outperforms advanced ODE solvers at very low budgets.
2. **Win Rates & Statistical Significance:** Tables 1, 2, and 4 report HPSv2 win rates consistently between 79% and 96%. While impressive, such dominant win rates for a single-line additive tweak are unusually high and warrant scrutiny. ICLR reviewers typically expect variance across seeds or prompts, especially for preference metrics that can be sensitive to prompt distribution. Reporting confidence intervals or conducting statistical significance tests (e.g., binomial tests for win rates) would strengthen these claims considerably.
3. **Ablation Insights:** Table 9 shows that replacing the EMA history function $g(H_k)$ with random sampling, simple averaging, or weighted averaging yields nearly identical HPSv2 scores (0.255–0.261). This suggests the specific "momentum" formulation is not the primary driver of performance gains, and the improvement may stem more generically from any form of history regularization combined with the DCT/projection steps. The authors should acknowledge this and clarify whether the momentum analogy is essential or if the method is better understood as a generic history-smoothing regularizer.

**Writing, Clarity & Limitations**
The writing is generally clear and well-structured. The mathematical notation is consistent, and the algorithms are reproducible. However, the limitations section is underdeveloped. It briefly states that HiGS "inherits... biases and some limitations of the underlying diffusion models," but does not discuss practical failure modes (e.g., does it break down with highly abstract or out-of-distribution prompts? Does it interact poorly with negative prompts or specific architecture choices like Flow Matching?). Given the heavy reliance on DCT filtering, it would also be valuable to discuss applicability to non-image modalities (video, audio) where the frequency-domain assumptions may differ.

### Overall Assessment
HiGS presents a practical, training-free inference-time modification that consistently improves visual quality and reduces artifacts across several modern diffusion models, particularly under low CFG or low step budgets. The empirical results are extensive and the method is computationally efficient, aligning well with ICLR's interest in impactful sampling improvements. However, the contribution is weakened by a significant disconnect between its theoretical motivation and practical implementation: the convergence proof relies on a precise step-size-dependent weight that is entirely replaced by a heuristic schedule in practice, and the core history term requires three additional stabilizing components (scheduling, projection, DCT filtering) to function without introducing artifacts. Furthermore, the lack of comparison against modern few-step ODE solvers and the absence of statistical reporting for the exceptionally high win rates leave questions about the marginal utility and robustness of the approach. If the authors reframe the theoretical claims to match the implementation, provide stronger baselines against advanced few-step samplers, and transparently discuss the role of the empirical stabilizers, this will be a solid ICLR contribution.

# Neutral Reviewer
## Balanced Review

### Summary
The paper proposes HiGS, a training-free sampling enhancement for diffusion models that applies a momentum-like correction term derived from a weighted average of past denoiser predictions to guide the reverse diffusion trajectory. By integrating scheduled scaling, optional orthogonal projection, and DCT-based high-pass filtering, HiGS consistently improves image fidelity and reduces artifacts across diverse architectures, guidance scales, and sampling budgets without increasing neural function evaluations. Extensive experiments demonstrate robust gains in both human-aligned metrics and FID, including a state-of-the-art unguided FID of 1.61 on ImageNet 256×256 using only 30 steps.

### Strengths
1. **Zero Overhead & High Practical Utility:** HiGS requires no additional network forward passes, preserving the exact computational budget of standard CFG. The authors empirically verify identical runtime and memory usage (Appendix D) and provide complete pseudocode (Algorithms 1–3) with explicit hyperparameter tables per model (Tables 10–12), ensuring immediate applicability in production pipelines.
2. **Comprehensive Empirical Validation:** The evaluation spans multiple modern architectures (DiT, SiT, SDXL, SD3/3.5), distilled variants (SDXL-Lightning, SDXL-Flash), and diverse ODE solvers (DDIM, DPM++, PLMS, UniPC). Tables 1–3 and 6 consistently report improvements in HPSv2, FID, Precision, and Recall, with particularly strong gains in challenging low-NFE and low-CFG regimes.
3. **Thorough Ablation & Engineering Rigor:** The authors systematically validate each design choice in Appendix E, demonstrating that CFG-guided buffering, EMA averaging, weight scheduling, projection, and DCT filtering all contribute meaningfully to stability and quality. This structured ablation strengthens confidence in the method's components.
4. **Theoretical Error Analysis:** Appendix B provides a local truncation error analysis showing that the history-based correction can reduce the Euler solver's local error from $O(h^2)$ to $O(h^3)$ under specific weight schedules, offering a mathematical foundation that aligns with the observed empirical improvements in stability and convergence.

### Weaknesses
1. **Moderate Novelty Relative to Prior Work:** The use of historical predictions and momentum-like acceleration in diffusion ODEs is well-explored (e.g., DPM-Solver, PNDM, UniPC, and multistep extrapolation methods). While HiGS frames history as a plug-and-play post-correction, the conceptual overlap with existing variance-reduction and multistep techniques is substantial. The connection to the STORM optimizer is asserted but not deeply differentiated from prior momentum-based diffusion samplers.
2. **Heuristic Stabilization Components Lack Formal Justification:** Key practical components—the DCT high-pass filter and orthogonal projection—are introduced empirically to mitigate color shifts and oversaturation. While effective, the paper does not provide theoretical or empirical analysis explaining why these specific operations are optimal, how they interact with the underlying score estimation dynamics, or whether simpler alternatives could achieve similar stability.
3. **Hyperparameter Tuning Limits the "Plug-and-Play" Claim:** Despite the label "plug-and-play," optimal performance requires per-model tuning of multiple hyperparameters ($w_{\text{HiGS}}, t_{\min}, t_{\max}, \alpha, \eta, R_c, \lambda$), as shown in Tables 10–12. The robustness ranges in Appendix E demonstrate tolerance, but the absence of a principled auto-tuning strategy or cross-model transfer experiment slightly overstates the out-of-the-box readiness.
4. **Limited Diagnostic Analysis of Trajectory Dynamics:** The evaluation relies heavily on end-to-end metrics and visual comparisons but lacks deeper introspective analysis (e.g., denoising trajectory plots, score bias/variance measurements, or timestep-wise error distributions). Including such diagnostics would clarify *how* and *when* the history term corrects sampling deviations, strengthening the scientific narrative.

### Novelty & Significance
**Novelty:** Moderate. The core idea of leveraging past predictions aligns with established multistep and momentum-based ODE solving strategies. The novelty lies primarily in the pragmatic post-hoc formulation and the integration of practical stabilization techniques (DCT filtering, projection, scheduling) rather than a fundamentally new sampling paradigm. 
**Clarity:** High. The paper is well-structured, with a logical flow from motivation to method, rigorous ablation, and extensive experiments. Mathematical notation is consistent, and the algorithm pseudocode is clear.
**Reproducibility:** High. The authors provide complete implementation details, exact hyperparameters per model, public code references, and explicit runtime/memory verification. The reproducibility standards align well with ICLR expectations.
**Significance:** High. As a zero-overhead, training-free enhancement that meaningfully improves low-step and low-CFG generation, HiGS offers immediate practical value to researchers and practitioners. The demonstrated compatibility with distilled models and diverse samplers, alongside the SOTA unguided ImageNet result, underscores its utility in the current diffusion ecosystem.

### Suggestions for Improvement
1. **Explicitly Benchmark Against Multistep/Momentum Samplers:** Include a direct comparison with methods that internally use historical predictions (e.g., DPM-Solver-2, UniPC, or velocity extrapolation) under matched NFE schedules. Clarify whether HiGS provides complementary gains or outperforms integrated multistep formulations, and discuss the trade-offs.
2. **Justify the DCT Filtering and Projection Mathematically or Empirically:** Analyze the frequency spectrum of $\Delta D_{t_k}$ across timesteps to demonstrate why low-frequency components correlate with artifacts. Provide a geometric or optimization-based rationale for the orthogonal projection, or show empirical comparisons with alternative regularization techniques.
3. **Evaluate Cross-Model Hyperparameter Transfer:** Test whether a single set of default hyperparameters performs competitively across fundamentally different architectures (e.g., latent vs. pixel-space, distilled vs. non-distilled). Quantify the performance gap when using "universal" defaults versus per-model tuned values to calibrate the "plug-and-play" claim.
4. **Add Trajectory & Error Diagnostics:** Include visualizations of denoising trajectories with/without HiGS, or plot the norm and cosine similarity of the correction term $\Delta D_{t_k}$ across timesteps. This would concretely illustrate when and how the history guidance corrects score estimation errors.
5. **Tighten the Error Analysis Assumptions:** Clarify how the theoretical weight $w_k = 2h_k/h_{k-1}$ derived in Appendix B maps to the empirical square-root schedule used in practice. Explicitly state the scheduler assumptions under which the $O(h^3)$ local bound holds, and discuss potential discrepancies with standard variance-expoding or learned schedulers.

# Spark Finder Review
## How to Improve This Paper

### Missing Experiments (top 3-5 only)
1. Compare HiGS against dedicated training-free low-CFG/acceleration methods (e.g., CFG-Rescale, Guidance-Interval, ADC, PAG) on ImageNet and T2I. Without this, the reported gains may simply reflect the known suboptimality of naive CFG at low scales rather than a uniquely effective sampling mechanism.
2. Report statistical significance testing (e.g., bootstrapped confidence intervals over 5+ seeds) for the FID=1.61 and HPSv2 improvements, alongside explicit diversity metrics (e.g., pairwise LPIPS or MS-SSD). FID and preference scores are highly variance-sensitive; without variance bounds, the state-of-the-art and fidelity claims are statistically unverified.
3. Provide end-to-end wall-clock latency and peak VRAM profiling that includes the DCT filtering and orthogonal projection steps. The claim of "practically no additional computation" is directly undermined if high-frequency tensor operations or vector projections introduce measurable overhead (>5%) on large latents.

### Deeper Analysis Needed (top 3-5 only)
1. Rigorously analyze why raw history guidance induces severe low-frequency color drift that requires DCT filtering and projection to fix. If the momentum direction is fundamentally misaligned with the data manifold, the theoretical grounding in STORM/variance reduction is practically inconsistent.
2. Address the consistent drop or stagnation in Recall metrics across Tables 1 and 3, which contradicts the implied claim of improved diversity. The method appears to trade off mode coverage for sharpening (moving along the precision-recall Pareto frontier), which must be explicitly analyzed rather than masked by aggregate metrics.
3. Reconcile the ODE truncation error improvement ($O(h^2)$ to $O(h^3)$) with the highly non-smooth, piecewise behavior of modern DiT denoisers and CFG. The smoothness assumptions required for the proof are violated by ReLU/GELU activations and guidance switching, making the theoretical convergence guarantee unreliable for actual sampled trajectories.

### Visualizations & Case Studies
1. Provide explicit failure cases where HiGS degrades structural coherence, introduces over-smoothing artifacts, or severely misaligns with the prompt compared to baseline CFG at identical NFEs. Defining operational boundaries is mandatory to assess whether the method generalizes or merely works on curated prompts.
2. Plot the actual denoising trajectories (e.g., in PCA of latents or along the sampling energy landscape) and frequency spectra before/after HiGS application. This would directly reveal whether the history term provides a true guidance vector or acts as an ad-hoc, high-pass smoothing regularizer that artificially inflates sharpness metrics.
3. Render a comprehensive sensitivity heatmap for $w_{HiGS}$ versus $t_{min}/t_{max}$ showing precise regions where quality collapses. Visualizing the narrowness of the operational sweet spot is required to validate the robustness implied by the "plug-and-play" claim.

### Obvious Next Steps
1. Derive a principled, model-agnostic rule for setting the 5+ core hyperparameters ($w_{HiGS}, \eta, t_{min}, t_{max}, \alpha, R_c$) instead of relying on manual, per-model grid search. Heavy hyperparameter tuning directly contradicts the plug-and-play positioning and must be automated to meet ICLR's usability expectations.
2. Benchmark HiGS on Rectified Flow/Flow Matching models, not just standard DDPM/CFG setups. Since the field has rapidly shifted toward flow matching with linear ODE trajectories, proving the momentum history provides similar or complementary benefits on modern architectures is essential for relevance.
3. Release the complete hyperparameter search protocol, exact seed lists, and standardized evaluation scripts. Reporting single-point FID/HPSv2 without search ranges or evaluation seeds prevents independent verification and severely undermines the credibility of the claimed state-of-the-art results.

# Final Consolidated Review
## Summary
This paper introduces HiGS, a training-free, plug-and-play sampling enhancement for diffusion models that applies a momentum-like correction derived from an exponentially weighted average of past denoiser predictions. Combined with a timestep-dependent weight schedule, optional orthogonal projection, and DCT high-pass filtering, the method consistently improves image fidelity and reduces artifacts across diverse architectures and sampling budgets without increasing neural function evaluations. Extensive experiments demonstrate robust gains in both human-preference metrics and FID, achieving a new unguided state-of-the-art FID of 1.61 on ImageNet 256×256 in 30 steps.

## Strengths
- **Zero NFE overhead with consistent empirical gains:** HiGS requires no additional forward passes, which the authors empirically verify via runtime and memory profiling (Appendix D). The method delivers measurable improvements across modern architectures (DiT, SiT, SDXL/3), distilled variants, and multiple ODE solvers, particularly in computationally constrained low-NFE and low-CFG regimes.
- **Systematic ablation and engineering rigor:** The paper thoroughly validates each design choice in Appendix E, demonstrating that CFG-guided buffering, weight scheduling, projection, and DCT filtering collectively stabilize the sampling trajectory. This structured decomposition clearly separates the core history mechanism from necessary empirical regularizers.
- **Strong practical impact:** Achieving an unguided FID of 1.61 with only 30 steps on ImageNet, alongside consistent HPSv2 win rates (79–96%) across text-to-image benchmarks, establishes HiGS as a highly effective inference-time modifier that delivers state-of-the-art quality without retraining or architectural changes.

## Weaknesses
- **Disconnect between theoretical analysis and practical implementation:** Appendix B proves that incorporating history reduces the Euler solver's local truncation error from $O(h^2)$ to $O(h^3)$, but this relies on a precise step-size-dependent weight $w_k = 2h_k/h_{k-1}$. The actual implementation replaces this with a heuristic square-root time schedule (Eq. 6). Consequently, the theoretical convergence guarantee does not strictly apply to the method as deployed, leaving the improvement empirically validated but theoretically unmotivated in practice.
- **Insufficient benchmarking against dedicated low-NFE/low-CFG baselines:** While Table 6 shows HiGS improves results when applied atop DPM++ and UniPC, the paper lacks direct quantitative comparison against modern few-step ODE solvers (e.g., DPM-Solver-2/3, UniPC at 5–10 steps) and guidance-scheduling techniques (e.g., guidance-interval, PAG) that explicitly target the same low-budget regime. Without these comparisons, it remains unclear whether HiGS provides marginal additive gains or fundamentally outperforms integrated few-step formulations at very low step counts.
- **Overstated novelty of the momentum formulation:** Ablation results in Table 9 show that replacing the EMA history function with simple averaging, random selection, or uniform weighting yields nearly identical HPSv2 scores (0.255–0.261). This indicates the specific "momentum/variance-reduction" framing is not the primary driver of performance; rather, gains likely stem from generic temporal regularization combined with the DCT high-pass filter. The paper should temper the STORM optimization analogy and more clearly identify the core mechanism responsible for the quality boost.

## Nice-to-Haves
- Report statistical variance or confidence intervals for HPSv2 win rates and FID across multiple random seeds, given the unusually high and consistent win rates.
- Provide frequency-spectrum or trajectory diagnostics of the raw correction term $\Delta D_{t_k}$ to explain *why* low-frequency components cause color drift and how orthogonal projection interacts with the score field, moving beyond purely empirical justifications.
- Test cross-model hyperparameter transfer using a single default configuration across latent vs. pixel-space and distilled vs. non-distilled models to better validate the "out-of-the-box" claim.
- Briefly discuss applicability to Rectified Flow / Flow Matching paradigms, given the field's rapid shift toward these architectures.

## Novel Insights
The paper's most valuable contribution lies less in a new numerical integration scheme and more in treating the diffusion denoiser's output trajectory as a temporal signal sequence that benefits from high-pass filtering and directional projection. The finding that the exact *form* of history averaging is largely inconsequential, while frequency-domain filtering of the prediction residual is critical to preventing oversaturation and color shifts, reframes the method from "momentum-based variance reduction" to "temporal high-pass regularization of prediction dynamics." This perspective suggests that future sampling improvements may derive more from signal-processing corrections to model outputs than from pure ODE solver enhancements.

## Suggestions
- Include direct quantitative comparisons against state-of-the-art low-step samplers (e.g., DPM-Solver++, UniPC, or consistency trajectory samplers) and guidance-scheduling methods at matched low NFEs (e.g., 5–15 steps) to contextualize HiGS's marginal utility.
- Revise Appendix B to explicitly clarify that the $O(h^3)$ error bound applies only to a specific step-size scheduling rule not used in practice, reframing the analysis as conceptual motivation rather than a formal guarantee for the deployed algorithm.
- Analyze the frequency content and geometric alignment of the raw history correction term to provide a principled justification for the DCT filter and orthogonal projection, linking these stabilizers to the underlying score estimation error structure.
