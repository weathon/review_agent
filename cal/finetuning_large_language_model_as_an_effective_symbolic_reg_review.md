# Harsh Critic Review
## Section-by-Section Critical Review

**Title & Abstract**
The abstract makes a strong claim that Symbolic-R1 is the *"first LLM to exceed traditional numerical methods in both numerical precision and symbolic form accuracy."* While Table 2 supports this on the proposed SymbArena benchmark, the phrasing should be carefully qualified to avoid overgeneralization. Traditional SR encompasses a wide range of methods (e.g., sparse regression, hybrid symbolic-numeric solvers) that may outperform LLMs in specific regimes (e.g., high-noise, high-dimensional, or PDE discovery). Additionally, the claim of formulating the dataset as *"1.83 billion tokens"* is unconventional for SR literature. Dataset scale is typically reported in number of equations/samples. Clarifying why token count is highlighted and how it aligns with standard LLM training scales would improve clarity and direct comparison with other LLM benchmarks.

**Introduction & Motivation**
The motivation is well-grounded: the tension between pre-trained LLMs' probabilistic generation and the exactness required for symbolic regression is a recognized challenge. The identification of missing fine-tuning datasets as a bottleneck is valid. However, the test set construction introduces a methodological tension. Section 2.1 describes a *"reality enhancement"* step where an LLM retrieves similar human-known equations and filters test samples based on similarity scores. This practice introduces a subjective, model-dependent bias into an otherwise objective benchmark. More critically, filtering toward equations that resemble known scientific laws increases the risk of testing pre-training memorization rather than generalization, which partially contradicts the earlier goal of preventing contamination. The authors should justify how this step does not compromise the benchmark's integrity or inadvertently bias results toward LLM-friendly structures.

**Dataset & Benchmark Construction (Section 2 & 2.2)**
The tree-based generation pipeline is sound and standard in SR literature. Splitting train/test by equation skeletons to prevent structural leakage is a strong design choice. However, the proposed form-level consistency metric ($S_{struct}$, Equation 3) has a critical limitation for symbolic regression: it does not account for algebraic equivalence under commutativity or associativity. The rule-based component similarity relies on set overlaps and character-wise pattern matching. Mathematically equivalent expressions like $A \cdot B + C$ vs. $C + B \cdot A$, or $\log(A) + \log(B)$ vs. $\log(A \cdot B)$, will receive penalized scores despite being symbolically identical. This is a well-known pitfall in SR evaluation. The authors must either (1) incorporate canonicalization (e.g., sympy simplification or associative-commutative tree normalization) into the metric, or (2) explicitly acknowledge and quantify the resulting evaluation bias. The use of GPT-4o as a proxy helps but does not resolve the primary rule-based metric's fragility.

**Method & Algorithm Design (Section 3)**
- **Instruction Tuning (3.1):** Standard and well-described. Using 5 prompt variations per equation to improve robustness is reasonable.
- **Form-GRPO (3.2):** The reward design mixes discrete structural signals with continuous numerical signals. A major missing detail is whether coefficient optimization is applied **during training**. Section 3.3 explicitly mentions numerical optimization in the inference loop (HER), but Section 3.2 does not state if raw LLM-predicted coefficients are passed to the $R_{numerical}$ reward or if an external optimizer refines them before reward computation. If training uses raw coefficients, the numerical reward will be extremely sparse and noisy, potentially destabilizing GRPO. If training uses an optimizer, this must be explicitly stated for reproducibility, along with its computational overhead.
- **HER Inference (3.3):** The Hypothesis-Experiment-Revision loop is essentially an iterative self-correction pipeline combined with external tool use (coefficient optimization and memory recall). While effective, the paper should explicitly frame it as a multi-step agentic workflow rather than a simple inference strategy. The computational cost of 5 iterations $\times$ 6 hypotheses $+$ optimizer calls per hypothesis is substantial. This needs to be contrasted with the computational budget of traditional solvers in the experiments.

**Experiments & Empirical Evaluation (Section 4 & Appendix D)**
- **Baseline Fairness & Hyperparameters:** Appendix C.2 states that traditional SR baselines (PySR, gplearn, AFP, etc.) were run with *"default values as specified in their original publications."* Symbolic regression methods are highly sensitive to hyperparameters (max expression size, population, generations, time limits). Running them with defaults on a novel dataset with potentially different complexity distributions likely handicaps them. ICLR standards require reasonably tuned baselines or, at minimum, a compute/time-matched comparison. The authors should either tune baseline hyperparameters on a validation split or explicitly show wall-clock/FLOP parity.
- **Compute & Efficiency Claims:** Section 4.2 claims Symbolic-R1 achieves results *"only with one-fourth of the inference time cost"* compared to prior LLM baselines, and Table 2 shows it outperforming PySR. However, no wall-clock time, GPU hours, or FLOP measurements are provided. LLM iterative generation (30 total generations per test case) plus coefficient optimization likely consumes orders of magnitude more compute than PySR's `niterations=40` with `maxsize=20`. A compute-matched table or explicit resource measurement is necessary to validate efficiency claims.
- **RL Dataset Scale:** Only 1,000 expressions are used for the Form-GRPO phase (Sec 4.1), compared to 146,590 for SFT. This is unusually small for RLHF/GRPO training. An ablation or justification for this scale is needed, especially since RL on small datasets risks overfitting to specific structural motifs.
- **OOD Generalization (Table 8):** Evaluating on classic benchmarks (Nguyen, Keijzer, etc.) is standard but problematic for LLMs due to pre-training data leakage. These exact equations are ubiquitous in scientific corpora. The strong performance here may reflect memorization rather than true regression capability. The authors should acknowledge this limitation and ideally supplement with structurally perturbed versions or strictly held-out synthetic families to prove generalization.
- **Noise Robustness (Appendix D.2):** Testing only $\sigma=0.001$ absolute Gaussian noise is insufficient for real scientific data, which often exhibits 1-5% relative noise or heteroscedasticity. The metric's sensitivity to higher noise levels should be explored or explicitly noted as a boundary condition.

**Limitations & Broader Impact**
The paper lacks a dedicated limitations section, which is expected for ICLR submissions. Key omissions include: (1) computational overhead of iterative LLM generation vs. continuous optimization; (2) metric sensitivity to non-canonical equivalent forms; (3) inability to handle differential equations, implicit relations, or high-dimensional feature spaces beyond the tested scope; (4) extrapolation performance outside the fixed `dom=10` training range. The broader impact statement is boilerplate. A more concrete discussion on risks (e.g., over-trusting LLM-hallucinated physical laws in scientific workflows, lack of formal guarantees compared to convex/sparse regression methods) would strengthen the paper's maturity.

---

### Overall Assessment
This paper introduces a timely and practically useful direction: adapting LLMs for symbolic regression via dedicated fine-tuning data, form-aware RL, and iterative inference. The SymbArena dataset is a valuable contribution to the community, and the integration of GRPO with structural rewards is methodologically sound. The empirical results are strong, and the ablation studies clearly demonstrate the value of each component. However, for ICLR's rigorous empirical standards, several issues must be addressed before acceptance. The rule-based evaluation metric's failure to handle algebraic equivalence is a significant flaw that could distort reported gains. The comparison with traditional SR baselines lacks hyperparameter tuning and compute parity, making the "outperforming traditional methods" claim difficult to fully validate. Additionally, the coefficient optimization step's role during RL training versus inference needs explicit clarification for reproducibility, and the small RL dataset size warrants justification. If the authors can address the metric's canonicalization limitations, provide compute-matched baselines, clarify the training/inference pipeline, and honestly contextualize generalization limits (memorization on classic benchmarks, noise sensitivity), this work would represent a solid, impactful contribution to LLM-driven scientific discovery.

# Neutral Reviewer
## Balanced Review

### Summary
This paper introduces SymbArena, a large-scale synthetic benchmark for symbolic regression, and Symbolic-R1, a fine-tuned LLM framework that integrates instruction tuning, a structure-aware Group Relative Policy Optimization (Form-GRPO), and a Hypothesis–Experiment–Revision (HER) iterative inference loop. The authors demonstrate that Symbolic-R1 outperforms both traditional evolutionary algorithm baselines and existing LLM-based symbolic regression methods across numerical accuracy and structural consistency metrics.

### Strengths
1. **Targeted Benchmark Construction:** SymbArena addresses a clear gap by providing a large-scale dataset (148k equations) with a deliberate train-test split based on equation skeletons, which effectively prevents structural data leakage during training and evaluation.
2. **Well-Motivated Reward Design:** The Form-GRPO mechanism logically decomposes the reward signal into format validity, structural similarity, numerical fidelity ($R^2$), and exact structural equivalence. The ablation study in Appendix D.1 systematically validates the chosen weighting scheme, demonstrating strong experimental rigor.
3. **Comprehensive Evaluation Framework:** Moving beyond binary correctness or pure numerical fit, the paper introduces a dual-view evaluation combining $R^2$/$Acc_\tau$ with a rule-based and LLM-judged form-level consistency metric. This provides finer-grained insight into where symbolic models succeed or fail.
4. **Clear Technical Pipeline & Reproducibility:** The methodology is logically structured and well-documented. Implementation details, including LoRA configurations, GRPO group sizes, HER iteration counts, and full prompt templates, are transparently reported in the text and appendix, meeting ICLR's high standards for reproducibility.

### Weaknesses
1. **Potentially Unfair Baseline Compute Budgets:** The comparison with traditional SR methods (Table 5) uses restrictive hyperparameters for PySR (`maxsize: 20, niterations: 40`) and GP baselines. Traditional genetic programming typically requires substantially more generations or runtime to converge on complex expressions. Without compute-matched baselines or an efficiency-vs-accuracy curve, the claim of "suppressing traditional methods" lacks rigorous benchmarking support.
2. **Small Test Set Size:** As noted in Section 2.1, the test split contains only 512 equations despite training on nearly 147k. Given the high variance inherent in symbolic discovery and the multi-variable operator space, a test set of this size limits the statistical power and generalization claims of the reported metrics.
3. **Ambiguous Role of Numerical Optimization:** Section 3.3 states that numerical optimization tools are used to refine coefficients for each generated hypothesis during inference. This conflates discrete structure search with continuous parameter optimization. It remains unclear whether the LLM's primary contribution is architectural discovery or if the numerical solver is responsible for the majority of the $R^2$ gains. The paper lacks a breakdown of performance before vs. after coefficient optimization.
4. **Heuristic Structural Metric Limitations:** The rule-based form-level consistency metric (Appendix C.5) relies on Jaccard similarity, character-position matching, and count ratios. This approach cannot recognize mathematical equivalences (e.g., commutative properties, $\sin(x) \leftrightarrow \sin(x+2\pi)$, or $\log(x^2) \leftrightarrow 2\log|x|$), potentially penalizing mathematically valid but syntactically different predictions and inflating structural disparity scores.

### Novelty & Significance
The paper presents a solid, incremental contribution to scientific machine learning. **Novelty** is primarily located in the systematic application of RL fine-tuning (GRPO) with custom structural rewards to symbolic regression, alongside the creation of the SymbArena benchmark. While iterative prompting and hybrid numerical-LLM pipelines are established, formalizing them with a structure-penalized reward function for SR is a meaningful adaptation. **Clarity** is strong; the paper is well-organized, formulas are standard, and the workflow is easy to follow. **Reproducibility** is well-supported through detailed hyperparameter tables, environment specifications, and promised code/data release. **Significance** is moderate-to-high for the AI4Science community, as it demonstrates that task-specific fine-tuning can effectively bridge the gap between LLMs' approximate reasoning and SR's precision demands. However, the reliance on post-hoc numerical optimization and potentially constrained traditional baselines tempers the broader impact claim until further validated under stricter, compute-balanced conditions.

### Suggestions for Improvement
1. **Standardize Baseline Compute Budgets:** Evaluate traditional SR methods (especially PySR) using standard benchmark settings or provide a compute-scaling analysis (e.g., performance vs. iteration count/time). This will clarify whether the LLM's advantage stems from better priors, faster convergence, or simply baseline handicapping.
2. **Isolate LLM Contribution from Coefficient Optimization:** Report structural and numerical performance *before* the numerical refinement step in the HER loop. Clearly frame whether Symbolic-R1 is intended as an end-to-end regressor or a discrete structure search module paired with a traditional solver.
3. **Strengthen the Structural Metric:** Augment the rule-based similarity metric with a symbolic algebra engine (e.g., SymPy's `equals` or canonicalization routines) to recognize mathematically equivalent transformations. This would make the metric more robust to commutativity, distributivity, and trigonometric/logarithmic identities.
4. **Expand Test Coverage & Report Variance:** Increase the test set size or implement $k$-fold cross-validation over the skeleton-split data. Report mean ± standard deviation across multiple random seeds to substantiate statistical significance.
5. **Quantify "Reality Enhancement" Impact:** Provide a brief analysis showing how the LLM-based reality filtering affects the test set distribution. Confirm that retained equations maintain sufficient novelty and do not inadvertently reintroduce pre-training contamination or narrow the operational domain to only textbook formulas.

## How to Improve This Paper

### Missing Experiments (top 3-5 only)
1. Add a compute-fair comparison against PySR and traditional baselines by matching wall-clock time or total FLOPs. Without controlling for inference budget, the claim that an LLM "exceeds traditional numerical methods" is unconvincing as iterative multi-step generation inherently consumes vastly more compute than genetic programming.
2. Evaluate the method on genuine empirical scientific datasets (e.g., real-world physics/chemistry time series with measurement noise) rather than solely on synthetic data filtered by an LLM. Synthetic generation cannot replicate the distributional shifts, missing variables, or irregular sampling found in real scientific discovery tasks.
3. Ablate the external numerical coefficient optimizer used during the Hypothesis–Experiment–Revision inference phase by disabling it. If performance drops drastically without it, the gains are driven by traditional local optimization rather than the LLM's fine-tuned symbolic reasoning, directly undermining the core methodological contribution.

### Deeper Analysis Needed (top 3-5 only)
1. Validate the proposed GPT-4o and heuristic form-consistency metrics against a rigorous mathematical baseline like SymPy's canonicalization and equivalence checking. LLM-as-judge for mathematical syntax is known to suffer from hallucinated similarity, making the reported structural fidelity scores inherently unreliable without ground-truth correlation analysis.
2. Quantify training data memorization versus compositional generalization by reporting exact structural overlap between the 148k training set and the 512-test set. With such a massive synthetic corpus, a 2-fold R² gain could simply reflect template memorization rather than learned symbolic regression capabilities.
3. Provide a theoretical and empirical breakdown of how the HER loop converges versus degrades with additional iterations. Iterative generation in SR often plateaus or diverges; proving that the reflection mechanism actively corrects reasoning errors rather than exhaustively sampling the search space is critical to validate the framework.

### Visualizations & Case Studies
1. Plot prediction accuracy and structural similarity across equation depth and operator aridity bins. This would expose whether the model genuinely learns compositional math rules or merely succeeds on low-complexity skeletons that are overrepresented in the synthetic training distribution.
2. Visualize the GRPO reward trajectories (Form vs. Numerical vs. Equivalent) across training steps with the reward weights ablated. Seeing how the policy balances these competing signals is necessary to prove the Form-GRPO formulation stabilizes training rather than causing reward collapse or mode-seeking behavior.
3. Provide a categorized error analysis showing failure cases broken down by operator type (e.g., nested trigonometry vs. rational functions) and structural ambiguity (e.g., non-unique forms fitting the same data). This reveals the specific boundaries of the model's symbolic reasoning capabilities and where the fine-tuning breaks down.

### Obvious Next Steps
1. Replace the heuristic 6-feature averaging and LLM-judged metrics with exact symbolic simplification and equivalence testing in both the reward function and final evaluation. Standardizing on a mathematically sound equivalence check is mandatory for credible SR evaluation and should have been the default pipeline.
2. Extend experiments to larger foundation models (e.g., 70B+) and math-specialized LLMs to establish scaling behavior and isolate backbone dependence. Evaluating only on a single 7B model leaves it ambiguous whether improvements stem from the fine-tuning methodology or the base model's pre-existing mathematical priors.
3. Remove the circular "LLM reality enhancement" filtering and instead release a strictly held-out subset of canonical scientific equations from peer-reviewed literature for evaluation. Prompting an LLM to validate synthetic equations against other LLM-fetched equations does not prevent pre-training contamination or prove real-world applicability.

# Final Consolidated Review
## Summary
The paper introduces SymbArena, a large-scale symbolic regression benchmark (148k equations) featuring a skeleton-based train/test split to prevent structural leakage, and Symbolic-R1, a fine-tuned LLM framework that combines instruction tuning, structure-aware reinforcement learning (Form-GRPO), and an iterative Hypothesis–Experiment–Revision (HER) inference loop. The authors demonstrate that this pipeline outperforms both traditional genetic programming baselines and existing LLM-based prompting methods on numerical accuracy and structural consistency metrics.

## Strengths
- **Well-Constructed Benchmark for LLM Fine-Tuning:** SymbArena addresses a critical data gap in the SR community. The skeleton-based train/test split is a rigorous design choice that effectively mitigates structural data leakage, enabling fair evaluation of generalization rather than memorization.
- **Effective Structural RL Reward Design:** Form-GRPO’s composite reward (format validity, structural similarity, numerical fit, and equivalence) successfully steers the model away from probabilistic approximation toward syntactically valid expressions. The systematic ablation in Appendix D.1 validates the weighting strategy.
- **Iterative Self-Correction Framework:** The HER inference loop leverages quantitative validation ($R^2$) and LLM-generated reflection to iteratively prune and refine candidates. This shifts the paradigm from single-shot prompt generation to a guided search process, yielding consistent empirical gains.
- **High Reproducibility Standards:** The methodology is transparently documented, with explicit hyperparameters, training/inference configurations, environment specs, and complete prompt templates provided, meeting rigorous empirical standards.

## Weaknesses
- **Fragile Structural Metric Undermines Core Validity Claims:** The rule-based $S_{struct}$ metric computes similarity via set overlaps (Jaccard) and character-wise pattern matching. It fundamentally fails to recognize algebraic equivalences under commutativity, associativity, or standard identities (e.g., $\log(A) + \log(B) \equiv \log(A \cdot B)$). This directly penalizes mathematically correct but syntactically reordered predictions, inflating structural disparity scores and making the primary "form-level consistency" claims unreliable.
- **Unfair Baseline Comparison & Unsupported Efficiency Claims:** Traditional SR baselines (PySR, GPs) are evaluated using static default hyperparameters without compute-matching or tuning. Symbolic regression performance is notoriously sensitive to expression size limits, population counts, and runtime budgets. The claim that Symbolic-R1 "exceeds traditional numerical methods" and achieves results with "one-fourth of the inference time" is unsupported without wall-clock/FLOP-matched baselines or explicit compute budget reporting.
- **Conflated Contribution of External Numerical Optimizer:** The HER inference phase explicitly employs numerical optimization tools to refine coefficients. However, the paper does not clarify whether this optimizer is also applied during the Form-GRPO training phase, nor does it disentangle the LLM’s discrete structural discovery capability from the continuous optimizer’s contribution to $R^2$ scores. Without this breakdown, it is impossible to assess how much of the numerical gain stems from the fine-tuned LLM versus off-the-shelf local optimization.
- **Limited Test Set Size & Unverified Generalization:** The test split contains only 512 equations, which provides limited statistical power for a high-variance task. Furthermore, strong performance on classic benchmarks (Nguyen, Keijzer, R rational) likely reflects pre-training memorization given their ubiquity in scientific corpora. The paper lacks a controlled analysis (e.g., structurally perturbed or strictly held-out synthetic families) to verify true compositional generalization beyond template recall.

## Nice-to-Haves
- Augment or cross-validate $S_{struct}$ with a symbolic algebra engine (e.g., SymPy canonicalization and equivalence checking) to verify that the heuristic metric aligns with mathematical equivalence.
- Report a performance breakdown before and after the HER coefficient optimization step to explicitly isolate the LLM's structural generation capability.
- Expand the noise robustness evaluation (Appendix D.2) to include realistic relative noise levels (e.g., 1–5%) and heteroscedastic error distributions.
- Add a dedicated limitations section addressing computational overhead of iterative LLM calls vs traditional solvers, extrapolation constraints outside the fixed `dom=10` range, and the reliance on tool-use during inference.

## Novel Insights
The work successfully demonstrates that bridging the gap between LLMs' probabilistic generation and symbolic regression's exactness requires explicit structural supervision during fine-tuning, rather than relying on prompt engineering or iterative sampling alone. The ablation studies reveal that numerical fitting and structural fidelity are often competing objectives in LLM reward design; optimizing heavily for $R^2$ without structural penalties leads to reward hacking, where over-optimized coefficients mask incorrect equation skeletons. By framing the LLM as a discrete structural search heuristic paired with external numerical validation, the paper highlights a viable path toward hybrid AI-scientific discovery, where iterative self-reflection replaces exhaustive stochastic search.

## Suggestions
- Clarify whether external numerical optimization is applied during the Form-GRPO training phase or strictly during HER inference. Provide an ablation that disables the optimizer in both stages to rigorously isolate the LLM's pure symbolic reasoning contribution.
- Re-evaluate traditional baselines under compute-matched conditions (e.g., matching wall-clock time or total function evaluations) and explicitly report resource consumption (GPU hours, FLOPs, or iteration counts) to validate the efficiency and superiority claims.
- Validate the heuristic $S_{struct}$ metric by running a subset of predictions through SymPy's equivalence checker. Report the correlation between the heuristic score and formal mathematical equivalence to ensure reported gains reflect true structural recovery.
- Strengthen the generalization analysis by introducing a strictly held-out test partition of structurally perturbed equations or novel operator combinations not present in the training skeletons, and report performance variance across multiple random seeds to substantiate statistical significance.
