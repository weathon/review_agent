# Harsh Critic Review
## Section-by-Section Critical Review

### Title & Abstract
- **Accuracy:** The title accurately reflects the core themes (evolution/IICLL, compression/IB, LLMs, categorization). However, the term "Evolution" might imply a more dynamic or population-level process than the simulated chain-based IICLL represents.
- **Clarity:** The abstract clearly outlines the problem, the two experimental paradigms (English naming and IICLL), and the main findings.
- **Supported Claims:** The abstract claims that "LLMs iteratively restructure... towards greater IB-efficiency" and that this demonstrates "human-aligned semantic categories can emerge in LLMs via the same fundamental principle that underlies semantic efficiency in humans." This risks over-generalizing. The results indicate that **only Gemini 2.0** robustly exhibits this behavior, while other state-of-the-art models converge to low-complexity solutions or degenerate. The abstract should more explicitly caveat that this emergence is highly model-dependent and not a universal property of the current LLM landscape. Additionally, the claim of the "same fundamental principle" is strong; the paper should clarify whether it posits that the LLM's in-context prior mathematically mirrors the human IB-optimizing prior, or merely that both systems arrive at similar efficiency tradeoffs via different mechanisms.

### Introduction & Motivation
- **Motivation & Gap:** The motivation is strong. Bridging cognitive science theories of semantic categorization (IB, Iterated Learning) with LLM analysis addresses a meaningful gap in understanding whether LLMs capture human-like inductive biases or merely memorize textual co-occurrences.
- **Contributions:** Contributions are clearly stated: analysis of English naming across 39 models, IICLL simulation to elicit biases, and comparison to human data.
- **Theoretical Linkage:** The introduction relies heavily on the Information Bottleneck (IB) framework and Iterated Learning (IL). A critical theoretical gap needs addressing: Standard IL theory (Griffiths & Kalish, 2007) proves that iterated learners converge to the learner's **prior distribution** over languages, $p(L)$. The paper asserts that LLMs converge to IB-efficient systems, implying the LLM's prior is an "IB-optimal prior." However, the introduction and background do not sufficiently justify why an LLM's prior should be expected to align with the IB objective function (Eq. 1). Is the bias for IB-efficiency equivalent to a bias for simplicity/regularity? The authors should clarify if they view IB-efficiency as the *cause* of the convergence (i.e., the prior is shaped by compression) or just a *metric* that happens to correlate with the resulting regularity. This distinction is vital for the claim that LLMs share the "fundamental principle" with humans.

### Method / Approach
- **Description & Reproducibility:** The method is generally well-described. The IICLL pipeline (Section 3, Appendix G) replicates Xu et al. (2013) with sensible adaptations (sliding window, constrained generation). Code and data availability statements are good.
- **Assumptions & Justification:**
  - **sRGB Representation:** The method uses sRGB coordinates for textual inputs. Appendix C reports that model performance "degraded across all models" when using CIELAB (a perceptually uniform space), leading to the decision to stick with sRGB. This is a **significant methodological concern**. If the goal is to study "human-aligned" categorization, relying on a color space where models fail on perceptually uniform inputs suggests the models may be learning sRGB text patterns or coordinate mappings rather than perceptual category boundaries. This weakens the claim of human alignment. The authors should discuss why models fail on CIELAB and whether sRGB results truly reflect color categorization or feature-space idiosyncrasies.
  - **Sanity Check Accuracy:** Appendix G notes a "stable comprehension accuracy of ~80%" for the training data recovery. In human IL experiments, transmission noise is a key parameter. A consistent 20% error rate in the sampled training data passed between generations differs from controlled human studies and may artificially accelerate convergence to majority labels (low complexity) or introduce path dependence. The impact of this noise level on the theoretical convergence guarantees of the IL chain should be discussed.
  - **Stochasticity:** The ICL generation uses temperature 0.6 and top_p 0.9 (Appendix J). This introduces stochasticity into the "production" phase. While this mimics human variability, the sampling of training data for the next generation is deterministic based on the previous output. The interaction between sampling noise in generation and deterministic sampling for training should be clarified.
- **Edge Cases/Failure Modes:** The method acknowledges that smaller models and window size 0 lead to degenerate systems (Appendix L, K). However, it is unclear if the "non-degenerate" systems in larger models are robust or if slight perturbations in the initial random seed or prompt phrasing would cause collapse. The robustness of the IICLL convergence is not fully stress-tested.

### Experiments & Results
- **Testing Claims:** The experiments address the claims. The English naming study shows variation in alignment/IB. The IICLL experiment attempts to show restructuring toward IB-efficiency.
- **Baselines:**
  - **Nearest Neighbor (App M):** The comparison to a Nearest Neighbor (NN) classifier on sRGB is useful but weak. An NN classifier on raw sRGB triples without clustering is expected to overfit or produce fragmented boundaries, especially with sparse training data ($6 \times k$ examples). A stronger baseline would be a rational model or a standard clustering algorithm (e.g., k-means, Gaussian Mixture Models, or Ward's linkage) applied to the training data and generalizing to the test set. If such a baseline achieves similar or better IB-efficiency, it would challenge the claim of a unique cognitive-like bias in LLMs.
  - **Lack of "Simplicity-Only" Baseline:** Since many models converge to "low-complexity solutions," it is essential to distinguish an *IB-optimal* bias from a *simplicity* bias. A baseline that minimizes complexity (e.g., assigning all to one term, or maximizing partition contiguity regardless of informativeness) should be included. The NN baseline checks against feature proximity, but not against structural simplicity.
- **Statistical Reporting:** The paper mentions "twenty iterated learning chains" for humans but does not explicitly state the number of chains/replicates per condition for the LLMs in the main results. ICLR reviewers expect clear reporting of the number of runs (chains) per model and condition, with error bars or variance metrics on the IB-efficiency and alignment plots. Claims like "significantly better" in Appendix M require specification of the statistical test used. If only a few chains are run for Gemini 2.0, the statistical power may be insufficient.
- **Cherry-picking/Scope:** The baseline comparison focuses on the $k=14$ condition as the "most challenging." While justifiable, results should ideally show baseline comparisons across all $k$ (2 through 6) to prove the trend holds generally and isn't an artifact of the high-vocabulary setting.
- **Dataset/Metrics:** The use of the WCS grid and NID/IB metrics is appropriate and standard in the field. The efficiency loss metric ($\epsilon$) is well-defined.

### Writing & Clarity
- **Clarity of Contribution:** The writing is generally clear, but the distinction between the **dynamics of Iterated Learning** and the **optimization of IB** is occasionally blurred. Phrases like "restructure... towards greater IB-efficiency" sound like gradient-based optimization, whereas IL is a sampling process from a prior. The text should clarify that "towards greater IB-efficiency" describes the trajectory of the systems relative to the IB bound, not an active minimization of Eq. 1 by the model during inference.
- **Figures/Tables:** (Based on the provided content) References to figures are clear. The mode maps and trajectory plots are standard for this type of work. However, the text describing the IICLL results (Section 4.2) appears fragmented in the extraction, but assuming the figures (Fig 3, 4, etc.) are complete, they need to clearly label model variability across chains, not just trajectories.
- **Instruction Bias:** The prompts (Appendix J) include instructions like "Based on the preceding examples... limit your response...". There is a risk that the LLM is not "evolving" a language but is **pattern-completing** a request for regularization. Frontier models are heavily RLHF'd to be helpful and structured. The model might be interpreting the task as "clean up these noisy labels," which correlates with regularity/simplicity. The authors should acknowledge this confound: the IICLL paradigm might be eliciting the model's instruction-following preference for structure rather than a latent prior over color terms.

### Limitations & Broader Impact
- **Acknowledged Limitations:** The authors acknowledge the lack of communication pressure, the unknown origins of the bias, and the need to test more domains.
- **Missed Limitations:**
  - **Black-box Nature:** The strongest result relies on Gemini 2.0, a closed-source model. This limits the ability to dissect whether the result stems from architecture (MoE), training data curation, or specific ICL capabilities. The paper should discuss how this limits the generality of the findings compared to open-weight models.
  - **Ecological Validity of Color:** As noted in Method, the failure on CIELAB and reliance on sRGB is a major limitation for claiming "human-aligned" categorization. Humans categorize based on perception, not hex/sRGB coordinates. This gap should be elevated in the limitations.
  - **Task Simulation vs. Cognitive Process:** The paper assumes IICLL reveals inductive biases. However, LLMs may simulate the *appearance* of cultural evolution because such patterns exist in their training data (e.g., texts describing language evolution or regularization). The discussion should distinguish between the model *possessing* a bias and the model *simulating* behavior consistent with that bias based on meta-knowledge.
- **Broader Impact:** The impact is primarily scientific, advancing our understanding of LLM alignment with cognitive principles. There are no immediate negative societal impacts, though reliance on LLM-generated categories in accessibility or design tools (as mentioned in Intro) carries risks if the models do not align with human perception (reinforcing the sRGB concern).

### Overall Assessment
This paper presents a compelling and novel intersection of cognitive science and LLM evaluation. The application of the Iterated In-Context Language Learning (IICLL) paradigm to probe for Information Bottleneck efficiency is creative and addresses a significant question about whether LLMs harbor human-like inductive biases for semantic compression. The finding that English alignment and IB-efficiency scale with model size and instruction tuning is a solid empirical contribution.

However, the contribution is tempered by several critical concerns that must be addressed:
1.  **Theoretical Specificity:** The link between IL convergence to a prior and IB-optimality is theoretically under-specified. The paper needs to disentangle whether the observed bias is specific to the IB tradeoff or a more general bias for simplicity/regularity common in IL.
2.  **Mechanism vs. Artifact:** The reliance on sRGB inputs (due to CIELAB failure) questions the perceptual alignment of the results. Furthermore, the possibility that Gemini 2.0 is simulating regularization due to instruction-tuning rather than exhibiting a latent cognitive bias must be rigorously discussed or controlled for.
3.  **Rigor of Baselines and Statistics:** The comparison to a weak NN baseline is insufficient to claim emergent IB-efficiency. Stronger clustering/rational baselines are needed. Additionally, statistical reporting on the number of IICLL chains and variance across replicates is required to support claims of significance.
4.  **Generalizability:** The strong claims in the title and abstract about "LLMs" are largely supported by a single model (Gemini 2.0). The narrative should be calibrated to reflect that this is a behavior of current frontier models with strong ICL, rather than a universal property of the class.

If the authors can strengthen the baseline comparisons, clarify the theoretical relationship between IL priors and IB, address the sRGB/CIELAB discrepancy, and provide robust statistical analysis of the IICLL chains, this work would be a strong addition to the ICLR program. Currently, the ideas are excellent, but the evidentiary support needs greater rigor.

# Neutral Reviewer
## Balanced Review

### Summary
This paper investigates whether LLMs naturally develop human-aligned, information-theoretically efficient semantic categories by replicating human color-naming experiments through a novel Iterated In-Context Language Learning (IICLL) paradigm. Across 39 models, the authors demonstrate that larger, instruction-tuned models exhibit higher complexity and alignment with English, and that frontier models possess a latent inductive bias toward Information Bottleneck (IB) efficiency, restructuring artificial systems toward near-optimal tradeoffs without explicit optimization.

### Strengths
1. **Theoretically grounded and methodologically novel**: The paper effectively bridges cognitive science and ML by adapting the IB framework (Zaslavsky et al., 2018) and iterated learning (Xu et al., 2013) into a prompt-based LLM workflow (IICLL), enabling direct quantitative comparison between LLM and human inductive biases.
2. **Systematic and large-scale empirical evaluation**: Testing 39 models across size, modality, and training stages provides clear scaling insights. The analysis of Olmo 2 checkpoints (Appendix F) compellingly isolates instruction-tuning as the primary driver of alignment improvements, adding developmental rigor.
3. **Strong baselines and validation**: The Nearest Neighbor sRGB baseline (Appendix M) convincingly shows that Gemini 2.0's evolved systems surpass simple feature-based clustering, supporting claims of non-trivial, emergent IB-efficiency. The use of NID and IB-efficiency loss provides standardized, reproducible metrics aligned with established literature.
4. **High reproducibility and transparency**: The paper provides full code, exact prompts, model identifiers, dataset sources, and detailed appendices on procedures (e.g., sliding window ablation in Appendix K), meeting and exceeding standard reproducibility expectations for empirical ML.

### Weaknesses
1. **Narrow domain scope limits generalization**: The entire empirical analysis focuses on color naming. While color offers uniquely rich human data, it is perceptually grounded and lower-dimensional than abstract linguistic categories. The claim that findings apply to "semantic systems" broadly is supported only by a brief mention of preliminary non-color results without empirical evidence.
2. **Methodological heterogeneity in decoding**: Frontier models (Gemini) use API-enforced constrained generation, while open-weight models rely on log-probability scoring of continuation tokens. This difference introduces potential biases in how models handle the constrained vocabulary, which could artificially influence complexity/accuracy measurements and generational stability.
3. **IICLL stability and heuristic design constraints**: The $k=14$ vocabulary condition shows high instability and frequent failures (Appendix G), and the sliding 10-interaction window, while empirically motivated, remains an ad hoc approximation of human short-term memory. The paper does not deeply analyze how context-window limits or API truncation affect cultural transmission fidelity over generations.
4. **Limited analysis of failure modes**: While the paper notes that most models collapse to low-complexity solutions, it lacks mechanistic or architectural investigation into why this occurs (e.g., attention saturation, instruction-tuning misalignment, or lack of in-context structural reasoning). A deeper diagnostic section would strengthen the technical contribution.

### Novelty & Significance
The paper presents high novelty by formalizing Iterated in-Context Language Learning (IICLL) as a rigorous paradigm for probing LLM inductive biases toward information-theoretic compression. For ICLR, the work offers strong significance: it shifts LLM evaluation from static benchmarking toward dynamic, evolution-inspired analysis of emergent semantic structure. The demonstration that human-like IB-efficiency can arise without explicit optimization has important implications for AI alignment, interpretability, and cognitive modeling of language models. Clarity is excellent, with logical progression, well-defined metrics, and comprehensive appendices. Reproducibility is strong, though API-dependent components may require careful resource tracking for independent verification. Overall, the work comfortably meets ICLR's bar for rigor and impact, with the primary limitation being domain scope rather than technical soundness.

### Suggestions for Improvement
1. **Expand to at least one abstract semantic domain**: Include a full IICLL experiment on a non-perceptual domain (e.g., action verbs, spatial relations, or emotion terms) to substantiate broader claims about semantic categorization and generalizability.
2. **Standardize constrained decoding across models**: Implement token-masking or identical logit-based constrained sampling for all models to eliminate the API vs. open-weight decoding discrepancy, ensuring that complexity/accuracy metrics reflect true inductive biases rather than inference artifacts.
3. **Deepen failure mode analysis**: Investigate why non-frontier models converge to low-complexity systems (e.g., via attention pattern visualization, representation probe analysis, or temperature/context ablations) to move beyond observational claims toward mechanistic understanding.
4. **Systematically evaluate IICLL hyperparameters and constraints**: Provide a formal study of how sliding window size, context length limits, and sampling temperature affect generational trajectories, clarifying the robustness of cultural evolution dynamics under different LLM architectural constraints.

# Spark Finder Review
## How to Improve This Paper

### Missing Experiments (top 3-5 only)
1. **Multiple seeds and runs per condition:** Report results across multiple independent IICLL seeds with error bars or confidence intervals; without statistical aggregation, the claim that models "recapitulate the wide range of near-optimal IB-tradeoffs" could reflect high-variance outliers rather than robust convergence.
2. **Shuffled-label control experiment:** Run IICLL where the initial pseudo-word labels are randomly permuted across the color space rather than spatially contiguous; if models still converge to efficient, clustered systems, the result demonstrates spatial clustering capability, not an inductive bias toward semantic compression.
3. **Entropy-aware IB computation:** Compute IB complexity using the full stochastic output distributions (via log-probabilities) rather than deterministic mode maps; using hard assignments artificially deflates complexity estimates and directly biases the conclusion that models converge to "low-complexity solutions."
4. **Cross-domain pilot or claim removal:** Include a minimal IICLL experiment in a non-color domain (e.g., geometric shapes or sound symbolism) to support the abstract's claim of domain generality; without it, the paper overgeneralizes from a single perceptual domain.

### Deeper Analysis Needed (top 3-5 only)
1. **Mechanism distinguishing Gemini from collapsing models:** Analyze attention patterns or effective context utilization to explain why Gemini discovers the IB tradeoff while other large instruct models collapse to low-complexity; without this, the "strongest in-context capability" explanation remains a correlation rather than a mechanistic insight.
2. **Chain degeneracy and survival rates:** Quantify how frequently chains collapse to single-category systems across models and vocabulary sizes; high mortality rates would undermine the "cultural evolution" framing, as human ILC chains in the referenced literature maintain structure without such frequent collapse.
3. **Impact of uniform sampling on IB bounds:** Discuss how sampling training examples uniformly in IICLL interacts with the IB objective, which theoretically assumes a specific meaning prior $p(m)$; if uniform sampling shifts the optimal tradeoff curve, the claim of "near-optimal efficiency" requires re-evaluation against the correct baseline.
4. **LLM response reliability analysis:** Measure test-retest consistency by querying the same LLM multiple times on identical prompts in a zero-shot setting; if stochastic noise dominates, the observed "category systems" reflect decoding temperature effects rather than stable semantic representations, invalidating IB interpretations.

### Visualizations & Case Studies
1. **Longitudinal partition evolution maps:** Provide side-by-side visualizations of category boundaries at Generation 1, 5, 10, and Final for both a successful chain (Gemini) and a collapsing chain; this directly tests the claim of "iterative restructuring" versus immediate simplification.
2. **Scatter of all chain endpoints on the IB plane:** Plot final-generation efficiency and alignment scores for *every* IICLL seed alongside the human WCS contour and optimal IB bound; this reveals distribution overlap and prevents cherry-picking supportive trajectories.
3. **In-context retrieval case study:** Visualize which specific training examples or recent interactions drive label assignments for ambiguous boundary colors; this exposes whether the model is merely performing nearest-neighbor lookups in context (trivial feature matching) or abstracting category boundaries (human-like bias).
4. **Context window sensitivity plot:** Show IB efficiency and alignment as a function of the sliding history window size (0, 5, 10, 20); this reveals whether the observed evolution is robust to memory constraints or an artifact of prompt engineering.

### Obvious Next Steps
1. **Ablation on context composition:** Isolate the effect of the 6*k bootstrap sample from the sliding window of 10 recent interactions by running IICLL with the window removed; without this, the results cannot be attributed to cultural transmission rather than short-term prompt coherence.
2. **Decoding strategy ablation:** Compare results using top-k sampling vs. nucleus sampling vs. deterministic greedy decoding at temperature 0; since ILC stability heavily depends on generation variance, failing to ablate decoding leaves the "inductive bias" claim entangled with hyperparameter choices.
3. **Comparison to simple iterative clustering baselines:** Run iterative K-Means or agglomerative clustering on the same color features and bootstrap procedure to establish a performance floor; without showing LLMs outperform trivial clustering algorithms on IB efficiency, the novelty of the "emergent bias" claim is questionable.
4. **Retraction or empirical support for cross-domain claims:** Delete statements about applicability to other semantic domains unless accompanied by data; ICLR reviewers penalize unsupported generalization, and retaining these claims without evidence weakens the paper's scientific rigor.

# Final Consolidated Review
## Summary
This paper investigates whether large language models harbor human-like inductive biases for semantic compression by adapting iterated learning paradigms to an in-context prompt format (IICLL). Evaluating 39 models on color naming, the authors show that alignment and Information Bottleneck (IB) efficiency scale with model size and instruction-tuning, and that frontier models (specifically Gemini 2.0) can iteratively restructure artificial naming systems toward near-optimal IB tradeoffs akin to human cultural transmission.

## Strengths
- **Methodologically novel paradigm:** The introduction of Iterated in-Context Language Learning (IICLL) effectively bridges cognitive science and LLM evaluation, providing a rigorous, prompt-native framework to simulate cultural transmission and directly compare LLM inductive biases with human behavioral data.
- **Comprehensive and transparent empirical scope:** Testing 39 models across families, sizes, modalities, and training checkpoints (e.g., Olmo trajectory analysis in Appendix F) yields clear, actionable insights into how instruction-tuning drives alignment. The detailed procedural appendices, open code, and explicit prompt specifications strongly support reproducibility.
- **Demonstrated emergence beyond trivial feature matching:** The systematic comparison showing Gemini 2.0's evolved systems surpassing a Nearest Neighbor baseline in both IB-efficiency and human-alignment convincingly rules out simple coordinate memorization as the sole driver of results.

## Weaknesses
- **Input representation limits claims of human-aligned perception:** The paper acknowledges that models fail to produce coherent systems when prompted with perceptually uniform CIELAB coordinates, leading to a reliance on sRGB numeric triples (Appendix C). This strongly suggests the models are learning coordinate-to-label mappings or textual sRGB priors rather than genuine perceptual category boundaries. Framing these results as "human-aligned categorization" is therefore overstated without addressing why perceptual uniformity breaks down and how it impacts the ecological validity of the color domain.
- **Insufficient statistical aggregation and baseline rigor for IICLL:** While the paper notes 20 human chains in the referenced literature, it does not explicitly report the number of independent IICLL runs per LLM condition, nor does it provide variance estimates, confidence intervals, or statistical tests for convergence claims. Without multiple seeds and error bands, trajectories that appear robust could reflect high-variance paths or prompt sensitivity. Furthermore, the NN baseline, while useful, is structurally weak for an iterated setting; the absence of iterative clustering or rational learner baselines (e.g., iterated k-means, Bayesian category models) leaves it unclear whether the observed efficiency stems from a unique LLM compression bias or simply standard feature-space regularization amplified over generations.
- **Conflating inductive bias with instruction-tuning artifacts:** Frontier models like Gemini 2.0 are heavily instruction-aligned to produce structured, helpful outputs. The IICLL setup may inadvertently trigger a "clean up these noisy labels" pattern-completion behavior rather than revealing a latent linguistic prior for compression. The paper acknowledges that the origins of the bias are unclear but does not attempt to disentangle RLHF-driven regularization from genuine inductive structure, which is critical for claims about emergent cognitive alignment.
- **Overgeneralized cross-domain claims without evidence:** The abstract and conclusion suggest findings apply to broader semantic systems, yet the only non-color evidence is described as "initial" without empirical support in the main text. Retaining broad generalizability claims without concrete experiments or explicit scoping weakens the scientific precision of an otherwise focused study.

## Nice-to-Haves
- Compute IB complexity using full stochastic output distributions (via log-probabilities) rather than deterministic mode assignments to capture the true entropy of the LLM's categorical uncertainty.
- Run a minimal IICLL ablation in an abstract domain (e.g., verbs or spatial prepositions) to validate cross-domain applicability, or explicitly limit the scope to perceptually grounded categorization.
- Clarify the theoretical relationship between Iterated Learning convergence to $p(L)$ and the IB metric in the background section, specifying whether IB-efficiency is treated as an emergent property of the prior or the underlying optimization pressure.

## Novel Insights
The work successfully shifts LLM evaluation from static benchmark performance to dynamic, evolution-inspired analysis, revealing that modern instruction-tuned models possess a latent regularization pressure that closely mirrors human semantic compression. The sharp divergence between Gemini 2.0 and other equally large or newer models suggests that effective in-context structural reasoning and information-theoretic optimization are not monotonic functions of scale, but likely emerge from specific intersections of in-context learning capacity, alignment training, and architectural priors. This reframes how we should evaluate emergent cognition in LLMs: not as a guaranteed byproduct of pretraining, but as a contingent property of how models are optimized to handle iterative, low-data generalization.

## Suggestions
- Explicitly report the number of independent IICLL chains/seeds per model and condition, and plot mean trajectories with variance shading or error bars. Include statistical tests (e.g., cluster-based permutation tests or ANOVAs across generations) when claiming "significant" improvements.
- Introduce iterative rational/clustering baselines (e.g., iterated k-means, Gaussian Mixture, or a Bayesian learner with a simplicity prior) using the exact same bootstrap sampling protocol to establish a performance floor and isolate LLM-specific inductive biases.
- Add an ablation or discussion section that directly tests or addresses the instruction-tuning confound, for example by comparing models with varying RLHF exposure, or by running a "shuffled label" control to see if regularization persists without spatial contiguity cues.
- Revise the framing around color inputs in the main text to accurately reflect the sRGB limitation, positioning the findings as evidence of "feature-space alignment" or "symbolic category learning" rather than direct perceptual categorization.
