# Harsh Critic Review
## Section-by-Section Critical Review

**Title & Abstract**
The title accurately reflects the paper's scope. However, the abstract's claim that EmbodiedMAE *"consistently outperforms state-of-the-art vision foundation models (VFMs) in both training efficiency and final performance"* requires tightening. "Training efficiency" in the context of representation learning typically refers to pre-training compute/data efficiency, or downstream fine-tuning sample efficiency. The experiments (Figure 6) primarily show faster policy convergence (fewer environment steps to reach higher success rates), but this is not explicitly quantified against baselines (e.g., steps to reach 80% success, or wall-clock training time). I recommend clarifying whether this refers to downstream sample efficiency, and providing explicit metrics to support the claim rather than relying on visual inspection of learning curves.

**Introduction & Motivation**
The motivation is well-grounded: existing 3D VFMs suffer from a domain gap between large-scale static scenes and precise tabletop manipulation, and naive 3D integration often degrades policy performance. The contributions are clearly stated. One conceptual gap: the introduction positions the lack of efficient/scalable 3D architectures as a primary bottleneck, but the proposed architecture is a multi-modal MAE with cross-attention decoding, which closely follows MultiMAE (Bachmann et al., 2022). The architectural novelty lies more in the adaptation to robotics (stochastic modality masking, distillation from a Giant model, and domain-specific pre-training data) than in fundamental architectural changes. I suggest explicitly framing the contribution around the *training paradigm and data-centric scaling* rather than implying a novel architecture, to avoid overclaiming.

**Methodology**
- **Section 2.1 (3D Data Collection):** The use of the ZED SDK for temporal fusion and metric depth extraction is practical, but the pipeline lacks technical transparency. How is temporal consistency actually enforced under fast robot arm motions or dynamic occlusions common in manipulation? The paper claims "superior and consistent depth quality" (Figure 2) but provides no quantitative depth error metrics (e.g., RMSE against LiDAR ground truth if available, or reprojection error). Without this, the claim of "high-quality" remains qualitative. Additionally, the dataset release status is unclear. ICLR requires clear statements on whether DROID-3D will be publicly released or restricted by licensing, as this heavily impacts reproducibility and impact.
- **Section 2.2 (Multi-Modal Encoder):** The stochastic masking via symmetric Dirichlet distribution is sound and well-motivated by prior work. However, the point cloud patchification raises a structural concern: RGB and Depth are gridded, yielding a fixed $L = HW/256$ tokens, while point clouds yield $L=N$ tokens via FPS/KNN ($N=8192$ points downsampled, but how many tokens are kept after grouping?). How are these heterogeneous token sequences concatenated when the total mask ratio is fixed? The sequence length will vary across samples depending on which modality contributes more unmasked tokens. Does this require dynamic padding or attention masking that differs across batches? This operational detail is missing and affects reproducibility.
- **Section 2.3 (Multi-Modal Decoder & Eq. 1):** The reconstruction loss for point clouds uses MSE on group center-normalized coordinates. In 3D representation learning, point cloud reconstruction is almost universally optimized with permutation-invariant geometric losses like Chamfer Distance or Earth Mover's Distance (EMD). MSE on normalized coordinates treats points as ordered sequences or ignores permutation invariance, which is atypical and may explain why the PC modality struggles with real-world noise (Section 3.4). I strongly recommend justifying this design choice with an ablation comparing MSE vs. Chamfer loss, or explicitly acknowledging it as a limitation.
- **Section 2.4 (Model Distillation):** The three-level feature alignment (Bottom, Middle, Top) is well-standardized. However, the rationale for using 90% masking during distillation (vs. 84% for pre-training) is briefly hand-waved as "aggressive masking... significantly decreases training costs without compromising representational quality." A short analysis of why the student benefits from higher masking when guided by a frozen teacher (e.g., does it force reliance on the alignment loss rather than lazy reconstruction?) would strengthen the methodological rigor.

**Experiments & Results**
- **Section 3.1 (Setup & Baselines):** The policy backbone (compact RDT, ~40M) is a good choice for isolating representation quality. However, the integration mechanism is underspecified. Are visual features frozen and passed via cross-attention as Keys/Values (per Appendix A.1), or is the entire visual backbone fine-tuned? This drastically changes the interpretation of "training efficiency" and "scaling behavior." ICLR expects explicit fine-tuning protocols (frozen vs. LoRA vs. full tuning, LR differences, etc.).
- **Section 3.2 (MAE Predictions / RQ1):** The qualitative reconstruction experiments (Figure 3) are visually appealing. However, the claim that the recoloring experiment *"suggests EmbodiedMAE has implicitly learned object-level semantic segmentation"* is an over-interpretation. Propagating an altered color to a single region during reconstruction could simply stem from local patch continuity, low-frequency color smoothing, or the decoder's reliance on structural priors, without true semantic boundary awareness. I recommend toning down this claim to "object-level structural correspondence" unless a quantitative segmentation probe is added.
- **Section 3.3 (Overall Comparison / RQ2):** Table 1 and Figure 6 demonstrate clear improvements over strong baselines. However, statistical reporting is absent. Learning curves (Figure 6) lack shaded confidence intervals across the 3 seeds × 50 trials, making it difficult to judge whether the gap over SPA or DINOv2 is statistically significant, especially in the "Very Hard" MetaWorld category where performance is volatile. ICL review standards typically require error bars or variance reporting for such claims.
- **Section 3.4 (Real-World / RQ3):** This section contains a critical methodological gap. Appendix A.2 states the SO100 platform only has *dual RGB cameras* with no depth sensor. Yet, Figure 8 reports results for `EmbodiedMAE-RGBD` and `EmbodiedMAE-PC` on the SO100. How are depth and point clouds generated in the real-world SO100 experiments? If they are derived via multi-view stereo or an AI depth estimator, this must be explicitly stated, as it conflates the claimed "high-quality ZED SDK pre-training data" with noisy estimated depth at inference time. If they are only simulated or estimated, the real-world multi-modal claims for SO100 should be clarified or removed. Additionally, reporting only 10 trials per task is low for robotics evaluation; success rates should ideally be reported over 30-50 trials with confidence intervals to account for hardware jitter (which the authors themselves acknowledge is significant on the SO100).
- **Section 3.5 (Ablations):** The ablation studies (Table 4, Appendix Tables 2-3) efficiently cover masking ratios, alignment placement, and policy architecture (RDT vs. ACT). The inclusion of ACT is valuable for generalization claims. However, the ablation table formatting in the main text is ambiguous due to PDF parsing, and the experiments are only run on LIBERO. Running these on MetaWorld or real-world tasks would strengthen the conclusions, given the domain shifts involved.

**Writing & Clarity**
The paper is generally well-structured and readable. The separation of RQ1/RQ2/RQ3 helps organization. However, some terminology is slightly overloaded (e.g., "Giant" vs "Large" variants are sometimes used interchangeably in the text, causing minor confusion about which scale is reported in specific tables). The distinction between pre-training compute and policy fine-tuning compute should be clarified throughout. No major logical flow impediments were found.

**Limitations & Broader Impact**
The limitation section correctly identifies the lack of native language instruction support. However, several critical practical limitations are omitted:
1. **Hardware dependency:** The dataset relies on ZED SDK, which ties the pre-training distribution to a specific stereo depth sensor pipeline. Does this limit generalization to LiDAR (like RealSense L515/D435) or monocular depth?
2. **Point cloud sensitivity:** The paper thoroughly documents PC degradation in the real world (Section 3.4, Appendix B.3) but frames it purely as a "sensor noise" issue. As noted in Methodology, the use of MSE for PC reconstruction may compound this. A more honest limitation would acknowledge that MAE with MSE loss may not be the optimal pre-training objective for raw geometric data compared to diffusion-based or geometric losses.
3. **Compute transparency:** The paper mentions a 1.1B parameter Giant model pre-trained from scratch, but provides no wall-clock training time, total GPU hours, or energy/carbon footprint. ICLR increasingly expects compute reporting for foundation models.

### Overall Assessment
This paper presents a solid, well-engineered contribution to embodied representation learning, with a clear motivation, a practical dataset enhancement (DROID-3D), and comprehensive empirical validation across simulation and real-world platforms. The results are generally convincing and address a recognized gap in 3D foundation models for manipulation. However, the paper falls short of ICLR's top-tier standards due to several unresolved technical and empirical details. Specifically, the lack of clarity on how multi-modal inputs are acquired on the dual-RGB SO100 platform, the unconventional use of MSE for point cloud reconstruction, insufficient statistical reporting (confidence intervals, trial counts), and vague claims around "training efficiency" need addressing. The architectural novelty is incremental, lying primarily in the data-centric adaptation and distillation paradigm rather than fundamental algorithmic advances. With clarifications on the experimental setup, stronger statistical validation, and more precise acknowledgment of methodological limitations (particularly regarding PC reconstruction and SO100 depth acquisition), this work would be a strong fit for ICLR. As is, it is competitive but requires substantive revisions to meet the bar for empirical rigor and reproducibility.

# Neutral Reviewer
## Balanced Review

### Summary
This paper introduces EmbodiedMAE, a unified multi-modal masked autoencoder designed to learn spatial-aware visual representations for robot manipulation from synchronized RGB, depth, and point cloud inputs. To train the model, the authors construct DROID-3D by processing the full original DROID dataset with high-fidelity, temporally consistent depth maps using ZED SDK. Through stochastic cross-modal masking and feature-aligned distillation, EmbodiedMAE achieves strong scaling behavior and consistently outperforms existing vision foundation models across 70 simulation tasks and 20 real-world manipulation benchmarks on two distinct robot platforms.

### Strengths
1. **Comprehensive and Rigorous Empirical Evaluation:** The evaluation spans both simulation (LIBERO, MetaWorld) and real-world platforms (SO100, xArm), testing across 90 total tasks with diverse input modalities (RGB, RGBD, Point Cloud) and policy backbones (diffusion-based RDT and ACT). This breadth strongly aligns with ICLR's emphasis on thorough representation learning evaluation.
2. **High-Value Dataset Contribution:** DROID-3D directly addresses a critical bottleneck in embodied AI by providing temporally consistent, metric depth and point cloud annotations for 76K trajectories. The systematic comparison against existing datasets (BridgeDataV2, RH20T, original DROID) and prior estimation methods (SPA/CrocoV2) clearly justifies the dataset's necessity and quality.
3. **Practical Multi-Modal Design and Distillation Pipeline:** The stochastic Dirichlet masking strategy effectively forces cross-modal inference without introducing modality bias, while the three-level feature alignment distillation (Bottom/Middle/Top) enables efficient scaling from a 1.1B Giant teacher to sub-100M variants without significant performance loss (Table 4). The qualitative reconstructions (Figure 3) convincingly demonstrate learned object-level semantic and geometric reasoning.
4. **Strong Reproducibility and Engineering Practices:** The paper provides detailed hyperparameters (Table 8), policy architecture specifications (Appendix A.1), explicit benchmark configurations, and latency analysis (Table 13). Adhering to HuggingFace Transformers conventions and committing to open-source code ensures high downstream usability.

### Weaknesses
1. **Incremental Methodological Novelty:** The core architecture relies on well-established components: Vision Transformer backbones, MAE reconstruction, cross-attention fusion, and knowledge distillation. While the application to embodied 3D data is timely, the paper introduces no new learning objectives, architectural primitives, or theoretical insights, making the contribution primarily empirical and engineering-focused rather than algorithmically novel.
2. **Limited Statistical Robustness in Real-World Experiments:** Real-world evaluations are conducted with only 10 trials per task. Given the inherent variability in physical robotics (hardware jitter, lighting, friction), this sample size is insufficient for reliable statistical claims. No error bars, confidence intervals, or significance testing are provided in Figures 7/8 or Table 9.
3. **Unbalanced Baseline Comparisons and Modality Handling:** Several vision-centric baselines (R3M, SigLIP, VC-1) are known to underperform on precise manipulation tasks, which may inflate perceived gains. Additionally, the DINOv2+Depth baseline is explicitly described as a "naïve" patch-summing approach, which does not reflect optimized modern 3D adaptations. Furthermore, the point cloud modality requires extensive external post-processing (radius filtering, stereo-guided depth correction, voxel downsampling; Appendix B.3) to achieve competitive real-world performance, raising questions about the robustness of the raw representation.
4. **Lack of Language Grounding Limits Contemporary Relevance:** As noted by the authors, EmbodiedMAE is strictly a vision backbone. Given the rapid shift of the field toward vision-language-action (VLA) models (OpenVLA, π0, Octo), the paper does not demonstrate how these visual representations integrate with or improve instruction-conditioned policies, limiting immediate impact on current VLA research trajectories.

### Novelty & Significance
- **Novelty:** Moderate. The architectural contributions (Dirichlet masking, cross-aligned distillation) are solid adaptations of existing MAE and distillation paradigms to a new domain. The primary novelty lies in the DROID-3D dataset construction and the systematic empirical validation of multi-modal 3D representations for tabletop manipulation.
- **Significance:** Moderate to High. The paper addresses a genuine gap in scalable, domain-aligned 3D representations for robotics. DROID-3D and the distillation recipe provide practical, high-value resources for the community. While vision-only, the strong empirical performance and clear pipeline design make it a useful baseline for 3D perception in manipulation.
- **Clarity:** High. The methodology, training pipeline, and experimental protocols are well-structured and logically presented. Figures and tables effectively support the claims (modulo parser artifacts).
- **Reproducibility:** High. Detailed hyperparameters, compute specifications, dataset processing pipelines, policy architectures, and a commitment to open-source code meet ICLR's reproducibility standards.

### Suggestions for Improvement
1. **Strengthen Real-World Statistical Reporting:** Increase the trial count per task to at least 20-30, or explicitly report variance (error bars/standard deviation) and perform statistical significance testing to substantiate real-world claims.
2. **Ablate Masking Distribution Hyperparameters:** Provide results varying the Dirichlet concentration parameter `α` to demonstrate sensitivity and justify the chosen symmetric distribution, rather than only ablating the total masking ratio.
3. **Improve Baseline Fairness:** Replace or supplement the "naïve" DINOv2+Depth baseline with a properly optimized 3D-aware alternative (e.g., a LoRA-finetuned DINOv2 with a learnable depth fusion module) to ensure gains are not partially attributable to weak baseline engineering.
4. **Demonstrate VLA Compatibility:** Even without full language pre-training, include a preliminary experiment plugging EmbodiedMAE features into a standard VLA (e.g., OpenVLA or a frozen LLaVA-style language adapter) to show that the learned spatial representations transfer effectively to instruction-following pipelines, addressing the field's current trajectory.

# Spark Finder Review
## How to Improve This Paper

### Missing Experiments (top 3-5 only)
1. Add a direct comparison against parameter-efficient fine-tuning baselines (e.g., LoRA on DINOv2 or SigLIP) under identical compute budgets. Without it, claims of superior "training efficiency" and scaling could simply reflect pre-training compute disparities rather than genuine architectural advantages.
2. Report real-world RGBD success rates on the xArm platform. The paper heavily emphasizes RGBD's theoretical robustness and superiority over point clouds, but fails to test it in reality, leaving a critical gap between simulation findings and practical deployment claims.
3. Include strong, natively 3D representation baselines (e.g., RVT-2, fine-tuned PonderV2, or a properly tuned 3D-VLA vision encoder). Comparing only against a naive DINOv2 depth patchifier and DP3 inadequately substantiates the "outperforms SOTA" claim in multi-modal settings.

### Deeper Analysis Needed (top 3-5 only)
1. Isolate the specific contribution of 3D data from dataset curation by training EmbodiedMAE-2D on raw DROID alongside the 3D variant. This ablation is required to prove that synchronized depth/point clouds directly drive performance gains rather than cleaner trajectories or better preprocessing.
2. Quantify how much the downstream policy actually relies on 3D inputs during action generation. Analyze cross-attention attribution maps or run modality-dropout evaluations on the policy network itself to verify that 3D information isn't just ignored or washed out by the frozen representation.
3. Provide standardized compute metrics (GPU-hours, FLOPs, peak VRAM, and wall-clock training time) across all models and baselines. ICLR requires explicit resource accounting to validate claims of "computational efficiency" and "scaling behavior," which are currently unsupported qualitative assertions.

### Visualizations & Case Studies
1. Provide side-by-side trajectory rollouts specifically contrasting RGB-only vs. RGB-D inputs on identical xArm tasks. This is necessary to visually expose *when and how* depth cues prevent the grasp misalignments or collisions that the authors claim baseline models suffer from.
2. Plot representation similarity metrics (e.g., Centered Kernel Alignment or linear probe accuracy) across RGB, depth, and point clouds before and after the decoder's cross-attention. Without this, the claim of "effective explicit modal fusion" lacks mathematical evidence beyond pixel-level reconstruction errors.
3. Visualize the exact raw sensor output versus the "enhanced" point cloud inputs alongside policy rollouts. This will immediately expose whether the model's success relies on its learned architecture or the heavy, manual filtering pipeline required to make real-world sensor noise passable.

### Obvious Next Steps
1. Implement a robust sim-to-real transfer analysis by injecting realistic depth sensor noise (dropout, multi-path interference, specular highlights) into the LIBERO and MetaWorld simulators. Testing only against perfect synthetic depth entirely masks the primary domain gap that cripples 3D models in reality.
2. Provide quantitative depth evaluation metrics (e.g., RMSE, AbsRel, or boundary alignment scores) comparing DROID-3D, SPA's AI-estimated depth, and ground-truth LiDAR. Qualitative figure comparisons are insufficient for ICLR to accept DROID-3D as a rigorously validated dataset contribution.
3. Ablate the Dirichlet stochastic masking strategy against fixed or uniform masking schedules. Without proving that dynamically shifting modality visibility strictly improves downstream policy transfer over simpler masking, the core methodological novelty remains unverified.

# Final Consolidated Review
## Summary
This paper presents EmbodiedMAE, a unified 3D multi-modal masked autoencoder framework designed for robot manipulation, paired with DROID-3D, a large-scale dataset supplementing DROID with temporally consistent, metric depth and point clouds. By employing stochastic Dirichlet masking across modalities and a feature-aligned distillation pipeline, the model learns spatially coherent representations that consistently outperform state-of-the-art vision foundation models in downstream policy sample efficiency and final success rates across 90 simulation and real-world tasks.

## Strengths
- **Domain-aligned dataset construction:** DROID-3D directly tackles the scarcity of high-quality 3D manipulation data by processing 76K trajectories with ZED SDK temporal fusion and metric depth extraction. The explicit comparison against raw DROID, BridgeDataV2, and AI-estimated depth (Section 2.1, Figure 2) clearly justifies its value for pre-training spatial awareness in tabletop settings.
- **Practical scaling and distillation recipe:** The three-level feature alignment distillation (Bottom/Middle/Top) effectively transfers knowledge from a 1.1B-parameter teacher to sub-100M student models with minimal performance degradation. Combined with the stochastic masking strategy, this yields a computationally efficient pipeline that demonstrates clear scaling behavior without requiring proportional increases in fine-tuning compute (Table 4, Finding 2).
- **Comprehensive multi-platform validation:** Evaluating across LIBERO, MetaWorld, and real-world SO100/xArm setups with both diffusion (RDT) and transformer (ACT) policy backbones provides robust evidence of representation generalizability. The consistent margins over vision-centric (DINOv2), language-grounded (SigLIP), and embodied-specific (SPA, DP3) baselines substantiate the core claim that natively fused 3D representations improve manipulation performance.

## Weaknesses
- **Unspecified fine-tuning protocol for visual backbones:** While Section 3.1 and Appendix A.1 detail the policy network architecture, the paper does not explicitly state whether the pre-trained VFM encoders are frozen, partially adapted (e.g., via LoRA), or fully fine-tuned during policy optimization. Given that "training efficiency" and "scaling behavior" are central claims, ambiguity regarding gradient flow, backbone learning rates, and update steps makes it difficult to disentangle gains from representation quality versus differential fine-tuning dynamics.
- **Qualitative claims exceed empirical support:** The recoloring experiment (Figure 3, column 12) is interpreted as evidence that the model has "implicitly learned object-level semantic segmentation." In masked autoencoding, localized color propagation can readily arise from patch-adjacency smoothing, low-frequency priors, or decoder inductive biases rather than categorical boundary detection. Additionally, repeated assertions of "training efficiency" rely on visual inspection of learning curves without explicit quantitative benchmarks (e.g., environment steps or wall-clock time to reach a fixed success threshold), weakening the rigor of comparative efficiency claims.

## Nice-to-Haves
- Quantify depth quality metrics (e.g., stereo reprojection error or consistency scores) for DROID-3D versus raw DROID and SPA's estimated depth to further substantiate the dataset's "high-fidelity" claim beyond qualitative side-by-sides.
- Include a controlled 2D-vs-3D ablation (e.g., training an EmbodiedMAE-2D variant on the identical DROID-3D trajectories) to isolate the performance contribution of the 3D modalities from potential dataset curation or preprocessing benefits.
- Report real-world evaluation variance (standard deviation or confidence intervals) across the 10 trials per task on both SO100 and xArm to better account for acknowledged hardware jitter and environmental stochasticity, aligning with broader robotics evaluation standards.
- Compare against parameter-efficient fine-tuning baselines (e.g., LoRA on DINOv2 or SigLIP) under matched compute budgets to verify that "training efficiency" gains are intrinsic to the representation rather than artifacts of full fine-tuning vs. PEFT training dynamics.

## Novel Insights
The work effectively reframes 3D representation learning for robotics as a data-alignment and scaling challenge rather than an architectural one. By demonstrating that a standardized MAE framework, when pre-trained on temporally consistent manipulation-specific depth data and scaled via structured distillation, robustly outperforms heavily engineered implicit-3D and vision-centric models, the paper establishes that current policy failures in spatial reasoning stem primarily from pre-training distribution shifts (static/in-the-wild data vs. precise tabletop interaction) rather than backbone limitations. This redirects community efforts from designing complex 3D-specific encoders toward curating temporally coherent multi-modal data and optimizing efficient scaling recipes for existing transformer paradigms.

## Suggestions
- Explicitly clarify the visual backbone fine-tuning protocol (frozen vs. partial/full tuning, learning rate schedules, and optimizer separation from the policy head) in Section 3.1 or Appendix A.1 to ensure the "training efficiency" and scaling claims are interpretable and reproducible.
- Temper the interpretation of the recoloring visualization to "structural correspondence" or "spatial coherence," and supplement efficiency claims with a concrete metric (e.g., steps to 80% success rate or fine-tuning wall-clock parity) to meet ICLR's quantitative rigor standards.
- Expand the inference latency analysis (Table 13) to include end-to-end policy rollout latency (encoder + RDT/ACT action generation), providing a more complete picture of real-time deployment feasibility for closed-loop manipulation pipelines.
