# Harsh Critic Review
## Section-by-Section Critical Review

### Title & Abstract
- **Does the title accurately reflect the contribution?** Yes. It clearly signals the focus on monotonic neural networks and the mechanism (constrained learning).
- **Does the abstract clearly state the problem, method, and key results?** The problem (partial monotonicity enforcement), method (chance-constrained primal-dual learning), and claimed results (flexible trade-off, architecture-agnostic, adaptive) are present. 
- **Are any claims in the abstract unsupported by the paper?** The claim that the algorithm needs "only small extra computations" is questionable. Computing input-gradient-based monotonicity penalties requires backpropagating through the computation graph with respect to inputs, effectively doubling (or significantly increasing) the forward/backward pass cost compared to standard weight updates. The abstract does not acknowledge this overhead, which may mislead readers about computational efficiency. Additionally, claiming "no pre-processing such as tuning of the regularization" is slightly overstated: while Lagrange multipliers are updated automatically, the method introduces new hyperparameters (`α`, `t`, dual learning rate `γ_μ`) that still require specification.

### Introduction & Motivation
- **Is the problem well-motivated? Is the gap in prior work clearly identified?** Yes. The motivation for monotonicity in interpretability and safety-critical control is strong. The dichotomy between architecture-constrained methods and heuristic regularization-based methods is clearly articulated, highlighting pain points (reduced expressivity, manual regularization tuning, expensive certification).
- **Are the contributions clearly stated and accurate?** The three stated contributions (Flexibility, Advanced Capability, Adaptability) are accurate relative to the text. However, the novelty of applying chance constraints + primal-dual updates to neural network training is incremental relative to established stochastic constrained optimization literature (e.g., Eisen et al. 2019, Chow et al. 2017 on risk-sensitive RL), which the introduction does not sufficiently distinguish from.
- **Does the introduction over-claim or under-sell?** Slight over-claim regarding "does not impose any constraints on neural network architectures." Gradient-based monotonicity enforcement inherently assumes a differentiable computational path w.r.t. the monotonic features. Architectures using operations like Batch Normalization, residual connections with subtractive terms, or non-monotonic activation combinations can naturally violate partial monotonicity regardless of training constraints, or cause optimization instability. This architectural reality is not acknowledged.

### Method / Approach
- **Is the method clearly described and reproducible?** Algorithm 1 is well-structured. The derivation from Eq. 3 → 4 → 6 is logically sequenced. The use of uniform sampling over the input domain `X` to enforce global monotonicity (rather than just empirical data) is a thoughtful design choice.
- **Are key assumptions stated and justified?** The method assumes continuous differentiability (Sec 2.1). However, experiments and common practice use ReLU, which is non-differentiable at zero. The paper does not clarify how subgradients are handled in `∂f/∂z_m` or how this affects the chance constraint satisfaction. Additionally, Section 3.1 introduces auxiliary `t > 0`, but the implementation section states `t` is often "fixed to a small positive constant." Fixing `t` deviates from the adaptive update rule (9b) and breaks the derivation's equivalence; this choice needs theoretical justification or empirical validation against the proposed update.
- **Are there logical gaps in the derivation or reasoning?** Claim 1 establishes a convex upper bound on the indicator function, which is standard in chance-constrained programming. However, the paper does not analyze the tightness of this relaxation or how `α` and `t` jointly control the feasible set geometry. If `t` is too large, the constraint becomes vacuous; if too small, the `[·]_+` operator becomes sharp and gradients vanish/wrap. The interaction is not discussed.
- **Are there edge cases or failure modes not discussed?** Primal-dual methods can suffer from dual variable explosion if constraints are consistently infeasible or if primal-dual learning rates are mismatched. Algorithm 1 projects `µ` and `t` to the non-negative orthant but lacks convergence guarantees for the highly non-convex landscape of deep networks. The paper cites convex stochastic primal-dual literature but does not address how non-convexity impacts saddle-point attainment or whether the method might converge to a point that violates the constraint due to local minima or mode collapse in the Lagrangian.
- **For theoretical claims: are proofs correct and complete?** Claim 1's proof sketch is mathematically sound as an application of Markov-like bounding. However, it lacks formal treatment of sample complexity: how many uniform samples `N` are needed to approximate the expectation over `X` within a desired confidence? Given the curse of dimensionality, bounding gradients over a continuous high-dimensional input domain is notoriously difficult (akin to certified robustness), and this fundamental limitation is not addressed.

### Experiments & Results
- **Do the experiments actually test the paper's claims?** They test prediction performance on standard datasets and a control task. However, they fail to directly measure the core claim: *monotonicity satisfaction*. The chance constraint `α=0.1` implies ~90% of inputs should satisfy non-negative gradients. This empirical violation rate is never reported in tables or figures. Without it, we cannot assess if the "flexible trade-off" works or if monotonicity is silently violated.
- **Are baselines appropriate and fairly compared?** Baselines are comprehensive and recent. However, the evaluation protocol is non-standard and suspicious: "report the mean and standard deviation of the best five results" out of ten runs. ICLR standards typically require reporting the full set of independent runs to avoid selection bias. Reporting only the top 5 inflates perceived stability and masks failure modes of the primal-dual updates.
- **Are there missing ablations that would materially change conclusions?** Yes. Critical ablations are missing: 
  1. `α` sweep: Does `α=0.0` (strict monotonicity) work, or does performance collapse? How does `α=0.5` degrade performance?
  2. Update `t` vs. fix `t`: Does the theoretical advantage of updating `t` (9b) materialize?
  3. `N` (uniform samples) effect: How many constraint samples are needed before training time blows up?
  4. Compute overhead: Wall-clock training time vs. unconstrained and SMNN is absent, contradicting the "small extra computations" claim.
- **Are error bars / statistical significance reported?** Standard deviations are present but calculated over a cherry-picked subset (best 5/10). No statistical significance tests (e.g., paired t-tests or Wilcoxon) are performed to confirm improvements over baselines like SMNN or LMN.
- **Do the results support the claims made, or are they cherry-picked?** The control task (Sec 4.2) plots only the "best results" from five runs. Given the sensitivity of RL training, this selection likely hides instability inherent to the primal-dual updates in the RL loop. The paper should report mean ± std for control costs as well.
- **Are datasets and evaluation metrics appropriate?** Datasets are standard for this subfield, but Heart Disease (n=303, 61 test) and Auto MPG (n=392, 79 test) are extremely small for deep learning claims. Improvements on such datasets can be dominated by seed variance. ICLR typically expects larger-scale validation or a rigorous discussion of statistical power when using small datasets.

### Writing & Clarity
- **Are there sections that are confusing or poorly explained?** Section 3.1's transition from Eq. 5 to Eq. 6 is mathematically clear, but the practical interpretation of `t` is muddled. The text states "fixed the auxiliary variable `t=1×10^{-4}`" in experiments, which renders Eq. 9b and the theoretical derivation of updating `t` moot. This discrepancy between theory (adaptive `t`) and practice (fixed `t`) should be clarified or the fixed choice justified.
- **Are figures and tables clear and informative?** Figure 2 control trajectories are informative, and Figure 3 nicely shows input-output behavior and output truncation. Tables 1 & 2 are readable, though missing monotonicity violation metrics reduces their informational value for the core contribution.

### Limitations & Broader Impact
- **Do the authors acknowledge the key limitations?** The conclusion mentions extending to general inequality constraints but does not discuss the method's own limitations. There is no "Limitations" section.
- **Are there fundamental limitations they missed?** Several critical limitations are absent: 
  1. **Incompatibility with modern blocks:** Architectures using normalization layers (BatchNorm/LayerNorm) mix monotonic and non-monotonic features, making partial monotonicity guarantees unstable or impossible without modification.
  2. **Curse of dimensionality in constraint sampling:** Sampling from `Uni(X)` to bound gradients becomes ineffective as input dimension grows, similar to randomized smoothing or certified robustness. The method likely struggles on high-dimensional data (e.g., images, text embeddings).
  3. **Optimization stability:** Primal-dual updates on non-convex losses can lead to oscillatory dual variables or training collapse, which is not mitigated or discussed.
- **Are there failure modes or negative societal impacts not discussed?** The ethics statement is boilerplate. While monotonicity often promotes fairness, enforcing monotonicity *incorrectly* (e.g., assuming monotonicity in loan defaulter prediction based on sensitive proxies like zip code or education length without domain validation) can bake in systemic biases. The paper should briefly acknowledge that monotonicity must be domain-validated, as blind enforcement can harm model accuracy or reinforce flawed assumptions.

### Overall Assessment
This paper proposes a well-motivated and practically relevant approach to enforcing partial monotonicity in neural networks using chance-constrained primal-dual learning. The formulation is clean, the baseline suite is comprehensive, and the application to safe frequency control demonstrates potential beyond standard supervised benchmarks. However, the submission currently falls below the ICLR acceptance bar due to several structural and reporting issues. The most significant concerns are: (1) the absence of empirical monotonicity violation rates, which prevents verification of the core constraint satisfaction claim; (2) the non-standard reporting protocol ("best 5 of 10 runs") across both supervised and RL experiments, which likely masks instability and overstates performance gains; (3) the lack of ablations for newly introduced hyperparameters (`α`, `t`, dual learning rate) and missing compute/overhead analysis, undermining the "adaptive and low-cost" claims; and (4) insufficient discussion of practical architectural incompatibilities (e.g., BatchNorm, residual subtractions) and theoretical gaps regarding non-convex primal-dual convergence and high-dimensional constraint sampling. The contribution is solid, but to meet ICLR standards of rigor and reproducibility, the authors must report full run statistics, include monotonicity violation metrics, provide hyperparameter ablations and compute benchmarks, and explicitly address the method's architectural and dimensional limitations. With these revisions, the paper could be competitive, but in its current form, the experimental evidence is insufficient to support the broad claims made.

# Neutral Reviewer
## Balanced Review

### Summary
This paper proposes a stochastic primal-dual learning framework to enforce partial monotonicity in neural networks with general architectures. By reformulating monotonicity as a differentiable chance constraint and introducing an adaptive dual-variable penalty, the method eliminates the need for manual regularization tuning while allowing a controllable trade-off between monotonicity satisfaction and predictive performance. The algorithm is evaluated on multiple classification, regression, and a safety-critical frequency control task, demonstrating competitive or superior performance compared to architecture-constrained and regularization-based baselines.

### Strengths
1. **Architecture-Agnostic Design:** The framework operates directly on the gradient-based training loop without requiring specialized layers, weight constraints, or lattice structures. This preserves the expressivity of standard networks, as evidenced by the successful application across diverse tasks and the parameter efficiency reported in Tables 1 and 2 (e.g., fewer parameters than SMNN/DLN on Auto MPG and Heart Disease).
2. **Automated Constraint Adaptation:** The primal-dual formulation adaptively scales the monotonicity penalty via the dual variable $\mu$ (Algorithm 1, Eq. 9c), directly addressing the well-known limitation of empirical regularization tuning in prior work (Liu et al., 2020; Sivaraman et al., 2020). This makes the method immediately deployable to new datasets without case-by-case hyperparameter sweeps.
3. **Practical Flexibility via Chance Constraints:** The introduction of $\alpha \in [0, 1]$ allows practitioners to explicitly trade off strict monotonicity against overall prediction loss (Section 3.1). This is practically valuable for domains where monotonicity serves as a strong tendency rather than a hard safety requirement.
4. **Broad Empirical Scope & Reproducibility Details:** The evaluation spans five public benchmarks and extends to a realistic reinforcement learning control setting (39-bus power system), with clear trajectory and cost comparisons in Figures 2-3. Appendices A and B provide dataset splits, network structures, learning rates, and optimizer settings, aligning well with ICLR's reproducibility expectations.

### Weaknesses
1. **Lack of Theoretical Guarantees for Non-Convex Setting:** The paper does not analyze the convergence properties of the proposed SPDG updates (Eq. 9) under non-convex neural network losses, nor does it bound the final monotonicity violation rate. While Claim 1 provides a conservative surrogate, the gap between the surrogate expectation and the true chance constraint violation remains unquantified, leaving practitioners without theoretical guarantees on constraint satisfaction.
2. **High-Dimensional Sampling Inefficiency:** Constraint gradients are estimated using samples from $\text{Uni}(\mathbf{X})$ (Section 3.1, Algorithm 1). For high-dimensional inputs like the Blog Feedback dataset (276 features), uniform sampling over a hypercube is exponentially sparse and may poorly approximate the true violation distribution in practice. The paper does not discuss this curse of dimensionality or propose mitigations.
3. **Incomplete Statistical Reporting:** While mean ± standard deviation is reported over multiple runs, statistical significance tests (e.g., paired t-tests or bootstrap confidence intervals) are absent. Some reported gains fall within the variance of baselines (e.g., Blog Feedback RMSE and Loan Defaulter accuracy in Table 1), making it difficult to conclusively attribute improvements to the proposed method rather than stochastic training variance.
4. **Ambiguous Terminology in Constraint Surrogate:** Claim 1 refers to the surrogate $E[[\mathbf{t} - \partial f/\partial \mathbf{x}_m]_+] \le \alpha \mathbf{t}$ as an "inner approximation." Mathematically, this is an outer approximation of the indicator function that defines a strictly smaller (more conservative) feasible parameter set. This phrasing may confuse readers regarding feasibility guarantees.

### Novelty & Significance
The novelty lies in adapting stochastic primal-dual chance-constrained optimization specifically to enforce partial monotonicity in deep networks. While constrained optimization and CVaR-style surrogates are established in machine learning, their targeted formulation for monotonicity with an automated dual-adaptation mechanism is a meaningful and timely contribution. The significance for the ICLR community is moderate-to-high: it addresses a practical bottleneck (rigid architectures and manual regularization) while maintaining state-of-the-art predictive performance. The work falls short of groundbreaking theoretical innovation but provides a robust, easily implementable tool that aligns well with ICLR's emphasis on practical, well-evaluated methodological advances.

### Suggestions for Improvement
1. **Add an $\alpha$-Tradeoff Ablation Study:** Since flexibility is a core claimed contribution, provide an empirical ablation sweeping $\alpha \in [0, 0.01, 0.05, 0.1, 0.2]$ to visualize and quantify the monotonicity violation rate vs. test accuracy/RMSE trade-off on at least two datasets. This would strongly validate the practical utility of the chance constraint formulation.
2. **Clarify Surrogate Mathematics & Empirical Violation Metrics:** Correct the "inner approximation" terminology in Claim 1 to accurately reflect that it is a conservative surrogate. Additionally, report the empirical post-training monotonicity violation rate (e.g., percentage of uniform test samples where $\partial f/\partial x_i < 0$) to quantify how well the surrogate translates to actual constraint satisfaction.
3. **Address High-Dimensional Constraint Sampling:** Discuss the limitations of $\text{Uni}(\mathbf{X})$ sampling in high dimensions. Experimentally compare it with alternative strategies (e.g., training distribution sampling with domain shift penalties, or adaptive boundary sampling) or provide a theoretical/practical justification for why $N=128$ uniform samples suffice despite the dimensionality.
4. **Strengthen Statistical Rigor:** Include paired statistical significance tests across random seeds to confirm that performance gains over strong baselines (SMNN, LMN, Certified MNN) are statistically meaningful rather than within training noise. Ensure all baselines are reported consistently across tables, or explicitly justify omissions due to incompatibility (e.g., ReLU-only certification limits).

### Deeper Analysis Needed (top 3-5 only)
1. **Analyze convergence and stability of the stochastic primal-dual updates in this non-convex, non-smooth Lagrangian setting**; standard convergence proofs for convex constrained optimization do not apply, leaving the adaptive mechanism theoretically unjustified and prone to oscillatory training.
2. **Critically evaluate the uniform sampling strategy over X for high-dimensional features** (e.g., 276-dimensional Blog Feedback); the curse of dimensionality makes uniform sampling statistically vacuous, meaning the constraint is only enforced on irrelevant, low-density regions, which directly undermines the claimed generalization to unseen inputs.
3. **Formalize the role and bounds of the auxiliary variable t**; since t acts as a smoothing parameter controlling the tightness of the chance-constraint approximation, fixing it to 1e-4 contradicts the adaptive claim, while its gradient-based update requires analysis to prevent explosion or vanishing that would effectively turn the method into a static regularizer.

### Visualizations & Case Studies
1. **Plot 1D partial dependence or 2D surface slices along multiple monotonic feature dimensions for COMPAS and Loan Defaulter**; this will directly reveal whether the network learns truly monotonic behavior across the input space or merely exploits dataset-specific correlations to satisfy a loose statistical constraint.
2. **Track the trajectory of the dual variable μ and the batch-wise constraint violation over training epochs**; visualizing these will prove the primal-dual mechanism is actively detecting and penalizing non-monotonic behaviors rather than functioning as a passive, opaque penalty term.

### Obvious Next Steps
1. **Scale the framework to modern, larger architectures (e.g., ResNet-style MLPs, attention layers, or large tabular backbones)** and benchmark on larger datasets to prove the claim that the method "inherits advantages of state-of-the-art networks" beyond tiny, under-parameterized models.
2. **Ablate α=0 (strict constraint) vs. α>0 (chance constraint) to quantify the exact performance gain from allowing violations**; this should have been included to empirically justify the introduction of chance constraints and show scenarios where relaxing monotonicity is genuinely beneficial versus harmful.
3. **Detail the practical implementation of the required gradient-of-gradient computation (∂²f/∂x∂θ)** and benchmark it against standard double-backprop libraries; omitting the computational bottleneck and efficiency strategy leaves the method unimplementable as a drop-in module for standard deep learning pipelines.

# Final Consolidated Review
## Summary
The paper proposes a stochastic primal-dual optimization framework to enforce partial monotonicity in neural networks with general architectures. By reformulating gradient-based monotonicity as a differentiable chance constraint over a uniform input distribution, the method uses an adaptive dual penalty to automatically balance constraint satisfaction against predictive loss, eliminating manual regularization tuning. The approach is evaluated on tabular classification/regression benchmarks and a safety-critical power grid control task, demonstrating competitive accuracy and parameter efficiency compared to architecture-constrained baselines.

## Strengths
- **Architecture-agnostic enforcement:** The framework operates directly on the gradient-based training loop without requiring restrictive structural modifications (e.g., lattice layers, non-negative weights, or min-max pooling), preserving the expressivity and trainability of standard networks.
- **Automated constraint adaptation:** The primal-dual formulation dynamically scales the monotonicity penalty via the dual variable $\boldsymbol{\mu}$, directly addressing the well-documented bottleneck of manual regularization tuning in prior certification-based or heuristic methods.
- **Principled performance-monotonicity trade-off:** The chance-constraint formulation ($\alpha$) provides a clear mechanism to relax strict monotonicity when it conflicts with predictive performance, offering practical flexibility for domains where monotonicity acts as a strong tendency rather than a hard safety limit.
- **Broad empirical validation:** The evaluation spans standard tabular benchmarks and extends to a realistic reinforcement learning control setting (39-bus frequency regulation), with detailed reporting of network architectures, hyperparameters, and control trajectories in the appendices.

## Weaknesses
- **Missing empirical verification of the core constraint:** The central contribution is enforcing a chance constraint on gradient signs, yet the paper never reports the empirical monotonicity violation rate or the distribution of negative partial derivatives on held-out data. Without quantifying how often the constraint is actually satisfied (or how $\alpha=0.1$ maps to real-world violation percentages), the effectiveness of the constraint cannot be validated, rendering performance tables insufficient to assess the method's primary function.
- **Curse of dimensionality in constraint sampling:** The framework estimates constraint expectations by sampling uniformly from the input domain $\mathcal{X}$. For high-dimensional features (e.g., Blog Feedback with 276 dims), uniform hypercube sampling is exponentially sparse and statistically vacuous, meaning the penalty is likely enforced on low-density, out-of-distribution regions rather than data-relevant subspaces. The paper does not address this limitation or propose domain-aware sampling alternatives.
- **Non-standard evaluation protocol:** Reporting results as the "mean and standard deviation of the best five results" out of ten runs (Sec 4.1) is statistically non-standard and likely masks optimization instability inherent to primal-dual training. This selective reporting inflates perceived stability and prevents rigorous statistical comparison with baselines that report full-run aggregates.
- **Theory-practice discrepancy and unquantified computational overhead:** The theoretical framework proposes adaptively updating the smoothing parameter $\mathbf{t}$, but all experiments fix it to $10^{-4}$, effectively reducing the mechanism to a static hyperparameter despite claims of adaptivity. Furthermore, the abstract's claim of "only small extra computations" overlooks that computing $\nabla_{\boldsymbol{\theta}}(\partial f / \partial \mathbf{x})$ requires second-order backpropagation, which introduces non-trivial memory and runtime costs that are neither benchmarked nor discussed.

## Nice-to-Haves
- Sweep $\alpha$ across a meaningful range to empirically plot the monotonicity-accuracy trade-off curve on representative datasets, explicitly demonstrating the claimed flexibility.
- Include qualitative visualizations such as partial dependence plots along monotonic dimensions, and track the trajectory of the dual variable $\boldsymbol{\mu}$ during training to confirm the adaptive penalty mechanism functions as intended rather than stagnating.
- Clarify the practical implementation of the second-order gradient term (e.g., autograd hooks vs. double backprop) and provide wall-clock training time comparisons to contextualize actual throughput vs. baselines.

## Novel Insights
The paper meaningfully bridges constrained optimization and monotonic neural network design by framing gradient sign constraints as chance-constrained expectations differentiable via stochastic primal-dual updates. While primal-dual learning and smooth surrogate constraints are established concepts, their targeted formulation to automate regularization strength for partial monotonicity—without modifying network topology or resorting to expensive MILP/SMT certification—is a pragmatic advancement. The method shifts the burden from manual heuristic tuning to a principled, gradient-driven constraint satisfaction loop that naturally integrates with standard deep learning pipelines, offering a more scalable pathway for enforcing domain priors in tabular and control applications.

## Suggestions
- Compute and report the empirical monotonicity violation rate (fraction of test/uniform samples with $\partial f/\partial x_i < 0$) for all trained models to directly validate the chance-constraint formulation and the impact of $\alpha$.
- Replace the "best 5 of 10" reporting with full statistics across all independent random seeds for both supervised and RL tasks, and apply appropriate statistical tests to confirm significance over strong baselines like SMNN.
- Discuss or experimentally compare constraint sampling strategies for high-dimensional inputs (e.g., sampling from the empirical training distribution, Sobol sequences, or adaptive boundary sampling) to mitigate the statistical vacuity of uniform hypercube sampling.
- Explicitly benchmark wall-clock training time and peak memory usage, and justify the decision to fix $\mathbf{t}$ experimentally (e.g., via a brief ablation showing that adaptive $\mathbf{t}$ updates introduce instability without meaningful performance gains).
