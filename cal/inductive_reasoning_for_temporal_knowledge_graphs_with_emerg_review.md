# Harsh Critic Review
## Section-by-Section Critical Review

### Title & Abstract
- **Accuracy:** The title accurately captures the core problem (inductive reasoning) and the specific setting (temporal KGs with emerging entities).
- **Clarity of Abstract:** The abstract clearly states the limitation of closed-world TKG models, the empirical observation (~25% emerging entities causing degradation), the proposed TRANSFIR framework (codebook clustering + interaction chain transfer), and the key quantitative result (28.6% avg MRR gain).
- **Support for Claims:** The claims are well-supported by the paper's content. The 28.6% average improvement aligns with the reported gains in Table 1. No unsupported or exaggerated claims are present.

### Introduction & Motivation
- **Motivation & Gap:** The problem is well-motivated by the realistic scenario of entities arriving without interaction history. The gap is clearly identified: existing TKG methods assume a closed entity set, and existing inductive KG methods (e.g., InGram, ULTRA) are designed for static graphs where unseen entities typically have at least some known connections, failing to address zero-history temporal emergence.
- **Contributions:** Contributions are explicitly stated and accurate relative to the proposed pipeline.
- **Calibration:** The introduction slightly risks overstating the "widespread" nature of emerging entities by tying the 25% figure directly to the 5:2:3 chronological split. While the split reveals these entities, the authors should clarify whether this proportion reflects natural data evolution in real-world streams or if it is an artifact of the evaluation partitioning strategy. The contrast with prior inductive work is solid, but a brief discussion on how this differs from text-based zero-shot/knowledge transfer methods would strengthen the positioning.

### Method / Approach
- **Clarity & Reproducibility:** The Classification–Representation–Generalization pipeline is logically structured. However, there are notable methodological gaps that affect reproducibility:
  - **VQ Gradient Routing (Sec 4.1, Eq 1):** The cluster assignment `π(e)` uses an `argmin` over frozen embeddings, which is non-differentiable. The paper does not specify how gradients flow through this discrete step to update the codebook `C` or downstream modules. Is a Straight-Through Estimator (STE), Gumbel-Softmax, or soft assignment used during training? This is critical for reproducing optimization behavior.
  - **Empty Interaction Chain Handling (Sec 4.2, Eq 5 & 8):** By definition in Sec 2, for an emerging entity at `tq = te(e)`, the historical interaction set is empty. Consequently, the Interaction Chain `C_q` for the query entity is empty. Equation 8 computes relation-guided attention over `C_q`, but the paper does not explicitly state how `h_IC_eq` is initialized or computed when the sequence length is zero. Does the model default to a zero vector, the cluster prototype, or skip this step for the query? This must be clarified.
  - **Top-K Relation Filtering (Eq 6):** Selecting historical interactions based on cosine similarity to the *query relation* `h_rq` assumes static relation embeddings capture temporal relevance well. There is no justification or ablation showing whether this heuristic outperforms temporal proximity or uniform sampling, nor how it handles cases where the query relation is novel or dissimilar to all past relations.
  - **Transfer Regularization (Eq 11):** The mechanism `h_tilde_e = h_e + ω_e · c_dyn` adds transferred dynamics without magnitude constraints on `ω_e`. Without regularization (e.g., L1/L2 on `ω_e` or a KL penalty), the MLP `Ψ` could trivially learn to suppress the cluster signal (`ω_e → 0`) and rely solely on the static `h_e`, undermining the transfer objective.
- **Logical Gaps:** The feedback loop between cluster assignment and pattern transfer is conceptually sound but technically underspecified regarding gradient flow. The assumption that frozen BERT embeddings provide sufficient semantic grounding for dynamic TKG clustering is reasonable but limits adaptation to domain-specific jargon that appears post-training.

### Experiments & Results
- **Claims Testing:** The experiments rigorously test the core claim. TRANSFIR consistently outperforms baselines on the defined zero-history emerging entity setting.
- **Baselines:** The baseline pool is extensive (13 methods across graph, path, and inductive families). However, for a 2026 ICLR submission focusing on zero-history reasoning, the absence of strong **text-to-graph cold-start baselines** or simple **MLP-on-text+time** models is noticeable. While LLM-based methods (GenTKG, ICL, PPT) are included, their adaptation protocols are briefly mentioned. A stronger comparison against a frozen-text-only baseline or a fine-tuned text-embedding baseline would isolate the value of the VQ+IC transfer mechanism versus raw semantic similarity.
- **Ablations:** The ablations (-IC, -Codebook, -Pattern Transfer, -Textual encoding) adequately cover the main components. However, a critical ablation is missing: **"-Frozen Text" vs. "Fine-tuned Text"**. The authors claim frozen embeddings prevent representation collapse (Sec 4.1), but do not empirically show that fine-tuning textual embeddings actually causes collapse or degrades performance on this task. Adding this would directly validate a core design choice.
- **Statistics & Metrics:** Results are averaged over 3 seeds with error bars reported (Fig 9). Metrics (MRR, Hits@k) are standard. No obvious cherry-picking; gains are consistent across ICEWS and GDELT.
- **Robustness:** The sensitivity analysis (Sec 5.5) and temporal split variation strengthen confidence in robustness. The discussion on GDELT noisy text is honest and correctly identifies a failure mode of textual encodings.

### Writing & Clarity
- **Overall Clarity:** The paper is well-structured and the narrative flows logically from problem motivation to empirical study to methodology. The pseudocode (Alg 1) greatly aids understanding.
- **Confusing Sections:** 
  - The definition of the **Collapse Ratio** (Sec 3, App C.2) is mathematically sound but its direct connection to link prediction performance is correlative rather than mechanistic. The paper should briefly state how higher collapse ratio *causes* the observed ranking drop (e.g., by reducing margin between positive and negative entities in scoring Eq 12).
  - Equation 13's loss formulation notation is slightly dense due to parser artifacts, but the intent (cross-entropy over ranked candidates) is clear from context.
- **Figures/Tables:** Figures are informative. Fig 2 effectively motivates the problem, and Fig 4 validates the representation improvement. Table 1 is comprehensive.

### Limitations & Broader Impact
- **Acknowledged Limitations:** The authors correctly identify that noisy/abbreviated entity descriptions (e.g., in GDELT) degrade performance and note this in Sec 5.4. They also propose future work on handling emerging relations and long-term semantic evolution.
- **Missed Limitations:** 
  - **Domain Shift & Semantic Aliasing:** The method heavily relies on text semantic similarity to guide cluster assignment. If an emerging entity shares keywords with known entities but operates in a completely different interaction regime (semantic aliasing), TRANSFIR may transfer incorrect patterns. This risk is not discussed.
  - **Scalability to Massive Entity Sets:** While Appendix D.3 analyzes complexity, the `O(EKd)` codebook update and `O(Emd)` transfer step scale linearly with the full entity set `E`. For industrial KGs with billions of entities, this may become a bottleneck. The paper should clarify if lazy updates or approximate nearest neighbor search are viable.
- **Broader Impact/Societal:** The ethics statement is standard and appropriate. TKGs are used in forecasting and risk analysis; the authors should briefly note that erroneous pattern transfer to emerging entities could compound forecast errors in high-stakes domains (e.g., medical or crisis early-warning), warranting caution in deployment.

### Overall Assessment
TRANSFIR addresses a highly relevant and practically important gap in Temporal Knowledge Graph reasoning: inductive generalization to entities with zero historical interactions. The empirical investigation is thorough, the performance gains over strong baselines are significant and consistent, and the integration of a VQ codebook with temporal interaction chains offers a novel and intuitive solution to representation collapse. However, to meet ICLR's rigorous standards for methodological completeness, several critical points need clarification: (1) the gradient routing through the discrete VQ assignment must be explicitly specified; (2) the handling of empty interaction chains for the query entity itself requires clarification; (3) the assumption of frozen text embeddings needs empirical validation via a fine-tuning ablation; and (4) stronger baselines representing text-driven cold-start methods should be included or discussed to isolate the contribution of the proposed transfer mechanism. With these clarifications and minor experimental additions, the paper presents a solid, impactful contribution that stands well within ICLR's acceptance bar.

# Neutral Reviewer
## Balanced Review

### Summary
This paper addresses the underexplored challenge of inductive reasoning for emerging entities in Temporal Knowledge Graphs (TKGs) that lack historical interactions at query time. Motivated by empirical findings of widespread representation collapse in existing models, the authors propose TRANSFIR, a framework that leverages frozen textual embeddings with a VQ codebook to assign entities to latent semantic clusters, and transfers temporal interaction patterns within these clusters via sequential Interaction Chain encoding and dynamic prototype pooling. Extensive experiments across four benchmarks demonstrate that TRANSFIR significantly outperforms diverse baselines under strict zero-history conditions while maintaining computational efficiency.

### Strengths
1. **Rigorous empirical motivation & quantitative analysis:** The paper systematically establishes the problem through a three-part empirical study (Sec. 3). It quantifies that ~25% of entities are unseen during training and introduces a novel "Collapse Ratio" metric to concretely demonstrate representation collapse in baselines (Fig. 2c, App. C.2), moving beyond qualitative claims and clearly motivating the proposed solution.
2. **Strong empirical performance & comprehensive evaluation:** TRANSFIR achieves substantial improvements (~28.6% avg MRR gain) over 13 baselines across four datasets using a strict 5:2:3 chronological split designed to maximize emerging entities (Table 1). The inclusion of robustness tests (varying temporal splits, different PLMs, hyperparameter sweeps, and an "Unknown" history setting) thoroughly validates the method's reliability (Sec. 5.4-5.5, App. F).
3. **High reproducibility & methodological clarity:** The paper provides a clear three-stage pipeline (Classification–Representation–Generalization), explicit pseudocode (Alg. 1), complexity analysis, and a public code repository. Baseline adaptations, data partitioning protocols, and failure cases are thoroughly documented in the appendices, aligning well with ICLR's reproducibility expectations.

### Weaknesses
1. **Untexted dependency on textual priors:** The framework's initial clustering relies entirely on frozen BERT embeddings. Many compared TKG baselines operate purely on structural/ID representations. While acknowledged (Sec. 5.4), the paper does not isolate how much of the performance gain stems from the rich textual semantics versus the novel temporal pattern transfer mechanism, potentially introducing a confounding variable in the baseline comparison.
2. **Architectural novelty is incremental:** While the problem formulation is timely, the core technical components (VQ clustering, Transformer-based sequence encoding, and prototype pooling) are well-established in representation learning and static graph literature. The contribution lies primarily in their effective integration rather than in fundamental methodological innovation.
3. **Under-justified Pattern Transfer formulation:** The transfer mechanism $\tilde{h}_e = h_e + \omega_e \cdot c^{dyn}_{\pi(e)}$ (Eqs. 10-11) is essentially a simple residual scaling operation. The paper lacks a comparative ablation or theoretical rationale explaining why this specific design is optimal compared to more expressive alternatives (e.g., attention-based cluster aggregation or gated updates).
4. **Baseline exclusion nuances:** Several strong recent inductive or LLM-augmented methods (e.g., zrLLM, POSTRA) are discussed in Appendix B but omitted from the main comparison table. While implementation or metric constraints are noted, their absence slightly weakens the claim of comprehensive SOTA comparison against the most closely related contemporary work.

### Novelty & Significance
The paper tackles a highly relevant, real-world limitation in TKG reasoning (the zero-history cold-start problem). **Novelty** is moderate; it adapts proven representation learning primitives (VQ, sequence transformers, prototype transfer) to a new temporal inductive setting rather than introducing fundamentally new mathematical or algorithmic components. However, the **clarity** is high: the logical progression from empirical diagnosis to architectural solution is well-articulated, and figures/appendices effectively support the claims. **Reproducibility** is excellent, with open code, detailed algorithms, and explicit experimental protocols that allow straightforward verification. **Significance** is strong, as open-world TKGs are ubiquitous in practice, and providing a robust, efficient solution for emerging entities has direct practical value for forecasting and reasoning systems in dynamic domains. Overall, the paper meets ICLR's bar for solid empirical validation and clear problem formulation, even if architectural novelty is incremental.

### Suggestions for Improvement
1. **Isolate the impact of textual vs. structural clustering:** Add an ablation or control experiment using a purely structural clustering method (e.g., random initialization or static graph Laplacian clustering) or compare against variants with intentionally degraded/noisy text. This would precisely quantify the contribution of the temporal pattern transfer mechanism independent of semantic priors.
2. **Expand the Pattern Transfer ablation:** Systematically compare the proposed residual transfer against at least two alternative prototype aggregation strategies (e.g., attention-weighted fusion, MLP gating, or self-calibration) in the ablation study to justify the architectural choice and demonstrate that simpler or more complex alternatives are suboptimal.
3. **Strengthen baseline parity discussion:** Explicitly clarify whether baselines were provided equivalent multi-modal inputs (if applicable) or formally discuss how TRANSFIR's use of text is a deliberate architectural advantage. Consider including a stronger recent inductive baseline in the main table, or provide a clearer, more rigorous justification for their exclusion beyond metric incompatibility.
4. **Clarify notation & computational scaling:** Tighten the notation in Eqs. 10-11 to explicitly define $\omega_e$'s dimensionality and activation bounds. Additionally, while memory efficiency is highlighted, briefly discuss strategies to mitigate the $O(k^2)$ attention bottleneck for extremely long interaction chains or high-throughput streaming scenarios to strengthen deployment readiness.

# Spark Finder Review
## How to Improve This Paper

### Missing Experiments (top 3-5 only)
1. Add a control baseline that uses identical frozen BERT entity embeddings with a standard temporal decoder, because comparing against models using random initialization confounds the claimed architectural gains with the simple benefit of better entity initialization.
2. Evaluate on standard chronological splits (e.g., 8:1:1), because the custom 5:2:3 split artificially inflates the emergence rate and the 28.6% MRR improvement cannot be trusted to generalize under community-accepted evaluation protocols.
3. Test queries where both subject and object are emerging entities at timestamp `tq`, because the current setup only isolates single-emerging entities and fails to stress-test the inductive pipeline in the more realistic, highly sparse scenarios the paper claims to solve.

### Deeper Analysis Needed (top 3-5 only)
1. Quantitatively validate codebook cluster quality using external ontology labels or Adjusted Rand Index, because qualitative t-SNE plots cannot prove the VQ module learns transferable semantic types rather than superficial lexical groupings that may not generalize across domains.
2. Establish a direct correlation between the proposed "Collapse Ratio" metric and per-query link prediction scores, because reporting the metric in isolation does not causally prove that preventing representation collapse is the actual mechanism driving the performance gains.
3. Explain how the Interaction Chain encoder handles the exact `tq = te(e)` (zero-history) edge case, because Section 4.2 requires collecting past interactions to build chains, creating a logical gap for the strict inductive setting where the emerging entity has zero temporal context at query time.

### Visualizations & Case Studies (top 3-5 only)
1. Provide an end-to-end trace showing actual Interaction Chain sequences, similarity scores, and Transformer attention weights for a specific emerging entity query, because the current abstract case study cannot verify whether temporal pattern transfer actively functions or if the model defaults to a smoothed cluster prior.
2. Visualize the temporal stability of the codebook prototypes and cluster sizes across training snapshots, because highly imbalanced assignments or frequent prototype oscillations would expose the fragility of the pattern transfer and directly undermine claims of stable semantic clustering.
3. Show failure cases where the codebook assigns an emerging entity to an incorrect semantic cluster, explicitly tracing how this misclassification propagates to erroneous predictions, because this would reveal whether the method's bottlenecks lie in textual representation, clustering, or the transfer mechanism.

### Obvious Next Steps (top 3-5 only)
1. Re-initialize all baseline models with the exact same frozen textual embeddings used in TRANSFIR, because failing to standardize initialization makes it impossible for reviewers to disentangle the paper's novel contributions from a confounding variable that could be implemented in any existing method.
2. Rigorously investigate why removing textual encoding *improves* performance on GDELT (Sec 5.4), because this directly contradicts the core premise that semantic textual priors are necessary for inductive reasoning and requires either a dataset-specific ablation or a hybrid-prior mechanism to justify the method's universal applicability.
3. Formalize the computational complexity of the chronological codebook updates during inference, because ICLR expects strict efficiency analysis for real-time forecasting methods and the current pseudocode suggests recomputing VQ assignments per timestamp, which could bottleneck deployment on large-scale graphs.

# Final Consolidated Review
## Summary
This paper addresses the zero-history cold-start problem in Temporal Knowledge Graph (TKG) reasoning, where ~25% of entities emerge at inference time without temporal interactions. The authors diagnose representation collapse in existing transductive methods and propose TRANSFIR, a three-stage pipeline that clusters entities via a frozen-text VQ codebook, encodes historical Interaction Chains (ICs), and transfers cluster-level temporal prototypes to zero-history queries. The method achieves substantial gains over 13 baselines across four benchmarks and includes comprehensive ablations and robustness checks.

## Strengths
- **Rigorous empirical diagnosis of a practical problem:** The paper moves beyond qualitative claims by explicitly quantifying entity emergence rates (~25%) and introducing a rotation-invariant "Collapse Ratio" metric. This provides a concrete, measurable characterization of why standard TKG models fail on emerging entities.
- **Strong and consistent empirical performance:** TRANSFIR delivers large, reproducible gains (avg. +28.6% MRR) across diverse temporal event datasets. The evaluation includes temporal split variations, hyperparameter sweeps, PLM sensitivity, and an "Unknown" history setting, demonstrating robust inductive generalization beyond strict zero-history constraints.
- **Clear pipeline and high reproducibility:** The Classification–Representation–Generalization architecture is logically presented, accompanied by detailed pseudocode, explicit complexity analysis, failure case documentation, and a public codebase, aligning well with ICLR standards.

## Weaknesses
- **Severe confounding of textual priors vs. architectural contribution:** The method's core clustering mechanism relies entirely on frozen BERT embeddings, while nearly all baselines use randomly initialized or purely structural representations. Without a strong text-aware baseline (e.g., identical frozen BERT + standard temporal decoder) or an ablation comparing frozen vs. fine-tuned text, it is impossible to disentangle whether gains stem from the proposed VQ/IC transfer pipeline or simply from injecting rich semantic priors. This fundamentally undermines the isolatability of the claimed contribution.
- **Methodological underspecification on zero-history edge cases:** By definition, an emerging entity queried exactly at its emergence time `tq = te(e)` has no past interactions. Section 4.2 constructs Interaction Chains `C_q` from past interactions, but the paper never specifies how the Transformer encoder (Eq. 8) handles an empty sequence for the query entity itself, nor how `h_IC_eq` is initialized in this strict setting. Additionally, the transfer mechanism `h̃_e = h_e + ω_e · c^{dyn}` (Eq. 11) lacks regularization or gating, leaving the model free to trivially nullify the transfer via `ω_e → 0`, which weakens the claim of enforced pattern propagation.
- **Incremental architectural novelty with unexamined design choices:** The components (VQ clustering, Transformer sequence encoding, prototype pooling) are well-established in representation learning. The primary novelty lies in their integration for a TKG setting. Crucially, the paper provides no ablation or theoretical justification comparing the proposed residual transfer against more expressive alternatives (e.g., attention-weighted fusion, gated updates), nor does it justify the heuristic Top-K relation filtering in Eq. 6 against temporal proximity or random baselines. This leaves key architectural choices empirically unvalidated.
- **Evaluation protocol inflation and lack of quantitative cluster validation:** The custom 5:2:3 chronological split is explicitly designed to maximize the emergence ratio. While useful for stress-testing, omitting standard splits (e.g., 8:1:1) raises concerns about the practical prevalence of the addressed setting and the generalizability of the reported margins. Furthermore, cluster quality is only validated via qualitative t-SNE plots; without quantitative metrics (e.g., ARI against known entity types/ontology), claims of learning "transferable semantic clusters" remain speculative.

## Nice-to-Haves
- Correlate the proposed Collapse Ratio directly with per-query ranking margins to mechanistically prove that preventing collapse drives performance gains.
- Visualize codebook prototype stability and cluster assignment entropy across temporal snapshots to assess whether semantic groupings remain coherent as the graph evolves.
- Provide a step-by-step attention trace for the IC encoder to verify whether the model actively extracts temporal patterns or relies predominantly on static cluster priors.

## Novel Insights
The paper identifies representation collapse as the primary failure mode for zero-history temporal reasoning and proposes decoupling semantic grounding from temporal supervision. By anchoring the representation space to frozen textual embeddings and using a VQ codebook to induce semantic clusters, the model stabilizes the embedding manifold for unseen entities. This allows temporal dynamics to be distilled into cluster-level prototypes rather than per-entity embeddings, effectively bypassing the cold-start problem through semantic-structural alignment.

## Potentially Missed Related Work
- **zrLLM (Ding et al., NAACL 2024) & POSTRA (Pan et al., arXiv 2025):** Both are discussed briefly in Appendix B but omitted from the main comparison. zrLLM explicitly tackles zero-shot TKG reasoning with textual descriptions, and POSTRA enables cross-dataset transfer via positional encoding. Including a direct comparison or a rigorous parity discussion would strengthen the evaluation landscape.

## Suggestions
- **Add a text-matched control baseline:** Train a standard temporal sequence model (e.g., LogCL or a Transformer decoder) using the exact same frozen BERT embeddings. Report its performance side-by-side to isolate the marginal gain of the VQ/IC transfer mechanism.
- **Clarify the zero-history query pipeline:** Explicitly state how the model computes `h_IC_eq` when `C_q` is empty for the query emerging entity. If a default vector or prototype substitution is used, document it formally in Section 4.2 and Alg. 1.
- **Report on standard splits:** Evaluate TRANSFIR on conventional 8:1:1 or 7:1:2 chronological splits alongside the 5:2:3 protocol. This contextualizes the true prevalence of emerging entities and demonstrates that performance gains hold under standard community benchmarks.
- **Quantify cluster validity:** Report Adjusted Rand Index (ARI) or Normalized Mutual Information (NMI) between VQ assignments and available entity type labels/ontologies across datasets to objectively verify that the codebook learns transferable semantic structures rather than arbitrary lexical groupings.
