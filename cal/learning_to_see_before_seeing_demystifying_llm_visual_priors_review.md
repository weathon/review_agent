# Harsh Critic Review
## Section-by-Section Critical Review

### Title & Abstract
- **Title accuracy:** The title accurately captures the core investigation: how text-only pretraining induces latent visual capabilities ("priors") and how to cultivate them deliberately.
- **Abstract clarity:** The abstract clearly states the problem (LLMs develop visual priors despite text-only training), the method (systematic controlled experiments, data mixing, scaling to 1T tokens), and key results (separable perception/reasoning priors, distinct scaling origins, and a balanced data recipe).
- **Claim support:** The claims are largely supported by the subsequent sections. However, the abstract's emphasis on "data-efficient visual adaptation" (a known phenomenon) is slightly disconnected from the paper's actual experimental design, which does not explicitly measure data efficiency during adaptation. The abstract also states the work is "grounded in over 100 controlled experiments consuming 500,000 GPU-hours," which establishes strong empirical grounding.

### Introduction & Motivation
- **Motivation & Gap:** The introduction effectively motivates the problem by synthesizing three observed phenomena (programmatic visual knowledge, data-efficient adaptation, and LLMs as vision encoders). It correctly identifies the gap: prior work documents the emergence of visual priors but lacks a systematic dissection of their structure, data origins, and deliberate cultivation strategies.
- **Contributions:** The three contributions (structure, source, and vision-aware recipe) are clearly stated and map directly to Sections 3.5, 3.2/3.3, and 3.4/4.
- **Over-claim/under-sell:** The introduction slightly overclaims by framing the work as explaining "data-efficient visual adaptation" (Point 2), yet the experimental pipeline uses a fixed-size instruction tuning dataset (Sec B.1). The paper demonstrates that priors *boost final performance*, not that they *reduce the multimodal data required* during adaptation. This framing should be tightened to match the empirical scope.

### Method / Approach
- **Clarity & Reproducibility:** The training and adaptation protocols (Sec 2, App B) are clearly specified: Llama-3 architecture, 340M–13B scales, 30B token baseline, two-stage alignment+SFT, fixed seeds, and deterministic parsing. The methodology is reproducible in principle.
- **Assumptions & Justification:** The core assumption is that adapter-style MLLMs serve as a clean, standardized probe for isolating LLM-internal priors. This is justified by citing Cambrian-1 and prior literature, but it inherently limits claims about encoder-free or fully joint-trained multimodal models (acknowledged in Limitations).
- **Logical Gaps:** The data classification into "reasoning" and "visual" categories (App F) relies on a 32B auxiliary LLM. This introduces a potential confound: if the classifier's own priors align with the target model's training distribution, the categorization may reflect semantic labeling rather than the underlying structural properties the model actually learns during pretraining. The paper should discuss this potential circularity or validate the classification with a lexicon/human-based baseline.
- **Edge Cases/Failure Modes:** The method thoroughly handles varying model sizes and vision encoders (Sec 3.6). However, it does not control for the base language proficiency impact: as reasoning data increases, general perplexity/accuracy degrades slightly (Table 2). The paper assumes this trade-off is acceptable, but does not discuss failure modes where over-indexing on reasoning data causes catastrophic forgetting of foundational commonsense.

### Experiments & Results
- **Claim testing:** The experiments rigorously test the structural and origin claims via ablation-style mixing experiments (Fig 4, Table 1/2) and cross-encoder universality tests (Fig 6).
- **Baselines & Fairness:** Comparisons are fair within the controlled setup. Using Cambrian-1's benchmark suite ensures standardized evaluation across domains.
- **Missing Ablations & Statistical Reporting:** A critical missing ablation is a direct test of *adaptation data efficiency*. If visual priors truly reduce the need for multimodal instruction tuning, the paper should demonstrate that the "Balanced" model achieves parity with the "Language-Favorable" model using 25% or 50% of the SFT data. Furthermore, error bars, standard deviations across multiple random seeds, or statistical significance tests are entirely absent. Given the ~1.3 absolute point gain on Avg VQA at the 1T scale (Table 3), statistical validation is necessary for ICLR standards.
- **Result Interpretation & Cherry-picking:** Results appear comprehensive and not cherry-picked. However, the inclusion of the "Blind Visual Instruction Tuning" trick (Sec K) muddies the waters. While it boosts performance (Table 6), the authors explicitly note it encourages hallucination and is not a best practice. Presenting it alongside the main pre-training recipe conflates the effect of *pre-training priors* with *instruction-tuning initialization*. It should be moved to an appendix or clearly isolated from the primary contribution.
- **Datasets & Metrics:** Benchmark selection is appropriate and covers perception (General, OCR) and reasoning (Knowledge, Vision-Centric). The newly introduced MLE-Bench (App I/J) is a sensible, segmentation-grounded diagnostic tool, though its simplicity means it only captures coarse existence, not detailed perception.

### Writing & Clarity
- **Confusing sections:** Section 3.5's correlation matrix interpretation warrants caution. The authors label the 0.37 (General-OCR) and 0.33 (Knowledge-Vision-Centric) correlations as "moderate" and the ~0.07 cross-cluster correlations as evidence of "statistical independence." In representation learning, correlations this low can also arise from benchmark noise or metric saturation rather than true structural separation. The text should acknowledge this ambiguity.
- **Figures & Tables:** Despite PDF-parser artifacts in the prompt, the conceptual layout of Figures 2, 4, 5, 6, and 7 is logical and directly supports the narrative. Table 2 is dense but effectively communicates the interpolation recipe. The qualitative examples in Figures 9, 13, and 14 are highly informative for illustrating reasoning depth vs. perceptual saturation.

### Limitations & Broader Impact
- **Acknowledged limitations:** The authors correctly identify scope boundaries: focus on adapter architectures, exclusion of safety/bias audits, and restriction to static images.
- **Missed fundamental limitations:** As noted, the lack of an explicit adaptation data-scaling curve is the most significant methodological limitation. Additionally, the paper does not discuss compute trade-offs: pretraining on higher fractions of code/math/reasoning data may increase training instability or require different learning rate schedules, which is not addressed.
- **Societal impacts:** The Broader Impact section appropriately flags that text corpora contain biases that will transfer to visual priors, potentially manifesting as harmful stereotypes in downstream MLLMs. This is well-calibrated for the work.

### Overall Assessment
This paper presents a highly systematic, large-scale empirical investigation into how text-only pretraining shapes latent visual capabilities. The scope, computational investment (500k GPU-hours), and methodical data-mixing ablations make it a valuable contribution to the MLLM community, aligning well with ICLR's emphasis on rigorous foundational analysis. However, the manuscript has a notable gap between its motivational claims and experimental execution: it asserts that visual priors enable "data-efficient visual adaptation" but never empirically tests whether the proposed balanced recipe reduces the required multimodal instruction-tuning data. Additionally, the absence of error bars or statistical significance testing for the 1T-scale results (~1.3% absolute gain) weakens the robustness of the scaling claims, and the confounding effect of the "blind instruction tuning" trick blurs the line between pre-training priors and downstream initialization effects. Addressing these points—particularly by demonstrating actual adaptation efficiency gains, reporting statistical variance, and cleanly separating the blind-tuning probe from the main recipe—will substantially strengthen the paper. The core contribution stands as a strong, actionable empirical study that shifts the discourse from observing emergent priors to deliberately engineering them.

# Neutral Reviewer
## Balanced Review

### Summary
This paper systematically investigates how language-only pre-training cultivates latent visual capabilities in LLMs. Through extensive controlled experiments, it decomposes these visual priors into distinct reasoning and perception components, demonstrating that reasoning scales progressively with code/math/academia data while perception emerges diffusely from diverse corpora and saturates quickly. The authors propose and empirically validate a data-mixing recipe that improves downstream multimodal performance, supported by a newly introduced benchmark for scale-dependent object perception.

### Strengths
1. **Exceptional empirical scope and rigor:** The study encompasses over 100 controlled experiments across five model scales (340M–13B), 16 distinct data sources, and systematic mixing ratios, consuming ~500k GPU-hours. This scale of ablation provides high statistical confidence in the reported trends (Sec 3.1–3.3).
2. **Actionable, well-validated findings on data curation:** The decomposition of visual priors into reasoning vs. perception components is clearly demonstrated through proportion-ablation studies (Fig 4) and cross-encoder universality tests (Fig 6). The resulting data-mixing recipe (e.g., `mix6` in Table 2) offers a practical, evidence-based guideline for pre-training vision-aware LLMs without compromising language proficiency.
3. **Introduction of a targeted diagnostic benchmark:** MLE-Bench effectively addresses a gap in evaluating fine-grained visual perception by stratifying object existence tasks by spatial scale. Results showing scale-dependent performance (Sec A.1, Table 5) provide a valuable tool for diagnosing perception bottlenecks in MLLMs.
4. **Clear experimental design and robust evaluation protocol:** The use of a standardized two-stage MLLM adaptation pipeline, robust VQA parsing strategy (Appendix H), and consistent evaluation across 16 public benchmarks ensures that performance differences can be reliably attributed to pre-training variations rather than downstream tuning noise.

### Weaknesses
1. **Heavy reliance on correlational evidence for structural claims:** The assertion that visual priors consist of "separable" perception and reasoning components rests largely on a Spearman correlation matrix across benchmark categories (Fig 5). Benchmarks themselves often mix perception and reasoning, and correlation does not establish true architectural or representational independence. Without mechanistic probing (e.g., representation similarity, linear probes, or circuit-level analysis), this claim remains suggestive rather than definitive.
2. **Vague characterization of the perception prior's origin:** While the paper convincingly links reasoning to structured domains (code/math), the perception prior is broadly attributed to "web-crawl" and diverse corpora (Sec 3.5). No targeted linguistic, syntactic, or semantic analysis is provided to explain *what specific features* of these corpora drive perceptual learning, limiting actionable takeaways for practitioners seeking to optimize this component.
3. **Limited scaling validation of the proposed recipe:** The primary validation of the balanced data mixture is conducted on 3B and 7B models trained on 1T tokens. Given ICLR's emphasis on scaling behaviors and the non-linear dynamics of data mixtures at larger scales, it is unclear whether the identified optimal ratios (e.g., ~52% reasoning-centric, 15% visual-combination proxy) generalize to 13B+ or foundation-scale models.
4. **Ambiguity in benchmark categorization:** The division of the 16 VQA datasets into "perception-heavy" (General/OCR) and "reasoning-heavy" (Knowledge/Vision-Centric) is a useful heuristic but arguably oversimplified. Tasks like AI2D or ChartQA inherently require both fine-grained perception and multi-step logical inference, which may confound the correlation analysis and ablation results.

### Novelty & Significance
**Novelty:** Moderate-to-high. While the existence of visual priors in text-only LLMs has been observed anecdotally, this work is among the first to systematically decompose these priors, trace their origins to specific data categories, and derive a prescriptive pre-training recipe. The methodological contribution lies in the controlled mixing framework and the introduction of MLE-Bench.
**Clarity:** Strong. The paper follows a logical progression from hypothesis formulation to controlled ablation, recipe derivation, and large-scale validation. Figures and tables effectively communicate scaling trends and trade-offs.
**Reproducibility:** Promising but computationally constrained. The training protocols, evaluation pipelines, and classification prompts are detailed in the appendices, which aligns with ICLR standards. However, the massive compute footprint (~500k GPU-hours) makes independent verification difficult without open-sourcing the exact data sampling scripts, mixture configurations, and evaluation code.
**Significance:** High. The findings directly impact how the community constructs next-generation MLLMs, shifting pre-training from heuristic data collection to principled, capability-aware curation. This reduces trial-and-error in multimodal alignment and offers a cost-effective pathway to stronger vision-language models.

### Suggestions for Improvement
1. **Strengthen structural claims with mechanistic analysis:** Supplement the benchmark-level correlations (Fig 5) with representation-level diagnostics, such as linear probing for perception vs. reasoning tasks, CKA/representational similarity analysis, or activation-patching experiments, to more rigorously demonstrate the independence of the two priors.
2. **Provide targeted analysis of the perception prior:** Conduct a linguistic or statistical analysis of the high-performing "diverse" corpora to identify which features (e.g., spatial prepositions, descriptive adjective density, concrete noun frequency, or syntactic complexity) correlate with perceptual gains. This would transform the observation into an actionable curation guideline.
3. **Validate the mixture recipe at larger scales:** Extend the 1T-token validation to at least one larger model regime (e.g., 13B–14B or 70B if feasible). Reporting whether the optimal reasoning/visual ratio shifts, plateaus, or inverts at scale would significantly strengthen the generalizability of the findings.
4. **Refine benchmark categorization or acknowledge overlap:** Either provide a task-level ablation showing that individual benchmarks cleanly separate perception from reasoning, or reframe the analysis around continuous axes (e.g., "reasoning dependency score") rather than binary categories to avoid conflating task design with inherent model capability.
5. **Enhance reproducibility commitments:** Given the compute scale, explicitly commit to releasing the data mixture metadata, filtering scripts, classification prompts, and evaluation code. Providing a smaller-scale reproducible recipe (e.g., 1B models on publicly available subsets) would also enable broader community validation.

# Spark Finder Review
## How to Improve This Paper

### Missing Experiments (top 3-5 only)
1. Report results averaged over multiple random seeds (≥3) with variance bars for all ablation plots; with only seed 42, the marginal performance differences between data mixes are indistinguishable from training noise, rendering the superiority of the "balanced mixture" statistically unsupported.
2. Benchmark the proposed data mixes against established open pretraining corpora (e.g., DCLM, FineWeb, RefinedWeb) on the identical 7B/1T setup to prove the recipe generalizes beyond the custom curated pool; without this, the gains may simply reflect corpus quality rather than the reasoning-to-visual ratio.
3. Evaluate the pretrained models using a fixed, standardized instruction-tuning dataset (e.g., full LLaVA-Instruct) rather than the custom subsampled Cambrian set to decouple the pretraining prior effect from the confounding variable of SFT data composition.
4. Include a controlled experiment testing for benchmark shortcuts, as the "blind visual instruction tuning" results prove VQA scores can be inflated via language-only hacking; you must measure performance with and without images to demonstrate that the reasoning prior improves scores specifically through cross-modal grounding and not just better question-answering without vision.

### Deeper Analysis Needed (top 3-5 only)
1. Quantify the "grounding delta" (performance with image minus performance without image) across all data mixes to prove the claimed visual reasoning prior is truly multimodal; if high-reasoning mixes maintain high scores in blind conditions, the paper's core claim of cross-modal transfer of reasoning is undermined.
2. Provide mechanistic evidence for the separability of perception and reasoning priors, such as representational similarity analysis (RSA) or probing across layers; you need to show that reasoning data alters abstract logic representations while visual data alters fine-grained object embeddings, rather than relying solely on downstream benchmark correlations which are noisy proxies.
3. Analyze the interaction between the pretraining mix and the vision encoder choice; since perception is claimed to be encoder-sensitive, you must demonstrate how the optimal pretraining recipe shifts when paired with fundamentally different encoders (e.g., SigLIP vs. MAE) to prove the recipe's generalizability and rule out overfitting to MetaCLIP's feature space.

### Visualizations & Case Studies
1. Present matched qualitative case studies where high-reasoning-prior models succeed with the image but fail or hallucinate on the same "blind" query; this visual contrast is the most direct evidence to convince reviewers that the model is utilizing the visual input for reasoning rather than retrieving textual knowledge.
2. Visualize attention maps or activation patterns when models trained on "visual combination" vs. "reasoning combination" process text describing a scene; showing distinct model focus on spatial relationship tokens vs. object name tokens would substantiate the claim of structurally distinct priors emerging from different data.
3. Show failure cases of the "Balanced" model on perception-heavy benchmarks in the 1T scaling run to transparently expose the trade-off; visualizing where the model errs (e.g., misidentifying textures or OCR errors despite good reasoning) defines the practical boundaries of the proposed recipe.

### Obvious Next Steps
1. Release the weights, training logs, and specific data sampling code for the 1T models to fulfill ICLR reproducibility standards; the community must be able to verify that the "vision-aware" prior holds across diverse evaluations and is not an artifact of the specific evaluation parsing script or hidden hyperparameters.
2. Develop and validate a mitigation for the "hallucination via blind tuning" phenomenon identified in the appendices; since you demonstrate that encouraging models to answer without images boosts benchmark scores, a critical next step is to propose a training objective or filtering mechanism that penalizes this shortcut to ensure genuine multimodal robustness.
3. Validate the data mixture recipe at larger model scales (13B+); given that scaling laws often exhibit phase shifts at higher parameter counts confirming that the 60% reasoning / 15% visual ratio remains optimal at scales where MLLMs actually compete is essential for the claim to be meaningful for the next generation of models.

# Final Consolidated Review
## Summary
This paper investigates how text-only language pre-training shapes latent visual capabilities in LLMs. Through an extensive series of controlled ablations (>100 experiments, ~500k GPU-hours) across model scales and 16 data sources, the authors decompose visual priors into a progressive reasoning component (driven by code/math/academia) and a saturating perception component (driven by diverse corpora). They derive and validate a balanced pre-training data mixture and introduce MLE-Bench to diagnose scale-dependent object perception.

## Strengths
- **Exceptional empirical scope and controlled design:** The systematic variation of data mixing ratios, model scales (340M–13B), and training steps cleanly isolates the impact of pre-training composition on downstream MLLM performance, providing high-fidelity empirical grounding rarely seen in this domain.
- **Actionable decomposition of visual priors:** The finding that visual reasoning scales linearly with structured text while perception emerges diffusely and saturates quickly offers a clear, practical heuristic for data curation, directly challenge-heuristic pre-training pipelines.
- **Useful diagnostic tooling:** MLE-Bench (leveraging SA-1B/ADE20K segmentation) effectively exposes scale-dependent perception bottlenecks, offering the community a standardized, non-trivial benchmark for probing fine-grained visual grounding beyond standard VQA accuracy.

## Weaknesses
- **Statistical fragility and single-seed reporting:** All results rely on a fixed random seed (42) with no error bars, variance reporting, or multi-run validation. Given that the proposed "balanced mixture" yields marginal gains (~1–2% absolute on Avg VQA at 1T tokens), these improvements fall well within typical pre-training noise, rendering the claimed superiority statistically unsupported.
- **Unsubstantiated "data-efficient adaptation" motivation & missing SFT scaling:** The abstract and introduction frame visual priors as key to data-efficient multimodal adaptation, yet the experimental protocol (Sec B.1) strictly fixes the instruction-tuning dataset size. The paper demonstrates that priors improve *final* performance but provides zero evidence that they actually *reduce* the amount of vision-language SFT data required, creating a notable gap between motivation and execution.
- **Heuristic evidence for "separable" priors:** The claim that perception and reasoning priors are structurally independent rests solely on Spearman correlations across benchmark suites (Fig 5). Standard VQA benchmarks inherently confound fine-grained OCR/perception with multi-step reasoning. Without mechanistic validation (e.g., CKA/representation similarity analysis, layer-wise linear probing, or activation patching), the decomposition remains a post-hoc correlational heuristic rather than a demonstrated architectural or representational fact.
- **Corpus quality vs. composition confound:** The recipe is validated exclusively on a custom 16-source pool and is never compared against modern open pre-training corpora (e.g., DCLM, FineWeb, RefinedWeb). Performance gains may simply reflect superior data filtering/quality of the authors' custom pool rather than the specific reasoning-to-visual ratio, severely limiting external validity and reproducibility.

## Nice-to-Haves
- Quantify the "grounding delta" (performance with image minus blind/no-image performance) to explicitly rule out textual shortcutting on VQA benchmarks.
- Release the exact data mixture metadata, sampling scripts, and classification prompts to enable community verification given the massive compute footprint.
- Extend the mixture validation to 13B+ or foundation-scale models to confirm the optimal ratio doesn't exhibit phase shifts at larger parameter counts.

## Potentially Missed Related Work
- **Shukor et al. (2025b) "Scaling laws for native multimodal models" & Chen et al. (2024) "AIOLI"** — Highly relevant for contextualizing data-mixing optimization frameworks and distinguishing adaptive pre-training strategies from brute-force curation.
- **Ruis et al. (2024) "Procedural knowledge in pretraining drives reasoning in LLMs"** — Provides theoretical grounding for why structured code/math data might generalize to non-linguistic reasoning tasks, which could strengthen the mechanistic discussion of why the reasoning prior transfers cross-modally.

## Suggestions
1. **Run multi-seed validation immediately:** Report mean ± std over ≥3 seeds for all key ablation plots and the 1T-scale comparison. If the confidence intervals overlap, temper language from "demonstrates superiority" to "suggests a trend."
2. **Execute an SFT data-scaling ablation:** To back the "data efficiency" claim, retrain the Balanced vs. Language-Favorable models using 25%, 50%, and 100% of the Cambrian SFT data. If the Balanced model reaches the baseline's 100% performance at 50% data, the core motivation is validated. Otherwise, revise the abstract/intro to focus on *performance ceilings* rather than *efficiency*.
3. **Add representational analysis to ground the structural claim:** Apply linear probes or Centered Kernel Alignment (CKA) comparing internal representations of models trained on high-reasoning vs. high-visual mixes. Showing that reasoning data shifts abstract/logical representations while visual data shifts descriptive/lexical ones would transform the correlational observation into a mechanistic finding.
4. **Benchmark against public corpora:** Evaluate the exact `mix6` recipe using identical model/training schedules but sampling from DCLM or FineWeb. This will definitively isolate whether gains stem from the *data ratio* or proprietary *data quality*, which is essential for ICLR-level novelty.
