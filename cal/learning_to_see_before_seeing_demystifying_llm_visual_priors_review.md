# Harsh Critic Review
## Section-by-Section Critical Review

### Title & Abstract
- **Does the title accurately reflect the contribution?** Yes. The title clearly signals the focus on emergent visual capabilities from text-only LLM pre-training and the paper's goal of dissecting them.
- **Does the abstract clearly state the problem, method, and key results?** The abstract effectively outlines the phenomenon (LLMs developing visual priors), the methodology (systematic deconstruction of ~100 experiments, scaling to 1T tokens, data mixing), and the core findings (dissociation of perception vs. reasoning priors, differential impact of reasoning vs. visual text, proposed data recipe). 
- **Are any claims in the abstract unsupported by the paper?** The claim that perception ability is "more sensitive to the vision encoder" is supported in Section 3.6 and Figure 6, but the abstract implies this is a primary finding without noting that the *trend* for reasoning is consistent across encoders, while perception performance varies. This is a minor framing issue, not a substantive overclaim.

### Introduction & Motivation
- **Is the problem well-motivated? Is the gap in prior work clearly identified?** The motivation is strong. The authors clearly identify a gap: while recent work observes that LLMs can be efficiently adapted to vision, the *origins* and *internal structure* of these text-derived visual priors remain poorly understood. Framing this as a move from "serendipitous emergence" to "deliberate cultivation" is compelling and aligns well with ICLR's interest in mechanistic and data-centric understanding of scaling.
- **Are the contributions clearly stated and accurate?** Yes. The three contributions (structure, source, and pre-training recipe) accurately map to Sections 3.5, 3.2-3.3, and 3.4/4.0. 
- **Does the introduction over-claim or under-sell?** The invocation of the "Platonic Representation Hypothesis" is framed as empirical support rather than proof, which is appropriate. The introduction does not over-claim; it stays within the bounds of what the experiments later demonstrate.

### Method / Approach
- **Is the method clearly described and reproducible?** The training protocol, evaluation suite, and data mixing strategies are well-documented in Sections 2 and 3, with extensive detail in the appendices. The use of a 32B LLM to classify pre-training text into reasoning/visual categories (Appendix F) is transparent, though reproducing the exact data mixtures requires access to that classifier or an equivalent pipeline.
- **Are key assumptions stated and justified?** The assumption that Spearman correlation across 105 model checkpoints reveals an "internal structure" of separable priors (Section 3.5, Figure 5) is methodologically thin. Correlation between model-level benchmark scores does not strictly prove separable representational priors; it could reflect shared task formats, evaluation difficulty, or dataset contamination. A more rigorous assumption or intervention (e.g., feature-level clustering, linear probing, or causal mediation analysis) would strengthen this claim.
- **Are there logical gaps in the derivation or reasoning?** In Section 3.3 and 3.4, the shift from a fine-grained 4-category reasoning/visual mix to a practical 6-source mix (web-crawl, encyc, etc.) is logical, but the mapping between the fine-grained LLM-classified categories and the coarse data sources is not explicitly bridged. Table 4 shows high multi-label overlap (e.g., `academia` is 81.7% math reasoning, but code/text sources likely contain multiple labels). How the mixing algorithm handles multi-label documents when targeting exact proportions (e.g., "75% reasoning") is underspecified.
- **Are there edge cases or failure modes not discussed?** The instruction-tuning ablation in Section 3.6 (Figure 7) removes perception or reasoning data but does not state whether the total dataset size was held constant. If removing 1.8M perception examples simply reduces training data, the observed performance drop could be a standard scaling effect rather than evidence of a distinct "perception prior." A controlled baseline replacing removed data with neutral samples is necessary.
- **For theoretical claims: are proofs correct and complete?** N/A (Empirical paper).

### Experiments & Results
- **Do the experiments actually test the paper's claims?** Largely yes. The mixing experiments (Sec 3.3/3.4) directly test how data proportions affect visual capabilities. The 1T scaling experiment (Sec 4) validates the recipe at a larger scale. However, the 1T section only compares two mixtures (`mix0` vs `mix6`). To claim "scaling" or generalizability of the recipe, at least one intermediate or contrasting mixture should be reported at 1T to rule out configuration-specific variance.
- **Are baselines appropriate and fairly compared?** The baselines are self-consistent (comparing different mixtures of the same architecture), which is appropriate for isolating the effect of data composition. However, the paper lacks comparison to public state-of-the-art open-source 7B MLLMs (e.g., LLaVA-OneVision, Qwen2.5-VL, InternVL2.5). Without this, it is unclear if the proposed "balanced" recipe yields competitive absolute performance or merely relative gains.
- **Are there missing ablations that would materially change conclusions?** Yes. The visual instruction tuning ablation (Sec 3.6) lacks a size-controlled baseline. Additionally, the "Blind Visual Instruction Tuning" trick (Appendix K) is presented as a performance booster, but it encourages the model to ignore missing visual context, directly conflicting with the paper's premise of cultivating *visual* reasoning. If VQA scores improve because the model shortcuts to language reasoning, it fundamentally challenges the "visual prior" narrative. This ablation is missing from the main experimental claims but appears in Table 6 and requires critical discussion.
- **Are error bars / statistical significance reported?** No. Despite mentioning "stabilized results" and fixed seeds, reporting variance across multiple random seeds or evaluation runs for key metrics (especially in Figure 2, 4, 6) would strengthen the robustness of the scaling and mixing trends.
- **Do the results support the claims made, or are they cherry-picked?** The results generally support the claims, but the interpretation of Figure 5 (correlation matrix) is slightly overstated. A Spearman correlation of ~0.37 between General/OCR and ~0.33 between Knowledge/Vision-Centric indicates loose coupling, but framing this as evidence of two distinct, separable cognitive "priors" is a stretch without additional representational analysis.
- **Are datasets and evaluation metrics appropriate?** The 16-benchmark suite grouped into four categories is comprehensive and well-aligned with contemporary MLLM evaluation. The robust parsing strategy (Appendix H) is a strong methodological choice that mitigates formatting bias in VQA evaluation.

### Writing & Clarity
- **Are there sections that are confusing or poorly explained?** The transition from the fine-grained 4-category mixing (Section 3.3) to the 6-source practical mixing (Section 3.4) is conceptually clear but technically abrupt. The exact algorithm for sampling from the 300B token pool while respecting multi-label overlaps (Table 4) should be explicitly formalized. Additionally, the "Blind Visual Instruction Tuning" trick is introduced somewhat casually in Section 4.2, but its implications are profound; moving it to the main text with a critical analysis of why it improves scores (likely by amplifying language shortcuts) would improve transparency.
- **Are figures and tables clear and informative?** (Setting aside parser artifacts) The figures are highly informative. Figure 4 clearly shows the saturation vs. progressive scaling. Figure 6 effectively demonstrates universality across vision encoders. Table 2's interpolation experiment is well-structured. Figure 5 could benefit from clearer labeling of the correlation values directly on the heatmap rather than a separate list, but it remains interpretable.

### Limitations & Broader Impact
- **Do the authors acknowledge the key limitations?** Yes. Section D (Limitations) appropriately notes the focus on adapter-style architectures, the unaddressed bias/safety implications of text-derived priors, and the restriction to static images.
- **Are there fundamental limitations they missed?** The reliance on LLM-as-a-judge/data-labeler for the 300B token classification pipeline introduces potential classifier bias and distributional drift as token counts grow. This is not discussed as a limitation. Furthermore, the performance gains from "Blind Visual Instruction Tuning" (Appendix K/L) reveal a core limitation: the models appear to conflate visual reasoning with format-following or language-based hallucination, which the authors note but do not fully integrate into the limitations discussion.
- **Are there failure modes or negative societal impacts not discussed?** The authors briefly mention bias in the Broader Impact section. However, they do not discuss how optimizing pre-training data for reasoning/visual text might inadvertently skew the model's knowledge distribution away from cultural, linguistic, or underrepresented domains present in the original `web-crawl` baseline, potentially reducing linguistic diversity or increasing Western/academic bias.

### Overall Assessment
This paper presents a rigorous, compute-intensive empirical study that successfully advances our understanding of how text-only pre-training shapes downstream multimodal capabilities. The disentanglement of visual priors into perception and reasoning components, along with the demonstration that reasoning-centric data progressively scales visual reasoning ability, is a valuable and timely contribution to the ICLR community. However, the manuscript would be significantly strengthened by addressing several experimental and interpretative gaps: (1) the instruction-tuning ablation (Fig 7) requires a dataset-size-controlled baseline to distinguish true perceptual prior degradation from simple data scaling effects; (2) the claim of "separable priors" relies on moderate benchmark correlations (~0.37) and would benefit from representational or intervention-level evidence rather than metric-level aggregation; (3) the 1T-token validation currently only compares two mixtures, which is insufficient to claim robust scaling behavior; and (4) the "Blind Visual Instruction Tuning" trick, while boosting VQA scores, reveals a reliance on language shortcuts and hallucination when images are absent, a methodological tension that directly contradicts the paper's framing of cultivating pure visual priors and must be addressed more candidly. Despite these concerns, the paper's scale, systematic design, and clear data-centric recipe make it a strong contribution that warrants publication pending clarification of these experimental controls and interpretative boundaries.

# Neutral Reviewer
## Balanced Review

### Summary
This paper systematically investigates how text-only pre-training cultivates latent visual priors in LLMs, demonstrating that these capabilities decompose into separable perception and reasoning components with distinct data origins and scaling behaviors. Through over 100 controlled experiments spanning model sizes from 340M to 13B parameters, the authors show that reasoning priors scale progressively with reasoning-centric data (e.g., code, math) and transfer universally across vision encoders, while perception priors emerge diffusely from broad corpora and depend heavily on the vision encoder and subsequent instruction tuning. Leveraging these insights, the work proposes a balanced pre-training data recipe validated at the 1T-token scale and introduces the Multi-Level Existence Benchmark (MLE-Bench) for evaluating scale-dependent visual perception.

### Strengths
1. **Exceptional empirical scale and experimental rigor:** The study conducts over 100 controlled experiments consuming ~500,000 GPU-hours, systematically varying model size (340M–13B), data source, mixing ratios, and vision encoders. The methodical grid search in Section 3.4 and the 7B/1T-token scaling validation (Table 3) provide robust, reproducible evidence that moves beyond anecdotal observations of emergent vision capabilities.
2. **Clear conceptual and empirical decomposition of visual priors:** The paper convincingly disentangles perception and reasoning priors using correlation analysis across 16 VQA benchmarks (Figure 5), cross-encoder universality tests (Figure 6), and targeted instruction-tuning ablations (Figure 7). The finding that reasoning priors are highly transferable while perception priors saturate quickly and depend on the vision encoder offers a nuanced, actionable framework for MLLM developers.
3. **Strong reproducibility and practical utility:** The authors provide detailed training protocols, fixed random seeds, explicit data classification prompts (Appendix F/G), and a transparent hierarchical answer-parsing strategy (Appendix H). The derivation of a balanced data mixture (mix6) that improves vision accuracy without degrading language metrics directly addresses a pressing need in the data-centric MLLM literature, and the commitment to release MLE-Bench further supports community reproducibility.

### Weaknesses
1. **Reliance on benchmark-driven correlations over mechanistic analysis:** The separation of perception and reasoning priors is primarily inferred from downstream benchmark performance correlations and behavioral outputs. While the paper includes kernel-based alignment metrics (Appendix B.2), it lacks internal representational analysis (e.g., probing, activation patching, or layer-wise RSA) to locate and characterize the neural circuits responsible for these priors, leaving the "separability" claim somewhat phenomenological.
2. **Limited generalizability of the data recipe and architecture scope:** The optimal mixture (mix6) is derived from a specific 16-source corpus pool and evaluated within an adapter-style MLLM pipeline. As acknowledged in Section D, the findings may not fully translate to end-to-end jointly trained models or architectures using discrete visual tokenization. The paper does not empirically test whether the proposed recipe holds under these alternative training regimes.
3. **Underdeveloped evaluation of MLE-Bench:** While conceptually valuable for probing scale-dependent perception, MLE-Bench is evaluated on only four frontier models in Appendix J (Table 5) and relies on distractors generated via LLM filtering from SA-1B/ADE20K. The benchmark lacks statistical analysis of distractor plausibility, controlled difficulty calibration, and extensive ablation across open-source models, making it difficult to assess whether it measures true perceptual granularity versus model-specific guessing biases.

### Novelty & Significance
The work demonstrates high novelty and strong significance, meeting ICLR's acceptance bar. Conceptually, it advances beyond the Platonic representation hypothesis by empirically mapping distinct linguistic data sources to separable visual priors, shifting the paradigm from serendipitous emergence to deliberate cultivation. Methodologically, the scale of controlled experiments and the 1T-token validation provide rare, actionable insights into data-centric foundation model development. For the MLLM community, the paper offers a practical, theoretically grounded recipe for pre-training language models to natively support efficient multimodal adaptation. The clarity of presentation, coupled with detailed appendices addressing training, evaluation, and data classification, ensures high reproducibility and immediate utility for both academic and industrial researchers.

### Suggestions for Improvement
1. **Integrate mechanistic representational analysis:** Complement the benchmark correlation results with techniques such as linear probing, representational similarity analysis (RSA), or circuit-level interventions to verify where and how perception vs. reasoning representations are encoded across transformer layers. This would ground the "separable priors" claim in neural dynamics rather than purely behavioral outputs.
2. **Strengthen MLE-Bench evaluation and robustness analysis:** Provide a broader evaluation across a wider range of open and closed models, conduct statistical analysis of distractor quality (e.g., semantic similarity, frequency bias), and include controlled difficulty scaling tests. This will help establish MLE-Bench as a reliable diagnostic tool rather than a preliminary pilot benchmark.
3. **Explicitly test architectural boundaries:** Given the noted limitation regarding adapter-style models, run a smaller-scale ablation (e.g., 1B-3B models) comparing the proposed data mixture under an end-to-end co-training setup or a discrete visual tokenizer framework. This would clarify the recipe's architectural invariance or highlight where adjustments are necessary.
4. **Reframe and critically discuss the "blind instruction tuning" trick:** The technique that improves benchmark scores by encouraging models to answer without images highlights a known shortcut behavior rather than improved perception. The paper should more explicitly separate benchmark optimization from genuine capability, discussing potential mitigation strategies (e.g., negative prompting, metadata-aware tuning) to prevent training models to hallucinate when visual inputs are absent.

# Spark Finder Review
## How to Improve This Paper

### Missing Experiments (top 3 only)
1. **Scale-aware data mixture validation:** The optimal mix was derived via grid search on a 3B model at 30B tokens; you must verify whether this ratio holds or shifts at larger scales (e.g., sweep at 500B tokens for the 7B model) because optimal mixtures typically drift with capacity, directly undermining the scalability of your proposed recipe.
2. **Instruction tuning sample efficiency ablation:** Fix the visual instruction data volume and compare the Balanced and Language-Favorable models at 10%, 25%, and 50% of standard tuning data sizes. Without proving the Balanced prior enables faster convergence or higher final performance with less tuning data, the claim that the LLM encodes a useful "visual prior" remains unproven against the null hypothesis that it just learns better text representations.
3. **Direct comparison to contemporary MLLM baselines:** Report standard public MLLMs of equivalent scale (e.g., LLaVA-1.6, Qwen2-VL-7B) evaluated on your exact 16-benchmark suite. The isolated ~1.3 point VQA improvement lacks critical context, and ICLR reviewers cannot assess if the "vision-aware" approach provides meaningful gains over conventional training without direct, controlled baselines.

### Deeper Analysis Needed (top 3 only)
1. **Layer-wise mechanistic analysis for prior separability:** Benchmark performance correlations do not prove distinct internal priors; compute layer-wise CKA, PCA visualizations, or logit-lens interventions to demonstrate where perception vs. reasoning representations localize in the LLM. Without mechanistic evidence of representational divergence, the core claim of separable priors is likely just a downstream evaluation artifact.
2. **Disentangling LLM prior contribution from vision encoder capacity:** The universality experiments swap encoders but do not isolate the marginal value of the text prior; include a control replacing the pretrained LLM with a randomly initialized text backbone or freezing all LLM layers post-alignment. This is necessary to prove that VQA improvements are actually driven by visual priors encoded during language pretraining rather than the inherent strength of the vision encoder.
3. **Vocabulary and semantic overlap analysis for visual data saturation:** Investigate whether the rapid saturation of "visual-world" data stems from lexical redundancy with standard web corpora or genuine conceptual limits of text. Without quantifying semantic overlap and concept density, the recommendation to cap visual-descriptive text may artificially limit model potential rather than reflect a true prior bottleneck.

### Visualizations & Case Studies (top 3 only)
1. **Attention/feature activation maps contrasting reasoning vs. perception tasks:** Visualize how high-reasoning prior models attend to image tokens when solving multi-step logic problems versus simple detection tasks. Showing that the model relies more heavily on textual projection embeddings than fine-grained visual features for reasoning tasks would visually validate the claimed functional separation of priors.
2. **Calibration curves for the MLE-Bench size-dependency claim:** Plot model accuracy against continuous ground-truth object area rather than categorical bins. This would expose if the "scale-dependency" finding is a robust prior phenomenon or an artifact of distractor difficulty and benchmark composition in specific size ranges.
3. **Failure mode catalog of the Balanced Recipe:** Provide qualitative examples where the Balanced model over-relies on textual structural priors and fails (e.g., correct logic applied to wrong visual elements or spatial hallucinations). Exposing where the recipe breaks is critical to show whether the reasoning prior genuinely compensates for perception deficits or merely masks them with confident, text-driven hallucinations.

### Obvious Next Steps (top 2 only)
1. **Validation on non-adapter architectures:** The entire study relies on MLP-projector MLLMs; test the cultivated priors on early-fusion models (e.g., Chameleon) or encoder-free architectures to prove the textual priors are truly modality-agnostic and not just optimizing the information bottleneck of the adapter design.
2. **Public release of data mixtures, classification prompts, and scaled weights:** The paper proposes a "data-centric recipe" but lacks the assets required for scientific validation; release the exact 300B token classification metadata, the precise mix6 composition lists, and the 1T pretrained checkpoints so the community can verify scalability and reproduce the balanced model gains.

# Final Consolidated Review
## Summary
This paper systematically investigates how text-only language model pre-training cultivates latent visual priors for downstream multimodal tasks. Through a large-scale experimental campaign spanning model scales from 340M to 13B parameters and training regimes up to 1T tokens, the authors demonstrate that visual priors decompose into separable perception and reasoning components with distinct data origins: reasoning priors scale progressively with reasoning-centric text (e.g., code, math) and transfer universally across vision encoders, while perception priors emerge diffusely, saturate quickly, and depend heavily on the vision adapter and instruction tuning. Leveraging these insights, the work derives a balanced pre-training data recipe and introduces a size-aware perceptual benchmark (MLE-Bench).

## Strengths
- **Exceptional empirical scale and systematic experimental design:** The study conducts over 100 controlled experiments, rigorously isolating the impact of data sources, mixing ratios, model sizes, and vision encoders. This moves beyond anecdotal observations of emergent vision capabilities to provide a reproducible, data-centric roadmap for foundation model development.
- **Clear empirical decomposition of prior types and scaling behaviors:** The paper convincingly demonstrates that reasoning and perception capabilities respond differently to pre-training signals. The finding that reasoning priors scale linearly with code/math/academic data and remain consistent across MetaCLIP, DINOv2, and MAE encoders (Figure 6), while visual-descriptive text saturates rapidly, offers actionable, nuanced guidance for the MLLM community.
- **Methodological care in evaluation and recipe derivation:** The hierarchical VQA parsing strategy (Appendix H) effectively mitigates formatting bias, and the grid-search-to-interpolation pipeline yields a practical data mixture (`mix6`) that improves visual capabilities without degrading core language proficiency, validated robustly at the 7B/1T-token scale (Table 3).

## Weaknesses
- **Claim of separable priors relies on behavioral correlations rather than internal representational evidence:** The core architectural claim that visual priors separate into distinct internal faculties is primarily inferred from moderate Spearman correlations (~0.37) between downstream benchmark scores. Task-level score correlation does not strictly prove separable neural representations; it may reflect shared evaluation formats, dataset contamination, or overlapping reasoning demands in supposedly perception-heavy benchmarks. Without layer-wise probing, representational similarity analysis, or causal interventions, the "separable priors" conclusion remains a behavioral observation rather than a mechanistic finding.
- **Uncontrolled dataset size in the instruction-tuning ablation:** Figure 7 ablates perception or reasoning instruction data to determine their source. However, removing 1.8M perception or 0.6M reasoning examples reduces the total fine-tuning volume without substitution. The resulting performance drops could plausibly stem from a standard data-scaling effect rather than the removal of a specific perceptual or reasoning prior. A size-matched control (e.g., replacing removed data with neutral language/instruction tokens) is necessary to isolate the prior contribution from the sheer quantity of tuning data.
- **Lacks contextualization against contemporary public baselines:** The paper reports a ~1.3 absolute VQA improvement of the `Balanced` model over its custom `Language-Favorable` baseline. While internally consistent, the absence of comparison to recent open-source 7B MLLMs (e.g., Qwen2.5-VL, LLaVA-NeXT/1.6, InternVL2.5) evaluated on the same benchmark suite makes it difficult to assess whether the proposed recipe yields competitive absolute performance or marginal gains over an artificially constrained baseline.

## Nice-to-Haves
- Demonstrate sample efficiency during adaptation: train the `Balanced` vs `Language-Favorable` backbones at 10%, 25%, and 50% of the visual instruction tuning data. Showing faster convergence or higher ceilings with less data would strongly validate the "prior" hypothesis over a simple representation capacity gain.
- Analyze the semantic/lexical overlap between the "visual-world" corpus and standard web crawls to determine if the rapid saturation of descriptive text stems from vocabulary redundancy or a genuine conceptual limit of text-only perception.
- Provide a continuous calibration curve for MLE-Bench (accuracy vs. exact object area percentage) rather than fixed bins (0-30%, etc.) to rule out distractor difficulty artifacts in the scale-dependency claim.

## Novel Insights
The paper fundamentally reframes how the AI community should view the "vision encoder + LLM" architecture: rather than treating the LLM as a purely linguistic module that magically generalizes to vision after adapters, the work shows it acts as a scalable, text-cultivated reasoning engine. The discovery that abstract reasoning learned from code and mathematics is inherently modality-agnostic and predictable in its scaling, while fine-grained perception is fundamentally bottlenecked by the visual adapter and post-training data, decouples the myth of monolithic cross-modal emergence into two tractable engineering problems. This shifts MLLM development from serendipitous scaling to deliberate, data-driven prior cultivation.

## Suggestions
- Run a controlled ablation for the instruction-tuning experiment that maintains constant total token count (e.g., by substituting the ablated perception/reasoning instruction data with neutral language data). This will definitively confirm that the observed performance changes originate from prior type rather than dataset size.
- Incorporate lightweight representational analysis (e.g., linear probing on frozen layer outputs or CKA/RSA across a subset of checkpoints) to ground the "separable priors" claim in internal model dynamics. If compute constraints prevent this, explicitly reframe the separation claim as behavioral rather than architectural in the text.
- Add a brief evaluation or contextual table comparing the `Balanced` 7B model against 2-3 recent open-source MLLMs of similar scale on the same 16-benchmark suite. This will anchor the paper's relative gains within the broader state-of-the-art landscape and underscore the practical utility of the proposed data recipe.
