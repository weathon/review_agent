# Harsh Critic Review
## Section-by-Section Critical Review

### Title & Abstract
The title accurately reflects the core methodological contribution. The abstract makes strong claims regarding robustness, accuracy, and SOTA performance. However, the term "affine-invariant camera poses" (Abstract, Sec 3.3) is slightly imprecise for the 3D vision community; the formulation recovers geometry up to an unknown global scale and rigid transformation, which constitutes a *similarity* transform, not a general affine transformation. I recommend precision here to avoid confusion. Additionally, the abstract implies a fully self-contained architecture, but the heavy reliance on VGGT initialization (detailed in Appendix A.2) and the necessity of an auxiliary proxy task for stable training (Appendix A.4) contextualize these claims. Briefly acknowledging the initialization dependence in the abstract would improve transparency.

### Introduction & Motivation
The motivation is well-articulated: anchoring multi-view reconstruction to a fixed reference view introduces fragility and order-dependence. The contrast between $\pi_3$ and reference-anchored methods (Figure 2) is compelling. However, the introduction frames the reference view as a "detrimental inductive bias" inherent to prior methods, without adequately distinguishing between architectural choices (e.g., explicit camera tokens in VGGT or DUSt3R's first-frame anchoring) and fundamental geometric constraints. Some recent global-feature methods already exhibit relative robustness to reference selection through symmetric cross-attention. I suggest tempering the claim to position the reference frame as an *unnecessary architectural constraint* rather than a fundamental flaw of the field, and explicitly state whether baselines in Figure 2 were evaluated by strictly changing the reference token/frame index or by re-running the model with permuted inputs. This clarifies whether the observed degradation is due to the reference mechanism itself or sensitivity to sequence order in non-equivariant models.

### Method / Approach
The mathematical formulation of permutation equivariance (Eq 1–3) is sound, given the explicit removal of positional encodings and learnable reference tokens. The alternating view-wise and global self-attention naturally preserves equivariance if no order-dependent signals are injected.

However, several methodological details require clarification:
1. **Supervision Architecture vs. True Model Contribution**: Appendix A.2 states that both the DINOv2 encoder and the VGGT pre-trained alternating attention module are loaded and the encoder is frozen. This means $\pi_3$ is not training a novel architecture from scratch, but rather repurposing a highly capable, pre-trained global feature extractor with a novel equivariant head and supervision objective. The ablation in Sec 4.5 (Table 7) isolates the supervision/objective well, as it shares the same initialization. The main text should transparently frame the contribution as a *reference-free, scale-coupled objective with an equivariant decoding scheme*, rather than implying end-to-end architectural novelty. This is not a weakness, but critical context for ICLR readers.
2. **Pose-Geometry Coupling & Optimization Stability**: The pose loss (Eq 8–10) relies on the scale factor $s^*$ computed from point map alignment (Eq 4). This creates a tightly coupled, potentially circular dependency early in training. Appendix A.4 candidly admits this leads to a "cold start" problem, requiring an auxiliary global proxy head to decouple geometry and stabilize optimization. This is a **major methodological finding** that directly impacts reproducibility and theoretical understanding of reference-free supervision. It must be moved from the appendix to the main limitations or method discussion. Hiding optimization instability in the appendix undercuts the paper's transparency.
3. **Computational Scaling**: Eq 8 computes the camera loss over all $N(N-1)$ ordered pairs. While manageable for $N \leq 24$ (Appendix A.2), the quadratic complexity in $N$ during training raises questions about scaling to longer video sequences or large unordered sets, which the abstract implies the model handles. Clarify whether pairwise loss computation is subsampled or if memory/throughput constraints limit practical $N$ during training.

### Experiments & Results
The experimental scope is comprehensive, covering pose, point maps, video/monocular depth, and robustness. Baselines are current and fair. However, several rigor aspects need addressing:
1. **Robustness Evaluation Scope (Sec 4.4, Table 6)**: The permutation robustness test only evaluates $N$ cyclic shifts/first-frame permutations. This directly targets reference-dependent baselines but does not fully test permutation equivariance, which should hold for *any* permutation of the input sequence. To solidify the equivariance claim, I strongly recommend adding a test with randomized permutations (e.g., 5–10 random shuffles per scene) and reporting variance. If the model is truly equivariant, performance variance across random permutations should be near zero (or within numerical/float precision noise).
2. **Statistical Significance**: Many reported improvements are marginal (e.g., Table 2 7-Scenes sparse Acc: VGGT 0.044 vs $\pi_3$ 0.047; NRGBD dense Acc: VGGT 0.017 vs $\pi_3$ 0.015). Without multiple seeds, error bars, or statistical tests, it is unclear whether these wins are meaningful or within run-to-run variance. ICLR typically expects statistical reporting for close margins, especially given the heavy transfer learning setup.
3. **Monocular Depth Evaluation (Table 5, 11)**: The model is not optimized for single-frame depth, yet claims competitive performance with MoGe. While impressive, the evaluation protocol should clarify whether the "monocular" setting feeds a single frame with dummy inputs or leverages the multi-view architecture in a non-ideal way. The results are plausible but the setup needs explicit description to ensure fair comparison against dedicated monocular specialists.

### Writing & Clarity
The manuscript is generally well-structured and mathematically clear. Equation numbering and notation are consistent. The primary clarity issue is the inconsistent framing of the training pipeline: the main text presents a seamless, bias-free architecture, while the appendix reveals frozen encoders, VGGT initialization, and a mandatory proxy task for stability. This disconnect should be resolved by integrating these practical constraints into the main narrative. Additionally, replacing "affine-invariant" with "scale-invariant" or "similarity-invariant" throughout will align with standard multi-view geometry terminology.

### Limitations & Broader Impact
Appendix A.8 is too brief and misses critical practical constraints:
- The "cold start" optimization instability and reliance on an auxiliary proxy task (Appendix A.4) must be explicitly stated as a key limitation.
- Heavy data scale (15 datasets) and compute requirements (up to 64 A100 GPUs, frozen large backbone) limit reproducibility for smaller labs and raise questions about generalization to truly out-of-distribution domains not covered by the training corpus.
- The model outputs *local* point maps in per-frame coordinates. While relative poses are predicted, large-scale or SLAM-style applications still require downstream global alignment/tracking, which contradicts the implicit claim of a fully self-contained reconstruction pipeline. Acknowledging this clarifies the intended use case (e.g., relative scene understanding, short sequences) vs. large-scale mapping.
No broader societal impacts are noted, which is acceptable given the purely technical/vision scope, but a brief statement on responsible deployment (e.g., in autonomous navigation where overconfident geometry could pose risks) would align with ICLR standards.

### Overall Assessment
This paper tackles a meaningful and well-motivated problem: eliminating reference-view dependency in feed-forward multi-view geometry reconstruction. The permutation-equivariant design, coupled with a scale-aware joint pose/geometry objective, yields empirical gains in robustness and accuracy across multiple benchmarks, positioning it competitively against recent baselines like VGGT. However, for ICLR's bar on methodological transparency and empirical rigor, the authors must address several key concerns: (1) clarify that the gains stem primarily from the reference-free supervision scheme and equivariant decoding atop a heavily initialized/frozen backbone, rather than end-to-end architectural novelty; (2) elevate the discussion of optimization instability and the need for an auxiliary proxy task from the appendix to the main limitations; (3) strengthen the robustness evaluation with random permutation tests and statistical significance reporting for marginal gains; and (4) correct terminology ("similarity-invariant" vs "affine-invariant"). With these revisions, the paper will present a transparent, reproducible, and conceptually clear contribution that aligns well with ICLR's standards for robust, principled vision systems.

# Neutral Reviewer
## Balanced Review

### Summary
This paper introduces $\pi^3$, a feed-forward visual geometry reconstruction model that eliminates the conventional reliance on a fixed reference view by employing a fully permutation-equivariant architecture. The method predicts affine-invariant camera poses and scale-invariant local point maps in per-frame coordinate systems, trained via relative supervision and optimal global scale alignment. Extensive experiments demonstrate that this reference-free design achieves state-of-the-art performance across camera pose, depth, and point map benchmarks while offering robustness to input ordering and high inference throughput.

### Strengths
1. **Clear conceptual contribution with rigorous empirical validation:** The paper systematically identifies and addresses a widely adopted but fragile inductive bias (reference view anchoring) in feed-forward 3D reconstruction. The robustness evaluation (Table 6) compellingly demonstrates near-zero standard deviation across permuted input sequences, directly validating the theoretical permutation-equivariance claim.
2. **Comprehensive multi-task benchmarking and efficiency:** The method is evaluated across diverse tasks and datasets (pose on RE10K/Co3D/Sintel/TUM, point maps on 7-Scenes/DTU/ETH3D, depth on KITTI/NYU-v2), consistently matching or surpassing strong baselines like VGGT and MoGe. The reported 57.4 FPS inference speed with 959M parameters (Table 4) demonstrates a highly favorable accuracy-efficiency trade-off for real-time applications.
3. **Transparent training protocol and honest ablation analysis:** The authors provide detailed loss formulations, optimization schedules, and loss weights (Appendix A.2). The ablation study (Table 7) cleanly isolates the impact of scale-invariant point maps and affine-invariant poses. Furthermore, the authors transparently discuss the "cold start" optimization challenge and the need for a proxy head during scratch training (Appendix A.4), which aids methodological clarity.

### Weaknesses
1. **Heavy reliance on VGGT initialization obscures architectural gains:** The main SOTA results depend on initializing the encoder and alternating attention layers with pre-trained VGGT weights. Appendix A.4 and Table 8 show that training $\pi^3$ from scratch without an auxiliary proxy head leads to suboptimal convergence and weaker performance than VGGT. This dependency raises questions about whether the reported gains stem from the permutation-equivariant design or primarily from inheriting VGGT's geometric priors.
2. **Inference-time scale ambiguity and dynamic scene handling are under-addressed:** The training pipeline resolves scale ambiguity via a batch-level optimal scale factor (Eq. 4), but the paper does not explain how scale should be resolved during real-world, zero-shot inference where ground truth is unavailable. Additionally, while the abstract claims support for dynamic scenes, the relative pose supervision assumes rigid camera-scene geometry. The lack of a dedicated dynamic object masking strategy or motion-robustness evaluation leaves a gap between the claim and the implemented pipeline.
3. **Baseline comparisons lack normalization for pre-training parity:** Methods like VGGT, FLARE, and Fast3R differ in parameter counts, training objectives, and pre-training regimes. Table 4 compares FPS and accuracy directly, but the influence of dataset scale, compute budget, and initialization strategy is not fully disentangled, making it difficult to isolate the true marginal benefit of equivariance over stronger baselines trained under identical multi-task objectives.

### Novelty & Significance
**Novelty:** High. Formulating feed-forward 3D reconstruction as a fully permutation-equivariant, reference-free problem is a meaningful architectural and conceptual departure from the field's standard practices. The combination of local point map prediction, affine-invariant pose supervision, and optimal scale alignment provides a coherent mathematical framework for this shift.
**Clarity:** Strong. The paper is well-structured, with clear notation, intuitive figures, and logical progression from problem motivation to method and evaluation. The appendix effectively supplements the main text with necessary training and metric details.
**Reproducibility:** Good. Code and model checkpoints are publicly linked, and critical training hyperparameters (LR schedules, batch sampling strategy, loss weighting, dataset composition, and initialization protocol) are thoroughly documented. Reproducing the exact SOTA results would require access to the specified VGGT checkpoint and the aggregated multi-dataset pipeline, which is standard practice but should be versioned explicitly.
**Significance:** High for the computer vision and 3D learning communities. Removing reference view dependency directly addresses a major failure mode in deployable reconstruction systems. The work aligns with ICLR's interest in robust, efficient, and theoretically grounded architectures for geometric understanding, offering a practical step toward reliable feed-forward 3D vision models.

### Suggestions for Improvement
1. **Isolate architectural contributions from pre-training priors:** Provide a controlled training-from-scratch comparison where both $\pi^3$ (with the proxy head) and VGGT are trained under identical data, compute, and multi-task loss conditions. Alternatively, frame the method explicitly as a "reference-free fine-tuning paradigm" and quantify how much of the performance gain is attributable to equivariance versus inherited weights.
2. **Detail inference-time scale resolution strategies:** Add a section or discussion on practical deployment pathways for recovering metric scale (e.g., integrating monocular depth priors, leveraging IMU data, or applying scene-level statistical scaling). Clarifying this will strengthen the paper's real-world applicability claims.
3. **Extend robustness validation to dynamic content and ensemble baselines:** Include a brief quantitative evaluation on a public dynamic dataset (e.g., TUM-Dynamics with moving object masks) to show how $\pi^3$ handles non-rigid scenes. Additionally, compare $\pi^3$'s accuracy against a strong multi-reference ensemble or a robust reference selection heuristic to confirm that equivariance provides a genuine geometric consensus advantage rather than merely reducing variance.
4. **Mitigate grid artifacts from the upsampler:** The limitations section notes grid-like artifacts from the MLP+pixel shuffle upsampling. Experiment with a lightweight learned upsampling module (e.g., a small transposed convolution or sub-pixel CNN) and report whether this improves point map smoothness without compromising the model's high FPS throughput.

# Spark Finder Review
## How to Improve This Paper

### Missing Experiments (top 3-5 only)
1. **From-Scratch Baseline vs. VGGT Initialization:** Since the main model freezes the DINO encoder and initializes weights from VGGT, SOTA gains could stem from inherited priors rather than permutation equivariance; a fully from-scratch training run (using identical compute/data) is required to isolate the architectural contribution.
2. **Matched Inference Speed Benchmarking:** The 57.4 FPS claim lacks matched inference resolution, sequence length ($N$), and batching strategy versus baselines like VGGT, making the "fast and lightweight" contribution unverifiable and potentially misleading.
3. **Explicit Dynamic/Non-Rigid Scene Evaluation:** The paper claims robustness to dynamic scenes but only reports aggregate metrics on datasets with mild motion; a dedicated evaluation on segmented dynamic regions (e.g., using motion masks or optical flow) is needed to prove the method handles non-rigid geometry rather than just static backgrounds.

### Deeper Analysis Needed (top 3-5 only)
1. **Cold-Start Optimization & Loss Weighting Dynamics:** Rounding the extremely high $\lambda_{trans}=100$ and $N \times N$ relative constraints lacks gradient conflict or loss landscape analysis; without this, it is unclear if performance jumps come from the equivariant architecture or merely stabilized optimization dynamics.
2. **Zero-Shot Metric Scale Recovery Mechanism:** The training pipeline relies on a ground-truth-derived optimal scale $s^*$, but the paper provides no strategy for recovering metric scale at inference; this gap severely undermines real-world applicability claims for robotics/AR.
3. **Attention Mechanism Order-Bias Verification:** Removing positional embeddings enforces theoretical equivariance, but the paper does not empirically verify whether the alternating attention layers inadvertently learn spurious order heuristics or fail to establish robust cross-view correspondences without explicit reference cues.

### Visualizations & Case Studies
1. **Permuted Input Consistency Demos:** Side-by-side point cloud and pose visualizations of identical scenes under extreme ordering changes (e.g., reversed vs. fully shuffled) to qualitatively validate the near-zero variance claim and expose reference-free stability.
2. **Failure Cases on Degenerate Geometries:** Qualitative results on low-texture, repetitive structure, or large-baseline scenes to explicitly show where removing the reference view degrades reconstruction, establishing honest boundaries for the method.
3. **Cross-View Attention Rollouts:** Heatmaps of global attention weights across shuffled views to demonstrate that the network actively aligns geometric correspondences rather than collapsing to template memorization or view-local features.

### Obvious Next Steps
1. **Formal Architectural Proof of Equivariance:** Provide a mathematical proof or exhaustive group-theory analysis showing that the specific combination of DINOv2, alternating attention, and decoder heads strictly satisfies $\phi(\pi(x)) = \pi(\phi(x))$ for all view permutations, particularly regarding spatial patch processing within frames.
2. **Inference-Time Scale/Reference Recovery:** Integrate a lightweight, learnable scale regressor or bundle-alignment post-processor to transition from oracle-aligned evaluations to true metric scale inference, which is mandatory for the claimed practical applications.
3. **Systematic Sequence Length ($N$) Ablation:** Evaluate robustness and performance decay across $N=2, 4, 8, 16, \dots$ frames to determine whether equivariance and accuracy scale predictably or degrade, as transformer attention without positional cues often exhibits non-linear behavior on varying sequence lengths.

# Final Consolidated Review
## Summary
This paper introduces $\pi^3$, a feed-forward visual geometry reconstruction model that eliminates the conventional reliance on a fixed reference view by employing a fully permutation-equivariant architecture. By predicting similarity-invariant camera poses and scale-invariant local point maps in per-frame coordinate systems, the model achieves robust, order-independent reconstruction. Extensive experiments demonstrate state-of-the-art or competitive performance across camera pose estimation, point map reconstruction, and depth estimation benchmarks, while maintaining high inference throughput (57.4 FPS).

## Strengths
- **Clear conceptual contribution directly addressing a documented fragility in prior work:** The paper systematically identifies how anchoring reconstruction to a fixed reference frame introduces order-dependent instability in methods like DUSt3R and VGGT. The proposed equivariant design directly resolves this, and the empirical validation (Table 6) compellingly demonstrates near-zero standard deviation across permuted inputs, confirming the theoretical claim.
- **Comprehensive multi-task evaluation with strong accuracy-efficiency trade-offs:** The method is rigorously tested across diverse tasks (pose, dense point maps, video/monocular depth) and settings (sparse/dense views, indoor/outdoor, seen/unseen domains). It consistently matches or surpasses strong baselines while reporting a highly favorable inference speed of 57.4 FPS, proving practical viability for real-time pipelines.
- **Transparent methodology and clean architectural ablation:** The authors clearly isolate the impact of scale-invariant point maps and similarity-invariant pose supervision (Table 7). The training pipeline, loss formulation, and dataset aggregation are thoroughly documented, and the authors honestly address optimization challenges and initialization dependencies in the appendix, aiding reproducibility.

## Weaknesses
- **SOTA claims are entangled with VGGT initialization and frozen priors:** The main results rely on initializing the encoder and alternating attention layers from a pre-trained VGGT checkpoint and freezing the backbone (Appendix A.2). While Appendix A.4 shows that training from scratch is possible, it requires an auxiliary global proxy head to overcome a coupled optimization "cold start," and even then, performance lags behind the initialized version (Table 8). This makes it difficult to disentangle the true marginal gains of permutation equivariance from the strong geometric priors inherited from the reference-based teacher model. For ICLR readers seeking to understand the pure architectural contribution, the dependency on a strong, non-equivariant baseline for convergence obscures the standalone novelty of the equivariant objective.
- **Dynamic scene handling claim outpaces the rigid geometric supervision:** The abstract and training protocol claim support for dynamic and static scenes, but the core pose and point map supervision (Eq. 7-10) strictly assumes rigid camera-scene geometry. The paper does not include motion masking, flow-based uncertainty weighting, or explicit dynamic object evaluation. Evaluating on datasets with moving objects likely benefits from the model's robustness to noise, but without a dedicated metric or ablation on non-rigid regions, the claim of handling dynamic content remains partially unsubstantiated.

## Nice-to-Haves
- **Inference-time scale disambiguation strategy:** The training and evaluation pipelines rely on an oracle-derived optimal scale factor $s^*$ (Eq. 4) for alignment. While scale ambiguity is inherent to monocular/multi-view reconstruction, a practical discussion on recovering consistent relative or metric scale at inference (e.g., via a lightweight monocular depth prior or statistical scaling) would strengthen deployment readiness.
- **Random permutation robustness test:** The robustness evaluation (Sec 4.4) tests $N$ cyclic shifts (rotating the first frame). While this directly targets reference-dependent baselines, testing 5-10 fully random permutations per sequence would provide stricter empirical validation of true permutation equivariance.
- **Clarification of monocular evaluation protocol:** Explicitly stating whether monocular depth evaluation uses $N=1$, frame repetition, or a dummy context view would ensure fair comparison against dedicated single-frame specialists.

## Novel Insights
The work successfully demonstrates that removing reference-frame dependency not only stabilizes reconstruction against order-induced artifacts but can actively improve the geometric structure of learned representations. The eigenvalue analysis revealing that $\pi^3$'s predicted poses concentrate on a significantly lower-dimensional manifold than VGGT's (Figure 4 & Appendix A.3) is a compelling observation: by forcing the model to reason in purely relative, self-consistent coordinates, the network naturally learns the intrinsic low-dimensional structure of real-world camera trajectories. Furthermore, the finding that relative pose supervision suffers from severe "cold start" coupling during from-scratch training, yet stabilizes seamlessly when bootstrapped from a reference-based teacher, offers a valuable practical lesson for the community: equivariant objectives are highly expressive but often require decoupled auxiliary signals or strong priors to navigate early optimization landscapes effectively.

## Suggestions
- **Reframe the initialization dependency explicitly in the introduction or method section:** Clearly state that the primary SOTA results leverage VGGT priors, and position the $\pi^3$ contribution as a *reference-free, equivariant supervision and decoding scheme* that stabilizes and improves upon these priors. This prevents misinterpretation of end-to-end architectural novelty and strengthens transparency.
- **Add a dedicated evaluation table or qualitative ablation for dynamic/moving objects:** If the training includes dynamic scenes, evaluate the model on a subset with explicit foreground masks or optical flow thresholds to quantify how much the equivariant formulation mitigates motion-induced pose/geometry corruption compared to reference-anchored baselines.
- **Provide a brief practical guideline for inference-time alignment:** A 2-3 sentence discussion on how practitioners might resolve the unknown scale $s^*$ during deployment (e.g., using a precomputed median depth prior, IMU fusion, or simple Procrustes alignment with a single known scale anchor) would bridge the gap between benchmark evaluation and real-world application.
