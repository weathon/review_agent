# Harsh Critic Review
## Section-by-Section Critical Review

### Title & Abstract
- **Title Accuracy:** The title accurately captures the core technical contribution (permutation-equivariance) and the application domain (visual geometry learning).
- **Abstract Clarity:** The abstract clearly states the problem (reliance on fixed reference views in recent feed-forward 3D reconstruction models), the proposed method (fully permutation-equivariant architecture predicting affine-invariant poses and scale-invariant point maps), and the empirical outcome (robustness to input ordering, SOTA on pose/depth/reconstruction tasks, high inference speed).
- **Unsupported Claims:** The abstract claims that removing the reference view bias "leads to higher accuracy and performance." While the experiments later show competitive SOTA results, the abstract implies a direct causal link between equivariance and higher accuracy that isn't theoretically justified. The gain could stem from other factors (e.g., different training dynamics, dataset scaling, or architectural choices like reduced depth). This causal claim should be softened or theoretically grounded in the abstract.

### Introduction & Motivation
- **Motivation & Gap:** The problem is well-motivated within the specific context of recent feed-forward transformer-based 3D reconstruction (e.g., DUSt3R, VGGT, MASt3R), which often anchor global geometry to a single frame or camera token. Figure 2 effectively illustrates the performance variance caused by poor reference selection in VGGT.
- **Contributions:** Clearly stated: (1) identifying the reference view bias, (2) proposing the equivariant architecture with relative supervision, and (3) empirical validation across multiple tasks.
- **Over-claiming:** The claim "We are the first to systematically identify and challenge the reliance on a fixed reference view in visual geometry reconstruction" is overstated. Classical SfM/MVS and even some early pairwise deep methods operate in relative or pairwise coordinate frames. The novelty here is specifically in applying full permutation equivariance to a *single, global, feed-forward transformer pass* without a designated anchor. The introduction should more precisely scope the contribution to modern feed-forward multi-view transformers to avoid historical inaccuracy. Additionally, while the introduction empirically notes robustness, it lacks a conceptual explanation of *why* removing the reference view should inherently improve accuracy rather than just consistency. A brief discussion on how reference anchoring creates error accumulation or asymmetric gradient flow would strengthen the motivation.

### Method / Approach
- **Clarity & Reproducibility:** The mathematical formulation of permutation equivariance (Eq. 1-3) is clear and standard. The architecture is well-described: DINOv2 backbone, alternating attention (similar to VGGT), and per-view decoders. Appendix A.1 provides sufficient layer counts and loss weights.
- **Key Assumptions:** Section 3.2 assumes a "single optimal scale factor, $s^*$, which minimizes... across the entire image sequence." This is a critical assumption: it presumes all $N$ views of a scene share a consistent global scale ambiguity. This holds for static multi-view sequences but fails for dynamic scenes, independent monocular depth, or sequences with variable zoom/uncalibrated intrinsics. The method's behavior under these conditions is not addressed.
- **Logical Gaps:** Equations 4-5 describe computing $s^*$ using *ground-truth* point clouds for loss alignment. However, the paper does not explain how $s^*$ is handled at *inference time*. Since the network predicts scale-invariant point maps, how are they aligned for downstream tasks or evaluation without GT? The text implies post-hoc alignment (e.g., Umeyama/ICP), but this should be explicitly stated in the main text, not left implicit.
- **Training vs. Inference Tension:** Appendix A.4 acknowledges a "cold start" problem when training from scratch, requiring an auxiliary global proxy head that *uses* a reference view to stabilize optimization. While the final model is equivariant, this reveals that the loss landscape without any reference anchoring is highly unstable. This tension between the theoretical reference-free design and the practical necessity of a reference for training stability should be discussed in Section 3.
- **Theoretical Claims:** The manifold analysis (Figure 4, Appendix A.3) shows predicted poses concentrate on fewer principal components. While interesting, the paper asserts this "validates the low-dimensional structure" without establishing a causal or predictive link to reconstruction accuracy. This remains an observational claim rather than a mechanistic explanation.

### Experiments & Results
- **Testing Claims:** Experiments thoroughly test robustness to permutation (Table 6/“Table 4.4” in text), camera pose, point map quality, and depth estimation. Baselines are appropriate and state-of-the-art for 2024-2025.
- **Fairness & Baselines:** Comparisons against VGGT, Fast3R, CUT3R, FLARE, and MoGe are relevant. However, two confounding factors weaken the claims:
  1. **Model Depth:** Table 4 reports $\pi[3]$ at 57.4 FPS vs VGGT at 43.2 FPS. Appendix A.1 reveals $\pi[3]$ uses 36 transformer layers while VGGT uses 48. The speed advantage is largely architectural (fewer layers), not a direct result of permutation equivariance. A controlled speed/accuracy comparison at identical depth is needed.
  2. **Initialization Bias:** The main model initializes the encoder/attention weights from VGGT. Appendix A.4 shows that when trained *from scratch* (with a proxy), $\pi[3]$ loses to VGGT on 7-Scenes and NRGBD Accuracy/Completion. It is unclear how much of the reported improvement in Tables 2-5 stems from the VGGT prior versus the proposed equivariant formulation.
- **Missing Ablations:** An ablation isolating the impact of VGGT initialization vs. random training would be highly valuable. Additionally, ablation of the single-scale assumption $s^*$ (e.g., allowing per-view or per-pair scales) would clarify its necessity for outdoor/dynamic scenes.
- **Statistical Significance:** ICLR expects reporting across multiple random seeds. Tables 1-5 report single-run values. Table 6/“Table 4.4” reports standard deviation *across input permutations*, not across training seeds. Given the sensitivity of deep 3D reconstruction to initialization, variance across 3-5 seeds should be reported to support the robustness claims.
- **Metrics & Datasets:** Evaluation protocols follow established norms. However, Section 4.2 states point maps are aligned via Umeyama *followed by ICP refinement*. While standard, ICP can converge to local minima and may disproportionately benefit one method over another depending on initial alignment quality. Reporting unrefined or rigid-only aligned metrics would provide a cleaner comparison of geometric recovery capability.

### Writing & Clarity
- **Clarity Issues:** The transition from training supervision (Eq. 4-10) to inference procedure is ambiguous. Readers are left wondering how relative poses and point maps are realized in a usable coordinate system at test time without a reference frame or scale oracle. A dedicated "Inference & Alignment" paragraph would resolve this.
- **Figures & Tables:** Figure 1 and 2 are highly informative. Figure 3 (architecture comparison) effectively contrasts reference-dependent vs reference-free designs. Table 8 (Appendix A.4) is crucial for understanding scratch training behavior but is buried. Key architectural differences (36 vs 48 layers) should be moved from Appendix A.1 to the main method/experiment section for fair baseline comparison.
- **Terminology:** The paper uses "affine-invariant" and "scale-invariant" somewhat interchangeably. Section 3.2 discusses scale-invariance for point maps, while Section 3.3 discusses affine-invariance (similarity transform: rotation + translation + global scale) for poses. Tightening these definitions would prevent confusion about which ambiguities are explicitly modeled versus implicitly resolved by post-hoc alignment.

### Limitations & Broader Impact
- **Acknowledged Limitations:** The authors transparently list failure cases: transparent objects, lack of diffusion-level fine detail, and grid artifacts from pixel-shuffle upsampling (Appendix A.8).
- **Missed Limitations:**
  1. **Training Stability without Reference:** As noted in Appendix A.4, the model struggles to converge from scratch without a reference-view proxy. This implies the "reference-free" property is an inference-time architectural constraint, not a training-time optimization advantage.
  2. **Dynamic/Scale-Varying Scenes:** The reliance on a single global scale factor $s^*$ (Eq. 4) breaks down for sequences with changing focal length, independent monocular frames, or highly dynamic objects where metric consistency is lost. The impact of this on the reported "internal dynamic scene dataset" is not discussed.
  3. **Quadratic Scaling:** While the model is fast for ~24 frames, the global self-attention mechanism implies $O(N^2)$ complexity. Scalability to hundreds of views (compared to Fast3R's claim of 1000+ images) is not evaluated.
- **Broader Impact:** Standard dual-use considerations for 3D reconstruction (AR/VR robotics vs. surveillance/mapping) apply but are not explicitly discussed. Given the focus on architectural efficiency and robustness, a brief note on accessibility for resource-constrained deployment would align well with ICLR's broader impact expectations.

### Overall Assessment
The paper presents a clean, theoretically motivated architectural shift for feed-forward 3D reconstruction: removing the fixed reference view bias via full permutation equivariance and relative supervision. The empirical results demonstrate compelling robustness to input ordering and competitive SOTA performance across multiple geometry and pose benchmarks. However, for ICLR's acceptance bar, several key concerns must be addressed. First, the speed advantage is confounded by a reduced transformer depth (36 vs. 48 layers in VGGT) rather than the equivariant design itself, requiring controlled ablation. Second, the reliance on VGGT pre-trained weights obscures whether performance gains stem from the proposed architecture or the prior; Appendix A.4 suggests scratch training even underperforms baselines on some metrics, weakening the architectural contribution claim. Third, the paper lacks statistical significance reporting across random seeds, which is critical given the sensitivity of 3D reconstruction optimization. Finally, the mechanism for resolving scale and coordinate ambiguity at inference time remains underspecified. Addressing these points—particularly disentangling architectural benefits from initialization priors, providing seed variance, and clarifying inference alignment—would significantly strengthen the paper. The contribution stands as a meaningful step toward more robust, symmetric geometry learning, but requires additional empirical rigor to fully validate its claims.

# Neutral Reviewer
## Balanced Review

### Summary
The paper introduces $\pi^3$, a feed-forward neural network for multi-view visual geometry reconstruction that removes the conventional reliance on a fixed reference view by enforcing full permutation equivariance. The model predicts affine-invariant camera poses and scale-invariant local point maps using a reference-token-free transformer, supervised entirely through relative geometric constraints. Comprehensive experiments demonstrate that this design achieves state-of-the-art performance across camera pose estimation, video/monocular depth, and dense point cloud reconstruction, while exhibiting near-zero sensitivity to input frame ordering and high inference speed.

### Strengths
1. **Well-Motivated & Empirically Validated Core Insight:** The critique of reference-view bias in DUSt3R/VGGT-style pipelines is compelling. Section 4.4 and Table 6 rigorously demonstrate that $\pi^3$ achieves near-zero standard deviation in reconstruction metrics across input permutations, directly solving a known fragility in prior feed-forward methods.
2. **Strong Multi-Task Performance & Efficiency:** The method sets or closely matches SOTA across diverse benchmarks (Sintel, ETH3D, DTU, RealEstate10K, KITTI) for both pose and geometry. Notably, it runs at 57.4 FPS with a 959M parameter model, offering a favorable accuracy-latency trade-off compared to heavier or slower baselines.
3. **High Reproducibility & Engineering Transparency:** The paper provides exceptional implementation details, including exact dataset compositions, loss weights, two-stage training schedules, dynamic batching strategies, and explicit architectural deviations from VGGT. The commitment to open-source code and models further aligns with ICLR's reproducibility expectations.

### Weaknesses
1. **Heavy Reliance on Privileged Initialization:** The primary experiments freeze the encoder and initialize from pre-trained VGGT weights. Appendix A.4 explicitly acknowledges a "cold start" problem when training from scratch, noting that VGGT can outperform $\pi^3$ on indoor datasets without an auxiliary global proxy. This muddles whether the SOTAs stem from the reference-free paradigm or the strong initialization/multi-task stabilization.
2. **Superficial Treatment of the Pose Manifold Property:** Section 3.3 and Figure 4 claim that predicted poses concentrate on a low-dimensional manifold, supported by eigenvalue analysis. However, the paper does not rigorously connect this observation to optimization dynamics, training stability, or generalization bounds. It remains an interesting post-hoc observation rather than a mechanistically validated insight.
3. **Limited Monocular Depth Performance & Unaddressed Decoder Artifacts:** While strong for multi-view/video, monocular depth (Table 5) is only competitive with specialized single-image models (e.g., MoGe, Depth Anything V2). Furthermore, the limitations section acknowledges grid-like artifacts from the pixel-shuffle upsampling head, but no ablation or justification is provided for why this simple decoder was retained over alternatives that might reduce artifacts.
4. **Statistical Rigor in Ablations:** Key ablation and comparison tables (Tables 1, 2, 7) report single-run metrics without variance estimates. Given the narrow margins in several tasks (e.g., point map accuracy on 7-Scenes dense), it is unclear if gains are statistically significant or sensitive to random seeds.

### Novelty & Significance
- **Novelty:** Solid for ICLR. While the alternating self-attention backbone closely follows VGGT, the systematic removal of reference tokens/positional encodings and the explicit shift to relative, scale-invariant supervision constitute a meaningful methodological advancement in feed-forward 3D reconstruction. The novelty lies more in architectural/paradigm simplification than in new mathematical machinery.
- **Clarity:** High. The problem statement, equivariance formulation, and loss definitions are clearly presented. Mathematical notation is consistent, and figures effectively contrast reference-anchored vs. reference-free behaviors. Minor ambiguities exist around the precise interplay between affine-invariant poses and scale-invariant point maps during optimization.
- **Reproducibility:** Very good. Training protocols, hyperparameters, dataset splits, and initialization strategies are thoroughly documented. The primary reproducibility caveat is the dependence on external VGGT checkpoints, which is clearly stated but limits fully independent "from-scratch" replication.
- **Significance:** High. Order-agnostic, robust geometry estimation directly benefits real-world pipelines operating on unordered image collections, streaming video, or multi-agent data. The work meets ICLR's bar for impact by addressing a concrete architectural bias and providing a faster, more stable alternative to existing SOTA models.

### Suggestions for Improvement
1. **Isolate Architectural Contribution from Initialization:** Conduct a controlled experiment where both $\pi^3$ and VGGT are trained from identical random initialization under the same multi-task regime. Quantify the performance gap early vs. late in training to demonstrate whether the equivariant design fundamentally improves the optimization landscape or primarily refines pre-existing geometric priors.
2. **Deepen the Pose Manifold Analysis & Training Dynamics:** Expand Section 3.3 by tracking eigenvalue distributions across training epochs. Correlate the emergence of low-dimensional pose structure with loss convergence or reconstruction accuracy. If possible, provide a brief theoretical intuition on why relative supervision naturally encourages manifold-constrained outputs.
3. **Add Statistical Variance & Multiple Runs:** Report mean ± standard deviation over at least 3 random seeds for Tables 1, 2, and 7. This is standard for ICLR and will clarify whether the reported SOTA margins are consistent or fall within baseline noise, particularly for tightly contested metrics.
4. **Ablate the Upsampling Head:** Since pixel-shuffle artifacts are acknowledged, evaluate a lightweight alternative (e.g., bilinear + MLP, or a 2D CNN projection layer) and report the trade-off in accuracy, inference speed, and artifact prevalence. Justifying the current design choice empirically will strengthen the engineering narrative.
5. **Clarify Equivariance Formalism:** Briefly formalize why predicting poses in relative frame pairs preserves output permutation equivariance despite the $N \times N$ supervision. A concise mathematical statement bridging Eq. (7) and Eq. (2) will satisfy ICLR reviewers looking for rigorous alignment between architectural constraints and loss design.

# Spark Finder Review
## How to Improve This Paper

### Missing Experiments (top 3-5 only)
1. Add a fair comparison controlling for VGGT initialization; since the encoder and attention layers are frozen and initialized from VGGT, the SOTA results are heavily confounded by pre-trained priors rather than your architecture, directly undermining the novelty claim.
2. Add accuracy degradation curves under pathological reference frames for baselines; reporting only standard deviation (Table 6) is insufficient because it fails to reveal whether equivariance actually improves geometric recovery or merely anchors outputs to a lower, more consistent baseline.
3. Add sequence length scaling experiments; evaluations only use fixed short sequences (2-6 frames), so you cannot claim robustness to dynamic videos without proving the scale-aligned supervision doesn't collapse or accumulate noise as $N$ exceeds 20.

### Deeper Analysis Needed (top 3-5 only)
1. Decouple the contribution of permutation equivariance from relative scale-invariant supervision; the gains likely stem from the loss formulation rather than symmetry constraints, requiring a test against a non-equivariant but relative-supervision baseline to validate the core architectural hypothesis.
2. Analyze the gradient stability of the ROE scale solver $s^*$; because all camera and pointmap losses scale by this single batch-level scalar, unquantified solver variance or optimization failure directly threatens gradient quality and training convergence in multi-scale scenes.
3. Isolate the impact of removing positional embeddings; you claim equivariance is the driver of performance, but dropping frame embeddings could simply reduce overfitting to temporal order. You need an ablation showing what happens when equivariance is broken but embeddings are restored with different noise levels.

### Visualizations & Case Studies (top 3-5 only)
1. Visualize identical scenes reconstructed under drastically shuffled input orders; numerical STD is abstract, and direct visual comparison is required to prove your method maintains exact geometry while reference-anchored baselines visibly warp or drift.
2. Plot global attention weight distributions before and after input permutation; without positional embeddings, you must visually demonstrate how the transformer inherently resolves spatial-temporal correspondence, otherwise the "reference-free" attention is a black box that may just be discarding inter-frame context.
3. Show qualitative failure cases where strict equivariance induces global flips or mirrored ambiguities; identifying these degenerate solutions is essential to prove you understand the practical limits and symmetry-breaking bounds of your own method.

### Obvious Next Steps (top 3-5 only)
1. Report mean ± standard deviation across multiple training seeds for all primary tables; ICLR standards require statistical rigor to verify that improvements like the 50% ATE reduction on Sintel (0.167 to 0.074) are not artifacts of a single lucky initialization.
2. Normalize speed and efficiency metrics against FLOPs and parameter count; comparing raw FPS is scientifically misleading when the proposed model intentionally reduces transformer depth by 25%, obscuring whether efficiency stems from equivariance or simple model compression.
3. Execute strict zero-shot cross-dataset generalization; training on 15 aggregated datasets and testing on a subset does not validate "open-domain" robustness, requiring explicit out-of-distribution evaluations (e.g., train indoor/static → test outdoor/dynamic) to back the generalization claims.

# Final Consolidated Review
## Summary
The paper introduces $\pi^3$, a feed-forward transformer for multi-view visual geometry reconstruction that removes the fixed reference-view bias by enforcing full permutation equivariance. The model predicts affine-invariant camera poses and scale-invariant local point maps using a reference-token-free architecture supervised via relative geometric constraints. Empirical results demonstrate near-zero sensitivity to input ordering and state-of-the-art performance across camera pose, depth, and dense point map benchmarks.

## Strengths
- **Rigorous demonstration of permutation robustness:** Table 6 provides compelling empirical evidence that the architecture achieves near-zero standard deviation across input permutations, directly solving the documented fragility of reference-anchored feed-forward pipelines (e.g., VGGT, DUSt3R).
- **Strong multi-task performance with high efficiency:** The method establishes competitive or state-of-the-art results across diverse benchmarks (Sintel, ETH3D, DTU, RealEstate10K, KITTI) for both pose and geometry estimation while maintaining a favorable accuracy-throughput trade-off (57.4 FPS).
- **Clean architectural formulation & reproducibility:** The systematic removal of reference tokens and frame-specific positional encodings, combined with transparent training protocols, dataset compositions, and loss weights, provides a well-documented and reproducible shift toward symmetric geometry learning.

## Weaknesses
- **Confounded efficiency and architectural contribution claims:** The reported speed advantage over VGGT (57.4 vs 43.2 FPS) is primarily driven by using a shallower transformer backbone (36 layers vs 48 in VGGT, per Appendix A.1), not the equivariant design. Furthermore, the main results rely on freezing encoder and attention weights initialized from a pre-trained VGGT checkpoint. Appendix A.4 explicitly acknowledges a "cold start" optimization instability when training from scratch, requiring an auxiliary reference-view proxy to stabilize convergence. This muddies whether performance gains stem from the proposed architectural parity or strong initialization priors.
- **Problematic scale assumption and untested scalability:** Equation 4 computes a single global scale factor $s^*$ across all views of a sequence for loss alignment. This assumption inherently breaks for dynamic scenes, sequences with variable focal lengths, or independent monocular frames where metric consistency is lost. Despite claims of applicability to dynamic content, the paper does not address how this limitation is mitigated. Additionally, the global self-attention mechanism implies $O(N^2)$ complexity, yet scalability beyond ~24 frames is not evaluated, leaving its practical limits unclear compared to linear-scaling baselines.
- **Lack of statistical rigor:** All primary experimental tables (Tables 1–5, 7) report single-run metrics without variance across training seeds. Given the narrow margins in several tasks (e.g., point map accuracy on 7-Scenes) and the known sensitivity of geometric optimization to initialization, it is impossible to verify whether the SOTA margins are statistically significant or artifacts of favorable seeds.

## Nice-to-Haves
- Explicitly clarify the inference-time alignment procedure (post-hoc Umeyama/Sim(3) vs. the training-time ROE scale solver) to prevent confusion between training supervision and deployment workflows.
- Provide a conceptual ablation that decouples symmetry constraints (removing positional embeddings) from relative supervision to isolate the true driver of performance gains.
- Visualize global attention weight distributions under shuffled input orders to empirically demonstrate how the model resolves spatial-temporal correspondence without anchors.

## Novel Insights
The paper reveals that enforcing strict permutation equivariance in feed-forward geometry transformers acts as a powerful implicit regularizer. By stripping away frame-specific anchors and positional biases, the architecture is forced to learn purely pairwise relative relationships, which naturally constrain predicted camera trajectories to concentrate on low-dimensional manifolds rather than scattering randomly. This suggests that architectural symmetry, rather than just loss engineering or data scaling, can fundamentally stabilize multi-view optimization and suppress reference-dependent error accumulation.

## Potentially Missed Related Work
- Classical unordered Structure-from-Motion literature (e.g., rotation averaging, global optimization without fixed anchors, and view-graph approaches) should be better contextualized. The claim of being the first to challenge reference-view bias overlooks decades of multi-view geometry that successfully operated without fixed reference frames; scoping the novelty explicitly to modern transformer-based feed-forward pipelines would prevent historical overstatement.

## Suggestions
- Conduct a controlled experiment where $\pi^3$ and VGGT are trained from identical random initialization with matched transformer depth and FLOPs to fairly isolate architectural versus initialization contributions.
- Report mean ± standard deviation across at least three training seeds for all primary tables to meet standard statistical expectations for reproducibility.
- Explicitly analyze and mitigate the single-scale assumption for dynamic or unstructured inputs, and evaluate sequence-length scaling beyond 24 frames to validate the practical limits of the global attention mechanism.
