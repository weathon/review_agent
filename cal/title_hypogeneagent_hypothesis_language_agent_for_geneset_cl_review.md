# Harsh Critic Review
## Section-by-Section Critical Review

### Title & Abstract
- **Does the title accurately reflect the contribution?** Yes. The title clearly specifies the target task (cluster resolution selection), the data type (Perturb-seq), and the approach (hypothesis-driven language agent).
- **Does the abstract clearly state the problem, method, and key results?** It outlines the subjectivity of resolution selection, introduces the LLM agent, defines intra/inter agreement metrics, and claims the method outperforms silhouette/modularity on K562 data. However, "alignment with known pathway" is vague and lacks a concrete quantitative metric in the abstract.
- **Are any claims in the abstract unsupported by the paper?** The claim of "fully automated, context-aware interpretation pipelines" is aspirational but currently constrained by proprietary API dependencies, fixed prompt engineering, and validation on only one cell line. The statement that the method selects granularities that exhibit better alignment than classical metrics lacks statistical backing or a formal definition of the "known pathway" ground truth in the abstract.

### Introduction & Motivation
- **Is the problem well-motivated? Is the gap in prior work clearly identified?** Yes. The tension between statistical clustering metrics (modularity, silhouette) and biological interpretability is well-established. The gap that LLMs have not been used to *close the loop* for hyperparameter optimization is clearly stated and relevant.
- **Are the contributions clearly stated and accurate?** Four contributions are listed: coupling clustering with annotation, formalizing ICS/ICD, defining Resolution Score, and validation on perturb-seq data. This is accurate to the manuscript, though the "formal definitions" are more heuristic scoring rules than theoretical guarantees.
- **Does the introduction over-claim or under-sell?** The introduction slightly over-claims by stating the metric is maximized when clusters are "simultaneously coherent and mutually exclusive" without addressing cases where biological programs are genuinely multifunctional or hierarchical. It would benefit from explicitly stating the biological prior for the K562 dataset (e.g., expected number of perturbation effects or cell states) to ground the "alignment" claim.

### Method / Approach
- **Is the method clearly described and reproducible?** Mostly, but key implementation details are missing or ambiguous. Section 3.2 and Appendix C describe clustering but do not specify *how many* highly-variable genes are used to construct the cluster signature fed to the LLM. Marker gene count heavily influences LLM context length and reasoning quality. The reliance on proprietary models (GPT-o3, text-embedding-3-large) limits open reproducibility, which ICLR increasingly scrutinizes.
- **Are key assumptions stated and justified?** The core assumption is that cosine similarity of LLM-generated free-text hypotheses reliably proxies biological coherence/distinctiveness. This is plausible but not justified. There is an implicit assumption that each cluster maps to a single dominant biological process, which ignores pleiotropy, hierarchical programs, or mixed perturbation effects.
- **Are there logical gaps in the derivation or reasoning?** Table 1 defines Inter-cluster distinctiveness (ICD) as a *cosine distance*, but Section 3.4 explicitly defines it as a mean cosine *similarity*: `ICD_k = (1/(C-1)) * sum(sim(h_k1, h_l1))`. The Resolution Score formula then uses `1 - ICD_k`, which only makes sense if `ICD_k` is similarity. This inconsistency needs correction. Additionally, the weight $w=1/3$ is described as chosen by a "small grid search" with no details on the search space, validation criterion, or risk of overfitting to K562.
- **Are there edge cases or failure modes not discussed?** If a cluster is biologically multifunctional, the agent will still return one top hypothesis, artificially inflating inter-cluster distinctiveness (since multifunctional clusters will share terms with multiple distinct clusters, increasing similarity/ICD and penalizing valid resolutions). The method does not account for ontology structure (GO terms have parent-child relationships; cosine similarity on free text may penalize semantically close but lexically different terms).

### Experiments & Results
- **Do the experiments actually test the paper's claims?** Stage 1 validates the LLM's annotation capability on curated GO sets. Stage 2 applies it to K562 resolution selection. The experiments test consistency, but do not rigorously validate that the selected resolution (r=0.4/0.5) actually recovers *true* biological states or perturbation mechanisms better than baselines. The "ground truth" for K562 clustering is not quantitatively established here; conclusions rely heavily on UMAP visual clarity and author expectation.
- **Are baselines appropriate and fairly compared?** Silhouette and modularity are standard, but comparing a biology-informed score to geometry/graph-informed scores is inherently asymmetric unless evaluated against a biological gold standard (e.g., expert-annotated cell types, or precision/recall on known targeted pathways). The comparison in 4.4.3 (applying RS/ICS/ICD metrics to classical GO enrichment results) is circular; using your own scoring function to score another method's output does not constitute an independent validation.
- **Are there missing ablations that would materially change conclusions?** Yes. (1) **Number of marker genes:** Does feeding 20 vs 100 genes change the optimal resolution? (2) **LLM robustness on K562:** Stage 1 shows GPT-o3 is best on curated GO, but does GPT-4o or Gemini-2.5 select the same resolution on K562? If resolution choice is highly model-dependent, the method's claims of objectivity weaken. (3) **Weight $w$:** Figure S5 shows $w$ sensitivity, but the choice of 1/3 is not statistically justified or shown to generalize.
- **Are error bars / statistical significance reported?** Box plots capture cross-cluster variance per resolution, but no statistical tests (e.g., permutation tests, bootstrap confidence intervals on the median RS) are provided to show that the RS peak at r=0.4 is statistically significant or distinct from neighboring resolutions.
- **Do the results support the claims made, or are they cherry-picked?** Figure 3b and 4b use UMAP visualizations at the "optimal" resolution to claim clusters are "clear" or "well-separated." Relying on visual inspection undermines the paper's core premise of replacing subjective inspection. Quantitative metrics against known pathway databases or perturbation recovery rates are needed.
- **Are datasets and evaluation metrics appropriate?** Validation on a single K562 Perturb-seq dataset is insufficient for ICLR's generalizability standards. The paper claims applicability to "single-cell, perturb-seq and multi-omics analyses" but provides zero evidence beyond one leukemia cell line. Metric definitions need correction (similarity vs distance inconsistency).

### Writing & Clarity
- **Are there sections that are confusing or poorly explained?** Section 3.4 contains contradictory definitions of ICD (Table 1 says distance; text says similarity). The transition from Stage 1 (LLM benchmarking) to Stage 2 (resolution selection) assumes that high GO-annotation accuracy directly translates to better resolution scoring, but the connection is not explicitly quantified (e.g., does higher $S_k[\cos]$ correlate with higher $RS_k$?).
- **Are figures and tables clear and informative?** Tables and figures are generally clean. Figure 1 (prompts) and Figure 2 (workflow) are helpful. However, Figures 3c/d and 4c/d plot "average inter score" and "average intra score" without clarifying if these are raw similarity values or transformed metrics. The y-axis labels in supplementary figures lack units or clear scales, making cross-panel comparison difficult.

### Limitations & Broader Impact
- **Do the authors acknowledge the key limitations?** They mention scalability (Human Cell Atlas), generalizability to other ontologies/modalities, LLM cost, and prompt sensitivity. This is a good start.
- **Are there fundamental limitations they missed?** **Pretraining data leakage:** Stage 1 benchmarks on 100 curated GOBP sets using frontier LLMs. Frontier models are heavily trained on biomedical literature and GO databases, so Stage 1 likely measures memorization/retrieval rather than novel reasoning. This must be quantified (e.g., testing on novel/synthetic gene sets held out from web corpora). **Proprietary dependency:** Relying on closed models conflicts with open computational biology standards and introduces cost barriers for academic labs. **Single-dataset scope:** No discussion of how cell-type diversity, batch effects, or sparsity in other tissues (e.g., brain, tumor) might degrade signature quality and LLM performance.
- **Are there failure modes or negative societal impacts not discussed?** Automated resolution selection without human oversight could lead to overconfident biological conclusions in clinical or drug-discovery pipelines. The paper should briefly discuss integrating human-in-the-loop validation or uncertainty thresholds for automated deployment.

### Overall Assessment
This paper proposes a conceptually interesting framework to bridge unsupervised single-cell clustering and automated functional annotation using LLM-derived consistency metrics. The core idea of using intra/inter semantic agreement to optimize the Leiden resolution parameter addresses a real pain point in computational biology. However, the manuscript in its current form falls short of ICLR standards for methodological rigor and empirical validation. Key concerns include inconsistent metric definitions (similarity vs. distance in ICD), reliance on a single dataset (K562), lack of statistical significance testing, heavy dependence on proprietary black-box models, and an absence of quantitative biological ground-truth validation beyond visual UMAP inspection. The ablation studies are incomplete (marker gene count, weight $w$, LLM selection on actual resolution task are missing). To be competitive at ICLR, the authors must: (1) formalize and correct the ICD/RS definitions, (2) validate on at least two additional, biologically distinct datasets with known ground-truth labels, (3) replace subjective visual claims with quantitative pathway recovery metrics, (4) provide rigorous ablations, and (5) explicitly address pretraining data leakage risks in Stage 1. The contribution has promise, but requires substantial empirical and methodological strengthening before acceptance.

# Neutral Reviewer
## Balanced Review

### Summary
This paper introduces **HYPOGENEAGENT**, an LLM-based framework that automates cluster resolution selection and functional annotation in single-cell and Perturb-seq data. It quantifies the biological coherence of cluster partitions by computing intra-cluster agreement and inter-cluster distinctiveness from semantic embeddings of LLM-generated Gene Ontology hypotheses, combining them into a composite Resolution Score. Applied to a K562 CRISPRi Perturb-seq dataset, the framework identifies clustering granularities that appear biologically interpretable and claims to outperform geometric baselines like silhouette and modularity.

### Strengths
1. **Addresses a well-known bottleneck in single-cell analysis**: The manual tuning of clustering resolution and subsequent functional annotation is highly subjective. The paper frames this problem clearly and proposes a principled, data-driven alternative that directly links unsupervised partitioning to biological interpretability (Sec. 1, 3).
2. **Systematic LLM configuration analysis**: Stage 1 provides a useful empirical characterization of how prompt design (general vs. hypothesis), temperature, model class ("thinking" vs. non-thinking), and embedding choices affect semantic accuracy on curated GO sets. The finding that model-generated confidence scores correlate with ground-truth cosine similarity (Fig. S3) is a valuable practical insight.
3. **Clear metric formulation and visualization**: The decomposition into Intra-Cluster Agreement (ICS) and Inter-Cluster Distinctiveness (ICD), followed by a combined Resolution Score curve across a resolution sweep (Fig. 3a, 4a), provides an intuitive, reproducible diagnostic plot for practitioners to replace heuristic manual inspection.

### Weaknesses
1. **Narrow empirical scope and qualitative validation**: The core application is evaluated on a single dataset (K562 Perturb-seq, ~25k cells). Claims of biological alignment rely heavily on qualitative inspection of UMAP visualizations and subjective statements about "known pathways" (Figs. 3b, 4b). Without quantitative ground-truth comparisons (e.g., NMI/ARI against expert annotations, correlation with orthogonal phenotypic readouts, or benchmark datasets like scRNA-seq atlases with established cell-type labels), it is difficult to verify that the selected resolutions are objectively superior.
2. **Heavy reliance on proprietary models and API opacity**: The pipeline depends on commercial LLMs (GPT-4o, GPT-o3) and OpenAI's text-embedding-3-large. While practical, this limits reproducibility and raises cost barriers for the community. The paper does not sufficiently explore open-source alternatives or demonstrate that results are robust across model classes and embedding spaces.
3. **Arbitrary weighting and lack of optimization theory**: The Resolution Score uses a fixed weight `w = 1/3` for ICS versus `1-w` for ICD, justified only by an unspecified "small grid search" (Sec. 3.4). Figure S5 shows that the score trajectory is sensitive to `w` per cluster, yet no principled method for adaptive weighting or multi-objective trade-off analysis is provided.
4. **Insufficient comparison to biology-informed baselines**: While silhouette and modularity are compared, modern single-cell pipelines already exist for objective resolution selection (e.g., MultiK, Clustree, marker-gene stability metrics). The paper does not benchmark against these methods, making it unclear whether the improvement stems from LLM semantic reasoning or simply from leveraging differential expression patterns more effectively.

### Novelty & Significance
The conceptual idea of using semantic consistency of LLM-generated hypotheses to guide clustering hyperparameter selection is interesting and aligns with ICLR’s growing interest in AI for Science. However, from an ICLR perspective, the methodological novelty is incremental: the framework chains existing components (retrieval-augmented LLMs, sentence embeddings, cosine similarity scoring, and grid search over a clustering resolution parameter) without introducing new learning objectives, architectural innovations, or formal optimization guarantees. The significance is currently constrained by single-dataset evaluation and qualitative validation. If the authors can demonstrate robust, quantitative improvements across diverse benchmarks and clarify the conditions under which LLM semantics meaningfully outperform traditional statistical or marker-based metrics, the work could provide a valuable utility to the broader ML-for-biology community.

### Suggestions for Improvement
1. **Expand validation with quantitative ground truth**: Evaluate HYPOGENEAGENT on 2–3 additional, biologically diverse datasets (e.g., PBMCs, developmental trajectories, or tissues with expert cell-type annotations). Report standard clustering metrics (ARI, NMI, cluster stability) and demonstrate statistically that the Resolution Score peak correlates better with ground truth than silhouette/modularity benchmarks.
2. **Benchmark against biology-aware resolution selection tools**: Compare directly with methods that already incorporate marker-gene stability or differential expression feedback (e.g., MultiK, Clustree, SC3 consensus). This will isolate the specific value added by LLM semantic reasoning.
3. **Improve reproducibility and robustness analysis**: Test the pipeline on open-source LLMs (e.g., Qwen, Llama 3) and open embedding models (e.g., BGE, E5). Report how resolution rankings change across models, and discuss computational cost (API calls, latency) versus traditional pipelines.
4. **Formalize the weighting scheme**: Replace the fixed `w=1/3` with a principled approach, such as (a) multi-objective Pareto front selection, (b) automatic weight tuning via correlation with held-out biological metadata, or (c) sensitivity analysis with confidence intervals. Discuss how to handle outlier clusters where ICS and ICD conflict.
5. **Clarify metric definitions and statistical rigor**: Provide clean mathematical definitions for ICS, ICD, and RS in the main text. Add statistical validation for score peaks (e.g., permutation tests, bootstrapped confidence bands) to distinguish meaningful optima from stochastic LLM or embedding variance.

# Spark Finder Review
## How to Improve This Paper

### Missing Experiments (top 3-5 only)
1. Add cross-dataset validation on at least two biologically distinct single-cell or CRISPR screening datasets. Without this, the claim that HYPOGENEAGENT generalizes to "millions of cells" or "multi-omics modalities" is entirely speculative and unsupported by data.
2. Benchmark against automated annotation and resolution selection tools (e.g., MultiK, Clustree, scCATCH, CellTypist) instead of only silhouette and modularity. Geometric baselines ignore biology by design, making them insufficient to prove the method's claimed biological superiority.
3. Compute a quantitative metric against known perturbation ground truth, such as guide-to-cluster concordance or Adjusted Rand Index against curated labels. Subjective UMAP inspection claiming "alignment with known pathways" lacks the quantitative rigor required for ICLR.
4. Perform a strict component ablation removing the retrieval step, chain-of-thought, and confidence scoring. Without it, it is unclear which architectural choices actually improve biological reasoning versus simply adding latency and cost.

### Deeper Analysis Needed (top 3-5 only)
1. Re-evaluate the `w=1/3` combination weight using cross-validation or theoretical derivation rather than a grid search on the target dataset. Optimizing a hyperparameter on the exact test data invalidates the claim of an objective, unbiased scoring criterion and introduces overfitting.
2. Prove that sentence-embedding cosine distances correlate with true functional relatedness (e.g., GO semantic similarity scores or Jaccard overlap of gene sets). If embeddings capture linguistic phrasing rather than biological meaning, the entire intra/inter-score foundation is methodologically unsound.
3. Provide statistical significance testing (e.g., bootstrap or permutation of cluster labels) for the Resolution Score across resolution sweeps. Without confidence intervals or null distributions, the selected "optimal" resolution may just reflect stochastic embedding variance, undermining the claim of reliable optimization.

### Visualizations & Case Studies (top 3-5 only)
1. Provide side-by-side marker gene dot plots and standard GO enrichment heatmaps comparing the LLM-selected resolution against silhouette/modularity-selected resolutions. This would directly expose whether the agent isolates biologically interpretable states that geometric scores miss.
2. Show a deliberate failure case (e.g., highly overlapping biological states or weak perturbations) where the agent produces hallucinated or low-confidence annotations. Demonstrating how the metric degrades or flags ambiguity is essential to trust the agent as an "objective adjudicator" rather than a forced scorer.
3. Plot the Resolution Score and its components against the *actual number of clusters* generated, not just the Leiden resolution parameter. This reveals whether the score peak simply defaults to a mid-range cluster count rather than reflecting a data-driven biological optimum.

### Obvious Next Steps (top 3-5 only)
1. Validate the full pipeline on at least one open-weight LLM (e.g., Llama-3.1, Qwen). Heavy reliance on proprietary closed APIs prevents independent verification, raises data privacy concerns for sensitive biomedical data, and limits true reproducibility.
2. Conduct a blinded expert review where biologists independently rank LLM-generated GO terms versus classical enrichment outputs. This provides a necessary human baseline to substantiate claims of "reduced human bias" and superior interpretability.
3. Demonstrate the framework on a standard scRNA-seq tissue atlas with expert-curated cell type labels. Proving accurate resolution tuning for baseline cell-type discovery, not just CRISPR perturbations, is required to justify the claimed "general-purpose tool" contribution.

# Final Consolidated Review
## Summary
HYPOGENEAGENT proposes an LLM-driven framework that optimizes clustering resolution in single-cell and Perturb-seq datasets by maximizing biological interpretability. It computes intra-cluster agreement and inter-cluster distinctiveness from semantic embeddings of LLM-generated Gene Ontology hypotheses, combining them into a composite Resolution Score to replace heuristic parameter tuning. The method is currently validated solely on a single K562 cell line, with optimal resolutions identified through qualitative inspection and internal consistency metrics rather than external biological ground truth.

## Strengths
- **Addresses a recognized methodological gap** — The paper correctly identifies that traditional resolution metrics (silhouette, modularity) ignore biological structure and that post-hoc annotation does not feed back into clustering hyperparameter selection. The proposed framework directly couples partition quality with functional interpretability (Sec. 1, 3).
- **Systematic LLM configuration benchmarking (Stage 1)** — The empirical characterization of prompt design, temperature, model architecture ("thinking" vs. non-thinking), and embedding choices on curated GO sets is thorough. The demonstration that model-generated confidence scores correlate with external semantic similarity (Fig. S3) is a valuable practical insight for automated pipeline design.
- **Transparent and intuitive diagnostic formulation** — Decomposing cluster quality into Intra-Cluster Agreement (ICS) and Inter-Cluster Distinctiveness (ICD) yields a clear, interpretable score curve across a resolution sweep. This provides practitioners with a concrete, reproducible alternative to subjective UMAP inspection (Sec. 3.4, Figs. 3–4).

## Weaknesses
- **Narrow empirical scope and lack of quantitative biological ground truth** — Validation is restricted to a single K562 Perturb-seq dataset (~25k cells). Claims that the selected resolutions "align with known pathways" rely on subjective UMAP visual inspection and author expectation rather than rigorous external benchmarks (e.g., ARI/NMI against expert-verified labels, perturbation recovery F1, or pathway concordance against orthogonal assays). — Without multi-dataset, quantitative validation, it is impossible to verify whether the method genuinely recovers biological structure or merely overfits to geometric patterns in one specific cell line.
- **Methodological inconsistencies and unvalidated optimization choices** — Table 1 defines Inter-Cluster Distinctiveness (ICD) as a cosine *distance*, while Section 3.4 explicitly computes it as a mean cosine *similarity*. The Resolution Score then applies `1 − ICD_k`, which only functions if ICD is similarity, revealing a critical drafting or definitional error. Furthermore, the weighting parameter `w = 1/3` is justified solely by an unreported "small grid search" on the target data. — These issues introduce mathematical ambiguity and risk circularity/overfitting, directly undermining the claim that the framework provides an "objective" and "unbiased" adjudication of clustering quality.
- **Inadequate baselines and circular comparative evaluation** — The method benchmarks exclusively against geometry/graph-based metrics (silhouette, modularity), which are insensitive to biology by design. The comparison to classical GO enrichment (Sec. 4.4.3) is fundamentally circular: the authors apply their *own* ICS/ICD/RS metrics to traditional enrichment outputs to claim validation, rather than using an independent gold-standard annotation method. Missing comparisons to biology-aware resolution selection tools (e.g., MultiK, Clustree, marker-stability frameworks) prevent isolation of the actual utility added by LLM reasoning. — Without fair, biology-informed baselines, it remains unknown whether performance gains stem from genuine semantic reasoning or simply from leveraging differential expression more aggressively.
- **Fragility to proprietary dependencies and potential pretraining contamination** — The pipeline relies heavily on closed-weight models (GPT-o3, OpenAI text-embedding-3-large) without demonstrating that the Resolution Score optimum remains stable across open-source alternatives. Additionally, Stage 1 benchmarks frontier LLMs on standard GOBP gene sets that are heavily represented in web corpora; this strongly measures retrieval/memorization rather than novel biological reasoning. — Heavy proprietary reliance limits reproducibility and data privacy compliance, while unquantified memorization in Stage 1 casts doubt on whether the agent's Stage 2 outputs reflect true hypothesis generation or database regurgitation.

## Nice-to-Haves
- Provide ablations on the number of top marker genes supplied to the LLM and on individual pipeline components (retrieval, chain-of-thought, confidence weighting) to quantify their necessity versus latency/cost trade-offs.
- Plot the Resolution Score against the *absolute number of clusters* generated (rather than only the Leiden `r` parameter) to verify that the optimum does not simply bias toward mid-range partition sizes.
- Include a deliberate failure-case analysis (e.g., weak perturbations, highly pleiotropic clusters) to demonstrate how the framework scores low-confidence annotations or flags ambiguous biological states, which is essential for real-world deployment.

## Novel Insights
The paper’s most compelling conceptual contribution is the inversion of standard single-cell pipelines: instead of treating functional annotation as a static, downstream interpretation step, it leverages LLM-generated biological descriptions as a dynamic optimization signal for unsupervised clustering hyperparameters. The empirical observation that proprietary models' internal confidence calibration closely tracks external semantic similarity with curated GO terms (Fig. S3) is a non-trivial finding. It suggests that large language models have internalized reliable uncertainty proxies for genomic tasks, which could be repurposed as quality-control signals in automated bioinformatics workflows independent of ground-truth labels, potentially reducing reliance on manual expert curation.

## Potentially Missed Related Work
- MultiK (Liu et al., 2021) & SC3 consensus stability metrics — Direct, biologically motivated baselines for objective cluster number and resolution selection in scRNA-seq.
- Clustree (Zappia & Oshlack, 2018) — A widely adopted tool that visualizes and quantifies cluster stability across resolution gradients; highly relevant for benchmarking resolution selection claims.
- Ontology-aware semantic similarity frameworks (e.g., Resnik/Lin GO-sim) — Provide structured, graph-based GO term similarity metrics that could be cross-validated against the free-text cosine similarities to prove biological grounding.

## Suggestions
1. **Formalize and statistically validate the scoring mechanism:** Correct the ICD definition mismatch in Table 1 vs. Section 3.4. Replace the fixed `w=1/3` with a principled approach (e.g., multi-objective Pareto selection, cross-validation on held-out metadata, or sensitivity bounds with permutation tests). Add bootstrap confidence intervals or null-model comparisons to demonstrate that the Resolution Score peak is statistically distinguishable from stochastic LLM/embedding variance.
2. **Establish quantitative biological ground truth and expand benchmarks:** Evaluate the method on at least two additional, biologically distinct datasets (e.g., PBMC atlas, developmental tissue). Replace subjective UMAP claims with standard clustering metrics (ARI, NMI against expert annotations, perturbation concordance rates) to prove the selected resolutions objectively outperform baselines. Compare directly against biology-aware resolution tools (MultiK, marker-stability methods) rather than purely geometric indices.
3. **Mitigate proprietary dependency and validate reasoning vs. retrieval:** Test pipeline robustness using open-weight LLMs (e.g., Llama-3, Qwen) and open embeddings (e.g., BGE, E5). Explicitly quantify potential pretraining leakage in Stage 1 by benchmarking on synthetically generated or temporally held-out gene sets that are absent from public model training corpora, ensuring the agent demonstrates genuine hypothesis generation rather than database recall.
