# Harsh Critic Review
## Section-by-Section Critical Review

### Title & Abstract
- **Accuracy:** The title accurately captures the core contribution: an LLM agent framework that uses hypothesis generation and semantic similarity to guide clustering resolution selection.
- **Clarity:** The abstract clearly outlines the problem (subjective resolution/annotation), the method (LLM hypothesis generation → sentence embeddings → Resolution Score based on intra-agreement and inter-distinctiveness), and the key result (selection aligns with known biology better than silhouette/modularity on a K562 Perturb-seq dataset).
- **Unsupported Claims:** The claim that this "paves the way for fully automated, context-aware interpretation pipelines" is stated too strongly given the validation is currently limited to a single cell-line dataset without quantitative downstream performance validation. The abstract also implies the method is broadly model-agnostic, while the text heavily relies on specific proprietary models and embeddings.

### Introduction & Motivation
- **Motivation & Gap:** The motivation is strong. Clustering resolution selection is a well-known bottleneck in single-cell analysis, and traditional metrics often ignore biological interpretability. The gap—that current LLM annotation tools act post-hoc rather than providing feedback for resolution selection—is clearly identified.
- **Contributions:** The contributions are clearly stated and align with the method. However, the introduction slightly under-specifies how this differs fundamentally from traditional functional enrichment feedback loops (e.g., using GSEA/GSVA scores to drive resolution). The specific advantage of LLM-generated text embeddings over direct ontology overlap metrics is not sufficiently motivated.
- **Over-claiming:** The introduction claims the method is "model-agnostic," yet the experimental setup relies on specific Azure OpenAI models and embeddings. This contradiction weakens the generality claim. Additionally, while the related work section is comprehensive, it does not clearly position the method against existing "biology-aware" clustering validation tools that use gene set overlap rather than language models.

### Method / Approach
- **Clarity & Reproducibility:** The workflow is logically structured, but there is a significant conceptual ambiguity regarding the definition of **Intra-cluster Agreement (ICS)**. In Table 1 and Section 3.4, ICS is computed as the similarity among the top 5 hypotheses generated for a *single cluster signature*. This measures the LLM's internal self-consistency or hypothesis variance, not the biological coherence of cells within that cluster. This is a misnomer compared to traditional clustering definitions and creates confusion about what the metric actually captures.
- **Assumptions & Justification:** The method assumes that high semantic similarity between LLM hypotheses implies biological correctness. This ignores the risk of "consistent hallucination," where the LLM generates plausible but incorrect annotations that are highly self-consistent. The choice of the weighting parameter $w=1/3$ is described as chosen by a "small grid search" (Section 3.4), but no criteria for this search are provided, raising concerns of tuning to the observed dataset.
- **Logical Gaps:** The global resolution selection strategy is not formally defined. Section 3.4 defines a per-cluster score $RS_k$, but the paper relies on maximizing the *median* $RS$ across all clusters (Figure 3a). The aggregation method is not stated in the method, making the optimization procedure incomplete. Additionally, the aggregation of the "Gene-to-cluster assignment matrix" and "Perturbation-to-cluster assignment matrix" (Section 3.2 / Appendix C) is described, but their exact role in the final agent input is unclear, as the agent appears to just take log-fold-change ranked gene lists.

### Experiments & Results
- **Testing Claims:** The experiments test the resolution selection claim but rely on qualitative validation (UMAP separation and visual inspection of dot plots) rather than quantitative biological ground truth. For ICLR, demonstrating that a metric selects the "correct" resolution requires a gold standard (e.g., known cell-type hierarchies, downstream differential expression power, or recovery of known perturbation effectors), which is largely missing.
- **Baselines & Ablation:** Baselines (Silhouette, Modularity, Functional Enrichment) are appropriate. Stage 1 ablations (model choice, prompt type, temperature) are useful for agent design. However, crucial ablations are missing: the impact of the weighting parameter $w$ on resolution selection across different datasets is shown only as sensitivity plots (Figure S5) without a clear strategy for handling this hyperparameter in practice. A comparison against a random text baseline or shuffled gene sets to prove the embeddings capture biological signal rather than linguistic artifacts is needed.
- **Statistical Significance & Granularity:** The resolution grid is coarse ($r \in [0.1, 1.0]$ with step 0.1). It is difficult to claim $r=0.4$ or $r=0.5$ is truly optimal without finer-grained sampling or interpolation. No error bars, p-values, or stability metrics (e.g., running the stochastic LLM multiple times per resolution) are reported, making it impossible to assess if the peak is statistically significant.
- **Dataset Appropriateness:** The validation is exclusively on the Replogle et al. K562 Perturb-seq dataset. While large, K562 is a single cancer cell line. The claim of superior biological interpretability is not supported by tests on primary tissues, developmental trajectories, or multi-omics data where clustering challenges differ.

### Writing & Clarity
- **Confusing Sections:** As noted, the definition of ICS in Table 1 labels it "Intra-cluster agreement" but describes it as cosine distance/similarity among the agent's own hypothesis outputs for one cluster. This is confusing and should be renamed (e.g., "Hypothesis Consistency" or "Annotation Stability") to avoid conflating it with geometric cluster compactness.
- **Figures:** Figures 3 and 4 are informative and clearly show the score distributions. However, Table 1 contains a typo: it defines ICS involving "Cosine distance" while Section 3.4 uses "cosine similarity" in the formula. This inconsistency should be corrected.
- **Overall Clarity:** The writing is generally professional and the pipeline steps are easy to follow. However, the distinction between validating the *LLM's annotation quality* (Stage 1) and validating the *resolution selection capability* (Stage 2) could be sharper. Stage 1 results don't directly prove the Resolution Score works; they only prove the LLM prompts work.

### Limitations & Broader Impact
- **Acknowledged Limitations:** The authors correctly identify scalability to large atlases, generalizability across modalities/ontologies, LLM cost, and prompt sensitivity as limitations.
- **Fundamental Missed Limitations:**
    - **Hallucination Risks:** The method assumes higher consistency equals better biology. It does not discuss how to detect if the LLM is confidently but consistently annotating distinct clusters with the same high-level, generic terms (e.g., "cell cycle"), which could artificially inflate ICS or ICD in biologically misleading ways.
    - **Circularity / Training Data Bias:** The LLMs are trained on public literature and GO terms. There is a risk that the model is simply recovering what it memorized from databases rather than inferring novel biology. This bias is not discussed.
    - **Proprietary Dependency:** The reliance on closed-source embeddings (OpenAI text-embedding-3-large) hinders reproducibility and raises concerns if the embedding space changes in the future.
- **Impact:** The societal impact is positive (democratizing analysis), but the discussion misses the risk of "automation bias," where biologists may trust LLM-derived resolutions over expert intuition without verification, potentially masking biological nuance.

### Overall Assessment
The paper addresses a relevant and practical problem in single-cell genomics: the subjective nature of clustering resolution selection. The core idea of using LLM-generated semantic consistency as a proxy for biological interpretability is clever and timely. However, the current submission falls short of ICLR's rigor standards in several key areas. First, the evaluation relies on a single dataset (K562) and lacks a quantitative biological gold standard against which to measure the "correctness" of the selected resolution; claims of superiority over silhouette/modularity remain qualitative. Second, the method suffers from definition ambiguities, particularly conflating LLM hypothesis consistency with intra-cluster biological coherence, and the global resolution selection aggregation strategy is under-specified. Finally, the work does not adequately address the risks of LLM hallucination, embedding dependence, or the coarse resolution grid. While the ablation studies on prompts and models are solid, the empirical validation needs to be broadened (multiple datasets or modalities) or deepened (downstream task improvement, finer resolution sweeps, and statistical robustness checks). The contribution is promising but currently more suited to a specialized workshop or application track in its present form. Substantial revisions addressing the grounding of the metric in biological truth and clarifying the mathematical formulation would be necessary for acceptance.

# Neutral Reviewer
## Balanced Review

### Summary
HYPOGENEAGENT introduces an LLM-driven framework that transforms the subjective task of selecting clustering resolution in single-cell and Perturb-seq data into a quantifiable optimization problem. By prompting an LLM to generate ranked Gene Ontology hypotheses for each cluster and embedding these descriptions, the authors compute intra-cluster agreement and inter-cluster distinctiveness metrics, which are combined into a single Resolution Score. Applied to a K562 Perturb-seq dataset, the method selects clustering granularities that align with expected biological programs, outperforming traditional geometry-based metrics like silhouette and modularity scores.

### Strengths
1. **Clear Motivation & Problem Framing**: The paper accurately identifies a well-known bottleneck in single-cell analysis—the reliance on heuristic, manual tuning of clustering resolution—and proposes a principled, data-driven alternative grounded in biological interpretability (Section 1).
2. **Methodological Clarity in Semantic Scoring**: The introduction of Intra-Cluster Agreement (ICS) and Inter-Cluster Distinctiveness (ICD) via sentence embeddings provides a transparent mechanism to quantify biological coherence and separation. The use of calibrated confidence scores and ranked hypotheses (Section 3.3) adds structure to the LLM's free-text outputs.
3. **Comprehensive Ablation on LLM & Prompt Design**: Stage 1 experiments systematically evaluate embedding models, temperature settings, prompt templates, and multiple LLM backbones. The finding that "thinking" LLMs (e.g., GPT-o3) perform more reliably on hypothesis generation with confidence calibration is practically valuable and well-supported (Figures S1–S3).
4. **Direct Comparison to Classical Metrics**: The explicit benchmarking against silhouette and modularity scores effectively highlights the limitations of purely geometric indices (e.g., sensitivity to convexity, graph-density bias) and demonstrates where semantic metrics provide complementary, biology-aware signal (Sections 4.4.1–4.4.2).

### Weaknesses
1. **Limited Dataset Evaluation & Generalization**: The framework is validated exclusively on a single K562 Perturb-seq dataset. Without evaluation on diverse cell types, tissues, or alternative modalities (e.g., standard scRNA-seq, primary tissues), claims of generalization and robustness remain insufficient for a venue that expects cross-domain empirical validation.
2. **Heuristic Metric Formulation & Weighting**: The Resolution Score combines ICS and ICD using a fixed weight ($w=1/3$), which the authors note was chosen via a "small grid search" (Section 3.4). This lacks theoretical grounding, formal optimization justification, or statistical sensitivity analysis, making the scoring function appear ad-hoc.
3. **Overreliance on Qualitative Validation**: Biological alignment is primarily demonstrated through UMAP visual inspection and statements that results "align with known perturbation effects" (Figures 3 & 4). Quantitative ground-truth benchmarks—such as recovery rates of known perturbation modules, Adjusted Rand Index against curated states, or statistical tests for marker gene specificity—are absent, weakening empirical rigor.
4. **Unaddressed Computational & Scalability Concerns**: The abstract claims the approach is "computationally efficient," but no runtime analysis, API cost breakdown, or latency profiling is provided. For large atlases, repeated LLM inference across resolution grids could be prohibitively expensive, a critical oversight for practical adoption.

### Novelty & Significance
The core novelty lies in repurposing LLM-generated semantic consistency as a quantitative objective function for unsupervised clustering resolution selection, effectively closing the feedback loop between partitioning and functional annotation. While the algorithmic ML contribution is incremental—combining established LLM agent workflows with standard cosine similarity metrics—the conceptual shift from geometry-first to biology-first clustering evaluation is meaningful. For ICLR, the paper's significance is moderate as a foundational ML work but notably higher as an applied AI-for-science contribution. Its impact will depend on broader empirical validation, clearer theoretical or optimization justification for the scoring metric, and demonstrated efficiency/scale.

### Suggestions for Improvement
1. **Expand Empirical Validation**: Evaluate the framework on at least 2–3 additional datasets spanning different biological contexts (e.g., tissue-specific scRNA-seq, alternative CRISPR screens, or multi-omics data). Include datasets with established expert annotations to enable quantitative benchmarking (e.g., ARI/NMI against reference labels, cell-type mixing entropy, or perturbation recovery rates).
2. **Provide Quantitative Biological Benchmarks**: Supplement UMAP figures with statistical measures of cluster quality. Report enrichment specificity (e.g., average adjusted p-values per cluster, GO-term redundancy scores), marker gene concordance with established literature, or downstream analytical improvements to objectively validate the selected resolution.
3. **Formalize or Systematically Optimize the Resolution Score**: Replace or justify the fixed $w=1/3$ weighting. Provide a formal sensitivity analysis across $w \in [0,1]$, or frame the score as a multi-objective optimization problem (e.g., identifying Pareto-efficient resolutions balancing cohesion and separation) to remove arbitrary hyperparameters.
4. **Include Computational Profiling & Scalability Strategies**: Report wall-clock time, token usage, and API costs per dataset and resolution grid size. Propose practical scaling mitigations, such as embedding caching, representative cluster sampling, or fine-tuning distilled open-source models to reduce reliance on proprietary LLMs.
5. **Clarify Confidence Calibration & Reproducibility Pipelines**: Detail how the LLM's confidence scores are derived and calibrated (e.g., logit normalization, isotonic regression, or prompt-constrained scoring). Ensure the final submission includes exact API versions, random seeds, temperature controls, and a complete, runnable pipeline to meet ICLR reproducibility standards.

# Spark Finder Review
## How to Improve This Paper

### Missing Experiments (top 3-5 only)
1. **Validation on independent, biologically distinct datasets:** Relying solely on K562 Perturb-seq undermines the claim of a generalizable resolution metric. Test on at least two additional datasets (e.g., different tissue origin, non-leukemic lineages, or pure scRNA-seq) to prove the score isn't capturing dataset-specific transcriptional biases.
2. **Quantitative ground-truth alignment:** "Alignment with known pathways" is stated qualitatively and cannot support a core methodological claim. Compute ARI or NMI between the agent-selected partitions and true perturbation labels or expert cell-type annotations to objectively prove superior biological recovery.
3. **Comparison to modern biology-aware clustering baselines:** Comparing only to geometry-based metrics (silhouette, modularity) sets up a strawman. Benchmark against consensus/automated resolution tools (e.g., `clustree`, `SC3`, or marker-tree methods) to demonstrate the LLM actually outperforms existing domain-aware pipelines.
4. **Non-targeting control (NTC) stress test:** Process only non-targeting guide cells through the full pipeline. If the agent produces high ICS/ICD scores for biologically null partitions, it proves the metric is driven by linguistic coherence rather than true biological modularity.

### Deeper Analysis Needed (top 3-5 only)
1. **Statistical significance of score peaks:** Resolution selection currently relies on visual peak-picking from boxplots. Provide permutation tests or bootstrapped confidence intervals to prove the selected resolution's score is statistically distinct from adjacent grid values and traditional metric peaks.
2. **Weight (`w`) and prompt sensitivity quantification:** The `w=1/3` weight stems from an opaque search, and LLM generation is highly instruction-sensitive. Show how the selected resolution shifts under alternative weights and minor prompt edits; without robustness data, the optimization is unstable and unreproducible.
3. **RAG vs. closed-book hallucination ablation:** Isolate the impact of external GO/KEGG retrieval by comparing it against a pure closed-book LLM. This is necessary to prove the score improves from factual biological grounding rather than the LLM's inherent bias toward generating linguistically plausible but hallucinated annotations.
4. **Failure mode analysis on low-signal clusters:** Quantify how ICS and ICD behave in partitions with weak differential expression or high technical noise. LLMs routinely over-interpret random patterns; showing score degradation or calibration under low-signal conditions is required to trust real-world applicability.

### Visualizations & Case Studies
1. **Predicted GO vs. ground-truth perturbation overlay:** For each selected resolution, plot a side-by-side heatmap of agent-predicted GO terms against actual targeted genes/pathways per cluster. This directly confirms whether high Resolution Scores correspond to correct biological annotation or semantic mismatch.
2. **Score landscape vs. independent biological validity:** Overlay the Resolution Score curve across all grid resolutions with an orthogonal biological metric (e.g., pathway enrichment p-values or perturbation variance explained). This exposes whether the agent's peak genuinely tracks biological signal or merely aligns with arbitrary embedding similarities.
3. **Concrete success vs. failure decomposition:** Detail one cluster where high ICS/ICD correctly isolates a known perturbation and one where the score falsely peaks on mixed biology. Analyzing specific marker genes, LLM confidence miscalibration, and retrieval outputs in these cases will expose the framework's actual operating boundaries.

### Obvious Next Steps
1. **Cost-latency vs. performance trade-off reporting:** ICLR requires practical overhead evaluation for AI-heavy methods. Report total wall-clock time, token/API cost, and compute footprint, then explicitly compare against the millisecond-scale calculation of classical metrics to justify the methodological burden.
2. **Open-weight and local embedding benchmarking:** Heavy reliance on proprietary GPT-o3 and OpenAI embeddings undermines reproducibility and accessibility. Benchmark the pipeline against at least one strong open-weight LLM and an open sentence-transformer to prove the method isn't locked to specific paid APIs.
3. **Production-ready pipeline integration:** Demonstrate exactly how the agent plugs into standard `Scanpy`/`Seurat` workflows with a minimal, reproducible wrapper script. Claims of enabling "fully automated pipelines" are unconvincing without concrete, biologist-ready deployment instructions or a public Docker/Colab environment.

# Final Consolidated Review
## Summary
HYPOGENEAGENT proposes an LLM-driven framework to automate clustering resolution selection in single-cell and Perturb-seq data. By generating ranked functional hypotheses per cluster and computing semantic similarity metrics (intra-cluster agreement and inter-cluster distinctiveness), it derives a Resolution Score to quantitatively replace heuristic tuning. Preliminary validation on a K562 dataset suggests the approach better aligns with expected biological programs compared to geometry-based metrics.

## Strengths
- **Conceptual bridge between partitioning and annotation:** Transforms a traditionally subjective, manual tuning step into an optimizable, biology-aware objective function by using LLM-generated hypothesis consistency as a proxy for cluster quality.
- **Rigorous agent and prompt ablation (Stage 1):** Systematically evaluates LLM backbones, embedding methods, temperature, and prompt structures, providing clear empirical guidance that "thinking" models with structured hypothesis prompts yield more stable, calibrated gene-set annotations.
- **Transparent semantic scoring mechanism:** The ICS/ICD formulation via sentence embeddings offers an interpretable, biology-aware alternative to purely geometric indices, effectively contextualizing the limitations of silhouette and modularity scores in functional annotation tasks.

## Weaknesses
- **Core claims rest on qualitative rather than quantitative validation** — The paper asserts superiority over classical baselines based on UMAP visual inspection and descriptive alignment with expected biology. Without hard ground-truth metrics (e.g., ARI/NMI against known perturbation labels, differential expression recovery rates, or statistical enrichment specificity), it remains unproven that the Resolution Score selects objectively better or more biologically faithful partitions.
- **Ad-hoc metric formulation and unspecified global aggregation** — The weighting parameter ($w=1/3$) is justified only by an opaque "small grid search" lacking theoretical or multi-objective grounding. Furthermore, while per-cluster scores $RS_k$ are mathematically defined, the methodology omits the formal aggregation strategy (e.g., median maximization) used to select a single global resolution, relying instead on implicit visual interpretation.
- **Single-dataset validation limits generalization claims** — All resolution selection experiments are confined to the Replogle et al. K562 cancer cell line. The framework's robustness across diverse biological contexts (e.g., primary tissues, developmental trajectories, or high-noise standard scRNA-seq) is untested, leaving broad claims of applicability premature.
- **Semantic consistency is not validated against biological modularity** — The method assumes high ICS/ICD reflects true cluster coherence but lacks null-model ablation (e.g., non-targeting controls, shuffled gene sets, or low-signal partitions). This leaves open the risk that the metric captures linguistic regularities, LLM priors, or "consistent hallucination" rather than experimentally driven biological structure.

## Nice-to-Haves
- **Statistical robustness analysis:** Permutation tests or bootstrapped confidence intervals for the Resolution Score peaks would strengthen claims beyond visual inspection of coarse grid plots.
- **Computational profiling and scalability assessment:** Reporting wall-clock time, token usage, and API costs, alongside proposed caching or sampling strategies, would better contextualize the practical overhead compared to sub-second classical metrics.
- **Benchmarking against domain-specific resolution tools:** Comparing against established automated selection methods (e.g., clustree, consensus clustering) would better position the framework within the existing computational biology ecosystem.

## Novel Insights
The work introduces a compelling inversion of standard LLM-for-biology pipelines: rather than using AI purely for post-hoc interpretation, it treats semantic consistency as a direct optimization target for unsupervised hyperparameter tuning. This conceptually shifts clustering validation from geometry-first heuristics toward biology-first interpretability. However, its practical utility fundamentally depends on disentangling whether high semantic alignment genuinely tracks underlying transcriptional modularity and experimental ground truth, or merely reflects the LLM's capacity to generate linguistically coherent, functionally plausible annotations regardless of biological validity.

## Suggestions
- Incorporate quantitative ground-truth benchmarks by computing ARI, NMI, or perturbation effect recovery metrics against known guide RNA targets for the K562 dataset, and ideally validate on one additional, biologically distinct dataset.
- Formalize the Resolution Score aggregation by explicitly defining how per-cluster $RS_k$ values are combined for global selection, and provide a structured sensitivity analysis or Pareto-front justification for the $w$ weighting parameter.
- Implement a non-targeting control (NTC) or permuted gene-set stress test to demonstrate that ICS/ICD scores appropriately decay on biologically inactive or random partitions, confirming the metric responds to true signal rather than generic linguistic coherence.
