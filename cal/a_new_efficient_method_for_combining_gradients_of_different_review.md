# Harsh Critic Review
## Section-by-Section Critical Review

### Title & Abstract
- **Does the title accurately reflect the contribution?** The title (aside from the parser typo) suggests a general gradient-combination optimization method, but the paper only addresses convex quadratic objectives and relies on finite-difference approximations of Hessian-vector products. The title is therefore overstated relative to the actual scope.
- **Does the abstract clearly state the problem, method, and key results?** No. The abstract is severely underdeveloped: it contains grammatical errors ("Whave", "steplenth"), fails to specify the mathematical setting (quadratic objectives only), does not define what "combination based on the products of Hessian matrices of different orders" entails computationally, and makes an unsupported claim about "faster convergence rates" without providing any quantitative result or benchmark context.
- **Are any claims in the abstract unsupported by the paper?** The claim of "faster convergence rates" for higher-order variants is never theoretically proven in the text. The only empirical support comes from a single synthetic quadratic problem in Sec. 4 with no wall-clock or computational-effort metric, making the abstract's performance claim unsubstantiated per ICLR standards.

### Introduction & Motivation
- **Is the problem well-motivated? Is the gap in prior work clearly identified?** The paper motivates the study via classical iterative methods for convex quadratics (Eq. 1) and surveys traditional step-length rules (Cauchy, BB, CBB, SDA/SDC, etc.). However, the gap is poorly articulated. The literature review lists several step-size strategies but does not critically analyze why they fail or what specific limitation the proposed method overcomes. Crucially, there is no connection to modern machine learning optimization (non-convex landscapes, stochastic gradients, ill-conditioning in deep networks), which is a primary expectation for ICLR.
- **Are the contributions clearly stated and accurate?** The contributions are not explicitly enumerated, and the stated novelty (combining Hessian products to achieve "third-order or higher") is mathematically imprecise. In standard optimization terminology, "order" refers to derivative information used or asymptotic convergence rate, not the number of gradient combinations or step sizes. The introduction does not clarify this non-standard usage.
- **Does the introduction over-claim or under-sell?** It over-claims generality by framing the method as a broad new optimization paradigm, while the actual derivation and analysis are strictly confined to diagonalizable quadratic forms. It also under-sells the method by not positioning it against well-known related ideas (e.g., Chebyshev semi-iterative methods, Krylov subspace methods, or preconditioned gradient descent), making the contribution appear more isolated than it is.

### Method / Approach
- **Is the method clearly described and reproducible?** Partly. Eq. (19)–(22) attempt to generalize a two-step update to an $m$-step update in the eigenbasis, and Eq. (23)–(24) plus Algorithm 1 show how to approximate $Ag$ and $A^2g$ using finite differences. However, Algorithm 1 mixes notation inconsistently (e.g., $g_k^1$, $g_k^2$, $dAg_k$), and the hyperparameter $d$ (finite-difference step) is introduced without selection criteria or theoretical justification. Reproducibility is hampered by missing details on $d$, stopping criteria specifics, and how $m$ generalizes beyond $m=3$.
- **Are key assumptions stated and justified?** The method assumes: (1) exact quadratic structure (or local quadratic validity), (2) symmetric positive definite $A$, (3) exact gradient evaluations, and (4) fixed finite-difference step $d$ is sufficiently accurate to treat $(g_k - g_k^1)/d \approx Ag_k$. These are never justified for general non-convex objectives or stochastic settings. The finite-difference approximation introduces $O(d)$ error for non-quadratics, which is not discussed.
- **Are there logical gaps in the derivation or reasoning?** Yes. The jump from eigenvalue analysis (Sec. 2) to the general update (Eq. 22) assumes diagonalization commutes with the update rule, which holds for quadratics but is not explicitly justified for non-diagonal $A$. Furthermore, claiming "SD is first-order, while the CBB method is second-order" (Sec. 3, before Eq. 22) contradicts standard classification: both use only gradient information and are first-order methods. Using gradient differences to approximate $A^2g$ does not make the asymptotic convergence rate "third-order"; it merely constructs a higher-degree polynomial preconditioner, a concept well-studied in Krylov/Chebyshev acceleration that is not referenced.
- **Are there edge cases or failure modes not discussed?** Critical edge cases are ignored: (1) When $r_k$ approaches an eigenvalue $a^{(i)}$, the denominators in Eq. (20)–(21) vanish, leading to numerical explosion. (2) Inexact or stochastic gradients will corrupt the finite-difference Hessian approximations. (3) Non-positive-definite $A$ (saddle points/local minima) breaks the $r_k$ spectral interpretation entirely.
- **For theoretical claims: are proofs correct and complete?** No convergence proof is provided. The claim of "faster convergence rates" rests entirely on heuristic eigenvalue damping arguments (Fig. 2) and a single quadratic experiment. Standard Q-linear/R-linear convergence guarantees for BB/CBB do not automatically extend to the proposed update without rigorous spectral radius analysis, which is absent.

### Experiments & Results
- **Do the experiments actually test the paper's claims?** No. The experiments only evaluate a synthetic, diagonal, convex quadratic (Eq. 25) with arithmetic progression eigenvalues. This does not test the method's claimed generality, its behavior on non-quadratic objectives, or its viability in stochastic/deep learning settings central to ICLR.
- **Are baselines appropriate and fairly compared?** The baselines (BB and CBB) are relevant classical first-order methods, but the comparison omits modern, widely-used optimizers (CG, L-BFGS, AdamW, RMSProp, or modern line-search methods). Without these, the claim of being a "more efficient method" is incomplete and unconvincing in a 2026 context.
- **Are there missing ablations that would materially change conclusions?** Yes. Critical ablations missing include: (1) sensitivity to the finite-difference step size $d$, (2) impact of higher orders ($m>3$), (3) effect of condition number variations (only one $\kappa \approx 10^7$ setting is used), (4) wall-clock time and gradient-evaluation count (GOC uses 3–4 gradient evaluations per update, which drastically changes efficiency claims).
- **Are error bars / statistical significance reported?** No. Results are single deterministic runs on hand-crafted problems. No statistical analysis, confidence intervals, or multiple random seeds are provided.
- **Do the results support the claims made, or are they cherry-picked?** The results show fewer iterations to convergence on one specific problem, but without accounting for per-iteration computational cost, this is misleading. The fixed initial conditions ($x^{(i)}_0 = 10000$ or random in $[0,10000]$) are arbitrarily chosen, raising cherry-picking concerns regarding alignment with the eigenbasis.
- **Are datasets and evaluation metrics appropriate?** The evaluation metric is iteration count to a fixed norm tolerance. This is inappropriate for comparative optimization claims because it ignores function/gradient evaluations, linear algebra overhead, and numerical precision costs. The "dataset" (synthetic quadratic) is not representative of ML loss landscapes, which are non-convex, noisy, and possess clustered/sparse eigenspectra rather than arithmetic progressions.

### Writing & Clarity
- **Are there sections that are confusing or poorly explained?** Sec. 3 is heavily confusing. The derivation of Eqs. (20)–(22) introduces symbols like $\mu_k^{(i)}$ without clear definition, and the binomial expansion in Eq. (22) lacks step-by-step justification. Algorithm 1 contains notational inconsistencies (e.g., mixing $g_k$, $g_k^1$, $g_0$, $g_0^1$, and $Ag_k$ vs. $A^2g_k$) and does not specify the order of operations clearly enough for direct implementation.
- **Are figures and tables clear and informative?** Figs. 2 and 3 attempt to illustrate eigenvalue scaling and convergence, but their captions and axis labels are insufficient. Fig. 2 plots $\mu = 1 - x/r$ for arbitrary fixed values without linking them to actual convergence trajectories. Fig. 3 shows log-norm vs. iterations but does not clarify whether gradient evaluations are normalized, making it difficult to assess true efficiency.

### Limitations & Broader Impact
- **Do the authors acknowledge the key limitations?** No limitations section is present.
- **Are there fundamental limitations they missed?** Several: (1) The method's theoretical foundation collapses for non-quadratic objectives due to finite-difference approximation errors. (2) It requires multiple sequential gradient evaluations per step, making it poorly suited for distributed/stochastic training. (3) Numerical instability near $r_k \approx a^{(i)}$ is not addressed. (4) Memory/compute scaling with $m$ is ignored.
- **Are there failure modes or negative societal impacts not discussed?** Beyond optimization failure modes (divergence under ill-conditioning, noise amplification), there is no discussion of computational overhead potentially increasing carbon footprint in large-scale training, nor is there guidance on when practitioners should avoid the method (e.g., non-smooth or stochastic problems).

### Overall Assessment
The paper proposes a step-combination strategy that algebraically approximates Hessian-vector powers for convex quadratic optimization and claims higher "order" convergence. However, the contribution falls significantly below ICLR's acceptance bar. The theoretical framing misuses standard optimization terminology, lacks a convergence proof, and ignores well-established literature on polynomial preconditioning and Krylov-subspace acceleration. The experiments are restricted to a single synthetic quadratic, omit critical computational-effort metrics (time/gradient evaluations), use inadequate baselines, and provide no ablation or statistical rigor. Furthermore, there is no bridge to non-convex, stochastic, or large-scale machine learning settings, which severely limits relevance for an ML venue. To meet ICLR standards, the authors must: (1) rigorously position the method against established higher-order/Krylov methods, (2) provide convergence analysis under non-quadratic/stochastic conditions or explicitly restrict claims to quadratic settings, (3) report computational-cost-aware metrics (wall-clock time, gradient evaluations, Hessian-vector calls), and (4) include modern deep learning or non-convex benchmarks with proper baselines. Without these fundamental revisions, the current contribution remains an incomplete classical optimization note rather than an ICLR-worthy advance.

# Neutral Reviewer
## Balanced Review

### Summary
The paper introduces GOC (Gradient Order Combination), an iterative optimization method for convex quadratic problems that combines gradient evaluations with finite-difference approximations of Hessian-vector products to construct higher-order update steps. By analyzing the parameter $r$ (related to the inverse step size) as a spectral anchor, the authors unify Steepest Descent and CBB methods as first- and second-order schemes, then extend the framework to third-order updates. The method demonstrates faster iteration-to-convergence on a synthetic, high-dimensional quadratic benchmark compared to baseline BB and CBB variants.

### Strengths
1. **Unified spectral perspective:** Framing optimization steps through the damping parameter $r$ and analyzing how eigenvalue magnitudes influence component-wise reduction provides clear geometric and algebraic intuition for the zigzag behavior of SD and the improved damping of CBB.
2. **Algorithmic construction:** The use of binomial expansion $(1 - a_i/r)^m$ to achieve higher-order spectral damping, combined with finite-difference gradient evaluations to approximate $Ag$ and $A^2g$, offers a practical way to simulate higher-order steps without storing or computing the full Hessian.
3. **Targeted empirical demonstration:** The 100,000-dimensional quadratic experiment with an ill-conditioned eigenvalue spectrum plausibly validates the theoretical motivation, showing fewer iterations to reach the stopping tolerance than CBB, consistent with the claimed faster decay in problematic eigenvalue directions.

### Weaknesses
1. **Narrow scope and limited ICLR relevance:** The theory and experiments are strictly confined to deterministic, strictly convex quadratic objectives with exact gradients. The paper does not address stochastic, non-convex, or machine learning-specific settings, nor does it discuss robustness to noise, batch sampling, or non-quadratic curvature.
2. **Missing convergence and stability analysis:** There is no formal proof that the GOC update converges, nor any analysis of the finite-difference step size $d$. In practice, large $d$ violates the local quadratic/linearity assumption, while small $d$ introduces catastrophic cancelation and numerical instability. The paper does not bound approximation error or discuss step-size selection strategies.
3. **Insufficient experimental design and baselines:** Comparing only against basic BB and CBB omits standard, highly efficient methods for quadratics (Conjugate Gradient, Chebyshev acceleration, L-BFGS) and modern ML optimizers. The overhead of three gradient evaluations per GOC iteration is never accounted for in a wall-clock or FLOP-based comparison, making "efficiency" claims premature. Reporting that "BB could not satisfy stop condition" lacks iteration limits, divergence diagnostics, or tolerance breakdown.
4. **Technical clarity and terminology:** Significant grammatical/typographical issues and inconsistent equation numbering hinder readability. The phrase "Hessian-free method" is incorrectly applied to standard forward/central finite-difference approximations of $Ag$, which obscures the actual technique and distances it from established Hessian-free optimization literature.

### Novelty & Significance
The core concept of constructing polynomial damping $(I - A/r)^m$ to accelerate convergence aligns with classical Chebyshev semi-iterative and Krylov subspace acceleration methods. While the finite-difference combination is neatly derived and the spectral unification of SD/CBB/GOC is pedagogically useful, the methodological novelty is incremental relative to established polynomial acceleration and step-size combination literature. For ICLR, significance is currently limited due to the absence of non-convex/stochastic validation, missing theoretical guarantees, and lack of runtime/overhead analysis. The work would likely find a more appropriate venue in classical numerical optimization if rigorously formalized and contextualized, but it does not yet meet ICLR's expectations for broad machine learning impact.

### Suggestions for Improvement
1. **Broaden empirical scope:** Evaluate GOC on non-convex, stochastic, and standard machine learning tasks (e.g., logistic regression, shallow/deep neural networks on CIFAR/ImageNet subsets, regularized loss functions). Include established baselines: Conjugate Gradient, L-BFGS, Adam/SGD variants, and modern step-size schedules.
2. **Analyze finite-difference approximation:** Provide error bounds or empirical sensitivity studies for the perturbation step $d$, detailing how approximation noise propagates to $Ag$ and $A^2g$. Discuss stability under non-quadratic objectives and propose an adaptive or theoretically justified strategy for selecting $d$.
3. **Conduct complexity-aware evaluation:** Report wall-clock time, gradient evaluations, and FLOP counts alongside iteration counts. Since GOC requires three gradient computations per update, clarify under what conditions the reduced iteration count translates to actual computational savings.
4. **Improve theoretical framing and presentation:** Explicitly derive Eqs. (22)–(24), correct notation, and standardize terminology (e.g., use "finite-difference Hessian-vector product approximation" instead of "Hessian-free"). Position the method within the literature on polynomial acceleration, Krylov methods, and classical step-size blending to clarify its novelty and limitations.

### Missing Experiments (top 3-5 only)
1. Compare GOC against Conjugate Gradient (CG), L-BFGS, and Adam/Nesterov on identical test setups. BB and CBB are historical baselines; without showing performance against modern standard optimizers, the claim that GOC is "more efficient" is unsupported.
2. Evaluate on non-quadratic, non-convex loss surfaces (e.g., logistic regression, ResNet/MLP training). 3. Report wall-clock runtime and total gradient evaluations, not just raw iteration counts. Computing three gradients and assembling higher-order terms per step introduces significant computational overhead that can easily erase iteration-level gains, making iteration-only metrics misleading.

### Deeper Analysis Needed (top 3-5 only)
1. Provide a formal convergence rate bound as a function of the condition number $\kappa(A)$. The paper claims faster descent but provides no theoretical convergence proof, leaving the core algorithmic contribution unverified.
2. Rigorously analyze the numerical stability and step-size $d$ sensitivity of the finite-difference Hessian-vector products. Without proving how $d$ should scale with eigenvalue spread or quantifying truncation error, the method's reliability on ill-conditioned problems remains speculative.
3. Explain the computational complexity and memory scaling per iteration for high-dimensional settings. Ignoring the cost of constructing higher-order gradient combinations prevents reviewers from assessing practical feasibility compared to low-overhead baselines like SGD or Adam.

### Visualizations & Case Studies
1. Plot convergence curves normalized by wall-clock time and total gradient calls across multiple condition numbers. This would directly expose whether GOC’s iteration reduction translates to actual computational speedup or is offset by per-step overhead.
2. Visualize the error magnitude between the finite-difference estimate $(g_k - g^{(1)}_k)/d$ and the true $Ag$ on mildly nonlinear functions. This would concretely show how fast the core approximation degrades outside the quadratic assumption.
3. Include a sensitivity heatmap mapping final convergence success across a grid of $d$ values and initial spectral spreads. Without this, claims of robust descent are unsubstantiated, as a poorly chosen $d$ would cause numerical explosion or stagnation.

### Obvious Next Steps

3. State clear assumptions restricting the method's validity to quadratic forms, or extend the theoretical framework to handle general smooth non-linear objectives with bounded nonlinearity constants.

# Final Consolidated Review
## Summary
The paper introduces GOC (Gradient Order Combination), an iterative optimization technique for convex quadratic objectives that constructs higher-order update steps by combining multiple gradient evaluations. By framing step-length history through a spectral parameter $r$, the authors unify Steepest Descent and CBB methods as first- and second-order polynomial damping schemes, then extend this to third-order updates using algebraic expansions. The method achieves faster convergence in iteration count on a synthetic, high-dimensional ill-conditioned quadratic compared to classical BB/CBB variants.

## Strengths
- **Unified spectral perspective:** The paper successfully recasts classical gradient methods through the lens of eigenvalue damping parameters, offering clear geometric intuition into why SD exhibits zigzagging and how CBB mitigates it. This analytical lens is mathematically coherent for convex quadratics.
- **Hessian-free polynomial acceleration:** The algorithm constructs $A^k g$ terms purely from sequential gradient evaluations, avoiding explicit Hessian computation or storage. This provides a memory-efficient pathway to emulate higher-degree spectral filters.
- **Clear iteration-level gains on synthetic benchmark:** The 100k-dimensional quadratic experiment demonstrates a consistent reduction in iterations required to reach a fixed tolerance, empirically validating the theoretical damping intuition.

## Weaknesses
- **Misleading efficiency metrics:** The paper exclusively reports iteration counts while ignoring per-iteration cost. GOC requires at least three gradient evaluations and additional vector operations per step, whereas BB and CBB require one. Without reporting wall-clock time, total gradient evaluations, or FLOPs, the central claim of being a "more efficient method" is unsubstantiated and potentially reversed under standard computational accounting.
- **Missing convergence analysis:** Despite claims of "faster convergence rates" and higher "order" descent, the paper provides no formal convergence proof, spectral radius bounds, or rate analysis for the GOC update sequence. The argument rests entirely on heuristic binomial expansions and damping plots, which do not constitute a rigorous convergence guarantee.
- **Inadequate baselines and positioning for quadratics:** The method is positioned as a new optimization paradigm, yet it closely resembles classical polynomial acceleration (e.g., Chebyshev semi-iterative methods) and Krylov subspace approaches. Crucially, it omits standard, highly efficient quadratic solvers like Conjugate Gradient and L-BFGS. Without comparison against CG (which is provably optimal for quadratics in $n$ steps), the novelty and practical value of the approach cannot be properly assessed.
- **Non-standard terminology and presentation gaps:** Labeling SD as "first-order" and CBB/GOC as "second/third-order" misuses standard optimization terminology, where "order" typically refers to derivative information or asymptotic convergence rate, not polynomial degree. Additionally, inconsistent notation across equations and Algorithm 1, along with missing implementation details (e.g., how to choose the auxiliary step $d$), hinder reproducibility.

## Nice-to-Haves
- A sensitivity analysis or empirical study on the choice of the auxiliary step $d$, exploring how numerical precision and eigenvalue spread affect the stability of the $(g_k - g_k^{(i)})/d^i$ computations.
- Extension or theoretical discussion on how the method degrades for smooth non-quadratic objectives, where the exactness of the gradient differences no longer holds and truncation errors arise.
- Reframing the terminology to "polynomial spectral acceleration" or "step-combination acceleration" to better align with established literature and avoid confusion with higher-order derivative methods.

## Novel Insights
The core intellectual contribution is a clean, geometric unification of classical step-size rules through the recurrence of the inverse step parameter $r$. By viewing optimization as polynomial eigenvalue damping in the gradient basis, the authors reveal that history-based gradient combinations can systematically suppress problematic spectral components without storing Hessian approximations. While this perspective overlaps with classical Chebyshev and Krylov acceleration, it offers a lightweight, memory-free formulation that could be valuable in extreme high-dimensional settings where preconditioner storage is prohibitive, provided its stability and computational overhead are rigorously characterized.

## Suggestions
- Replace or supplement iteration-count plots with wall-clock time and total gradient evaluation counts across multiple condition numbers and dimensions to provide a fair efficiency comparison.
- Add standard quadratic and smooth optimization baselines, specifically Conjugate Gradient and L-BFGS, to properly contextualize GOC's performance and establish its novelty relative to existing polynomial/Krylov acceleration methods.
- Provide a formal convergence proof or derive a spectral radius bound for the GOC update sequence as a function of the condition number $\kappa(A)$, replacing heuristic damping arguments with rigorous theoretical guarantees.
