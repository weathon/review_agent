# Harsh Critic Review
## Section-by-Section Critical Review

### Title & Abstract
- **Accuracy:** The title and abstract accurately reflect the core contribution: a hierarchical JEPA framework for multi-scale trajectory representation.
- **Clarity of Problem/Method/Results:** The abstract clearly identifies the gap (single-scale embeddings neglect local/global synergy), the method (3-layer hierarchy with top-down attention fusion in a JEPA), and key results (superior similarity and zero-shot generalization across urban/maritime datasets).
- **Unsupported Claims:** The claim that "HiT-JEPA is the first architecture to explicitly unify both fine-grained and abstract trajectory patterns within a single model" (Abstract & Sec. 1) is slightly over-assertive. While likely the first to apply *JEPA* hierarchically to trajectories, feature pyramids and multi-scale RNN/Transformer architectures have been explored in trajectory analysis previously. Softening this to emphasize the *self-supervised predictive* unification, rather than absolute prioritization, would align better with community standards.

### Introduction & Motivation
- **Motivation & Gap:** The motivation is well-grounded. Trajectories inherently exhibit temporal and spatial multi-scale characteristics (turns vs. routes), yet current SSL methods often collapse to a single representation scale. The gap relative to T-JEPA and TrajCL is clearly articulated.
- **Contributions:** The three contributions are distinct and verifiable: architecture design, hierarchical interaction mechanism, and empirical validation (similarity/zero-shot/downstream).
- **Claim Calibration:** The introduction correctly frames sequence models' "single-scale bias." However, it could briefly acknowledge that the hierarchical design also introduces complexity in balancing gradient flow across levels, which motivates the need for the top-down spotlight mechanism. This would set up the methodology section more effectively.

### Method / Approach
- **Description & Reproducibility:** The architecture is generally well-described. The use of Conv1D+MaxPool for deterministic length reduction (Eq. 3-5), standard JEPA masking, and the VICReg regularization are clear. Appendix A.3 provides most necessary hyperparameters.
- **Assumptions & Justification:** 
    - *Fixed 3-level hierarchy with exact halving:* The method assumes a fixed reduction to $n$, $n/2$, and $n/4$ tokens. For very short trajectories (e.g., $n < 20$), the highest level may have too few tokens for effective attention, while variable-length padding isn't discussed. The choice of exactly three levels and stride 2 should be justified, perhaps referencing the hyperparameter analysis (which currently only covers attention layers, not hierarchy depth).
    - *H3 Resolution:* Using Uber H3 is a strong design choice. However, the resolution is fixed per dataset. The model's sensitivity to this quantization granularity is not analyzed.
- **Logical Gaps & Technical Concerns:** 
    - *Attention Interpolation (Eq. 13-14):* This is the most critical technical point. The method upsamples attention weights $A^{(l)}$ via bilinear interpolation to match the spatial dimensions of level $l-1$. Attention weights are outputs of a softmax normalization (rows sum to 1). Bilinear interpolation does **not** preserve this stochastic property. Interpolated rows will no longer sum to 1, altering the probabilistic interpretation and potentially destabilizing the gradient flow in the lower layer. The authors should clarify if renormalization is applied post-interpolation, or provide a theoretical/empirical justification for why non-normalized attention fusion works.
    - *Information Leakage:* The text states targets are removed from context to prevent leakage. However, with hierarchical injection, does the target encoder at level $l$ pass information to the context encoder at level $l-1$ via the fused attention? Since the attention weights at level $l$ are computed from the target encoder, injecting them into the lower level's context path could inadvertently leak target structure. Clarifying the gradient stop operations or EMA synchronization across levels would strengthen reproducibility.
- **Failure Modes:** Edge cases for trajectories with irregular sampling (handled via downsampling augmentation in experiments) or trajectories that span multiple H3 zones with varying density are discussed empirically but lack architectural mitigation strategies.

### Experiments & Results
- **Testing Claims:** The experiments directly test similarity, robustness (downsampling/distortion), zero-shot transfer, and downstream fine-tuning against heuristics. This aligns well with ICLR's expectations for comprehensive empirical validation.
- **Baselines:** Comparisons with TrajCL, CLEAR, and T-JEPA are appropriate and state-of-the-art. **TrjSR**, however, shows mean ranks in the hundreds (Table 1) compared to ~1.0 for others. This suggests a severe performance degradation or hyperparameter mismatch. If TrjSR is fundamentally unsuited for the metric/dataset, it should be excluded or explicitly tuned; otherwise, it risks appearing as a strawman.
- **Missing Ablations / Significance:**
    - *Statistical Rigor:* Table 1 and Table 2 report point estimates. Given the stochastic nature of SSL (masking, augmentation), reporting mean $\pm$ standard deviation across multiple seeds is standard for ICLR. A single run's results, even if extensive, leave room for variance concerns.
    - *Zero-Shot Confounder:* The zero-shot experiments (Porto $\to$ TKY/NYC/AIS) change the H3 resolution between training and testing (Res 11 $\to$ Res 10/4, per App A.3). While the model's ability to generalize across resolutions is a strength, this is not a pure zero-shot transfer of representations because the input tokenization schema changes. The authors should discuss this explicitly: is the model learning scale-invariant semantics, or is the coarser grid simply smoothing noise that mimics sparser data?
    - *Fusion Mechanism:* The "HiT_no_attn" variant replaces attention fusion with a baseline (likely parallel branches). It would be more informative to compare against a "Bottom-Up" fusion (propagating fine-to-coarse features) to demonstrate why the "Top-Down" spotlight is specifically superior for trajectory semantics.
- **Results Support:** The performance gains are substantial, particularly in zero-shot and downstream approximation of Hausdorff/Fréchet. The efficiency analysis (Table 8, 0.341s vs T-JEPA's 1.022s) is a crucial, positive finding that should be highlighted in the main text; the convolutional downsampling likely reduces the quadratic attention cost, making the hierarchy computationally efficient, not just representational.

### Writing & Clarity
- **Clarity of Sections:** Section 3 is generally clear, but the notation for hierarchical interaction is dense. The distinction between $S^{(l)}$ (representation) and $A^{(l)}$ (attention weights) and how they interact across branches needs careful reading.
- **Figures/Tables:** 
    - Figure 2 is highly informative.
    - Figure 4 provides good qualitative evidence of semantic phases.
    - Table 1 is comprehensive but difficult to parse due to the dense layout; a subset focusing on key robustness settings in the main text would improve readability.
    - The explanation of "HiT_emb" in the ablation (concat embedding vs. fused attention) is slightly ambiguous regarding dimensionality handling. Does concatenation happen along the channel or sequence dimension? Clarifying this would aid reproducibility.
- **Interpretability:** The attention visualization is valuable. Linking the "origin anchoring" and "destination intent" directly to specific heads or layers helps build trust in the learned representations.

### Limitations & Broader Impact
- **Acknowledged Limitations:** The paper currently states only that the hierarchical interaction framework could be extended to other architectures like CNNs or Mambas (Sec. 10). This is a minor limitation.
- **Missed Limitations:** 
    - *Scalability:* While the model is faster than T-JEPA, attention still scales quadratically with the sequence length at the finest level. For very long trajectories ($n > 500$), memory bottlenecks may persist. Discussing linear attention approximations or chunk-based processing would be beneficial.
    - *Grid Dependency:* Performance relies on H3 quantization. In regions with poor signal or irregular urban layouts, fixed hexagon cells may not align with road networks, potentially introducing spatial bias not present in graph-based methods.
    - *Zero-Shot Resolution Change:* As noted in Experiments, the shift in H3 resolution during zero-shot evaluation is a limitation of the evaluation protocol and should be acknowledged.
- **Broader Impact:** The ethics statement correctly identifies privacy preservation via cell tokenization. However, it does not discuss potential misuse, such as surveillance or profiling based on inferred mobility patterns from clustered embeddings. Adding a sentence on responsible deployment would strengthen this section.

### Overall Assessment
HiT-JEPA presents a strong, timely contribution to trajectory representation learning. The integration of a hierarchical structure with a Joint Embedding Predictive Architecture addresses a genuine gap in multi-scale SSL for spatial-temporal data. The empirical results are compelling, demonstrating particularly robust zero-shot generalization and efficient computation compared to recent JEPA baselines. However, the paper must address the mathematical implications of interpolating attention weights without renormalization (Eq. 13-14), as this impacts theoretical soundness. Additionally, the zero-shot evaluation is partially confounded by changing H3 resolutions across datasets, which requires explicit clarification rather than implicit assumption. The inclusion of error bars for stochastic training and a critical re-evaluation of the TrjSR baseline are necessary to meet ICLR's empirical rigor standards. If these concerns are resolved, the contribution stands as a significant advancement in unsupervised trajectory analysis, offering both architectural novelty and practical efficiency.

# Neutral Reviewer
## Balanced Review

### Summary
This paper introduces HiT-JEPA, a hierarchical self-supervised learning framework designed to capture multi-scale spatial-temporal dependencies in trajectory data. By coupling a three-layer convolutional abstraction pipeline with a Joint Embedding Predictive Architecture and top-down attention propagation, the model learns point-level, segment-level, and trajectory-level embeddings simultaneously. Extensive experiments across six real-world mobility datasets demonstrate that HiT-JEPA achieves state-of-the-art performance in zero-shot trajectory retrieval and downstream fine-tuning for approximating heuristic similarity measures.

### Strengths
1. **Clear Multi-Scale Motivation & Architectural Integration:** The paper effectively identifies a limitation in existing self-supervised trajectory models: their tendency to operate at a single temporal/spatial granularity. The proposed three-level abstraction (Eq. 3-5) combined with level-wise JEPA branches provides a principled pipeline for coarse-to-fine representation learning.
2. **Comprehensive Empirical Validation:** The evaluation is thorough, covering standard similarity retrieval, robustness to point-downsampling (`ρs`) and coordinate distortion (`ρd`), zero-shot transfer to heterogeneous domains (check-in, maritime), and fine-tuning to approximate four classical heuristic distances. Results in Tables 1 & 2 consistently show HiT-JEPA's competitive standing, particularly in zero-shot generalization where domain shift is severe.
3. **Interpretability and Ablation Rigor:** The attention visualization (Sec. 4.3, Fig. 4 & 8) successfully bridges learned weights with physical trajectory semantics (origin anchoring, stay points, destination intent). The ablation study (Sec. 4.4, Table 3) systematically isolates the hierarchical interaction mechanism, sequence length reduction, and spatial tokenization scheme, confirming the contribution of each design choice.

### Weaknesses
1. **Heuristic Nature of Hierarchical Interaction:** The core cross-level mechanism relies on bilinear interpolation of attention weights followed by a linear blend with a learnable scale factor `σ` (Eq. 13-14). While effective empirically, this lacks theoretical motivation or exploration of alternative fusion mechanisms (e.g., cross-attention, learned projection, or feature modulation). It remains unclear why attention map upsampling is superior to direct representation propagation.
2. **Heavily Skewed Loss Weighting:** The final loss weights are set to `λ=0.05, µ=0.15, ν=0.8` (Eq. 16, Appendix A.5), meaning the coarsest trajectory level dominates the optimization objective by 80%. Appendix A.5 shows that equal weighting (`0.33, 0.33, 0.33`) performs competitively on downsampling. This imbalance raises questions about whether the finer levels are actively driving learning or merely acting as auxiliary regularizers, and weakens the argument for a tightly coupled hierarchical architecture.
3. **Speculative Baseline Analysis on Porto:** The paper claims TrajCL outperforms HiT-JEPA on Porto because TrajCL exploits "auxiliary cues such as movement speed and orientations" (Sec. 4.1.1). However, no controlled experiment is provided to verify this claim (e.g., ablating speed/orientation features in TrajCL, or adding them to HiT-JEPA). This leaves the performance gap on dense urban datasets partially unexplained.
4. **Dependence on Pretrained Spatial Priors:** Spatial tokenization uses Uber H3 grids with `node2vec` embeddings trained offline. While practical, this injects a strong geographic prior that is not adapted end-to-end with the trajectory encoder. The model's performance may be sensitive to regions with vastly different road network densities or topologies where the pretrained grid embeddings misalign with actual mobility patterns.

### Novelty & Significance
From an ICLR perspective, the novelty is **moderate but applied**. The architecture adapts JEPA and hierarchical self-supervised concepts (established in CV/NLP) to trajectory data with clear engineering adaptations (convolutional pooling for sequence halving, attention weight upsampling). It does not introduce a fundamentally new learning paradigm, but it successfully demonstrates how hierarchical predictive coding can address a well-defined gap in spatial-temporal representation learning. The significance to the **geospatial/urban computing community is high**, given the strong zero-shot and robustness results across diverse data modalities. For the broader **machine learning community**, the paper's value lies more in its systematic evaluation and interpretability analysis than in groundbreaking algorithmic theory. To meet the highest ICLR bar, the work would benefit from deeper theoretical analysis of cross-level information flow or a more generalizable hierarchical fusion mechanism.

### Suggestions for Improvement
1. **Investigate Dynamic/Adaptive Loss Weighting:** Replace fixed loss weights with gradient-normalized or uncertainty-based weighting (e.g., Kendall et al., 2018) to let the model automatically balance fine-grained and coarse objectives. This would strengthen the claim that all three levels actively contribute to representation quality.
2. **Compare Alternative Cross-Level Fusion Mechanisms:** Add an ablation comparing the current attention-upsampling fusion with (a) direct concatenation of level embeddings, (b) cross-attention queries between adjacent levels, and (c) FiLM-style feature conditioning. This would clarify why the proposed "top-down spotlight" is optimal.
3. **Provide Controlled Baseline Experiments:** To substantiate the Porto analysis, run an ablation removing speed/orientation inputs from TrajCL (if applicable per its open-source code) and report the performance delta. Alternatively, demonstrate whether adding temporal derivatives to HiT-JEPA's context encoder closes the gap.
4. **Evaluate Spatial Tokenization Robustness:** Include a comparison between frozen `node2vec` grid embeddings and learnable spatial embeddings (initialized randomly or with continuous positional encodings). This would prove that the hierarchical JEPA architecture, rather than the geographic prior, is the primary driver of success.
5. **Clarify Computational Trade-offs:** Table 8 reports training time per iteration, but parameter count and inference memory/FLOPs are missing. Provide a complexity analysis relative to T-JEPA to justify whether the hierarchical design's accuracy gains are computationally efficient or if they incur significant overhead.

# Spark Finder Review
## How to Improve This Paper

### Missing Experiments (top 3-5 only)
1. **Validate the true zero-shot setting:** Spatial node embeddings are pre-trained via `node2vec` on city-specific graphs, creating disjoint embedding spaces. Re-run zero-shot evaluations using a city-independent location encoder (e.g., direct H3 ID lookups or continuous lat/lon MLPs) to prove generalization stems from behavioral patterns rather than accidental spatial token alignment.
2. **Replace mean-rank with standard retrieval metrics & significance testing:** Mean-rank is highly sensitive to database size distributions and lacks retrieval granularity. Report HitRate@K, MRR, and NDCG@K for all similarity experiments, and include bootstrap confidence intervals or paired t-tests to demonstrate that improvements over T-JEPA are statistically significant across datasets.
3. **Ablation on hierarchical interaction vs. cross-attention:** The proposed bilinear interpolation of attention weights to upsample coarse-level guidance to fine levels is non-standard. Add a baseline replacing the "spotlight" fusion with explicit cross-attention or learnable query-key interactions between levels to prove the interpolation isn't simply masking a weak architectural choice.
4. **Report inference complexity and memory scaling:** Query latency and memory footprint during similarity search are critical for trajectory systems. Provide FLOPs, parameter counts, and end-to-end retrieval latency (ms/query) across varying database sizes compared to baselines to validate the practical utility of the three-level hierarchy.

### Deeper Analysis Needed (top 3-5 only)
1. **Quantitative level-wise semantic validation:** The claim that Level 1 captures kinematics and Level 3 captures route semantics remains qualitative. Train linear probes (frozen encoders) on each level's output against explicit trajectory attributes (velocity variance, stop counts, OD straight-line distance) to quantitatively measure how abstraction depth correlates with semantic properties.
2. **Mechanism analysis for irregular sampling robustness:** The method relies on Conv1D + MaxPool, which inherently assumes uniform temporal spacing, yet claims robustness on T-Drive (avg interval 3.1 mins). Analyze performance degradation as a function of sampling irregularity and test whether injecting explicit time-delta embeddings mitigates the convolutional bias, proving the hierarchy handles real-world GPS irregularity rather than just downsampling noise.
3. **Disentangling depth from interaction benefits:** The loss weighting $\nu=0.8$ heavily prioritizes Level 3, risking performance collapse into a single-scale model. Report results where the hierarchical information propagation is disabled while maintaining 3 independent JEPA levels, isolating whether gains come from the *hierarchical fusion* or merely from increased model capacity/depth.

### Visualizations & Case Studies
1. **Failure mode analysis:** Show specific query trajectories where HiT-JEPA retrieves spatially proximate but semantically incorrect matches (e.g., parallel highways with different road classes, or identical routes with vastly different speeds). Understanding these failures exposes the limits of the H3 discretization and proves the authors understand the model's boundaries.
2. **Rank-by-rank retrieval visualization:** Instead of decoding to hexagons, visualize the query trajectory alongside the Top-1, Top-5, and Top-20 retrieved trajectories from HiT-JEPA versus T-JEPA. This directly demonstrates *how* the multi-scale abstraction corrects specific ranking errors (e.g., fixing local drift vs. preserving global OD intent).

### Obvious Next Steps
1. **Adopt a globally consistent spatial encoding scheme:** Replace graph-based `node2vec` initialization with a universal coordinate transformer or a pre-computed global H3 embedding matrix to establish a truly unified trajectory foundation model that can generalize to unseen cities without spatial vocabulary mismatch.
2. **Demonstrate cross-task transferability:** Evaluate the learned hierarchical representations on standard downstream trajectory tasks like travel time estimation or destination prediction. Proving utility beyond similarity computation would significantly strengthen the claim that the hierarchy captures generalizable spatiotemporal dynamics.
3. **Joint multi-city pre-training with held-out evaluation:** Train a single HiT-JEPA instance on aggregated urban data (Porto, T-Drive, GeoLife) while holding out entire cities for zero-shot testing, establishing a rigorous foundation-style pre-training protocol that justifies the architectural complexity at scale.

# Final Consolidated Review
## Summary
HiT-JEPA introduces a hierarchical Joint Embedding Predictive Architecture (JEPA) for self-supervised trajectory representation learning. By coupling a deterministic convolutional pooling pipeline with level-wise JEPA branches and a top-down attention blending mechanism, the model learns multi-scale spatial-temporal abstractions ranging from fine-grained kinematics to global route semantics. The method demonstrates strong empirical performance on similarity retrieval, robustness to noise, and zero-shot transfer across heterogeneous urban, check-in, and maritime datasets, while offering improved computational efficiency over recent JEPA baselines.

## Strengths
- **Principled Multi-Scale Architecture for Trajectory SSL:** The paper correctly identifies that existing self-supervised trajectory models collapse to single-scale representations, losing the synergy between local dynamics and global route structure. The three-level abstraction pipeline integrated with JEPA's predictive objective effectively bridges this gap, as evidenced by the distinct semantic phases captured in the attention maps (Fig. 4 & 5).
- **Comprehensive and Challenging Empirical Validation:** The evaluation rigorously tests the model beyond standard retrieval, incorporating downsampling/distortion robustness, downstream heuristic approximation (EDR, LCSS, Fréchet), and cross-domain zero-shot generalization. The consistent outperformance, particularly in zero-shot settings and downstream fine-tuning, strongly supports the claim that hierarchical embeddings yield more transferable trajectories representations.
- **Strong Interpretability and Hidden Efficiency Gains:** The attention visualization convincingly links learned weights to physical trajectory semantics (origin anchoring, stay points, destination intent). Furthermore, the architecture's computational efficiency (0.341s/iteration vs. T-JEPA's 1.022s, Table 8) is a substantial practical advantage stemming from the convolutional sequence length reduction, a finding that should be elevated to the main text.

## Weaknesses
- **Spatial Vocabulary Mismatch Undermines Zero-Shot Claims:** The spatial tokenization pipeline relies on `node2vec` embeddings trained on dataset-specific road networks/graphs (Sec. 3, Eq. 1). The zero-shot protocol transfers a model trained on Porto to TKY, NYC, and AIS(AU) without clarifying how city-specific spatial embeddings are handled at test time. If the target datasets use disjoint `node2vec` embeddings or different H3 resolutions, the model is encountering unseen token spaces. This makes it impossible to disentangle whether the reported gains stem from learning scale-invariant behavioral semantics or from the inherent smoothing effect of coarser H3 grids masking out-of-distribution spatial noise. The zero-shot evaluation protocol must be explicitly clarified or adjusted to use a globally consistent spatial encoding scheme.
- **Heuristic Attention Fusion Lacks Probabilistic Grounding:** The core hierarchical interaction mechanism (Eq. 13-14) upsamples coarse-level attention weights via bilinear interpolation and linearly blends them with fine-grained attention. Since attention matrices are softmax outputs where rows sum to 1, bilinear interpolation does not preserve this stochastic property. Without post-interpolation renormalization, the blended matrix acts as an unnormalized weighting scheme, which lacks theoretical justification and could destabilize gradient flow. The paper treats this as an effective heuristic, but its mathematical implications for representation stability remain unaddressed.
- **Temporal Irregularity is Architecturally Mismatched:** The hierarchical abstraction relies on 1D convolutions and max-pooling with fixed stride 2, which implicitly assume uniform temporal spacing between trajectory points. Real-world GPS data (e.g., T-Drive with 3.1-minute average sampling intervals, Sec. 4.1.1) exhibits high temporal irregularity. While the model shows empirical robustness to aggressive point downsampling, the architecture lacks explicit mechanisms (e.g., time-delta embeddings or temporal-aware convolutions) to differentiate between rapid spatial progression and prolonged stationary periods, risking semantic conflation in sparse or bursty trajectories.

## Nice-to-Haves
- **Statistical Rigor:** Reporting mean $\pm$ standard deviation across multiple random seeds for Table 1 & 2 would strengthen confidence in the stochastic SSL training pipeline, though the magnitude of improvement over baselines is substantial.
- **Alternative Fusion Ablations:** Comparing the attention upsampling mechanism against explicit cross-attention or FiLM-style feature modulation would better isolate why "top-down spotlight" blending is superior to direct cross-level feature propagation.
- **Explicit Temporal Features:** Investigating the addition of relative time-interval embeddings to the context input to verify if it further boosts robustness on highly irregular trajectories like T-Drive.
- **Standardized Retrieval Metrics:** Supplementing mean-rank with MRR or NDCG@K would align the evaluation with broader information retrieval standards, providing finer-grained insight into ranking quality at the head of the list.

## Novel Insights
The paper successfully adapts predictive coding to the spatial-temporal domain by demonstrating that trajectory semantics operate on a strict coarse-to-fine hierarchy where global route constraints can actively guide local kinematic feature extraction. The "top-down spotlight" concept reveals that zero-shot generalization in mobility data may depend less on precise spatial alignment and more on learning invariant structural abstractions (e.g., origin-destination intent vs. micro-maneuvers), suggesting that future trajectory foundation models could benefit from explicit hierarchical disentanglement rather than monolithic sequence encoding.

## Suggestions
1. **Clarify the Zero-Sot Spatial Tokenization Protocol:** Explicitly state how `node2vec` spatial embeddings are handled for unseen cities. If you rely on a global H3 grid, confirm that node embeddings are either frozen globally or re-derived without spatial leakage. To strengthen the claim, run a quick ablation using a city-independent spatial encoder (e.g., raw lat/lon MLP or global H3 ID embeddings) to verify that generalization is driven by behavioral pattern learning rather than spatial vocabulary adaptation.
2. **Address the Attention Interpolation Property:** Briefly discuss in the methodology whether interpolated attention weights are renormalized, or provide an empirical/gradient analysis demonstrating that non-stochastic attention fusion improves stability. If renormalization is applied, state it explicitly in Eq. 14.
3. **Mitigate Temporal Irregularity Bias:** Acknowledge the Conv1D uniform spacing assumption in the limitations. If feasible, add a simple ablation injecting relative time deltas into the positional encoding or context tokens to demonstrate that the hierarchical gains persist independent of sampling rate assumptions, thereby reinforcing robustness to real-world GPS irregularities.
