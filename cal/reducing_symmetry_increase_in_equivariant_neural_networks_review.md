# Harsh Critic Review
## Section-by-Section Critical Review

### Title & Abstract
- **Does the title accurately reflect the contribution?** Yes. The title precisely captures the core focus: analyzing and mitigating symmetry increase in ENNs.
- **Does the abstract clearly state the problem, method, and key results?** The problem (expressivity degradation due to unintended symmetry increase) and method (characterizing the symmetry infimum, providing computable algorithms, and proving genericity) are clearly stated. The key results (theoretical prediction of three degeneration types, generic isovariance under regularity, and QM9 validation) are summarized concisely.
- **Are any claims in the abstract unsupported by the paper?** The claim that *"for most equivariant maps, our guidelines effectively reduce symmetry increase"* is technically sound but relies on the measure-theoretic notion of "genericity" (full measure in the $C^\infty$ topology, Thm. 5.2). While mathematically rigorous, the abstract does not clarify that "most" refers to a topological/measure-theoretic genericity rather than empirical frequency over data distributions. Minor clarification would prevent misinterpretation by non-theorists.

### Introduction & Motivation
- **Is the problem well-motivated? Is the gap in prior work clearly identified?** Yes. The introduction effectively motivates symmetry increase using Curie's principle and recent ENN expressivity failures (Joshi et al., 2023; Cen et al., 2024). The gap is clearly articulated: prior work covers only extreme cases (full degeneration/collapse-to-zero) or relies on relaxing equivariance, lacking a general, predictive mathematical framework for *partial* increases (axial/half degeneration).
- **Are the contributions clearly stated and accurate?** The four contributions map directly to §§3–6 and are accurate. The progression from existence/uniqueness of the infimum (§3), to computation (§4), to genericity (§5), and empirical validation (§6) is logical and well-scoped.
- **Does the introduction over-claim or under-sell?** Neither. The claims are calibrated to the theoretical depth provided. It correctly positions the work as filling a mathematical gap rather than proposing a novel architecture.

### Method / Approach
- **Is the method clearly described and reproducible?** The mathematical framework is rigorous. Key concepts (isotropy subgroups, orbit types, symmetry infimum $I_G(Y,H)$, $p_Y$ operator) are defined in §2 and §3. Algorithms 1 & 2 (§4) provide a clear computational pipeline for high-multiplicity representations. The proofs are self-contained in the appendix, and the reliance on established criteria (Michel's, Ihrig-Golubitsky) is properly cited.
- **Are key assumptions stated and justified?** Yes. Assumptions include: compact Lie groups, finite-dimensional real representations, and the high-multiplicity condition $m(V, V_i) > \dim G$ for Prop. 4.2. The latter is well-justified for modern ENNs where channel counts typically exceed 3 or 8. The manifold hypothesis for data (§A.3, §5.1) is explicitly stated.
- **Are there logical gaps in the derivation or reasoning?** A conceptual gap exists between the theoretical setup and the experimental data. The theory assumes data lies on a *finite union of compact smooth G-invariant submanifolds* (§A.3, §5.1), enabling the use of differential topology, transversality, and Hausdorff measure for "almost isovariance" (Thm. 5.2). However, the experiments operate on discrete point clouds/molecules (QM9, synthetic k-folds). The paper does not explicitly discuss how continuous manifold-based genericity results translate to discrete, finite datasets where measure-zero sets are either non-existent or trivially sized. This discretization gap needs addressing to fully bridge theory and practice.
- **Are there edge cases or failure modes not discussed?** The computational complexity of Algorithms 1 & 2 for arbitrary subgroup lattices is not analyzed. While $SO(3)/O(3)$ subgroup chains are finite and precomputable, the enumeration of all adjacent supergroups could scale poorly for more complex groups beyond molecular point groups. Additionally, the paper focuses on global symmetry increase, but ENNs often use local message passing. The paper assumes global pooling/invariant outputs for graph-level tasks (§2 Ex 2.1), but does not explicitly address how local node-wise equivariance interacts with global symmetry increase before pooling.
- **For theoretical claims: are proofs correct and complete?** The proofs in the appendix (e.g., Thm. 3.1, 3.3, 5.1, 5.2) appear mathematically sound, correctly leveraging representation theory and stratification/transversality results (Bierstone, Field). Thm. 5.2's use of jet transversality to show dimension drop for non-infimum orbit types is standard and correctly applied.

### Experiments & Results
- **Do the experiments actually test the paper's claims?** §6.1 and §6.2 directly validate the predicted degeneration types and the independence of symmetry increase from model depth/width, strongly supporting the theoretical classification. §6.3 tests whether symmetry increase correlates with empirical performance degradation, which supports the "harmful" aspect of the claim.
- **Are baselines appropriate and fairly compared?** The experiments compare different feature degrees ($l_0$) within the same architecture (TFN, HEGNN), which is appropriate for isolating representation effects. However, there is no comparison to practical symmetry-breaking or symmetry-reduction techniques widely used in the literature (e.g., frame averaging, explicit reference frames, or injected random noise). The experiments are purely diagnostic; they show *what* goes wrong, but not *how well* the proposed guidelines compare to existing architectural fixes.
- **Are there missing ablations that would materially change conclusions?** A constructive ablation is missing. The paper shows that including degenerate features increases error (Fig. 6, 7), but does not demonstrate a case where *adding* a specific non-degenerate representation (following the §4.2 guidelines) strictly improves performance over a baseline that only uses safe features. A positive demonstration of "reduction" would strengthen the claim.
- **Are error bars / statistical significance reported?** Boxplots in Figs. 6 & 7 show error distributions across molecules. Table 21 uses a binary distinguishability metric with deterministic outcomes (±0.0). Given the stark order-of-magnitude differences in embedding norms and losses, statistical testing is less critical, but standard deviations or confidence intervals for MAE would improve rigor.
- **Do the results support the claims made, or are they cherry-picked?** Results align tightly with theory. The focus on the top-16 point groups in QM9 is reasonable, and the authors explicitly note low-sample groups. No cherry-picking is evident; the degeneration patterns follow the theoretical tables consistently across degrees.
- **Are datasets and evaluation metrics appropriate?** Synthetic k-fold structures and QM9 (isotropic polarizability) are appropriate. MAE is standard. Since polarizability is a scalar, testing equivariant feature expressivity via masked freezing is a valid diagnostic, though the final task does not require equivariance, which slightly weakens the real-world relevance of the performance drop observations.

### Writing & Clarity
- **Are there sections that are confusing or poorly explained?** The transition from abstract equivariant topology to practical ML guidelines is steep for a general ML audience. Sections 5 and Appendix D rely heavily on $C^\infty$ density, Whitney stratifications, and jet transversality. While rigorous, a brief high-level intuition (e.g., a diagram or plain-English summary) before the topological formalism in §5 would greatly improve accessibility.
- **Are figures and tables clear and informative?** Figures 2–4 effectively visualize the degeneration types and embedding collapse. Fig. 5's heatmap clearly shows the binary distinguishability pattern. Tables 1, 2, and the appendix symmetry infimum tables are comprehensive. The color-coding for degeneration types is explained but could be more prominently tied to the main text's discussion of practical guidelines.

### Limitations & Broader Impact
- **Do the authors acknowledge the key limitations?** The paper acknowledges the high-multiplicity requirement indirectly via Prop. 4.2 and notes that discrete degeneration has exceptional cases (light green in appendix tables). The reproducibility and ethics statements are standard and appropriate.
- **Are there fundamental limitations they missed?** 
  1. **Discrete Data Gap:** As noted, the manifold hypothesis and transversality arguments assume continuous data distributions. Real-world graph/molecular datasets are discrete and finite, where "almost everywhere" (measure zero) loses its theoretical meaning without smoothing or distributional assumptions.
  2. **Computational Overhead:** Computing symmetry infimum requires knowing the exact symmetry group of every input. In practice, molecules may have near-symmetries, experimental noise, or dynamic conformations where isotropy subgroups are ill-defined or numerically unstable. The guidelines assume exact symmetry knowledge.
  3. **Passive Mitigation:** The "reduction" strategy is purely architectural (feature selection/design). The paper does not address dynamic or learning-based symmetry breaking, which is how many practitioners handle this issue in practice.
- **Are there failure modes or negative societal impacts not discussed?** No direct negative societal impacts. Failure modes include misidentifying input symmetries due to numerical precision or dataset noise, which could lead to incorrect feature selection and unexpected performance drops. This practical caveat should be explicitly mentioned.

### Overall Assessment
This is a strong, theoretically rigorous paper that provides a novel and mathematically precise characterization of symmetry increase in ENNs, going significantly beyond prior work on full degeneration by quantifying partial increases (axial/half degeneration) via the symmetry infimum. The proofs are sound, the algorithms are computable for practical groups like $SO(3)/O(3)$, and the experiments cleanly validate the theoretical predictions. However, to meet ICLR's bar for impact, the authors must bridge the gap between continuous manifold-based theory and discrete molecular data, clarify how the guidelines compare to existing architectural symmetry-breaking methods, and ideally include a constructive demonstration showing that following the guidelines actively improves performance rather than just avoiding degradation. With these clarifications, the contribution will stand as a foundational reference for designing reliable, symmetry-aware representations.

# Neutral Reviewer
## Balanced Review

### Summary
The paper addresses the expressivity degradation of Equivariant Neural Networks (ENNs) on symmetric inputs, formalized as "symmetry increase," where output representations become invariant to transformations not present in the input. It establishes a rigorous mathematical framework proving that this increase is bounded below by a unique "symmetry infimum" determined by the feature space's algebraic structure, and provides computable algorithms and feature-design guidelines to control it. Theoretical predictions are validated through synthetic k-fold visualizations and molecular property prediction experiments on QM9, demonstrating alignment between predicted degenerations and empirical performance.

### Strengths
1. **Rigorous and Well-Structured Theoretical Foundation:** The paper successfully bridges abstract group theory (orbit types, fixed-point subspaces, Whitney stratification) with practical ENN expressivity. Theorems 3.1 and 3.3 precisely characterize the minimal achievable symmetry, while Theorem 5.2 leverages transversality and manifold topology to prove that "almost isovariance" is a generic property for dense equivariant parametrizations. Proofs in the appendix are self-contained and mathematically sound.
2. **Actionable Computational Framework:** Algorithms 1 and 2, paired with the high-multiplicity sufficiency criterion (Prop. 4.2), translate topological concepts into practical tools. The classification of degeneration types (full, axial/half/continuous, discrete) and the explicit guidelines in §4.2 give practitioners a clear methodology for selecting irrep degrees to avoid information loss.
3. **Targeted Empirical Validation:** The experiments systematically verify theoretical claims. The k-fold visualizations (Fig. 3 & 4) clearly demonstrate full, axial, and half degeneration matching Table 1's predictions. The QM9 polarizability experiment (Fig. 6 & 7) further shows that including features predicted to cause full degeneration actively harms performance, confirming the theory's practical relevance.
4. **Clear Exposition and Nuanced Definitions:** Despite dense mathematics, the paper is well-organized. The distinction between trivial kernel-induced symmetry increase and unintended increase, along with the relaxed "almost isovariant" definition (§5.1), reflects a mature understanding of both theoretical topology and machine learning practicalities.

### Weaknesses
1. **Indirect Empirical Validation for Training Dynamics:** The QM9 experiments (§6.3) rely on masking features from a *pretrained* encoder rather than training models end-to-end with the proposed guidelines. While this validates the theoretical prediction about feature spaces, it does not demonstrate whether constructing architectures according to the infimum guidelines improves convergence, sample efficiency, or final accuracy during actual SGD optimization, leaving a gap between theoretical genericity and learned network behavior.
2. **Heavy Reliance on High-Multiplicity Assumptions:** The sufficiency of Michel's Criterion (Prop. 4.2) and the correctness of Algo. 2 depend on $r > \dim G$. While common in modern ENNs, many efficiency-focused architectures use low multiplicities. The paper notes predictions hold for $r=1$ in Appendix C.4 but does not formally analyze how the symmetry infimum bounds degrade or become approximate when multiplicities are small, limiting immediate applicability to resource-constrained settings.
3. **Gap Between $C^\infty$ Density and Practical Optimization:** Theorem 5.2 assumes $C^\infty$-dense function families and the manifold hypothesis. Real-world ENNs (e.g., TFN/HEGNN with ReLU, finite depth/width, numerical precision limits, and non-convex loss landscapes) do not satisfy these smoothness or density conditions. The paper does not discuss how optimization dynamics (e.g., gradient flow, local minima, or numerical regularization) might inadvertently induce or mitigate symmetry increase in practice.
4. **Computational Complexity and Group Generality Not Addressed:** Algorithms 1 & 2 require enumerating closed supergroups and computing fixed-point dimensions. For complex scientific data (e.g., crystal structures with space groups, or large finite permutation groups), the subgroup lattice can be combinatorially large. The paper focuses exclusively on $SO(3)/O(3)$ without discussing algorithmic complexity, scalability, or adaptation to other symmetry groups common in geometric deep learning.

### Novelty & Significance
The work is highly novel. While prior literature has empirically observed or partially modeled ENN expressivity collapse on symmetric inputs (e.g., Curie's principle discussions, collapse-to-zero for k-folds), this paper provides the first general, computable, and topologically rigorous framework to predict and bound symmetry increase via the "symmetry infimum." By connecting orbit type stratification and stratified transversality to ENN design, it elevates a known heuristic issue to a principled architectural guideline. The significance is substantial for the geometric deep learning and scientific ML communities, offering a mathematical compass for selecting feature representations that preserve geometric discriminability without trial-and-error tuning.

### Suggestions for Improvement
1. **Include End-to-End Training Experiments:** Validate the guidelines by training two ENNs from scratch (or fine-tuning properly designed encoders) on a symmetric task: one constructed with degenerate feature components and one adhering to the infimum guidelines. Report differences in convergence speed, final error, and sample efficiency to bridge the gap between theoretical isovariance and practical optimization.
2. **Address Low-Multiplicity Regimes Explicitly:** Provide a theoretical or empirical analysis of how symmetry increase bounds behave when $r \leq \dim G$. Include a small-scale counterexample or sensitivity analysis showing where the high-multiplicity sufficiency breaks down and how a practitioner can approximate the infimum in low-channel settings.
3. **Discuss Optimization and Numerical Realities:** Add a subsection or discussion paragraph acknowledging the gap between $C^\infty$ density/generic maps and finite-parameter, SGD-trained networks. Discuss how techniques like weight initialization, activation smoothness, or numerical noise interact with symmetry preservation, and whether the theory provides guarantees for practical training.
4. **Analyze Algorithmic Complexity and Extensions:** Provide a complexity analysis for Algorithms 1 & 2 (e.g., in terms of subgroup lattice size and representation dimension). Discuss how the framework could be extended or approximated for non-point groups (e.g., $E(3)$, space groups, or finite graph automorphism groups), which would significantly broaden its practical impact.

# Final Consolidated Review
## Summary
This paper provides a rigorous mathematical framework to characterize and predict "symmetry increase" in equivariant neural networks (ENNs), where outputs become invariant to transformations beyond the input's intrinsic symmetries. It introduces the "symmetry infimum" as a unique, computable lower bound on output symmetry determined by the feature space's representation structure. The authors develop algorithms to compute this infimum, derive practical guidelines for selecting irreducible representations to avoid harmful degenerations (full, axial, half), and prove that under smooth manifold assumptions, almost-isovariant maps are generic for dense parameterizations. Theoretical predictions are validated through synthetic visualization tasks and diagnostic experiments on QM9.

## Strengths
- **Rigorous topological-to-algorithmic bridge:** The paper successfully elevates a known heuristic issue in ENNs into a formal theory using orbit type stratification, fixed-point subspaces, and $C^\infty$ transversality. The introduction of the "symmetry infimum" provides the first precise, computable lower bound on unintended symmetry increase.
- **Actionable and well-validated guidelines:** Algorithms 1 & 2 translate abstract group theory into concrete feature-selection rules for $SO(3)/O(3)$ representations. The clear classification of degeneration types directly informs which irrep degrees preserve or collapse geometric information, moving architecture design away from trial-and-error.
- **Precise diagnostic empirical validation:** The synthetic rotation experiments (§6.1–6.2) and the QM9 feature-masking study (§6.3) cleanly isolate representation effects from optimization dynamics. The binary collapse patterns in embedding norms and the sharp MAE increases for theoretically predicted degenerate degrees align exactly with the theoretical tables, strongly confirming the framework's diagnostic accuracy.

## Weaknesses
- **Gap between continuous theory and discrete data:** The genericity guarantees (Thm. 5.2) rely on the manifold hypothesis, Whitney stratification, and Hausdorff measure, which assume continuous, smooth data distributions. Real-world inputs (molecular graphs, point clouds) are discrete and finite, where "measure-zero" sets are ill-defined and exact isotropy subgroups are sensitive to numerical precision or thermal noise. The paper does not explicitly address how these continuous topological results translate to discrete datasets or how the framework handles near-symmetries.
- **Lack of end-to-end constructive validation:** The experiments deliberately freeze encoders or use random initializations to isolate representation properties, which is methodologically sound for diagnostic purposes. However, the paper does not demonstrate whether following the infimum guidelines actively improves end-to-end training dynamics (e.g., convergence speed, final task performance) compared to standard heuristic feature choices. This leaves the practical impact on optimization and generalization partially unverified.
- **Theoretical dependence on high-multiplicity representations:** The sufficiency of Michel’s criterion and the correctness of the infimum algorithms formally require channel multiplicities $r > \dim G$ (Prop. 4.2). While the authors note empirical consistency for $r=1$ in Appendix C.4, there is no theoretical analysis of how the symmetry infimum bounds degrade, become approximate, or fail in low-channel, efficiency-focused regimes commonly used in scalable ENNs.

## Nice-to-Haves
- A computational complexity analysis of the subgroup lattice enumeration in Algorithms 1 & 2, particularly regarding scalability to larger finite permutation groups or combined spatial-permutation symmetries.
- Discussion of how practical optimization factors (non-smooth activations like ReLU, finite network depth/width, SGD dynamics, and numerical regularization) interact with or mitigate symmetry increase in real training loops.
- Exploration of robustness bounds or approximation strategies for inputs with broken/approximate symmetries, which would better align the theory with noisy physical datasets.

## Novel Insights
The paper reframes symmetry-aware architecture design from an empirical tuning problem into a predictable algebraic constraint. By establishing the symmetry infimum as a fundamental lower bound, it reveals that certain high-degree features are not merely redundant but actively destructive when they force the representation space to collapse to a larger isotropy group. This topological lens explains why standard practices (like blindly stacking high-order spherical harmonics) can degrade model expressivity on symmetric inputs, offering a principled diagnostic compass for geometric deep learning that separates unavoidable kernel-induced invariance from harmful, avoidable degenerations.

## Suggestions
- Explicitly address the discrete-data limitation in the discussion. Clarify how the "almost everywhere" and transversality guarantees translate to finite, discrete datasets (e.g., by assuming empirical concentration on smooth manifolds or acknowledging the framework as an idealized limit that guides feature selection in practice).
- Include a small-scale end-to-end experiment (or ablation on trained checkpoints) where two models are compared: one following the infimum guidelines and one violating them. Report differences in final task performance or sample efficiency to validate that the theory translates to practical training benefits.
- Formally state the theoretical limits of the high-multiplicity assumption and provide a brief sensitivity analysis for $r \leq \dim G$, demonstrating how the infimum computation behaves (or can be relaxed) in low-channel settings to broaden the framework's applicability to efficient architectures.
