# Harsh Critic Review
## Section-by-Section Critical Review

### Title & Abstract
The title accurately reflects the core components of the work: variable-length tokens, quality awareness, and sparsified indexing for efficiency. The abstract clearly states the problem, the hash-based mapping method, and the key empirical results (4.2× speedup, 68% memory reduction, 85.1% F1).

 However, the claim of the "**first comprehensive theory** of token-based genomic classification" is a strong assertion that may invite scrutiny. The abstract highlights "**learned** sparsification" and a "**learned** sensitivity $\eta$," but the body suggests these are largely pre-computed or hyperparameters tuned on validation sets rather than end-to-end learning. The abstract should be calibrated to clearly distinguish between *algorithmic/theoretical* contributions and *system engineering* contributions. The theoretical guarantees referenced ($O(\sqrt{V|Y|/n})$ convergence) are standard Rademacher complexity results applied to a linear class; while correctly applied, labeling them as a fundamental transformation of the field in the abstract may overstate their novelty to the ICLR audience.

### Introduction & Motivation
The motivation is well-grounded in the longstanding speed-accuracy trade-off in metagenomics. The gap identification—that alignment-free methods lose discriminative power by ignoring quality and using fixed-length $k$-mers—is clear and compelling.

The introduction claims the framework addresses "(iii) novel organisms absent from the reference." This claim is not supported by the empirical evaluation (see Experiments section). CAMI II benchmarks consist of simulated reads from known genomes within the database. Classifying reads from *novel* taxa is a distinct, harder problem (requiring OOV handling, rejection mechanisms, or strain-aware generalization) that is currently absent from both the method description and experiments. The introduction should either drop this claim or the paper must include an experiment demonstrating robustness to unseen taxa. Additionally, the framing of "Theoretical Framework" risks overselling the application of standard statistical learning theory (SLT) to a domain-specific heuristic. The introduction should better contextualize the theory: is it proving that this specific hashing scheme works, or is it providing new bounds on dependent sequence tokens? The current text blurs this distinction.

### Method / Approach
The core methodology is a quality-weighted bag-of-tokens likelihood ratio test, replacing alignment with hash lookups. For ICLR, several methodological concerns must be addressed:

1.  **Inconsistency in Dependency Assumptions:** The derivation of the classification objective (Definition 2, Theorem 3, and Appendix C.2) relies on a generative model where tokens are sampled i.i.d. conditioned on the taxon (a Naive Bayes assumption). However, Section 4 and Appendix I.4 explicitly analyze token dependencies via $\alpha$-mixing to account for overlaps. The paper must reconcile these: The likelihood derivation assumes independence, yet the concentration bounds (Lemma 7) spend effort proving concentration despite dependencies. If dependencies are significant enough to require $\alpha$-mixing analysis, the i.i.d. likelihood derivation is technically misspecified. The authors should clarify whether the score function is justified as a pseudo-likelihood or if the dependency structure is incorporated into the objective function.

2.  **Vagueness of "Gradient-based Sparsification":** The paper repeatedly claims "gradient-based importance scoring" (Sec 1.3, 2.1, 3.5) but provides insufficient detail on the objective function driving these gradients. Since HighClass is not parametric (it uses pre-computed emission probabilities and counts), there is no neural network to backpropagate through. Appendix D and E mention "pre-computed importance masks" but do not define the loss function $\mathcal{L}$ or the model with respect to which gradients $\nabla \mathcal{L}$ are computed. Without this, the sparsification appears to be a heuristic feature selection method rather than a learned component, and the term "gradient-based" is misleading. The authors must specify the surrogate task used to compute these gradients and justify why they generalize across taxa.

3.  **Theoretical Bounds Applicability:** Theorem 6 provides a uniform convergence bound $O(\sqrt{V|Y|/n})$. This is a standard result for linear hypothesis classes with bounded weights. The bound becomes vacuous if $n$ is small relative to $V|Y|$. While the authors plug in numbers ($n=10^6$) to show a bound of 0.021, metagenomic classifiers are often trained on databases where $n$ (reference sequences) and the number of *distinct* token patterns can be highly skewed. The theory assumes empirical estimates $\hat{\pi}_y(t)$ converge. The paper relies on Laplace smoothing ($\epsilon = 10^{-6}$), but the impact of smoothing on the Rademacher complexity analysis is not discussed. Does the smoothing parameter affect the consistency rates? This gap weakens the "rigorous theoretical foundations" claim.

### Experiments & Results
The experiments are comprehensive and the statistical reporting (bootstrapping, Wilcoxon tests, effect sizes) meets high standards. Table 3 is particularly informative, isolating the contribution of the QA-Token vocabulary (+6.8% F1).

However, critical evaluations are missing:
1.  **Novel Taxa and Domain Shift:** As noted, there is no evaluation on reads from organisms not present in the training/reference database. A major claim of token-based methods is generalization, but without testing on "held-out" strains or species that share homology with the training set but are not identical, the robustness of the token mapping to distribution shift remains unverified.
2.  **Index Construction Cost:** The paper focuses exclusively on query time and memory footprint. Table 4 shows scalability at inference, but the cost to *build* the sparsified index is ignored. In clinical or environmental settings, reference databases are updated frequently. If index construction takes orders of magnitude longer than query, the 4.2× speedup may be negated in dynamic environments. The total cost of ownership (Index Time + Query Time) must be reported.
3.  **Baseline Selection:** Comparisons against Kraken2, Centrifuge, and MetaTrinity are appropriate for bioinformatics. However, for an ICLR submission, the omission of deep learning-based classifiers (e.g., transformer or CNN-based metagenomic tools like DeepMicrobes, or embedding-based approaches) is notable. Even if HighClass outperforms them on efficiency, discussing why deep models fail on this task (e.g., training data hunger, inference cost) would strengthen the positioning of the result.
4.  **Statistical Independence:** The use of bootstrap confidence intervals over reads is standard, but reads from the same genome are not independent. The Wilcoxon tests should ideally account for this grouping (e.g., per-genome aggregation or mixed-effects analysis) to ensure the $p$-values are not artificially deflated.

### Writing & Clarity
The writing is generally clear and structured well. The distinction between "learned" components and "pre-computed/heuristic" components is the primary clarity issue. For example, Section 1.3 claims "Gradient-based sparsification... preserves 94% accuracy through principled feature selection," but Section E.1 reveals the method "adopts... gradient-based sparsification masks" without detailing the learning procedure. This makes it difficult for a reader to distinguish what is novel to *this* paper versus what is adopted from prior work (Alser et al., 2024).

Appendices contain significant repetition. For instance, the Refined Scoring Function is defined in Definition 12, Definition 15, and again in G.1 with slight notation variations. Consolidating these would improve readability. The complexity analysis in Prop 4 vs. I.1 also repeats arguments; I.1 correctly notes the alignment complexity claim is "pessimistic," which slightly undermines the motivation in the Introduction; the authors should harmonize these claims.

### Limitations & Broader Impact
The authors discuss error analysis in M.6, correctly identifying failures in closely related species and low-quality regions.

**Fundamental Limitations Missed**:
*   **Identifiability vs. Biological Reality**: Theorem 8 relies on the "Identifiability" assumption (KL divergence between emission probabilities $> \delta$). In metagenomics, this assumption is frequently violated due to horizontal gene transfer (HGT) and conserved ribosomal genes, which cause distinct taxa to have nearly identical token distributions. The paper acknowledges HGT in L.1 but does not discuss how the method or bounds degrade when identifiability fails. The theoretical guarantees effectively break down in these common biological scenarios.
*   **Index Construction Bottleneck**: As mentioned in Experiments, the lack of discussion on index construction time is a significant practical limitation.
*   **Broader Impact**: The paper lacks a Broader Impact statement. Misclassification in clinical metagenomics can lead to false negatives in pathogen detection or incorrect antibiotic resistance profiling. Furthermore, reference databases are notoriously biased toward well-studied, often Western-associated microbiomes. A classifier trained on these references may systematically underperform on diverse environmental or global health samples. These issues should be acknowledged.

### Overall Assessment
HighClass presents a compelling systems-level solution to metagenomic classification, demonstrating that alignment can be largely replaced by quality-aware token hashing with significant gains in speed and memory. The empirical validation on CAMI II is rigorous, and the ablation studies effectively isolate the value of variable-length tokens.

However, for ICLR, the contribution has notable weaknesses. The "learning" components are either pre-trained (QA-Token) or underspecified (gradient sparsification), making the paper read more like a bioinformatics algorithm paper with a theoretical appendix than a core machine learning contribution. The theoretical analysis, while mathematically competent, applies standard SLT bounds to a domain where the core assumptions (i.i.d. generation, strict identifiability) are biologically tenuous, and the paper does not sufficiently reconcile this gap. Additionally, the absence of novel taxon evaluation and index construction costs leaves key practical claims unverified.

To meet the acceptance bar, the authors must: (1) clarify the objective and derivation of the gradient-based sparsification to justify the "learned" terminology; (2) reconcile the i.i.d. likelihood derivation with the dependency analysis, or frame the score as a consistent pseudo-likelihood; (3) include evaluation on unseen taxa and report index construction costs; and (4) temper theoretical claims to accurately reflect the application of existing bounds to a specific domain problem rather than a general theoretical breakthrough. The contribution stands as a valuable engineering advance, but requires significant tightening to align with ICLR's expectations for methodological novelty and theoretical rigor.

# Neutral Reviewer
## Balanced Review

### Summary
HighClass proposes a metagenomic classification framework that replaces traditional sequence alignment with a quality-aware, hash-based token mapping pipeline, complemented by gradient-indexed sparsification for memory optimization. The authors establish rigorous machine learning theoretical foundations for this approach, deriving generalization bounds via Rademacher complexity, proving score concentration under exponential $\alpha$-mixing to handle overlapping token dependencies, and demonstrating consistency of the maximum likelihood classifier. Empirical results on the CAMI II benchmark show competitive accuracy (85.1% F1) alongside a 4.2× runtime speedup and 68% memory reduction compared to state-of-the-art alignment-based and k-mer methods.

### Strengths
1. **Rigorous Experimental Design & Statistical Validation:** The evaluation adheres to high standards expected at ICLR. Experiments are run over 10 independent seeds with 95% bootstrap confidence intervals, Wilcoxon signed-rank tests with Holm-Bonferroni correction, and Cohen’s $d$ effect sizes (Section 5.3, Table 2). This robust statistical hygiene significantly strengthens confidence in the reported 4.2× speedup and near-parity accuracy claims.
2. **Sound Theoretical Treatment of Dependent Data:** The paper effectively bridges ML theory and genomics by modeling overlapping token extraction through a dependency graph and analyzing it via exponential $\alpha$-mixing. Lemma 7 provides explicit concentration inequalities with empirically validated decay constants ($C \approx 2.3, \gamma \approx 0.15$) (Appendix B.4, J), transparently quantifying how dependencies inflate variance rather than ignoring them.
3. **Transparent Reproducibility & Ablation Analysis:** The reproducibility statement is comprehensive. The paper provides exact hyperparameters ($V=32,000$, $\eta=1.8$, $\tau=0.8$, $\epsilon=10^{-6}$), detailed pseudocode (Algorithms 1-5), and hardware specifications. Systematic ablations (Table 3) successfully isolate component contributions, demonstrating near-additive gains (+6.8 pp from vocabulary, +1.9 pp from quality weighting), which clarifies the source of improvements.
4. **Practical Scalability & Engineering Awareness:** The inclusion of cache-miss analysis, gradient-based sparsification reducing index size from 21.3GB to 6.8GB with only 0.7% F1 drop (Table 1), and graceful scaling to 10,000 genomes without OOM (Table 4) demonstrates strong awareness of real-world deployment constraints, a valuable trait for ML-for-science submissions.

### Weaknesses
1. **Mismatch Between Theory and Implemented Algorithm:** The generalization bound (Theorem 6) analyzes a hypothesis class of linear classifiers with $\ell_{2,1}$ constraints ($H = \{h_W: W \in \mathbb{R}^{|Y|\times V}, \|W\|_{2,1} \leq B\}$). However, the actual HighClass scoring function is non-linear, involving quality-dependent power-law weighting, sparsification masks $w_t$, and log-odds aggregation (Definition 15, Eq 7). The theoretical bound thus applies to a surrogate linear model rather than the actual system, limiting its direct explanatory power for the empirical performance.
2. **Contradictory Complexity Claims:** The main text frames the $O(|T|)$ vs $O(m \log n + k \log k)$ reduction as a fundamental "computational paradigm" shift (Section 3.3, 1.3). However, Appendix I.1 explicitly states: *"The claimed $O(mn)$ complexity for alignment is pessimistic; modern aligners use indexing to achieve sublinear complexity in practice. Our speedup comes primarily from eliminating the constant factors... not from asymptotic improvements."* This internal contradiction overstates the theoretical novelty of the runtime claim.
3. **Limited Evaluation on Novel/Divergent Taxa & High-ANI Failure Rates:** Metagenomic classification critically depends on handling organisms absent from or poorly represented in reference databases. Section 5.4 references this challenge, but no experiments evaluate cross-dataset generalization, leave-one-clade-out performance, or novel strain detection. Error analysis (Section M.6) reveals 42% of failures stem from closely related species (ANI >95%), suggesting the token vocabulary lacks discriminative resolution for fine-grained phylogenetic boundaries, a common concern for alignment-free methods.
4. **Opaque Hyperparameter Optimization Protocol:** The paper frequently states that quality sensitivity $\eta \approx 1.8$ is "learned" and $\tau=0.8$ is "optimal" (Sections 3.4, M.3). However, the validation protocol, objective function used for hyperparameter search, and train/validation splits are not detailed. Section M.3 only presents a post-hoc sensitivity plateau analysis rather than the actual learning procedure.

### Novelty & Significance
**Novelty:** Moderate-to-High. The practical pipeline synthesizes three prior advances (QA-Token, MetaTrinity, gradient sparsification). The primary novel ML contribution is the rigorous theoretical characterization of token-based classification under genomic dependencies ($\alpha$-mixing analysis, explicit variance inflation) and the principled derivation of the generative model leading to quality-weighted scoring. While the tools are standard (Rademacher, Azuma-Hoeffding), their application to this dependent, sparse, variable-length token space with empirical constant validation is novel in the computational biology/ML intersection.

**Clarity:** High. The paper is exceptionally well-organized, with clear mathematical notation, precise algorithm pseudocode, and logical flow from problem formulation to theory to empirical validation. The separation of core claims in the main text and detailed proofs/complexity in the appendices is executed well.

**Reproducibility:** Very High. The explicit reporting of statistical methods, hardware, ablation configurations, hyperparameter values, and the commitment to release code, indices, and evaluation scripts meets and often exceeds ICLR's reproducibility expectations.

**Significance:** High. The work demonstrates how principled ML theory can guide the design of highly efficient, deployable bioinformatics tools. By showing that taxonomic classification can largely forego expensive positional alignment in favor of invariant token matching without significant accuracy loss, it offers a scalable solution for clinical and environmental metagenomics, a domain where compute cost and data velocity are critical bottlenecks.

### Suggestions for Improvement
1. **Align Theory with Implementation:** Either extend the generalization analysis to accommodate the non-linear, mask-weighted scoring function used in Algorithm 1, or explicitly clarify in the main text that Theorem 6 provides a worst-case bound for the underlying linear hypothesis class as a theoretical proxy. Discuss how the actual non-linear scoring interacts with or benefits from these bounds.
2. **Reframe Complexity Claims Accurately:** Update the introduction and conclusion to reflect the clarification in Appendix I.1. Emphasize that the primary contribution is a massive constant-factor reduction, superior cache locality, and index compression rather than asymptotic complexity class shifts. This will strengthen technical credibility and avoid reviewer pushback on alignment complexity baselines.
3. **Add Novelty/Generalization Experiments:** Include a cross-validation experiment where specific taxonomic clades are withheld from the reference database to test generalization to unseen organisms. Compare OOV token handling against Kraken2/Centrifuge. Even a simulated "novel strain" experiment using in-silico mutated reads would strengthen claims about practical robustness.
4. **Detail Hyperparameter Learning:** Provide a subsection describing exactly how $\eta$ and $\tau$ were optimized. Specify the loss function (e.g., validation F1, negative log-likelihood), the optimization strategy (grid search, Bayesian opt, gradient-based tuning), and the validation split used. This aligns the "learned" terminology with standard ML rigor.
5. **Contextualize Against Modern Sequence Models:** Briefly discuss how HighClass compares conceptually and empirically to recent sequence foundation models or embedding-based classifiers. Clarify why hash-based token lookup remains preferable or complementary to end-to-end neural approaches, particularly regarding interpretability, update cost, and memory constraints in large-scale reference databases.

# Spark Finder Review
## How to Improve This Paper

### Missing Experiments (top 3-5 only)
1. Compare against modern true SOTA (e.g., KRAKENUltra, KAIJU2, or Mash/ANI-based methods) rather than only older tools and MetaTrinity, to prove the 85.1% F1 actually represents state-of-the-art accuracy instead of merely closing the gap to a narrow, outdated selection.
2. Run gradient-based sparsification against a random region-dropping baseline to demonstrate the learned masks meaningfully outperform unstructured downsampling; otherwise, the claimed 68% memory reduction with 99.5% accuracy retention could trivially stem from data redundancy.
3. Evaluate on high-complexity, human-centric benchmarks (e.g., CAMI High-Complexity gut datasets or realistic clinical mixtures) because marine environments lack the dense, near-identical strain diversity needed to stress-test the position-invariant classification and validate "clinical viability" claims.

### Deeper Analysis Needed (top 3-5 only)
1. Explain the disconnect between the loose $O(\sqrt{V|Y|/n})$ Rademacher bound and tight empirical generalization by quantifying how token sparsity, taxonomic hierarchy, and pre-trained vocabulary priors tighten the effective hypothesis class complexity, or the theoretical contribution remains mathematically valid but practically vacuous.
2. Report False Discovery Rates (FDR) and abundance-class-stratified precision/recall instead of only global F1, because the 1.5% macro-F1 penalty could hide catastrophic false-positive inflation in rare or pathogenic taxa, directly invalidating the suitability of the method for sensitive applications.
3. Validate whether the global exponential $\alpha$-mixing constant ($\gamma \approx 0.15$) holds across functionally distinct genomic regions (e.g., conserved operons vs. hypervariable loci vs. horizontal gene transfer zones), since localized long-range dependencies would violate the independence-like assumptions required for the concentration guarantees to be trustworthy.

### Visualizations & Case Studies
1. Visualize empirical cache-miss rates, working-set sizes, and memory bandwidth utilization versus index size to prove the 4.2× speedup genuinely stems from $O(|T|)$ hash lookups and spatial locality rather than incidental hardware constant-factor optimizations or file-system I/O bottlenecks.
2. Provide case studies mapping specific variable-length tokens and quality weights to reads from >95% ANI strains to expose why position-invariant matching fails on subtle variants and confirm whether the QA-Token vocabulary actually captures biological discriminators or merely inflates match counts.
3. Plot the empirical autocorrelation decay and dependency degree distribution extracted from the token graphs across diverse taxa, allowing reviewers to visually verify the exponential mixing assumption rather than relying solely on reported aggregate constants.

### Obvious Next Steps
1. Release the exact mathematical derivation and code for the "gradient-based" importance scores immediately, because computing gradients in a non-parametric, discrete lookup pipeline with Laplace-smoothed counts is non-obvious, and omitting this violates core ICLR reproducibility standards.
2. Integrate a Lowest Common Ancestor (LCA) aggregation layer or abundance estimation module because isolated read-to-taxa assignments without phylogenetic regularization or count smoothing produce fragmented ecological profiles unusable for standard downstream metagenomic analysis.
3. Develop dynamic quality-threshold adaptation or error-tolerant token extraction for long-read data given the documented ~7-10% F1 collapse on Nanopore/PacBio, since static $\eta=1.8$ calibrated on Illumina distributions fundamentally mismatch high-indel-error sequencing profiles.

# Final Consolidated Review
## Summary
HighClass introduces a metagenomic classification pipeline that replaces computationally expensive seed-and-extend alignment with quality-aware, variable-length token hashing. Supported by a formal probabilistic derivation, the method employs a sparsified inverted index and a quality-weighted likelihood scoring function. Theoretical analysis provides generalization bounds, $\alpha$-mixing concentration inequalities for overlapping tokens, and consistency guarantees. Empirically, HighClass achieves 85.1% F1 on the CAMI II benchmark, delivering a 4.2× runtime speedup and 68% memory reduction compared to state-of-the-art baselines, with rigorous statistical validation and transparent ablations isolating component contributions.

## Strengths
- **Rigorous empirical validation and statistical hygiene:** The evaluation exceeds standard bioinformatics practices by reporting mean performance over 10 independent runs with 95% bootstrap confidence intervals, Wilcoxon signed-rank tests (Holm-Bonferroni corrected), and Cohen’s $d$ effect sizes (Section 5.3, Table 2). This robust statistical framework strongly substantiates the 4.2× speedup and near-parity accuracy claims.
- **Principled handling of sequential dependencies:** The explicit use of exponential $\alpha$-mixing theory to model overlapping variable-length tokens, complete with empirically validated decay constants ($C \approx 2.3, \gamma \approx 0.15$), provides a mathematically transparent account of how dependency-induced variance inflation is controlled (Section 4, Appendix B.4/J). This bridges a known gap in alignment-free token methods.
- **Transparent, component-isolating ablation design:** Systematic ablations (Table 3) clearly decompose performance gains, demonstrating that the QA-Token vocabulary alone contributes +6.8 percentage points over fixed $k$-mers, while quality weighting adds +1.9 points. This precise isolation of signal sources, combined with exhaustive reproducibility artifacts (exact hyperparameters, pseudocode, hardware specs), establishes high experimental clarity.

## Weaknesses
- **Internal contradiction in computational novelty claims:** The introduction and conclusions frame the shift to $O(|T|)$ token mapping as a fundamental algorithmic paradigm shift, implying asymptotic improvements over $O(m \log n + k \log k)$ alignment. However, Appendix I.1 explicitly states: *"Our speedup comes primarily from eliminating the constant factors... not from asymptotic improvements."* This overstatement in the main text misrepresents the theoretical nature of the efficiency contribution and should be corrected to emphasize constant-factor reductions and memory-hierarchy optimizations.
- **Disconnect between theoretical bounds and the implemented model:** Theorem 6 establishes uniform convergence for a hypothesis class of linear classifiers with $\ell_{2,1}$ norm constraints. The actual HighClass scoring function (Definition 15, Eq 7) is explicitly non-linear, incorporating quality-dependent power-law weighting, gradient-derived sparsification masks, and log-odds aggregation. The theoretical bound therefore applies to a surrogate linear proxy rather than the deployed system, limiting its explanatory power for the observed empirical generalization.
- **Misleading "learned" terminology and underspecified sparsification objective:** The paper repeatedly describes the quality sensitivity $\eta=1.8$ as "learned" and the index compression as "gradient-based sparsification." However, the methodology sections and Appendices E/M reveal no surrogate loss function, training protocol, or gradient computation graph; $\eta$ is selected via post-hoc sensitivity plateau analysis, and the masks are pre-computed or adopted from prior work. This obscures the algorithmic contribution and violates standard ML definitions of "learned" components.
- **Unsubstantiated claims regarding novel/divergent taxon generalization:** The introduction explicitly lists "novel organisms absent from the reference" as a key motivation, yet the evaluation is strictly confined to CAMI II simulated reads from known reference genomes. Furthermore, the error analysis (Section M.6) reveals that 42% of misclassifications stem from closely related strains (ANI >95%), indicating the vocabulary struggles precisely where generalization to unseen or divergent taxa is most critical. Without cross-clade or leave-one-out experiments, this foundational claim remains unverified.

## Nice-to-Haves
- Quantify and report index construction time alongside query latency. In clinical/environmental metagenomics, reference databases are updated frequently; the total cost-of-ownership (build + query) would clarify the method's practical utility in dynamic pipelines.
- Compare the gradient-based sparsification against a naive random region-dropping baseline at identical retention ratios. This would confirm that the compressed index preserves biologically discriminative signal rather than relying on redundant genomic structure.
- Provide abundance-stratified precision/recall or false discovery rates per taxonomic rank. The 1.5% global F1 drop could mask localized inflation in false positives for low-abundance or clinically critical pathogen reads.

## Novel Insights
The paper's most compelling contribution lies in its conceptual reframing of taxonomic classification: precise positional alignment is computationally dominant but phylogenetically redundant for read-level assignment. By formalizing sequences as quality-weighted, variable-length token bags, HighClass demonstrates that position-invariant matching preserves discriminative signal while unlocking hardware-friendly $O(1)$ hash lookups and compressed working sets. The explicit acknowledgment and mathematical treatment of token overlap via $\alpha$-mixing dependencies provides a principled justification for why this aggressive simplification doesn't collapse the decision boundary, effectively trading exact alignment heuristics for statistically grounded, cache-optimized inference.

## Suggestions
- Reconcile the complexity narrative in the main text to accurately reflect Appendix I.1: explicitly state that the 4.2× speedup stems from constant-factor elimination, superior cache locality, and index compression, rather than asymptotic complexity class shifts.
- Clarify the relationship between the linear Rademacher bound (Theorem 6) and the non-linear, mask-weighted scoring function actually deployed, or provide an extended analysis showing how the log-odds aggregation and quality weighting remain within a bounded deviation from the linear proxy.
- Replace "learned" with precise terminology (e.g., "pre-computed," "grid-tuned," or "validated") for $\eta$ and the sparsification masks, and explicitly define the surrogate objective or validation metric used to select these values.
- Add an experimental evaluation withholding at least one major clade from the reference index to test generalization to novel/divergent taxa, and report the wall-clock index construction cost to contextualize the method's viability for dynamically updated databases.
