# Harsh Critic Review
## Section-by-Section Critical Review

### Title & Abstract
- **Accuracy:** The title accurately reflects the core technical contributions: efficient classification, quality-aware token mapping, and sparsified indexing. 
- **Clarity:** The abstract clearly delineates the problem (accuracy-efficiency trade-off), method (hash-based token mapping, variable-length tokens, learned sensitivity, sparsification), and key results (85.1% F1, 4.2× speedup, theoretical bounds).
- **Claims vs. Support:** The claim of "transform[ing] the computational paradigm" is strong but defensible given the shift from alignment to token indexing. However, the abstract states a generalization convergence rate of $O(\sqrt{V|Y|/n})$. This notation is inconsistent with the actual bound derived in **Theorem 6 (Appendix B.5)**, which yields $\mathcal{R}_n(\mathcal{H}) \leq B\sqrt{\frac{V \log(2n|Y|)}{n}}$. The dependence on $|Y|$ is logarithmic inside the square root in the proof, not linear as implied in the abstract. This scaling discrepancy overstates the sample complexity benefit of the taxonomic class count and must be reconciled.

### Introduction & Motivation
- **Motivation & Gap:** The alignment vs. alignment-free dichotomy is well-motivated. The authors correctly identify that fixed k-mer methods lose accuracy on quality-heterogeneous reads and subtle variations, while alignment is computationally prohibitive at scale. The gap is clearly defined.
- **Contributions:** The three contributions (Theoretical, Algorithmic, Empirical) are explicit and match the paper content.
- **Claims:** The assertion that "positional information is largely unnecessary for taxonomic classification in practice" (**Section 1.2, Section 6.2**) is a strong claim that partially relies on the 1.5% accuracy drop vs. MetaTrinity. While the empirical result supports it for the tested benchmarks, the introduction frames this as a near-universal finding rather than an empirical observation on the CAMI II/Standard datasets. The introduction should temper this to reflect that position-invariance works well *under the tested conditions* but may fail for tasks requiring precise strain resolution or structural variant detection.

### Method / Approach
- **Description & Reproducibility:** The method is largely clear. The pipeline (Token Extraction → Hash Lookup → Candidate Identification → Refined Scoring) is well-structured and formalized in Algorithms 1-5. Hyperparameters ($|V|=32,000$, $\eta=1.8$, $\epsilon=10^{-6}$, sparsification 32%) are reproducible due to the Reproducibility Statement and public component references.
- **Assumptions:** The method assumes exponential $\alpha$-mixing for token dependencies. The authors validate this empirically (**Appendix J**) by fitting autocorrelation decay. However, fitting an exponential curve to empirical autocorrelation does not rigorously prove the $\alpha$-mixing condition holds for all genomic structures, particularly in the presence of long repetitive elements, transposons, or horizontal gene transfer sequences which can induce long-range dependencies. The assumption is plausible for CAMI II but stated as a guarantee without theoretical verification for worst-case genomic architectures.
- **Logical Gaps & Definitions:** There is a critical inconsistency regarding **sparsification**. **Section 2.1** and **Section 5.2** describe "gradient-based importance scoring" that computes "gradient magnitudes with respect to classification objectives." However, **Algorithm 6 (Appendix I.2)** defines the importance gain as $\Delta J(t) = I(t; Y) - \lambda H(t|Q)$, which is based on mutual information and entropy, not gradients. If the method uses mutual information, the "gradient-based" terminology is incorrect and misleading. If actual gradients of a surrogate model are used, the surrogate and differentiation target are undefined. This must be clarified as it impacts reproducibility and theoretical interpretation.
- **Edge Cases:** The method handles low-quality regions via thresholding ($\bar{q}(t) \geq \tau$) and sparsification. The authors correctly note that hash-based matching relies on the vocabulary covering the reads. With $|V|=32,000$ and 99.8% coverage, ~0.2% of reads may suffer out-of-vocabulary decay, which is acknowledged but could be quantified in the error analysis.
- **Theoretical Proofs:** The proofs for Theorem 6 and Lemma 7 follow standard Rademacher complexity and blocking/martingale arguments for dependent sequences. The logic is sound, provided the assumptions (bounded weights, mixing coefficients) hold. The complexity reduction claim of $O(|T|)$ assumes $O(1)$ average-case hash lookups. While practical with Robin Hood hashing (**Appendix K.2**), worst-case complexity degrades if posting lists are large or hash collisions spike. The theoretical section should explicitly state this is expected/average-case complexity.

### Experiments & Results
- **Testing Claims:** The experiments directly address the central claims. The speedup, memory reduction, and F1 retention are tested against strong baselines (MetaTrinity, Kraken2, Centrifuge, CLARK, Bracken).
- **Baselines:** The selection is appropriate. Comparing against MetaTrinity (seed-and-extend SOTA) and Kraken2 (k-mer SOTA) covers both paradigms. Missing explicit comparison to other *adaptive* tokenization schemes (e.g., context-aware BPE without quality) limits the isolation of the "quality-aware" benefit, though Table 3 attempts this.
- **Ablations:** Table 3 is strong, showing additive contributions. However, an ablation of the *sparsification ratio* is missing. The paper fixes it at 32% but only mentions "retaining 32% preserves 99.5% relative accuracy" in **Section 5.4.3**. A curve showing F1 vs. Memory for ratios from 10% to 100% would validate the "gradient-based importance" claim and show the Pareto frontier more rigorously.
- **Statistical Rigor:** The use of 10 independent runs, bootstrap confidence intervals, Wilcoxon tests, and Cohen’s $d$ is excellent and meets high ICLR standards. The reporting of both raw F1 drop and F1/hour improvement is transparent.
- **Consistency of Metrics:** There is a minor discrepancy in runtime reporting. **Table 2** reports 0.5 hours total runtime for CAMI II, while **Table 1/5** reports ~2.1-2.3 ms/read. For 0.5h = 1.8M ms, this implies ~780k-850k reads. If the full CAMI II Marine dataset is significantly larger, these per-read numbers likely reflect single-thread active compute time, while the wall-clock time reflects multi-threaded processing (48 cores). The authors should explicitly clarify if "ms/read" is wall-clock or CPU-time, and confirm dataset sizes to ensure the 4.2× speedup isn't an artifact of I/O bottlenecks or thread scaling differences between implementations.
- **Cherry-picking/Support:** Results are not cherry-picked; the paper honestly reports the 1.5% accuracy penalty and slight FDR increase (0.048 vs 0.043). The "3.8× improvement" in **Section 5.5** conservatively accounts for this, which is good practice.

### Writing & Clarity
- **Confusing Sections:** The derivation of the quality scoring function in **Section 3.4** contains garbled equations (parser artifact noted, but the surrounding text should define the quality weight clearly). More importantly, **Section 2.1** claims "Gradient-based Sparsification" but the algorithm uses Mutual Information (**Algorithm 6**). This terminology mismatch significantly impedes understanding and must be corrected.
- **Figures/Tables:** Tables are clear and informative. **Table 3** effectively isolates component contributions. **Table 6** (F1/hour) is a useful synthesis metric.
- **Structure:** The heavy reliance on appendices for proofs and algorithmic details is appropriate for a long paper. The flow from problem formulation to theory to experiments is logical.

### Limitations & Broader Impact
- **Acknowledged Limitations:** The authors discuss accuracy loss, hyperparameter sensitivity, and specific failure modes in **Section M.6** (closely related species, low-quality reads, conserved regions). This is good.
- **Missed Limitations:**
  1. **Rare Taxa & Sparsification:** The sparsification retains 32% of genomic regions. Does this disproportionately affect rare taxa in the database? If sparsification removes regions unique to low-abundance organisms, the classifier's ability to detect them in real samples may be impaired. An analysis of sparsification impact on *low-frequency* taxa recall is missing.
  2. **Vocabulary Generalization:** HighClass relies entirely on a pre-trained QA-Token vocabulary (**Gollwitzer et al., 2025**). If the test data comes from a radically different sequencing technology, error profile, or genomic domain (e.g., viral vs. bacterial), the fixed vocabulary and fixed $\eta=1.8$ may not generalize. The paper does not evaluate vocabulary robustness to domain shift.
  3. **Theoretical vs. Empirical Gap:** The theory assumes exponential mixing and bounded emission probabilities. In highly conserved regions or datasets with massive strain diversity, the identifiability condition (KL divergence $> \delta$) may be violated more severely than the model accounts for.
- **Broader Impact:** The potential for clinical diagnostics and surveillance is highlighted. A brief mention of the risk associated with the observed FDR increase (0.048 vs 0.043) in clinical settings would be responsible, as false positives/negatives in metagenomic diagnosis have direct patient implications.

### Overall Assessment
HighClass is a well-engineered, empirically rigorous submission that successfully bridges the gap between alignment-free speed and alignment-based accuracy by integrating quality-aware variable-length tokens and sparsified indexing. The paper stands out for its comprehensive ablation studies, robust statistical validation, and transparent reporting of trade-offs (F1 cost vs. compute gain). However, several critical issues must be addressed before acceptance: (1) the contradiction between "gradient-based" sparsification terminology and the mutual-information objective in Algorithm 6; (2) the discrepancy between the stated generalization bound $O(\sqrt{V|Y|/n})$ and the derived bound involving $\log(|Y|)$; (3) the need to clarify sparsification's impact on rare taxa detection; and (4) the clarification of runtime metrics (wall-clock vs. active compute time) to verify scalability claims. Addressing these will significantly strengthen the paper's reproducibility, theoretical consistency, and practical relevance. The contribution is solid and aligns well with ICLR's appreciation for principled method integration and thorough empirical validation, pending these clarifications.

# Neutral Reviewer
## Balanced Review

### Summary
This paper introduces HighClass, a metagenomic classification framework that replaces traditional sequence alignment with a hash-based, quality-aware token mapping pipeline using learned variable-length vocabularies and gradient-based index sparsification. The authors develop a theoretical foundation addressing overlapping token dependencies via exponential α-mixing, providing generalization bounds, concentration inequalities, and consistency guarantees. Empirically, HighClass achieves 85.1% F1 on the CAMI II benchmark, delivering a 4.2× runtime speedup and 68% memory reduction compared to state-of-the-art alignment-based baselines.

### Strengths
1. **Rigorous statistical validation:** The experimental protocol adheres to best practices with 10 independent runs, 95% bootstrap confidence intervals, Wilcoxon signed-rank tests with Holm-Bonferroni correction, and Cohen’s *d* effect sizes, providing statistically robust support for the claimed 4.2× speedup and minimal accuracy degradation (1.5 pp vs. MetaTrinity, Table 2).
2. **Well-motivated dependency modeling:** The application of exponential α-mixing theory to quantify token overlap dependencies is theoretically sound and empirically grounded, with validated decay constants (*γ* ≈ 0.15, *C* ≈ 2.3). The derived concentration inequality explicitly bounds variance inflation (≈31.7×), offering a principled explanation for why large vocabularies (32,000 tokens) maintain classification stability despite non-i.i.d. token extraction.
3. **Clear architectural ablation:** The component-wise breakdown isolates the impact of each design choice (e.g., variable-length tokens contribute +6.8 pp over fixed *k*-mers, quality weighting adds +1.9 pp, sparsification reduces index to 6.8 GB with only -0.7 pp accuracy loss). The near-additive nature of these contributions validates the modular design and supports practical deployment.

### Weaknesses
1. **Loose theoretical bounds with limited actionable insight:** While the Rademacher complexity bound (*O*(√*V*|*Y*|/*n*)) and α-mixing concentration are mathematically correct, they appear practically loose. The variance inflation factor of ~31.7 and the 0.021 excess risk bound do not tightly inform architectural decisions (e.g., why *V*=32,000 or sparsification ratio=32% are optimal) nor clearly predict the empirical 1.5 pp accuracy gap. The theory serves more as a justification than a predictive design tool.
2. **Narrow benchmark scope and baseline selection:** The primary accuracy-speed comparison focuses heavily on MetaTrinity, which is from the same research lineage and cited extensively. Broader evaluation against recent deep learning-based classifiers or diverse real-world microbiomes (beyond CAMI II Marine) would strengthen claims of generalizability. Additionally, long-read performance (PacBio/Nanopore) drops sharply (~10 pp F1, Table 8), indicating sensitivity to high-error sequencing technologies not fully addressed theoretically or empirically.
3. **Conflation of algorithmic complexity and implementation speedups:** The claimed *O*(|*T*|) complexity improvement over alignment is partially attributable to systems-level optimizations (Robin Hood hashing, AVX2 vectorization, cache locality via index compression) rather than purely algorithmic novelty. The paper does not cleanly disentangle asymptotic complexity gains from low-level engineering speedups, which is critical for fair methodological comparison at an ML-focused venue.

### Novelty & Significance
**Novelty:** High for the intersection of computational biology and machine learning, specifically in replacing alignment-centric pipelines with quality-aware token mapping under a unified dependent-learning framework. The theoretical adaptation of α-mixing to overlapping genomic tokens is a meaningful contribution, though it relies on established statistical learning machinery rather than introducing new theoretical paradigms. **Clarity:** The manuscript is well-structured with a logical progression from problem formulation to theory, implementation, and evaluation. Some claims are repetitive across sections, and the theory-to-practice bridge could be tightened, but the mathematical framework and algorithms are clearly specified. **Reproducibility:** Strong. The paper provides exact hyperparameters, standardized CAMI II protocols, 10-seed averaging, promised full code/indices release, and detailed implementation appendices (Appendix E–K). This aligns well with ICLR’s open science expectations. **Significance:** High for practical metagenomics and edge/clinical deployment, where memory and latency directly dictate usability. For ICLR specifically, the paper’s significance lies in its rigorous validation of learned tokenization under dependency structures, though broader impact on core ML theory or architecture design remains incremental.

### Suggestions for Improvement
1. **Decouple algorithmic and systems-level contributions:** Run ablations comparing a naive Python/standard-library implementation of HighClass against optimized alignment baselines to isolate the true algorithmic speedup attributable to *O*(|*T*|) token mapping versus cache/SIMD optimization. This will clarify the method's algorithmic novelty.
2. **Strengthen theory-practice linkage and long-read adaptation:** Extend the theoretical analysis to explicitly bound how vocabulary size *V* or quality threshold τ should scale with sequencing error rates. Include a dedicated subsection or supplementary experiment testing quality-adaptive token extraction or error-recovery strategies to mitigate the >10% F1 drop observed on Nanopore/PacBio data.
3. **Expand baseline comparisons and dataset diversity:** Benchmark against recent ML-driven alignment-free methods or deep metagenomic classifiers on additional standard datasets (e.g., CAMI synthetic gut/soil, mock communities with known strain abundances). Discuss how the framework integrates with or compares to foundation-model tokenizers in genomics to situate the work within the broader ML landscape.

# Spark Finder Review
## How to Improve This Paper

### Missing Experiments (top 3-5 only)
1. Report results on the CAMI II Strain, HMP Mock, and Zymo benchmarks (listed in Sec 5.3 but missing from tables), because species-level marine performance alone does not prove the claimed near-parity to SOTA generalizes across strain-level divergence or known abundance profiles.
2. Include end-to-end index/database construction and preprocessing times, because replacing alignment with token mapping shifts heavy overhead offline, and without construction metrics, the 4.2× query speedup fails to validate real-world pipeline efficiency.
3. Compare against optimized abundance-aware k-mer tools (e.g., Bracken, KrakenUniq) and modern ML metagenomic classifiers, because benchmarking only against seed-and-extend aligners does not prove HighClass sits on a true Pareto frontier rather than an artifact of unoptimized baselines.
4. Conduct cross-dataset transfer tests where sparsification masks or emission probabilities trained on one benchmark are evaluated on a held-out dataset, to prove the learned components do not overfit CAMI II simulation artifacts and handle real distribution shifts.

### Deeper Analysis Needed (top 3-5 only)
1. Re-derive the generalization bound to explicitly incorporate the mixing-dependent variance inflation factor (σ²_eff ≈ 31.7), because the current i.i.d.-style Rademacher complexity decouples the proven token dependency structure from the stated O(√(V|Y|/n)) convergence guarantee, leaving the theoretical contribution unanchored to the actual data distribution.
2. Analyze how the exponential α-mixing assumption (γ ≈ 0.15) scales in taxa with long homologous repeats or frequent horizontal gene transfer, because strong localized dependencies would violate the uniform concentration arguments used to justify the sufficiency of the 32,000-token vocabulary.
3. Quantify the phylogenetic enrichment of the 32% of genomic regions retained after gradient sparsification (e.g., single-copy marker gene density vs. random background), because without biological grounding, it is unclear whether the method preserves meaningful taxonomic signals or simply memorizes high-gradient, non-informative regions.
4. Compute and report Expected Calibration Error (ECE) across varying quality score distributions, because the maximum likelihood objective depends critically on Q-score reliability modeling, and systemic miscalibration would directly undermine the claimed quality-aware scoring optimality.

### Visualizations & Case Studies
1. Provide a taxonomic confusion matrix or hierarchical error tree showing exactly where the 1.5% F1 deficit versus MetaTrinity occurs, to reveal whether misclassifications concentrate in conserved homologous clades or in low-quality segments where token extraction fails.
2. Render genomic coverage tracks contrasting the sparsified index regions against standard reference alignments/exhaustive k-mer maps, to expose whether gradient pruning disproportionately removes conserved marker regions necessary for fine-grained taxonomy assignment.
3. Plot per-read confidence and score distributions across increasing simulated error rates (1%, 5%, 15%) to demonstrate whether the quality-aware weighting gracefully attenuates noise or collapses catastrophically when observation reliability drops below the learned threshold.

### Obvious Next Steps
1. Report full sensitivity landscapes for vocabulary size |V| and quality sensitivity η across multiple benchmarks instead of single-point estimates, because static optimal values without performance gradients strongly suggest hyperparameter overfitting rather than robust architectural design.
2. Evaluate on real, non-synthetic clinical or environmental cohorts with orthogonal ground-truth validation (e.g., spike-ins, qPCR, or curated isolate genomes), because simulated benchmarks lack true host contamination, unknown taxa dropout, and uneven coverage distributions that break theoretical identifiability assumptions.
3. Specify and benchmark worst-case hash collision handling and posting list expansion strategies for repetitive genomic segments, because theoretical O(|T|) assumes bounded retrieval costs and pathological collision rates in high-homology regions would invalidate the claimed linear query complexity.
4. Analyze failure modes explicitly on chimeric reads and horizontal gene transfer junctions, because the additive token evidence model assumes a single taxonomic origin and fundamentally breaks when individual reads contain mixed phylogenetic signals not captured by the generative process.

# Final Consolidated Review
## Summary
The paper introduces HighClass, a metagenomic classifier that replaces traditional sequence alignment with a hash-based pipeline using quality-aware, variable-length tokens and learned sparsification of the reference index. It provides a theoretical analysis of token dependencies via exponential $\alpha$-mixing, deriving generalization bounds, concentration inequalities, and consistency guarantees. Empirically, HighClass achieves near-SOTA accuracy (85.1% F1) on CAMI II with a 4.2× runtime speedup and 68% memory reduction over alignment-heavy baselines.

## Strengths
- **Rigorous statistical validation and transparent ablation:** The evaluation protocol exceeds standard practices for computational biology, employing 10 independent runs, 95% bootstrap CIs, Wilcoxon tests with Holm-Bonferroni correction, and Cohen’s $d$ effect sizes. Table 3 effectively isolates component contributions (variable-length tokens +6.8 pp, quality weighting +1.9 pp, sparsification -0.7 pp), with interaction effects <0.5 pp, lending strong credibility to the empirical claims.
- **Clear architectural decoupling of training and inference:** By leveraging a fixed, pre-trained vocabulary and pre-computed token-to-taxon inverted indices, the framework shifts heavy computation offline. This design eliminates online seed-and-extend operations, yielding substantial memory and latency reductions suitable for clinical or edge deployment.
- **Principled treatment of dependent token sequences:** Applying exponential $\alpha$-mixing theory to overlapping genomic tokens is a meaningful adaptation of statistical learning machinery to bioinformatics. The explicit derivation of concentration bounds with empirically validated mixing constants ($C \approx 2.3, \gamma \approx 0.15$) provides a formal justification for why large vocabularies remain stable despite non-i.i.d. token extraction.

## Weaknesses
- **Critical contradiction in sparsification methodology:** The manuscript repeatedly claims to use "gradient-based importance scoring" and "gradient magnitudes with respect to classification objectives" (Sec 2.1, Sec 5.2, Abstract) to select which genomic regions to retain. However, Appendix I.2 and Algorithm 6 explicitly implement a mutual information and conditional entropy objective: $\Delta J(t) = I(t; Y) - \lambda H(t|Q)$. No gradients, surrogate models, or differentiable objectives are defined or computed anywhere in the text. This fundamental terminological and methodological mismatch misrepresents how the index is pruned, severely undermining reproducibility and theoretical transparency.
- **Theory-practice misalignment and overstated complexity claims:** The theoretical analysis is presented as a design foundation, but in practice it functions as a post-hoc justification. Key hyperparameters ($|V|=32,000$, sparsification ratio $=32\%$, quality sensitivity $\eta=1.8$) are fixed empirically without theoretical prediction. Furthermore, the stated convergence rate $O(\sqrt{V|Y|/n})$ in the abstract/main text contradicts the proof of Theorem 6, which contains $\log(|Y|)$ inside the square root. Additionally, Appendix I.1 explicitly concedes that the empirical speedup stems from constant-factor elimination and cache efficiency, not asymptotic improvements, contradicting the $O(|T|)$ vs alignment novelty framing in Sections 1 & 6. At an ML venue claiming theoretical contributions, this gap between loose, non-predictive bounds and empirical system design is a substantive flaw.
- **Incomplete benchmark coverage and narrow evaluation scope:** Section 5.3 lists evaluation on CAMI II Strain, HMP Mock, and Zymo Standards, yet all main result tables report only CAMI II Marine performance. Without these results, claims of near-parity to SOTA are unverified across strain-level divergence and known abundance profiles. Moreover, Table 8 shows a severe ~9 pp F1 degradation on PacBio and Nanopore long-read data, indicating high sensitivity to elevated error profiles that are neither theoretically bounded nor algorithmically mitigated. The heavy reliance on MetaTrinity (from the same author lineage) as the primary accuracy baseline further weakens generalizability claims.

## Nice-to-Haves
- Report Expected Calibration Error (ECE) and provide a taxonomic confusion matrix to localize where the 1.5 pp accuracy deficit concentrates (e.g., conserved clades vs. low-quality segments).
- Quantify whether the 68% sparsification disproportionately removes markers for low-abundance taxa, potentially biasing recall in complex microbiomes.
- Clarify wall-clock vs. multi-threaded CPU time metrics, and explicitly analyze worst-case hash collision/posting-list expansion behavior for highly repetitive genomic regions.

## Novel Insights
The paper convincingly argues that precise positional alignment is largely unnecessary for coarse-grained taxonomic classification, demonstrating that position-invariant, quality-weighted token matching can recover nearly all discriminative signal while bypassing $O(m \log n)$ seed-and-extend bottlenecks. By framing sequencing quality not as a post-hoc filter but as an integrated exponent in the likelihood scoring ($\eta \approx 1.8$), the method adapts evidence aggregation directly to observation reliability. The modular, pre-computed index architecture effectively decouples the expensive vocabulary learning and database scanning from inference, suggesting a viable paradigm for scalable metagenomic analysis where theoretical guarantees on token dependencies ensure stability despite overlapping extraction windows.

## Potentially Missed Related Work
- **Recent genomic foundation models (e.g., Nucleotide Transformer, DNABERT-2, HyenaDNA):** The paper contrasts itself with deep encoders that use tokens as features, but does not benchmark against modern sequence language models fine-tuned for taxonomic classification, which increasingly offer competitive alignment-free accuracy.
- **Abundance-aware post-processors (e.g., Bracken, KrakenUniq):** Since HighClass reports species-level FDR and accuracy, comparing against tools that explicitly model relative abundance from k-mer counts would better situate its calibration and false-positive behavior on the Pareto frontier.

## Suggestions
- **Resolve the sparsification contradiction immediately:** Either correct the terminology throughout to reflect that the method uses mutual information/entropy-based pruning, or replace Algorithm 6 with the actual gradient computation pipeline, specifying the differentiable surrogate, loss function, and optimization procedure. Provide the exact code for mask generation to guarantee reproducibility.
- **Strengthen the theory-to-design bridge:** Instead of using Rademacher complexity and $\alpha$-mixing solely for post-hoc validation, explicitly derive how the variance inflation factor $\sigma^2_{\text{eff}} \approx 31.7$ informs practical thresholds. For example, derive a theoretically grounded lower bound for the sparsification retention ratio or quality threshold $\tau$ given target generalization error $\epsilon$, closing the loop between the stated bounds and the empirically chosen hyperparameters.
- **Complete the evaluation matrix:** Report results for CAMI II Strain, HMP Mock, and Zymo datasets as promised in Sec 5.3. Include an analysis or ablation for long-read pipelines (e.g., adaptive quality thresholds $\tau$ or error-robust token extraction) to address the ~9 pp F1 collapse on high-error sequencing platforms.
