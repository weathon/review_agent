# Harsh Critic Review
## Section-by-Section Critical Review

### Title & Abstract
- **Title Accuracy:** The title accurately reflects the paper’s scope: investigating adversarial robustness in LLM-based MAS within engineering contexts.
- **Abstract Clarity:** The abstract clearly outlines the problem (adversarial perturbations in engineering MAS can cause unsafe/incorrect numerical results), the method (controlled adversarial influence across four problem types varying prompts, task complexity, and communication order), and key findings (robustness depends on prompt framing, talking order, and error subtlety).
- **Unsupported Claims:** The claim of presenting the *"first systematic study of adversarial robustness of LLM-based MAS in engineering contexts"* is borderline. While the engineering focus is novel in its exact formulation, prior MAS robustness works (e.g., Huang et al., 2025a; Amayuelas et al., 2024; Ju et al., 2024) already systematically analyze misinformation propagation, order effects, and consensus failures. The abstract should clarify that the novelty lies specifically in *domain-grounded numerical/formal tasks* rather than being the first to study MAS adversarial robustness broadly.

### Introduction & Motivation
- **Motivation & Gap:** The motivation is well-grounded in the practical push toward agentic engineering workflows. The gap is identified as a lack of understanding of how agent prompting and communication structure jointly affect robustness in tasks requiring formal rigor. However, the introduction somewhat assumes engineering tasks are uniquely vulnerable without providing preliminary evidence or a theoretical mechanism explaining *why* domain-specific formalism interacts differently with MAS communication compared to, say, code generation or math reasoning (which are also heavily studied for LLM robustness).
- **Contributions:** The contributions are clearly stated: systematic evaluation across task types, quantification of error propagation under different prompts/orders, and identification of resilient design choices.
- **Over/Under-claiming:** The introduction carefully avoids extreme over-claims, but it under-theorizes the core research question. It frames the study primarily as an empirical survey of prompt/config variations rather than probing a deeper mechanistic hypothesis about why MAS collaboration fails under adversarial pressure in structured domains. For ICLR, positioning this as a systematic ablation study is fine, but the introduction should better articulate what fundamental principle of multi-agent robustness this paper aims to uncover.

### Method / Approach
- **Clarity & Reproducibility:** The method is described with sufficient detail for reproduction. The hierarchical 2-agent setup, turn-based communication, max 5 iterations, and "rethinking phase" are clearly outlined. Prompts and experimental configurations are thoroughly documented in the appendix.
- **Key Assumptions:** The approach assumes a *static, non-adaptive adversary*. The misleading advisor always proposes the same incorrect formula or value regardless of the leader’s counter-arguments or intermediate calculations. This is a significant limitation for an adversarial study, as real-world misaligned or adversarial agents can adapt their arguments, escalate persuasion tactics, or strategically pivot when confronted.
- **Logical Gaps & Edge Cases:** The "rethinking phase" is mentioned in Section 3.1 but its exact prompt, trigger conditions, and whether it includes the advisor in the loop are not rigorously specified. Does it run unconditionally after a preliminary decision? If so, it acts as a built-in verifier, yet its effectiveness is never isolated as an ablation. Additionally, the scaling of the turn-based protocol to multiple agents (Section 4.3) is not fully explained: how are rounds structured when there are 3+ advisors? Is it strictly sequential M→S→M, or do advisors speak simultaneously? The interaction topology for >2 agents lacks formal definition.
- **Theoretical Claims:** The paper is empirical and makes no theoretical proofs, which is acceptable. However, it misses the opportunity to model the interaction as a Markov decision process or influence propagation graph, which would strengthen the methodological depth.

### Experiments & Results
- **Testing Claims:** The experiments directly test the stated claims regarding prompt sensitivity, task complexity, communication order, and personalization. The TVD-based trial sizing (N=30) and use of Fisher’s Exact / Mann-Whitney U tests demonstrate statistical rigor.
- **Baselines & Comparisons:** The primary baseline is the paper’s own default configuration. There is a notable absence of comparison to established robust MAS paradigms (e.g., structured debate, self-consistency voting, or external verifier/critic agents). Without these, it’s unclear if the observed improvements (e.g., non-concise prompts or specific talking orders) outperform standard, literature-backed defense strategies.
- **Missing Ablations & Model Dependency:** **This is the most critical concern.** Appendix Table E.7 and Figure 7 reveal that stronger models (GPT-4o, o3-mini) achieve ~96–100% rejection rates with near-0% misleading rates. This strongly suggests the observed vulnerabilities and prompt-sensitivity are largely artifacts of using a weaker base model (GPT-4o mini) rather than fundamental properties of MAS architecture. The paper does not sufficiently analyze this confounder. If robustness scales trivially with model capability, the practical value of prompt engineering and order manipulation diminishes significantly.
- **Error Bars & Statistical Reporting:** While p-values are provided in Appendix Tables E.6–E.15, the main figures (Figures 3–6) completely omit error bars or confidence intervals. For ICLR, visual results should convey uncertainty directly, especially given that 30 trials per condition still yield non-negligible variance for binary outcomes (as acknowledged in the wide CIs in Appendix B).
- **Results Support & Cherry-picking:** The results generally support the claims, but some interpretations are hand-wavy. In Section 4.3, the surprising resilience of the "MM" (two misleading agents) configuration is attributed to the hypothesis that they *"support each other too obviously,"* but no qualitative conversation analysis or statistical interaction test is provided to validate this. Similarly, the trade-off between robustness and efficiency (high "no decision" rates in larger groups) is noted but not quantitatively optimized.
- **Datasets & Metrics:** Synthetic, well-defined engineering problems are appropriate for controlled evaluation. Metrics (misled/rejection/no-decision rates, correctness) are standard and well-chosen. However, treating "no decision" purely as an efficiency metric overlooks its safety implication: in engineering, a system that refuses to decide may be safer than a misled one, but the paper does not frame this trade-off in a safety-critical context.

### Writing & Clarity
- **Confusing Sections:** The methodology for scaling to multiple agents lacks clarity regarding round-robin scheduling and turn limits. Additionally, the "rethinking phase" mechanism is underspecified. The analysis in Section 4.3 oscillates between reporting results and speculating without clear demarcation.
- **Figures & Tables:** Figures 3–6 are informative but visually incomplete without error bars. Figure 1’s caption is slightly disjointed due to parser artifacts, but the logic flow chart is clear once read. The appendix tables (E.5–E.15) are comprehensive but dense; a summary visualization of effect sizes across configurations would greatly improve interpretability.

### Limitations & Broader Impact
- **Acknowledged Limitations:** The authors correctly note the combinatorial explosion of prompt combinations, non-linear agent behaviors, and the approximate nature of their findings.
- **Missed Fundamental Limitations:** 
  1. **Static Threat Model:** The adversary does not adapt to the leader’s reasoning, limiting real-world applicability.
  2. **Model Capability Confounding:** The results are highly sensitive to the underlying LLM’s reasoning strength, which is not integrated into the paper’s core narrative or limitations discussion.
  3. **Narrow Definition of Adversary:** Only semantic/numerical misdirection via system prompts is tested. Tool-use misuse, code injection, or environmental feedback manipulation are ignored.
- **Broader Impact / Safety:** The ethics statement is minimal but appropriate given the synthetic setup. The paper misses a deeper discussion on deployment risks: e.g., how findings apply to human-in-the-loop engineering vs. fully autonomous pipelines, or how adversarial robustness metrics should be standardized for safety certification in engineering AI.

### Overall Assessment
This paper presents a thorough empirical ablation study on how prompt design, communication order, and task complexity affect the robustness of LLM-based multi-agent systems in engineered numerical tasks. The statistical methodology is sound, and the identification of a strong "first-mover effect" and the critical role of explicit warnings are valuable practical insights. However, the contribution is significantly weakened by a core confounder: vulnerability largely disappears when using stronger base models (o3-mini, GPT-4o), suggesting the findings are more about model capability than fundamental MAS architecture flaws. The static, non-adaptive adversary further limits the adversarial rigor expected at top-tier ML venues. Additionally, the lack of comparison to established robust collaboration paradigms (debate, voting, verification) and the omission of error bars in main figures impede rapid assessment. To meet ICLR’s acceptance bar, the authors should (1) rigorously analyze the model-capability dependency and frame prompt/order findings as cost-effective defenses for mid-tier models, (2) incorporate at least one adaptive attack variant or defense baseline, (3) formalize the multi-agent interaction protocol, and (4) add uncertainty visualization to all main results. As it stands, the work is a solid applied study but lacks the mechanistic depth or adaptive threat modeling required for a strong ICLR acceptance.

# Neutral Reviewer
## Balanced Review

### Summary
This paper presents the first systematic empirical study of adversarial robustness in LLM-based multi-agent systems (MAS) applied to engineering and mathematical problem-solving. By introducing controlled misleading agents that inject subtle numerical or semantic errors, the authors evaluate how prompt design, communication order, agent count, and task complexity influence error propagation and decision accuracy across collaborative workflows.

### Strengths
1. **Rigorous Experimental Design and Statistical Validation:** The methodology employs a well-structured convergence analysis using Total Variation Distance (TVD) to justify a minimum of 30 trials per condition (Appendix B). Statistical significance is properly assessed using Fisher’s Exact Test for categorical outcomes and Mann-Whitney U tests for iteration counts, lending strong empirical credibility to the reported trends.
2. **Actionable, Well-Substantiated Findings:** The results yield clear, practical insights for MAS deployment. Figure 3 and Section 4.1 demonstrate that explicit warnings about misleading advice raise rejection rates to 87%, while non-concise/authoritative prompting styles significantly improve robustness. Section 4.3 and Figure 5 convincingly identify a "first-mover effect" in speaking order, with consistent empirical backing across multiple configurations.
3. **High Transparency and Documentation:** The appendix provides exhaustive details, including complete system prompts for all variants (Appendix D), quantitative result tables with p-values (Appendix E), and qualitative conversation excerpts. The reproducibility statement and commitment to releasing code align well with ICLR’s emphasis on open science and replicable empirical work.

### Weaknesses
1. **Narrow Attack Surface and Static Adversaries:** The adversarial setup relies entirely on predefined prompt instructions (e.g., "pretending the friction factor f is always 25/Re", Appendix D). This lacks adversarial optimization or dynamic red-teaming, limiting the generalizability of robustness claims to stronger, adaptive attack strategies typically expected in modern security evaluations.
2. **Exclusive Reliance on Prompt-Level Defenses:** The study explores only prompt-based mitigations (warnings, tone, role framing) without evaluating architectural or algorithmic safeguards common in MAS literature (e.g., majority voting, tool-augmented independent verification, or formal constraint checking). This restricts practical impact for engineering workflows where numerical accuracy cannot be guaranteed by prompting alone.
3. **Limited Model Diversity and Simplified Task Scope:** Core experiments focus primarily on GPT-4o-mini, while stronger models (GPT-4o, o3-mini) are relegated to the appendix and show near-perfect robustness, which weakens claims of systemic vulnerability. Additionally, the tasks (basic Darcy-Weisbach calculations, simple beam deflection, elementary matrix operations) are simplified textbook problems that lack the multi-step tool use, simulation coupling, and domain-specific constraints of real-world engineering MAS.
4. **Underanalyzed Counterintuitive Findings:** Several interesting empirical patterns lack mechanistic explanation. For instance, Section 4.3 states "MM performs surprisingly well... The reason for this is not entirely clear," and notes that all-supportive agent groups paradoxically lead to higher "no decision" rates. Without deeper qualitative coding or diagnostic analysis, these observations remain phenomenological rather than diagnostic.

### Novelty & Significance
**Novelty:** Moderate. The paper's main contribution is empirical rather than algorithmic, shifting MAS robustness evaluation from generic debate or coding tasks to engineering contexts and systematically isolating the effects of prompt framing, communication topology, and speaking order. It builds logically on existing MAS vulnerability literature (e.g., Ju et al., 2024; Huang et al., 2025a) but does not introduce new architectures, optimization methods, or theoretical bounds.  
**Clarity:** High. The manuscript is logically structured, with a clear progression from baseline setup to targeted ablations. Figures and tables are informative, and the prose is precise. Parsing artifacts do not impede comprehension.  
**Reproducibility:** High. Methodological transparency is strong, with explicit hyperparameters, convergence justification, complete prompt inventories, and detailed statistical reporting. Reliance on closed-source APIs introduces minor non-determinism, but the ≥30-trial protocol adequately stabilizes empirical conclusions.  
**Significance:** Moderate to High for applied AI safety and agentic systems research. The findings offer actionable design guidelines for practitioners deploying collaborative LLMs in numerically rigorous or safety-critical domains. While primarily an empirical study that may not meet ICLR's highest bar for methodological breakthroughs, it establishes a valuable diagnostic framework and benchmark for the rapidly expanding MAS subfield.

### Suggestions for Improvement
1. Incorporate stronger, automated adversarial baselines (e.g., gradient-free prompt optimization, LLM-as-attacker frameworks, or dynamic misinformation injection) to evaluate robustness against adaptive adversaries rather than static hand-crafted prompts.
2. Test architectural and algorithmic defenses alongside prompt engineering, such as cross-agent majority voting, tool-augmented verification (e.g., a detached Python/calculator agent), or self-consistency sampling, to provide a more complete and scalable defense landscape.
3. Deepen the analysis of anomalous findings (e.g., MM success, all-S indecision) through systematic conversation coding, attribution analysis, or ablation on the rethinking phase to explain *why* specific communication topologies trigger or suppress error propagation.
4. Expand the evaluation to include open-weight reasoning models and domain-specialized LLMs (e.g., math/physics-tuned models) to determine whether vulnerabilities are capability-dependent, architecture-specific, or universal.
5. Reframe claims in the introduction and conclusion to explicitly scope the work as an empirical diagnostic study of prompt/architecture interactions, emphasizing design insights over general robustness guarantees, and more clearly position relative to recent MAS debate/safety benchmarks.

### Missing Experiments (top 3-5 only)
1. Add comparisons against standard MAS defense baselines (e.g., independent chain-of-verification, majority voting, or tool-augmented calculation) because without them, the claim that simple prompt framing "significantly improves resilience" cannot be distinguished from trivial baselines or established safety methods.
2. Evaluate the attack and prompt variations across multiple open-weight models and scales (e.g., Llama 3.1, Qwen, Mistral) rather than relying almost exclusively on GPT-4 variants, as adversarial susceptibility is heavily alignment-dependent and your general claims are otherwise unproven beyond one model family.
3. Include an isolated ablation of the "rethinking phase" mechanism because it is structurally present in every trial; without disabling it, you cannot determine whether robustness improvements stem from prompt design/communication order or from the built-in self-correction step.
4. Test adaptive or optimized prompt attacks (e.g., automated red-teaming or gradient-based prompt optimization) rather than only static hand-crafted errors, because engineering MAS robustness claims must be validated against worst-case adversaries, not fixed single-turn mistakes.

### Deeper Analysis Needed (top 3-5 only)
1. Provide mechanistic categorization of leader failure modes (e.g., distinguishing blind compliance, flawed mathematical verification, or arithmetic copying errors) because attributing success to prompt "style" or "warnings" is speculative without tracing exactly where the logical override occurs in the reasoning chain.
2. Address the massive confidence intervals and high trial variance (e.g., baseline misleading rate CI of [20%, 70%]) by significantly increasing sample sizes or applying Bayesian uncertainty quantification, as the current statistical power is insufficient to reliably distinguish between experimental conditions at ICLR's acceptance bar.
3. Perform mediation or causal analysis on the "first-mover effect" to disentangle genuine information dominance from superficial anchoring bias, since reporting correlation does not explain why the first agent's output disproportionately weights the leader's final decision or whether this scales with reasoning depth.
4. Quantify the robustness-efficiency trade-off explicitly using a unified metric or Pareto frontier (e.g., rejection rate vs. token/iteration cost), because the paper notes safety often increases discussions but provides no principled guidance on optimizing this balance, weakening the practical utility of the design recommendations.

### Visualizations & Case Studies
1. Plot step-by-step reasoning trace timelines mapping the exact conversation turn where the leader abandons correct independent calculation and adopts the advisor’s incorrect value, as this directly reveals whether the MAS fails during problem parsing, intermediate verification, or final synthesis.
2. Provide side-by-side reasoning-depth or token-usage comparisons between "concise" and "authoritative/verbose" leader prompts to empirically validate the hypothesis that non-concise prompts succeed by forcing independent pre-computation, rather than leaving it as an unverified assumption.
3. Create an error propagation network diagram across varying agent topologies and communication orders to visualize how misleading information amplifies, dampens, or gets filtered in 3+ agent setups, exposing whether vulnerability scales with system size or is strictly localized to immediate adjacency.

### Obvious Next Steps
1. Implement and evaluate at least two lightweight, principled mitigation protocols (e.g., mandatory independent code execution or asynchronous cross-verification rounds) to provide validated defenses, as identifying vulnerabilities without testing structured countermeasures falls short of ICLR’s contribution standards for safety research.
2. Systematically ablate the communication protocol by comparing your linear turn-based approach against established MAS architectures (e.g., Tree-of-Thoughts or structured debate with voting) because many observed vulnerabilities may be artifacts of a single rigid interaction pattern rather than inherent LLM limitations.
3. Formalize the experimental design into a standardized benchmark suite that fixes attack surfaces, verification steps, and evaluation metrics, enabling direct community comparison and preventing these findings from remaining isolated phenomenological observations tied to custom, non-transferable prompts.

# Final Consolidated Review
## Summary
This work presents a systematic empirical study of adversarial robustness in LLM-based multi-agent systems applied to engineering and mathematical tasks. By introducing static, prompt-driven misleading agents, the authors quantify how system prompts, communication order, and task complexity influence error propagation and decision accuracy in collaborative reasoning workflows.

## Strengths
- **Methodological Rigor & Statistical Transparency:** The experimental design employs TVD-based convergence analysis to justify sample sizes and applies appropriate statistical tests (Fisher’s Exact, Mann-Whitney U) with full p-value reporting in the appendix. Prompt inventories, hyperparameters, and reproducibility commitments are thoroughly documented.
- **Actionable Empirical Findings:** The study isolates clear, practical design signals for MAS deployment, particularly the critical impact of explicit warning prompts, the vulnerability of concise leader personas, and the pronounced "first-mover effect" in turn-based communication topologies (Section 4.3, Figure 5).

## Weaknesses
- **Core Confounder: Model Capability Overrides Systemic Findings:** The paper’s central premise—that MAS architecture and prompt design are the primary drivers of adversarial vulnerability—is severely undermined by Appendix Table E.7 and Figure 7. Stronger reasoning models (GPT-4o, o3-mini) achieve ~97–100% rejection rates with near-zero misleading rates, essentially neutralizing the observed vulnerabilities. By focusing the narrative on GPT-4o-mini while burying capable models in the appendix, the paper conflates transient benchmark weaknesses of mid-tier LLMs with fundamental MAS architectural flaws, drastically reducing the significance and generalizability of the conclusions.
- **Static, Non-Adaptive Threat Model:** The adversarial setup relies on fixed, hand-crafted system prompts that inject predetermined errors regardless of the leader’s counterarguments or intermediate calculations. In 2024+, adversarial evaluation standards demand dynamic, optimized, or LLM-driven attackers that adapt to system defenses. A static, non-reactive adversary fails to stress-test the collaborative reasoning pipeline and overestimates the effectiveness of simple prompt warnings.
- **Absence of Structural & Algorithmic Defense Baselines:** The evaluation exclusively tests prompt-level interventions (role framing, warnings, verbosity) without comparing them to established MAS robustness mechanisms such as majority voting, independent tool-augmented verification, structured debate, or self-consistency sampling. Without these baselines, it is impossible to determine whether the reported robustness gains are meaningful improvements or trivially outperformable by standard, cost-effective architectural defenses.
- **Superficial Handling of High Variance & Counterintuitive Dynamics:** The paper reports wide confidence intervals (e.g., baseline misleading rate CI: [20%, 70%]) and high stochasticity in binary outcomes, yet does not sufficiently account for this uncertainty in the main claims. Additionally, paradoxical findings—such as two misleading agents (“MM”) outperforming larger mixed groups, or purely supportive teams increasing “no decision” rates—are attributed to unverified post-hoc hypotheses rather than mechanistic tracing or causal analysis.

## Nice-to-Haves
- Visualize uncertainty (error bars or confidence intervals) directly in Figures 3–6 for rapid assessment.
- Provide an isolated ablation of the "rethinking phase" to disentangle its self-correction contribution from prompt/communication effects.
- Map exact failure points in the reasoning chain (e.g., problem parsing vs. arithmetic verification vs. final synthesis) to explain *why* specific prompt styles succeed.

## Novel Insights
The paper compellingly demonstrates that MAS robustness in engineering contexts is not an emergent property of agent count or collaborative scaling, but is instead highly fragile to communication topology and explicit prompt framing. The pronounced first-mover effect, combined with the finding that verbose/non-concise leaders implicitly trigger more independent verification, reveals that LLM-based MAS vulnerability is often a byproduct of superficial anchoring bias and premature consensus rather than deep logical failure. Crucially, the stark performance jump in Appendix results highlights that what is initially measured as a systemic MAS flaw largely collapses when base model reasoning capacity crosses a certain threshold, reframing the core challenge as one of capability-dependent robustness rather than purely structural interaction design.

## Potentially Missed Related Work
- *Structured debate vs. voting for LLM robustness* (e.g., Du et al., 2023; Li et al., 2024 on consensus mechanisms) — would provide necessary architectural baselines to contextualize prompt-level findings.
- *Tool-augmented verification in agentic workflows* (e.g., Code interpreter integration, calculator agents) — highly relevant for engineering MAS where symbolic/tool execution trivially bypasses numerical hallucination.
- *Automated LLM red-teaming frameworks* (e.g., Gao et al., 2025; Zou et al., 2023 on adaptive prompt attacks) — would benchmark the static threat model against modern adversarial standards.

## Suggestions
1. **Reframe the Contribution Around Capability Scaling:** Explicitly analyze and discuss the model dependency shown in Table E.7. Position prompt framing and communication order as *cost-effective, model-agnostic heuristics* rather than universal robustness guarantees, and discuss how their utility degrades as base reasoning capabilities improve.
2. **Introduce Dynamic/Adaptive Attacks:** Replace or supplement hand-crafted prompts with an adaptive attacker (e.g., reinforcement learning prompt optimization, or an LLM adversary conditioned on the leader’s previous turn) to test if prompt warnings actually survive strategic misdirection.
3. **Add Structural Defense Baselines:** Evaluate at least two standard MAS mitigation strategies (e.g., majority voting across independent reasoning chains, or a tool-augmented verifier that cross-checks numerical outputs). Compare their performance and efficiency directly against prompt warnings.
4. **Quantify the Robustness-Efficiency Frontier:** Systematically report a unified metric balancing rejection rate against iteration/token cost. Explicitly show where prompt engineering or agent scaling hits diminishing returns, providing actionable deployment guidance rather than isolated percentage points.
