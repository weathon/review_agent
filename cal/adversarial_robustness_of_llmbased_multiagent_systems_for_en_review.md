# Harsh Critic Review
## Section-by-Section Critical Review

### Title & Abstract
The title accurately reflects the paper's scope. The abstract claims this is the "first systematic study" of adversarial robustness for LLM-based MAS in engineering contexts. While the literature review shows growing interest in MAS security, this claim is marginally acceptable but slightly overstates novelty, as adversarial prompt injection and misinformation propagation in multi-agent debates have been systematically studied in adjacent domains (code, math, general QA). The abstract's core claims (sensitivity to task type, error subtlety, speaking order, and prompt framing) are supported by the results, but the abstract omits a crucial caveat: the study relies exclusively on free-text deliberation without computational tool use, which is atypical for modern engineering workflows.

### Introduction & Motivation (Sec 1)
The motivation is clear: MAS are being deployed in engineering, and adversarial manipulation could lead to unsafe numerical outputs. The research gap is stated as a lack of analysis on how prompting and communication structure jointly affect robustness in engineering contexts. However, the introduction fails to justify *why* engineering tasks should exhibit fundamentally different adversarial dynamics compared to mathematical or code-reasoning MAS, which are already heavily studied. The claim that "engineering workflows demand formal rigor and numerical accuracy" is true, but the experimental setup treats engineering as a pure text-reasoning problem, ignoring that real engineering MAS typically delegate numerical computation to external solvers or code interpreters. Without this distinction, the "engineering" framing reads more as a domain selection than a conceptual advance. The contributions are clearly listed but lean heavily toward empirical observation rather than methodological or theoretical insight.

### Related Work (Sec 2)
The section adequately surveys recent work on MAS security, misinformation propagation, and engineering applications. However, the synthesis is largely descriptive. It lacks a critical comparison with foundational studies on debate-based verification, peer-review MAS architectures, and tool-augmented reasoning, which are highly relevant to the paper's central question of how agents cross-check each other's numerical claims. Furthermore, the paper cites many 2024–2025 preprints but doesn't clearly delineate how its experimental design advances beyond established findings on speaking order (Ju et al. 2024) or consensus fragility (Huang et al. 2025a). A sharper positioning against mechanistic studies of LLM persuasion or confidence calibration would strengthen the paper's academic contribution.

### Method & Experimental Setup (Sec 3 & App B)
**Threat Model & Adversary Design:** The adversarial agents are explicitly instructed via system prompt to mislead (e.g., `"pretending that the friction factor f is always 25/Re!"`). This is a weak, non-optimized threat model. In practice, adversarial MAS vulnerabilities arise from compromised fine-tuning, stealthy prompt injection, or optimized message perturbations, not from agents told to be overtly deceptive. This limits the external validity of the findings to realistic deployment scenarios.
**Task Selection & Baseline Capability:** The baseline tasks (pipe pressure, beam deflection, basic math, graphs) vary widely in difficulty and rely entirely on the LLM's internal parametric knowledge. Crucially, **single-agent baseline accuracy is never reported**. Without knowing how often `GPT-4o mini` solves each task correctly in isolation, it is impossible to disentangle MAS interaction effects from simple model capability gaps. For instance, the 50% misleading rate on the division task (Sec 4.2.3) appears driven by rounding confusion intrinsic to the model, not collaborative dynamics.
**Rethinking Phase:** The method mentions a "rethinking phase" triggered after a preliminary decision (Sec 3.1), but its implementation (prompt structure, forced output format, temperature) is not specified in the main text. Moreover, results never analyze whether this phase actually corrects errors or merely rubber-stamps decisions. This represents a missing analysis of a core component of the proposed MAS architecture.
**Statistical Methodology:** The use of Total Variation Distance (TVD) to determine 30 trials per condition is a reasonable convergence check, but the 0.05 threshold and 100-trial final distribution are arbitrary without justification. Additionally, the paper performs dozens of pairwise Fisher’s Exact and Mann-Whitney U tests against baseline without correcting for multiple comparisons, increasing the risk of Type I errors.

### Results & Analysis (Sec 4)
**Prompt Variations (Sec 4.1):** The finding that explicit warnings dramatically reduce misleading rates is expected and aligns with prior work on LLM adversarial prompting. The observation that "not concise" or "authoritative" styles improve rejection is interesting but explained post-hoc ("assumed that a non-concise leader is more likely to solve the problem on their own first"). This is speculative; the paper provides no transcript analysis or attention/activation tracing to validate the hypothesized mechanism.
**Task Complexity & Error Subtlety (Sec 4.2):** While the paper correctly notes that harder-to-spot errors increase misleading rates, it conflates *task difficulty* with *MAS vulnerability*. The graph traversal and division results heavily reflect known LLM weaknesses rather than novel collaborative failure modes. The beam deflection experiments are the most domain-relevant, but again, without isolating advisor-induced error from leader's own computational mistakes, the causal attribution is weak.
**Agent Number & Order (Sec 4.3):** The "first-mover effect" is well-documented and confirmed here. However, the paper's conclusion that "having initial support can enhance robustness" is severely confounded by the experimental design. Because the leader's system prompt explicitly warns of a misleading advisor (Sec D), S-only groups cause the leader to become hyper-cautious or wait for a non-existent threat, inflating the "no decision" rate. The authors acknowledge this ("When no M-agent is present, the leader keeps searching for one that does not exist"), but then proceed to generalize findings about multi-agent robustness without running a critical ablation: *What happens when the warning is removed?* This omission makes it impossible to know if S-only groups truly perform poorly in MAS, or if the prompt artifact engineered the failure.
**Personalization & Authority (Sec 4.4):** The finding that expert/named framing amplifies the first-mover effect is plausible and aligns with human social psychology literature, but again, the analysis stops at surface-level correlation. No mechanistic breakdown of how the leader's response generation shifts (e.g., increased deference, reduced verification steps) is provided.

### Limitations & Conclusions (Sec 5)
The authors correctly note the combinatorial explosion of prompt variations and non-linear agent behavior. However, several critical limitations are underemphasized:
1. The study is confined to text-only MAS, whereas production engineering systems rely on code execution, symbolic solvers, or CAD APIs. The findings may not transfer to tool-augmented workflows.
2. The adversarial agent is non-adaptive and explicitly instructed, ignoring adaptive/red-teaming behaviors.
3. There is no discussion of how to architect defenses beyond prompt engineering (e.g., confidence scoring, computational verification steps, structured debate protocols).
The ethics statement is standard and appropriately scoped. The reproducibility statement is solid, though code release is pending.

## Overall Assessment
This paper presents a thorough empirical investigation into how prompting, agent ordering, and error subtlety affect LLM-based MAS performance on engineering-style reasoning tasks. The experimental scale is commendable, and the statistical convergence methodology is carefully implemented. However, for ICLR's standards, the work currently falls short of the methodological rigor and conceptual depth required for acceptance. The core issues are: (1) the absence of single-agent baselines makes it impossible to isolate collaborative dynamics from inherent model capability gaps; (2) the adversarial threat model is artificially constrained and non-optimized, limiting real-world applicability; (3) key findings (e.g., multi-group robustness) are confounded by prompt artifacts that weren't properly controlled; and (4) the analysis remains largely correlational, lacking mechanistic insight into *how* or *why* agents accept misleading numerical claims. The paper reads more as an applied prompt-engineering study for a specific MAS configuration than a foundational contribution to robust AI or multi-agent theory. With stronger ablation controls (e.g., single-agent baselines, warning prompt removal, adaptive adversaries) and deeper mechanistic analysis of the communication traces, this work could become a valuable empirical reference. In its current form, the contribution is interesting but not yet robust or novel enough to meet the ICLR acceptance bar.

# Neutral Reviewer
## Balanced Review

### Summary
This paper presents a systematic empirical study on the adversarial robustness of LLM-based multi-agent systems (MAS) when solving formal engineering and mathematical problems. By varying agent system prompts, communication order, advisor composition, and task complexity, the authors quantify how deliberate misinformation propagates and identify design factors that influence system resilience. The results demonstrate that MAS vulnerability in engineering domains is highly sensitive to prompt framing, the subtlety of injected numerical/semantic errors, and the initial speaking order of agents.

### Strengths
1. **Domain-focused evaluation with clear practical motivation:** Unlike prior MAS security work focused on linguistic or generic reasoning tasks, this paper explicitly targets engineering problems (e.g., Darcy-Weisbach pressure loss, beam deflection) where formal rigor and numerical accuracy are critical. This contextual shift makes the findings highly relevant for real-world deployments. (Sec 1, 3.2)
2. **Methodologically rigorous experimental design:** The authors run ≥30 trials per condition and formally justify this threshold using Total Variation Distance (TVD) convergence analysis (Sec B). Coupled with appropriate statistical testing (Fisher’s exact test for categorical outcomes, Mann-Whitney U for iterations), the quantitative results are reliable and well-grounded. (Sec 4, Tables E.6–E.15)
3. **Clear, actionable ablations of MAS interaction dynamics:** The paper systematically isolates key design levers. For example, adding an explicit warning in the leader’s prompt reduces the misleading rate from 100% to 47% (baseline) and down to 13.3–0% with stronger phrasing (Sec 4.1, Fig 3). The discovery of a "first-mover effect" and the counterintuitive robustness of dual-misleading (MM) configurations provide nuanced insights into MAS persuasion dynamics. (Sec 4.3)
4. **Comprehensive documentation and reproducibility materials:** All prompt variants, problem definitions, model parameters, and evaluation metrics are explicitly provided in the paper and appendices (Sec B, D, E). The commitment to release code and the detailed convergence methodology meet high reproducibility standards expected at ICLR.

### Weaknesses
1. **Overly simplistic and static threat model:** The adversarial agents use hardcoded, non-adaptive incorrect values (e.g., enforcing `f = 25/Re`, fixed rounding errors like 0.614 for 8/13) rather than dynamically optimizing misleading prompts or executing prompt-injection techniques. This limits ecological validity and underestimates the capabilities of modern adversarial agents. (Sec 3.2, Table 1)
2. **Narrow MAS architecture scope:** The study evaluates a single hierarchical, synchronous turn-based protocol with a central leader. It does not test parallel, decentralized, or debate-style topologies that are common in contemporary MAS literature, making it unclear whether findings generalize to other interaction paradigms. (Sec 3.1, Fig 1)
3. **Lack of evaluated mitigation strategies:** While the paper identifies vulnerability factors, it stops at descriptive insights. No systematic defenses (e.g., tool-augmented verification, independent auditor agents, confidence-weighted voting, or external calculation APIs) are introduced or benchmarked, limiting the paper's prescriptive value for building robust systems. (Sec 5)
4. **Model-dependent conclusions limit practical impact:** The vulnerabilities largely disappear when using stronger reasoning models (GPT-4o and o3-mini achieve 93–100% rejection rates, Sec 7, Table E.7). The paper does not adequately discuss the implications of this, which raises questions about the long-term relevance of the findings as models rapidly improve.

### Novelty & Significance
- **Novelty:** The paper's primary novelty lies in its domain-specific framing of MAS adversarial robustness, shifting the evaluation focus from natural language tasks to quantitative engineering workflows. While the MAS setup and adversarial prompt variations are conceptually aligned with existing MAS security literature, the systematic ablation of prompt components, error subtlety, and communication order in formal reasoning tasks is a meaningful and timely contribution.
- **Clarity:** The manuscript is well-structured, logically progressing from motivation to methodology, results, and discussion. Writing is accessible, and the experimental variables are clearly isolated. (Minor parser artifacts aside, the narrative flow is strong.)
- **Reproducibility:** Excellent. The authors provide full prompt templates, exact API parameters, trial-count justification via TVD, statistical test choices, and extensive tabulated results in the appendix. Code will be released, satisfying ICLR standards.
- **Significance:** The work offers valuable empirical guidelines for practitioners designing engineering MAS, particularly around prompt framing, team sizing, and communication ordering. However, its significance is somewhat tempered by the narrow threat model, lack of evaluated defenses, and the observation that stronger reasoning models neutralize most vulnerabilities. It serves better as a sensitivity analysis and design heuristic guide than as a foundational security framework.

### Suggestions for Improvement
1. **Upgrade the adversarial threat model:** Replace static, hardcoded incorrect values with adaptive adversarial agents that dynamically craft misleading arguments, employ semantic obfuscation, or simulate prompt-injection techniques. This would better reflect real-world attack surfaces and stress-test MAS robustness more thoroughly.
2. **Evaluate diverse MAS topologies:** Extend experiments to include parallel/fully-connected debate structures, decentralized voting mechanisms, or tool-augmented workflows. This would clarify whether the "first-mover effect" and prompt-sensitivity findings generalize beyond the sequential leader-advisor hierarchy.
3. **Introduce and benchmark concrete defenses:** Move beyond prompt engineering. Implement and evaluate structural mitigations such as (a) an independent "auditor" agent that cross-checks numerical claims using a Python/symbolic engine, (b) dynamic confidence scoring before consensus, or (c) mandatory verification phases. Benchmark their impact on both robustness and efficiency.
4. **Contextualize model scalability and limitations:** Explicitly analyze why stronger models (GPT-4o, o3-mini) achieve near-perfect rejection. Discuss whether observed vulnerabilities are transitional (relevant for smaller/open-source models) or if they reveal fundamental coordination flaws that persist regardless of capability. Frame recommendations accordingly.
5. **Add a design guideline synthesis:** In the conclusion, include a concise table or checklist mapping experimental findings to actionable engineering workflow principles (e.g., optimal advisor count vs. decision latency, when expert framing is safe vs. dangerous, recommended prompt structure for high-stakes tasks). This would greatly enhance the paper's practical utility.

# Spark Finder Review
## How to Improve This Paper

### Missing Experiments (top 3-5 only)
1. Replace or augment the static, hardcoded adversarial prompts with automated red-teaming attacks (e.g., PAIR or LLM-driven adversarial prompt generation) because evaluating only a single fixed lie does not validate general "adversarial robustness" against adaptive threats expected at ICLR.
2. Include a tool-use or code-interpreter baseline because engineering MAS deployments inherently rely on external calculators to bypass LLM arithmetic limits; ignoring this obvious baseline makes the reported vulnerability rates unrealistically pessimistic and limits the paper's applicability to real systems.
3. Add standard aggregation defenses like majority voting or a dedicated critic agent to contextualize the proposed prompt tweaks, as isolated prompt modifications cannot be judged as effective without comparing against established, lightweight MAS security baselines.

### Deeper Analysis Needed (top 3-5 only)
1. Disentangle independent model failure from adversarial influence because the beam deflection tasks show correctness rates dropping to 29-55% even when the leader is *not* misled, indicating the leader fundamentally fails the task alone rather than just being successfully manipulated.
2. Mechanistically analyze *how* persuasion occurs (e.g., does the leader actually recalculate flawed math or just socially defer?) using an LLM-as-a-judge to score reasoning step-by-step, since final outcome metrics alone cannot distinguish logical failure from social compliance vulnerabilities.
3. Conduct a formal statistical ablation on the "first-mover effect" to prove causality rather than correlation, as the paper heavily relies on agent order influencing outcomes without rigorously controlling for the inherent quality variance of the first-turn responses.

### Visualizations & Case Studies
1. Provide a turn-level error propagation timeline that maps exactly which conversational step causes the leader to abandon its correct initial reasoning path and adopt the adversary's numerical values.
2. Include a side-by-side trace analysis of a successful rejection vs. a successful persuasion for the *identical* prompt setup, explicitly highlighting the exact linguistic or logical phrasing that triggered the leader to change its stance.

### Obvious Next Steps
1. Explore scalable, automated defense mechanisms (e.g., enforcing self-correction loops or automated formula verification steps) rather than relying solely on manual system prompt engineering, which the paper already admits suffers from combinatorial explosion and brittleness.
2. Investigate the impact of heterogeneous model capabilities (e.g., a strong reasoning agent defending against a weak adversary) since the results show o3-mini achieves near-zero misleading rates, rendering the robustness findings highly model-dependent rather than providing generalizable architectural insights.

# Final Consolidated Review
## Summary
This paper presents a systematic empirical study evaluating the adversarial robustness of LLM-based multi-agent systems (MAS) in formal engineering and mathematical reasoning tasks. By varying system prompts, communication order, advisor composition, and error subtlety, the authors quantify how deliberate misinformation propagates through hierarchical deliberation. The results demonstrate that MAS vulnerability is highly sensitive to explicit prompt warnings, the initial speaking order, and the plausibility of injected numerical errors, offering practical heuristics for designing more resilient collaborative workflows.

## Strengths
- **Rigorous empirical design with formal convergence justification:** The study employs a large-scale ablation framework across prompts, agent topologies, and task domains. Trial counts are formally justified using Total Variation Distance (TVD) convergence analysis, and results are validated with appropriate statistical tests (Fisher’s exact, Mann-Whitney U), ensuring quantitative reliability (Sec B, Tables E.6–E.15).
- **Actionable, domain-specific interaction insights:** The paper cleanly isolates key design levers: explicit warnings reduce misleading rates from 100% to baseline 47%, while subtle numerical errors (e.g., rounding vs. categorical mistakes) and the "first-mover effect" dominate outcomes. These findings shift MAS security focus from pure linguistic alignment to formal reasoning fragility, providing concrete guidance for engineering deployments.

## Weaknesses
- **Static, non-adaptive threat model limits ecological validity:** Adversarial agents are hardcoded with fixed incorrect values or explicit system prompt instructions (e.g., `pretending that f is always 25/Re`). Real-world MAS vulnerabilities typically involve optimized prompt injections, stealthy semantic manipulation, or adaptive red-teaming. This design choice risks underestimating attack surfaces and may not generalize to more sophisticated adversarial behaviors.
- **Conflates inherent LLM capability gaps with MAS failure modes:** The paper lacks pure single-agent baselines across all tasks. High misleading rates (e.g., 50% on division, correctness dropping to ~30–55% in non-misled beam tasks) likely stem partly from baseline model calibration and arithmetic limitations rather than collaborative dynamics. Without isolating solo capability, the causal attribution of failures to MAS interaction remains ambiguous.
- **Vulnerabilities are highly model-dependent, raising long-term relevance questions:** Stronger reasoning models (GPT-4o, o3-mini) achieve 93–100% rejection rates (Table E.7), effectively neutralizing the tested adversarial strategies. The paper does not adequately discuss whether the observed phenomena reflect transient capability gaps in smaller models or fundamental coordination vulnerabilities that persist across capability scaling.
- **Superficial mechanistic analysis of persuasion:** Findings remain at the outcome level. Explanations for why certain prompt styles (e.g., "not concise") improve robustness are post-hoc and speculative. The paper does not trace whether leaders abandon correct reasoning due to logical confusion, social deference, or computational overload, leaving the underlying persuasion mechanism unverified.

## Nice-to-Haves
- Include a tool-use or code-interpreter baseline to contextualize how external calculators or symbolic solvers might bypass the arithmetic vulnerabilities observed in text-only deliberation.
- Benchmark lightweight structural defenses (e.g., majority voting, independent critic/auditor agent, confidence-weighted aggregation) to contextualize the relative efficacy of prompt-level interventions.
- Apply standard multiple comparison corrections in the extensive ablation tables to strengthen statistical claims across dozens of pairwise tests.
- Formalize the "first-mover effect" analysis by controlling for the inherent quality variance of first-turn responses to isolate communication order from response content.

## Novel Insights
The study reveals that MAS robustness in formal domains is not an emergent property of scale or consensus, but a fragile function of interaction design and prompt framing. Counterintuitively, homogeneous "supportive-only" teams can underperform mixed teams due to warning-induced hyper-caution, while strong first-mover signals disproportionately anchor collective reasoning regardless of subsequent accuracy. This highlights a critical, often overlooked tension in MAS design: defensive prompt engineering can inadvertently degrade system efficiency and coordination, creating a zero-sum trade-off between vigilance and collaborative throughput that warrants architectural, rather than purely prompt-based, solutions.

## Suggestions
- Explicitly run and report single-agent baseline accuracy across all tasks to cleanly disentangle inherent LVM reasoning limits from MAS-specific vulnerability to misinformation.
- Add a step-by-step trace analysis (e.g., using an LLM-as-judge to score logical consistency vs. social compliance) to distinguish whether leaders are persuaded by flawed derivations or by authoritative framing, directly addressing the post-hoc speculation in Sec 4.1.
- Include a scoped discussion or preliminary comparison contrasting text-only deliberation with tool-augmented workflows, clarifying the applicability boundary of the findings for production engineering MAS.
- Introduce at least one lightweight defensive baseline (e.g., mandatory independent verification step or majority voting across S-agents) in the appendix to demonstrate how structural interventions compare to the proposed prompt modifications.
