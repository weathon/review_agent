# Harsh Critic Review
## Section-by-Section Critical Review

### Title & Abstract
- **Does the title accurately reflect the contribution?** Yes. The title clearly signals the focus on co-occurrence, dual prompts, and training-free zero-shot multi-label classification, which aligns with the paper's core contribution.
- **Does the abstract clearly state the problem, method, and key results?** The abstract concisely identifies the problem (CLIP's lack of co-occurrence exploitation in multi-label settings), the observation (correlative prompts help but cause hallucination), the method (dual-prompt calibration), and claims empirical superiority. 
- **Are any claims in the abstract unsupported by the paper?** The claim of a "causal inference framework" in the abstract sets a high theoretical expectation. However, the paper's actual derivation (Appendix A) relies on heuristic approximations rather than rigorous causal do-calculus or identifiability proofs. This creates a slight misalignment between the abstract's theoretical claim and the empirical-heuristic nature of the implementation.

### Introduction & Motivation
- **Is the problem well-motivated? Is the gap in prior work clearly identified?** Yes. The motivation is strong: zero-shot multi-label classification is practically important, CLIP's softmax-based global focus and lack of co-occurrence priors limit its multi-label performance, and prior works either require fine-tuning (DualCoOp, TaI) or architectural modifications (TagCLIP). The gap in purely prompt-based, training-free calibration is well-positioned.
- **Are the contributions clearly stated and accurate?** The contributions are clearly enumerated implicitly through the narrative: identifying the dual nature of co-occurrence, proposing the dual-prompt calibration, and providing causal framing.
- **Does the introduction over-claim or under-sell?** It slightly over-claims the novelty of the theoretical framing. Presenting a prompt addition/subtraction scheme as a "causal inference framework" that "removes the biased part of the prediction" suggests a level of mathematical identifiability and confounder control that the paper does not actually establish. The introduction would benefit from framing this as a *causally-inspired heuristic* rather than a formal causal solution.

### Method / Approach
- **Is the method clearly described and reproducible?** The high-level pipeline is reproducible: construct DiP and CoP, encode, compute similarities, and combine scores. However, the exact inference protocol for multi-label outputs is missing (critical flaw discussed below).
- **Are key assumptions stated and justified?** The causal graph in Figure 3 assumes prompts directly and exclusively activate either "discriminative" ($F_d$) or "correlative" ($F_c$) features via separate causal paths. This is an oversimplification of how transformer text encoders and cross-modal alignment work; features are deeply entangled. The assumption of independence between direct and indirect effects is not justified or empirically verified.
- **Are there logical gaps in the derivation or reasoning?** **Yes, this is the most significant weakness.** The transition from the subtraction form (Eq. 1) to the addition form (Eq. 2) is mathematically unsound. Appendix A introduces mutually exclusive events ($L_t^0$, $L_c^0$) that do not correspond to valid prompting operations, drops denominators arbitrarily, and concludes with a proportionality $\propto$ before simply setting $\lambda=1$ empirically. This is not a valid derivation of the Total Direct Effect (Pearl, 2001); it is a post-hoc justification for ensemble averaging. The causal mediation assumptions (e.g., sequential ignorability, no unmeasured confounders between prompts and latent features) are entirely absent.
- **Are there edge cases or failure modes not discussed?** Yes. The method assumes co-occurring labels are readily available or accurately estimated. It does not discuss scenarios with anti-correlated labels (where co-occurrence should be negative), rare/long-tail classes with no co-occurrence priors, or prompt length overflow when $m_k$ is large.
- **For theoretical claims: are proofs correct and complete?** No. As noted, Appendix A is not a rigorous proof. It mixes conditional probabilities with joint probabilities incorrectly, ignores the softmax normalization in Eq. 3, and relies on empirical tuning of $\lambda$. Theoretical claims should be either removed or replaced with a proper sensitivity analysis/empirical validation of the additive heuristic.

### Experiments & Results
- **Do the experiments actually test the paper's claims?** They test whether adding co-occurrence helps and whether DualPrompt improves over CoP/DiP, but they do not rigorously test the "calibration" claim. There is no controlled experiment injecting known hallucination-inducing priors to show that DualPrompt successfully suppresses them compared to DiP.
- **Are baselines appropriate and fairly compared?** Baselines are appropriate for the task. However, Table 1 and 2 show DualPrompt combined with TagCLIP uses "Original" resolution, while other methods are capped at 224×224. While this is noted, the marginal gains (+0.5% mAP on COCO) are small and likely within the variance of single-seed runs. Without variance reporting, it's hard to claim statistically significant improvements over TagCLIP alone.
- **Are there missing ablations that would materially change conclusions?** Yes. Critical missing ablations: (1) Sensitivity of $\lambda$ (if Eq. 2 is the core, how does performance change for $\lambda \neq 1$?); (2) Robustness to noisy/incorrect co-occurrence labels (what happens if ChatGPT or the 1% statistic is wrong?); (3) Ablation on the number of co-occurring labels $m_k$; (4) Prompt ensemble size for the standard 80 CLIP templates vs. dual-prompt overhead.
- **Are error bars / statistical significance reported?** **No.** All results use `random seed = 1` and single-run reporting. ICLR standards typically require multiple seeds or at least variance estimates, especially when claiming SOTA improvements that are sometimes marginal (0.3%–0.7%).
- **Do the results support the claims made, or are they cherry-picked?** Results are broadly consistent across datasets and not cherry-picked. However, the improvement over strong baselines like TagCLIP is incremental when DualPrompt is applied on top of it. The main gains come from using data-derived co-occurrence, which borders on weakly supervised rather than strictly zero-shot.
- **Are datasets and evaluation metrics appropriate?** Datasets (MS-COCO, VG-256, Objects365) and metrics (mAP, CF1) are standard. **However, a critical evaluation detail is missing:** Section 3 uses the standard CLIP softmax over all $q$ classes. Multi-label classification fundamentally requires independent sigmoid activations and thresholding, as multiple labels are simultaneously true. The paper does not state how softmax probabilities are converted to binary predictions for mAP/F1 calculation. If a single-label thresholding scheme is used on softmax outputs, it artificially penalizes correct multi-label predictions and invalidates the metric. This must be clarified immediately.

### Writing & Clarity
- **Are there sections that are confusing or poorly explained?** The causal derivation in Appendix A is highly confusing due to inconsistent notation and mathematically unjustified steps (e.g., mixing $p(y|x, L_t, L_c)$ with joint distributions in the numerator/denominator without clarifying the conditioning set). Figures 1, 3, and 7 are reused/referenced in ways that make the causal mechanism harder to follow. The shift between "prompts" and "labels" in equations occasionally blurs the distinction between textual input and latent variables.
- **Are figures and tables clear and informative?** Generally clear. Figure 5 effectively visualizes per-class improvements. Figure 8's retrieval visualization nicely illustrates the qualitative difference between DiP, CoP, and DualPrompt. Table 3 is cut off in the text but appears complete in structure.

### Limitations & Broader Impact
- **Do the authors acknowledge the key limitations?** Yes, they note that ChatGPT priors are generic and dataset statistics are superior, and that CoP can cause overfitting.
- **Are there fundamental limitations they missed?** Several: (1) Computational overhead scales linearly with the number of co-occurring labels per class; for $q=80$ and average $m_k=2$, this triples text encoder passes. (2) The method inherits CLIP's bias toward Western-centric, common-object co-occurrences, which may fail on domain-shifted or culturally distinct datasets. (3) Dependency on accurate co-occurrence estimation makes it fragile for open-vocabulary or long-tail label distributions where priors are sparse.
- **Are there failure modes or negative societal impacts not discussed?** The paper mentions object hallucination as a technical issue but does not discuss how this might propagate in downstream applications (e.g., medical imaging, autonomous driving, or surveillance). Over-reliance on co-occurrence priors could reinforce dataset biases (e.g., assuming "person" always co-occurs with "umbrella"), leading to systematic false negatives in edge cases. A brief discussion of bias amplification would strengthen the impact section.

### Overall Assessment
This paper addresses a relevant and practical problem: adapting CLIP to zero-shot multi-label classification without weight updates. The empirical observation that correlative prompts improve recognition but induce hallucination is insightful, and the proposed dual-prompt strategy is straightforward to implement. However, several substantial issues currently prevent it from meeting ICLR's acceptance bar. First, the causal theoretical framing is mathematically flawed (Appendix A) and should be reframed as an empirically motivated ensemble heuristic. Second, critical implementation details are missing, most notably how multi-label predictions are thresholded when using softmax similarities, which directly affects the validity of the reported mAP/F1. Third, the experimental section lacks variance estimation (single seed), robustness ablations (noise in co-occurrence, sensitivity to $\lambda$, prompt count), and careful isolation of the dual-prompt contribution from resolution/local-feature advantages when combined with TagCLIP. The authors should: (1) correct or replace the theoretical derivation with a clear empirical justification, (2) detail the inference and thresholding protocol, (3) add multi-seed variance and key robustness ablations, and (4) discuss scalability and bias amplification. With these revisions, the work could become a solid contribution to the prompt engineering and VLM adaptation literature.

# Neutral Reviewer
## Balanced Review

### Summary
This paper addresses the performance drop of CLIP in zero-shot multi-label image classification, attributing it to the model's failure to explicitly leverage label co-occurrence during pretraining and inference. The authors propose DualPrompt, which combines a standard discriminative prompt with a correlative prompt containing co-occurring label names, and uses a causally-motivated calibration to mitigate object hallucination caused by overfitting to co-occurrence patterns. Experiments across MS-COCO, VG-256, and Objects365 demonstrate consistent improvements over recent training-based and training-free baselines, with the best results achieved when combined with local-feature frameworks like TagCLIP.

### Strengths
1. **Clear problem formulation and intuitive methodology:** The paper effectively diagnoses CLIP's multi-label limitations (global feature dominance, lack of co-occurrence priors) and proposes a simple, interpretable solution. The distinction between the positive and negative effects of correlative prompts is well-articulated.
2. **Strong and comprehensive empirical validation:** The method consistently outperforms strong baselines (DualCoOp, TaICLIP, CLIP, TagCLIP) across multiple architectures (ResNet-101, ViT-B/16) and datasets (MS-COCO, VG-256, Objects365), as shown in Tables 1-3. The gains are particularly notable given the lack of gradient-based fine-tuning.
3. **Insightful analysis and visualization:** Figures 2, 5, and 7 clearly demonstrate the dual impact of co-occurrence information, showing empirically how DualPrompt retains performance gains while suppressing false positives. The text-image retrieval examples in Figure 8 provide intuitive qualitative evidence of the method's mechanism.

### Weaknesses
1. **Loose causal derivation and misleading equivalence claim:** The paper claims Equation (2) is an "equivalent form" of Equation (1), but Appendix A relies on proportionality ($\propto$) and an arbitrarily fixed trade-off parameter $\lambda=1$. This transforms a theoretically motivated subtraction (Total Direct Effect) into a heuristic addition without rigorous justification. ICLR standards require precise alignment between theoretical framing and practical implementation.
2. **Ambiguous training-free framing:** While marketed as training-free, the method relies on estimating co-occurrence probabilities using 1-2% of downstream training data. Although the authors correctly note this is insufficient for fine-tuning, it deviates from a pure zero-shot protocol and introduces dataset-specific bias that is not thoroughly quantified.
3. **Limited exploration of combination strategies and hyperparameters:** The method exclusively uses additive combination. There is no ablation investigating why addition outperforms alternatives (e.g., multiplicative fusion, max-pooling, or softmax re-weighting), nor is there analysis on the sensitivity to the number of co-occurring labels ($l$) or noisy LLM-generated priors.
4. **Narrow dataset coverage:** Evaluation is restricted to COCO-style detection/tagging datasets. Standard multi-label classification benchmarks like Pascal VOC or NUS-WIDE, which have different label distributions and co-occurrence structures, are absent, limiting claims of broad applicability.

### Novelty & Significance
The novelty is incremental but practically meaningful. Prompt engineering for multi-label tasks and causal debiasing in vision-language models are active areas, and the observation that adding co-occurrence induces hallucination aligns with recent VLM literature. However, the proposed solution is elegantly simple and empirically robust. For ICLR, the contribution sits in the middle tier: it lacks deep theoretical innovation but delivers a lightweight, highly reproducible technique that meaningfully advances training-free multi-label classification. The significance lies in its practicality, compatibility with existing frameworks, and clear demonstration that lightweight prompt calibration can rival parameter-heavy fine-tuning in low-data regimes.

### Suggestions for Improvement
1. **Reframe the theoretical foundation:** Clearly position Equation (2) as a robust heuristic combination rather than a strict causal equivalence. Provide an empirical ablation comparing addition against other fusion strategies (averaging, geometric mean, adaptive weighting) to justify the design choice.
2. **Clarify data dependency and baseline comparisons:** Explicitly position the method as "few-shot co-occurrence estimation" rather than purely training-free. Add experiments comparing LLM-only priors vs. dataset-estimated priors, and analyze performance drops when co-occurrence matrices are intentionally randomized or sparsified to test robustness.
3. **Broaden experimental scope:** Evaluate on standard multi-label datasets outside the COCO family (e.g., Pascal VOC 2012, NUS-WIDE) and include recent VLM prompting baselines (e.g., CoOp/CoCoOp adaptations for multi-label, or recent LLM-VLM retrieval methods) to strengthen empirical claims.
4. **Detail prompt construction and sensitivity analysis:** Provide a systematic analysis of how the number of co-occurring labels $l$ impacts mAP/F1, and discuss failure cases where co-occurrence priors are weak or misleading (e.g., rare object pairs). This will greatly improve reproducibility and practical deployment guidance.

# Spark Finder Review
## How to Improve This Paper

### Missing Experiments (top 3-5 only)
1. **Explicit Thresholding Protocol for F1:** The paper reports F1 without defining how decision thresholds are set. Add a consistent threshold selection protocol (e.g., grid search on a held-out validation split or per-class optimal thresholds) applied uniformly to all baselines, otherwise the claimed gains may simply reflect threshold tuning bias rather than true model improvements.
2. **Modern Open-Vocabulary/Zero-Shot Baselines:** Comparing only against 2022–2023 CLIP adaptations (DualCoOp, TaI, TagCLIP) ignores the current state of zero-shot tagging. Add comparisons against contemporary zero-shot multi-label/VLM methods (e.g., RAM++, instruction-tuned VLMs, or open-vocabulary detectors) to verify the SOTA claim is not outdated.
3. **Statistical Significance Across Seeds:** All results are reported from a single seed without variance metrics. Run each configuration across 3–5 random seeds, report standard deviations, and include statistical significance tests (e.g., paired t-tests or bootstrap intervals) to prove the margins over baselines are robust and not stochastic fluctuations.

### Deeper Analysis Needed (top 3-5 only)
1. **Rigorous Justification of the Causal Approximation:** The leap from the subtraction-based Total Direct Effect (Eq. 1) to the additive dual-prompt formula (Eq. 2) with an unexplained $\lambda=1$ is mathematically unsupported in the main text. Provide a formal derivation with clear assumptions, and empirically analyze how sensitive performance is to $\lambda$ to prove the additive form is theoretically sound rather than a convenient heuristic.
2. **Sensitivity to Co-occurrence Prior Quality:** The method relies heavily on accurate co-occurrence priors, yet degrades with LLM-generated labels. Quantitatively analyze how precision, recall, and noise levels in the co-occurrence graph propagate to prediction accuracy, identifying failure boundaries so users know when the prior will help versus harm.
3. **Inference Cost and Compute Overhead:** Combining discriminative and correlative prompts per class inherently increases text encoding costs and similarity computations. Report the exact additional latency, memory footprint, and FLOPs relative to vanilla CLIP to prove the method remains practical under real-world inference constraints.

### Visualizations & Case Studies (top 3-5 only)
1. **Activation/Attention Maps for Hallucination Suppression:** Figure 8's retrieval ranks are qualitative and insufficient to validate the mechanism. Provide Grad-CAM or ViT attention visualizations for true positives, CoP-induced false positives, and DualPrompt corrections to empirically demonstrate that the dual-prompt actually redirects focus from co-occurring background objects back to the target.
2. **Systematic False Positive/Negative Breakdowns:** Add a quantitative-qualitative table of the most common hallucinated pairs and missed labels, categorizing them by co-occurrence frequency (common vs. rare). This will expose whether the calibration uniformly works or only succeeds in high-frequency correlation regimes, directly testing the generalization claim.

### Obvious Next Steps
1. **Cross-Dataset Zero-Shot Transfer Evaluation:** To genuinely support the "training-free" claim, you should have evaluated co-occurrence matrices estimated on a source dataset (e.g., COCO) and applied them directly to target datasets (VG-256, Objects365) with zero target-specific data included. This directly validates whether the method relies on dataset-specific priors or captures universal correlations.
2. **Ablation Over Prompt Syntax and Template Design:** The performance gains may stem from CLIP's pretraining bias toward specific grammatical structures rather than the dual-prompt mechanism itself. Systematically test multiple prompt templates (e.g., "A photo containing A and B", "A scene with X, possibly Y") across both prompt branches to isolate the contribution of co-occurrence information from superficial linguistic matching.
3. **Ablation on Dual-Prompt Weighting Strategy:** The paper defaults to simple addition $(\lambda=1)$. Include a controlled ablation comparing the additive formulation, the subtractive causal formulation (Eq. 1), and a learnable/adaptive weighting scheme on a validation split to prove the chosen combination is optimal and not an arbitrary choice that masks underlying calibration flaws.

# Final Consolidated Review
## Summary
The paper addresses CLIP's degradation in zero-shot multi-label image classification by explicitly introducing label co-occurrence information at inference time. The authors identify a dual effect of correlative prompts: they recover subordinate objects but simultaneously induce hallucination. To calibrate this, they propose DualPrompt, an additive combination of discriminative and correlative prompt scores motivated by causal mediation analysis. The method yields consistent mAP/F1 gains over training-free and prompt-tuning baselines on MS-COCO, VG-256, and Objects365 without weight updates.

## Strengths
- **Clear empirical characterization of prior-induced hallucination:** The paper convincingly demonstrates that injecting co-occurrence into prompts acts as a double-edged sword, simultaneously boosting true positive recovery and increasing false positives due to prior overfitting. This analysis is well-supported by quantitative breakdowns (Figures 2, 5, 7) and qualitative retrieval visualizations (Figure 8), grounding the proposed calibration in observed model behavior rather than speculation.
- **Effective and lightweight inference-time calibration:** The dual-prompt strategy successfully retains co-occurrence benefits while mitigating hallucination, producing consistent 2–4.5% mAP improvements across ResNet and ViT backbones. The approach requires no gradient updates, integrates seamlessly with local-feature methods like TagCLIP, and uses minimal data (≤2% for prior estimation), offering a practical drop-in enhancement for zero-shot multi-label pipelines.

## Weaknesses
- **Overstated causal theory and unjustified mathematical equivalence:** The paper frames Equation 2 as an "equivalent form" of the Total Direct Effect, but Appendix A relies on proportionality approximations, arbitrarily drops joint probability terms, and empirically fixes $\lambda=1$ without validation. The causal graph (Figure 3) also assumes disentangled discriminative vs. correlative feature pathways that conflict with how transformer encoders entangle visual-linguistic representations. For ICLR, mechanistic rigor is expected; presenting a heuristic ensemble average as a derived causal calibration misaligns theoretical claims with implementation.
- **Ambiguous zero-shot framing and unquantified prior dependency:** While marketed as training-free, the method's peak performance relies on co-occurrence matrices estimated from 1–2% of downstream training labels. This introduces dataset-specific bias that edges into weak supervision. The paper does not systematically analyze how prior quality (e.g., precision of dataset statistics, spurious LLM correlations, or missing rare-class priors) propagates to prediction accuracy, leaving the true boundaries and robustness of the zero-shot claim undefined.
- **Unspecified multi-label evaluation protocol:** Section 3 computes predictions via a softmax over all $q$ classes, which is inherently a single-label normalization. The authors do not explain how these probabilities are converted to independent binary predictions for mAP and F1 calculation (e.g., global vs. per-class thresholds, sigmoid approximation, or raw cosine scaling). Without a transparent thresholding protocol applied identically across baselines, it is impossible to verify whether reported gains stem from the proposed method or from scoring/thresholding inconsistencies.

## Nice-to-Haves
- Systematic sensitivity analysis on the weighting parameter $\lambda$ and alternative fusion schemes (e.g., geometric mean, adaptive weighting) to empirically justify the additive formulation.
- Computational overhead reporting (FLOPs, latency, memory) to quantify the practical cost of additional text-encoder forward passes as the number of co-occurring labels scales.
- Variance reporting across 3+ random seeds, particularly to confirm that marginal gains over strong baselines like TagCLIP are statistically robust.

## Novel Insights
The paper’s most valuable contribution is reframing zero-shot multi-label classification from a feature-extraction bottleneck to an inference-time prior management problem. By empirically isolating the hallucination-inducing mechanism of correlative prompts, it demonstrates that lightweight prompt calibration can effectively decouple useful semantic co-occurrence from spurious linguistic bias. This shifts the design focus away from heavy fine-tuning or architectural modifications toward principled, causally-aligned inference heuristics that preserve CLIP's zero-shot generalization while patching its structural blind spots in multi-object scenes.

## Potentially Missed Related Work
- RAM / RAM++ (Zhang et al., CVPR 2024; Wu et al., ECCV 2024) — open-vocabulary image tagging models that explicitly model attribute-object co-occurrence and large-scale semantic graphs for zero-shot multi-label recognition; comparing against these would clarify the SOTA positioning.
- Causal debiasing frameworks for VLMs (e.g., prompt-based counterfactual reasoning, visual-linguistic mediation analysis) that more rigorously apply do-calculus to isolate visual evidence from language priors; relevant for grounding the proposed additive calibration in established causal inference practices.

## Suggestions
1. **Reframe the theoretical claims:** Explicitly position Equation 2 as an empirically motivated calibration heuristic rather than a strict mathematical equivalence. Remove or revise language implying a formal derivation of Total Direct Effect, and provide a clear empirical ablation over $\lambda$ and alternative fusion strategies to justify the default choice.
2. **Clarify the multi-label scoring pipeline:** Detail the exact post-processing step that converts softmax similarities into multi-label predictions. Specify the threshold selection method (fixed, validation-searched, or per-class), confirm it is applied uniformly to all baselines, and report AP/F1 accordingly to eliminate ambiguity in metric computation.
3. **Quantify prior quality dependency:** Conduct a controlled robustness evaluation by injecting noisy, sparse, or intentionally randomized co-occurrence matrices. Report performance degradation curves relative to prior precision/recall to establish when the method succeeds, when it fails, and how much downstream performance relies on accurate prior estimation.
4. **Benchmark against contemporary tagging models:** Extend the experimental comparison to recent zero-shot/open-vocabulary tagging architectures to verify that the proposed gains hold against modern baselines that natively encode semantic correlations, strengthening the claim of broad applicability beyond COCO-style corpora.
