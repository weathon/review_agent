# Harsh Critic Review
## Section-by-Section Critical Review

### Title & Abstract
- **Title accuracy:** The title accurately reflects the core contribution: a training-free dual-prompt strategy for zero-shot multi-label classification that leverages co-occurrence.
- **Abstract clarity:** Clearly outlines the problem (CLIP's degradation in multi-label due to ignored co-occurrence), the key empirical observation (correlative prompts yield mixed positive/negative effects), and the solution (dual prompts to calibrate predictions). The structure aligns well with ICLR expectations.
- **Claim support:** The claim that the method achieves "better performance than the state-of-the-art methods" is partially supported, but requires nuance. As seen in Table 1, `DualPrompt` alone with ViT-B/16 (67.7 mAP) trails `TagCLIP` (68.7 mAP). The SOTA result relies on combining DualPrompt with TagCLIP. The abstract should clarify that the method is complementary to local-feature extraction methods like TagCLIP, rather than a standalone SOTA across all backbones.

### Introduction & Motivation
- **Motivation & Gap:** Well-motivated. The distinction between single-label and multi-label zero-shot inference is clear, and the identification of CLIP's failure to exploit label correlations during inference is a valid and important gap.
- **Contributions:** Clearly stated. The paper introduces correlative prompts, observes the hallucination/overfitting side-effect, and proposes a dual-prompt calibration framed via causal mediation.
- **Claims & Framing:** The introduction over-attributes CLIP's inability to model co-occurrence to its contrastive pretraining objective. While contrastive learning emphasizes prominent objects, large-scale image-text pretraining inherently captures statistical co-occurrence. A more accurate framing would be that CLIP does not provide an *explicit, inference-time mechanism* to inject dataset-specific co-occurrence priors, which is sufficient for the paper's goals. The causal motivation is introduced effectively but should be positioned as an interpretive lens rather than a strict theoretical guarantee.

### Method / Approach
- **Clarity & Reproducibility:** The high-level pipeline is clear, but critical implementation details are missing, which hinders reproducibility. Specifically, Section 5 does not specify how the probabilities $p(y_k=1|\cdot)$ are normalized when combined in Eq. (2). Since CLIP's softmax is computed over the target label vs. all others, simply adding two probabilities derived from different prompt sets ($P^c$ and $P^d$) can produce uncalibrated outputs. Are you averaging raw logits, summing probabilities, or applying a temperature? This must be specified.
- **Assumptions & Justification:** The derivation in Appendix A transforming the subtraction form (Eq. 1) to the addition form (Eq. 2) relies on mathematically unjustified steps. Treating $\{\mathbf{x}, L_c, L_t^0\}$ and $\{\mathbf{x}, L_t, L_c^0\}$ as jointly distributed events with proportional likelihoods is heuristic. The claim that Eq. (2) is an "equivalent form" of the Total Direct Effect is misleading; it is an empirically motivated heuristic that shifts bias correction into direct effect enhancement. For ICLR standards, I strongly recommend reframing this as a *prompt ensemble with causal-inspired calibration* rather than a rigorous causal derivation.
- **Edge Cases & Failure Modes:** The method only injects *positive* co-occurrence labels. It does not account for mutually exclusive or negatively correlated labels, which are highly informative in multi-label settings. Additionally, the choice of $l=2$ co-occurring labels is justified by LLM noise, but no analysis is provided on how the choice of $l$ or the source of co-occurrence (LLM vs. dataset stats) affects robustness across different domains.

### Experiments & Results
- **Claim testing:** The experiments effectively isolate the contributions of DiP, CoP, and DualPrompt (Section 6.3), supporting the core narrative that calibrating CoP reduces hallucination while retaining gains.
- **Baselines:** Baselines are appropriate (DualCoOp, TaICLIP, CLIP, TagCLIP). However, the comparison in Table 1 is slightly uneven: TagCLIP is only reported for ViT-B/16, while DualPrompt is reported for both ResNet and ViT. A direct ViT-to-ViT comparison shows DualPrompt alone slightly underperforms TagCLIP, reinforcing that the method is synergistic rather than strictly dominant.
- **Missing ablations:** 
  - **Threshold selection:** Multi-label F1 scores heavily depend on thresholding. Section 6.1 does not specify how decision thresholds are chosen (fixed, per-class, or oracle?). If tuned on the validation set, this should be explicitly stated, as it impacts the reported F1 metrics.
  - **Sensitivity to combination weight $\lambda$:** Eq. (2) uses a fixed weight of 1. A sensitivity analysis showing performance stability across $\lambda \in [0.5, 2.0]$ would strengthen the claim of robust calibration.
- **Statistical significance & Error bars:** All results use a single random seed (Sec 6.1) and lack confidence intervals. Given that the SOTA gains (e.g., Table 2: 40.7 vs 39.6 mAP) are ~1.1 points, ICLR reviewers typically require multiple runs or bootstrap confidence intervals to rule out variance. Reporting standard deviations across 3-5 seeds is necessary.
- **Datasets & Metrics:** MS-COCO, VG-256, and Objects365 are appropriate. mAP and per-class F1 are standard and well-suited for multi-label evaluation.

### Writing & Clarity
- **Confusing sections:** The causal graph in Figure 3 and the subsequent explanation in Section 4 are intuitive but lack formal mapping to standard causal notation. The jump from "path $L_t \rightarrow F_d \rightarrow Y$ is disconnected" to the mathematical definition of TDE in Eq. (1) is abrupt. Clarifying that $F_d$ and $F_c$ are not separately observable features, but rather conceptual activation pathways induced by different prompts, would improve clarity.
- **Figures & Tables:** Table 1 and Table 2 are well-structured. Figure 8 (retrieval visualization) effectively illustrates the qualitative differences between DiP, CoP, and DualPrompt. However, the co-occurrence matrices in Figure 7 are visually dense; adding a brief explanation of how the off-diagonal values are normalized would aid interpretation.
- **Overall clarity:** The narrative flow from intuition to method to validation is logical. The main clarity gap remains in the precise mathematical formulation of Eq. (2) and the missing implementation details for probability combination and thresholding.

### Limitations & Broader Impact
- **Acknowledged limitations:** The paper acknowledges that CoP causes hallucination and that LLM-generated co-occurrence can be noisy. It also notes that TagCLIP requires a ViT backbone.
- **Missed limitations:** 
  - **Computational overhead:** DualPrompt requires encoding every label twice (discriminative + correlative). For datasets with hundreds of classes, this doubles the text encoder's similarity computations at inference time. The paper should discuss this latency trade-off and whether text embeddings are cached.
  - **"Zero-shot" vs. "Training-free":** The abstract claims "zero-shot," but Section 5.1 admits relying on 1% of training data to estimate dataset-specific co-occurrence probabilities. This is a valid *training-free* approach, but it is not fully zero-shot in the open-world sense (where no downstream data or domain priors are available). Clarifying this distinction is important for accurate positioning.
  - **Causal framing limitation:** As noted, the mediation framework is heuristic. Acknowledging that the derivation provides an interpretive explanation rather than a strict counterfactual guarantee would align the claims with the methodology.
- **Societal impact:** Not discussed. While standard for method-focused papers, briefly noting that reducing hallucination in multi-label tagging has downstream benefits (e.g., safer content moderation, more accurate medical/image retrieval tagging) would satisfy ICLR's broader impact expectations.

### Overall Assessment
The paper addresses a well-recognized problem in vision-language models: the degradation of CLIP in multi-label zero-shot settings due to unmodeled label correlations. The core proposal—combining discriminative and correlative prompts to balance co-occurrence benefits against hallucination risks—is practically valuable and empirically effective. The stepwise ablation cleanly demonstrates the "positive vs. negative" effect of co-occurrence injection, and the results consistently improve over training-free baselines. However, several issues must be addressed to meet ICLR's standards for methodological rigor. The causal derivation in Appendix A should be softened from a mathematical equivalence to a heuristic calibration inspired by mediation, and crucial implementation details (probability normalization, decision thresholding, inference cost) are currently missing. Additionally, the SOTA claim relies on synergizing with TagCLIP rather than outperforming it standalone, and the lack of multiple random seeds or confidence intervals weakens the statistical robustness of the reported gains. If the authors clarify the mathematical framing, provide standard missing experimental details, and report statistical significance across multiple runs, this will be a strong, practical contribution to zero-shot multi-label classification.

# Neutral Reviewer
## Balanced Review

### Summary
The paper investigates why CLIP underperforms in zero-shot multi-label image classification and identifies the absence of explicit label co-occurrence modeling during inference as a primary cause. It introduces a Correlative Prompt (CoP) to inject co-occurrence priors, observes that CoP induces object hallucination via overfitting, and proposes a DualPrompt method that additively combines CoP with a standard Discriminative Prompt (DiP) to balance correlative and discriminative signals, motivated by a causal calibration framework. Experiments across MS-COCO, VG-256, and Objects365 demonstrate consistent, statistically meaningful improvements over both training-free and parameter-efficient tuning baselines.

### Strengths
1. **Clear problem formulation and practical solution:** The paper precisely isolates CLIP's inability to leverage label dependencies in multi-label zero-shot settings and proposes a straightforward prompt-combination strategy. The method requires no gradient updates and is trivially deployable on frozen VLMs.
2. **Comprehensive and consistent empirical validation:** The authors evaluate across multiple datasets, backbones (ResNet-101, ViT-B/16), and resolutions. Figure 5 provides compelling per-class ablation evidence that DualPrompt recovers the performance drops of CoP across nearly all categories, directly supporting the core claim.
3. **Data-efficient co-occurrence estimation:** Table 1/2 and Figure 6 convincingly show that using merely 1% (or 2%) of labeled data for co-occurrence probability estimation outperforms methods that use the same budget for full prompt fine-tuning, highlighting the approach's sample efficiency.

### Weaknesses
1. **Causal derivation is heuristic and mathematically loose:** The transition from the proposed causal subtraction (Eq. 1) to the additive DualPrompt formulation (Eq. 2) relies on strong independence assumptions that are not stated. Appendix A introduces a trade-off parameter $\lambda$ that is ultimately fixed to 1 empirically, weakening the causal justification and making the method appear more like a heuristic logit averaging scheme.
2. **"Training-free" claim lacks precise scoping:** The strongest results depend on estimating co-occurrence statistics from 1%–2% of downstream training data. While this avoids parameter updates, it still requires supervised annotations, which blurs the line between pure zero-shot and weakly-supervised calibration. The performance degradation when relying solely on LLM-generated co-occurrences (which the authors note are imperfect) is not fully quantified.
3. **Missing computational and efficiency analysis:** Correlative prompts increase token length and require computing additional text embeddings per class. The paper reports no inference latency, FLOPs, or memory overhead comparisons, which are critical for evaluating the practicality of training-free methods, especially on datasets with hundreds of classes.
4. **Limited baseline comparisons for prompt/logit calibration:** The evaluation focuses on tuning-based methods (DualCoOp, TaI) and one other zero-shot method (TagCLIP). It omits direct comparisons to simpler probability calibration techniques (e.g., class-prior logit adjustment, temperature scaling, or multiplicative prompt ensembles), making it harder to isolate the specific contribution of the additive dual-prompt design.

### Novelty & Significance
**Novelty:** Moderate. Prompt ensembling and co-occurrence exploitation are well-known in VLM literature. The novelty lies in the specific diagnostic of CoP-induced hallucination in multi-label CLIP and the formulation of a dual-prompt calibration strategy. However, the causal framing is largely conceptual rather than formally rigorous.
**Clarity:** High. The paper is well-structured, motivations are intuitive, figures (especially Figures 2, 5, 8) effectively communicate the trade-offs and retrieval behaviors. The transition from problem to solution is logical.
**Reproducibility:** High. The method consists of standard prompt templating, cosine similarity scoring, and probability addition. The co-occurrence estimation procedure and hyperparameter settings are clearly described. No complex architectures or proprietary components are used.
**Significance:** Reasonably high. Training-free multi-label classification is a practical and growing use case. The method delivers consistent gains without modifying model weights, making it accessible for real-world deployment. It meets ICLR's empirical standards but requires tighter theoretical grounding and efficiency reporting to stand out among strong prompt-learning submissions.

### Suggestions for Improvement
1. **Formalize or reframe the causal derivation:** Either provide the necessary independence assumptions to rigorously justify Eq. 2 and $\lambda=1$, or explicitly reframe the causal discussion as an intuitive motivation and position the method as a calibrated logit/probability ensemble.
2. **Clarify data dependencies and "zero-shot" positioning:** Explicitly distinguish between parameter-free inference and the use of auxiliary labeled data for co-occurrence estimation. Include a table or ablation purely using LLM-generated priors vs. dataset-estimated priors to quantify the annotation dependency.
3. **Add efficiency metrics:** Report inference time, number of token evaluations, and memory usage per image for DiP, CoP, and DualPrompt, particularly on Objects365 (365 classes). This is essential for practical adoption.
4. **Expand baseline comparisons:** Include simpler calibration baselines such as class-prior-adjusted logits, multiplicative prompt fusion, or independent probability thresholding. This would strengthen the claim that additive dual-prompting is uniquely beneficial.
5. **Analyze synergy with TagCLIP:** The combined DualPrompt+TagCLIP achieves SOTA, but the paper lacks analysis on why these methods complement each other (e.g., global correlative prompts + local patch features). A brief mechanistic discussion or ablation would elevate the empirical contribution.

# Spark Finder Review
## How to Improve This Paper

### Missing Experiments (top 3-5 only)
1. Add a clearly defined zero-shot thresholding protocol for F1 scores (e.g., per-class validation median or fixed global percentile) and report sensitivity curves. Without it, the claimed F1 improvements could stem from implicit threshold optimization rather than genuine predictive capability, undermining the zero-shot performance claims.
2. Add an ablation sweeping the combination weights between $P(CoP)$ and $P(DiP)$ instead of fixing the coefficient to 1 empirically. Without demonstrating robustness to weight variations, the claim that the addition is grounded in causal Total Direct Effect rather than an arbitrary logit-averaging heuristic remains unconvincing.
3. Add baselines comparing against naive logit or feature averaging of individual co-occurring label prompts (e.g., averaging "A photo of {co1}", "A photo of {co2}"). If simple mean similarity performs equally well, the specific correlative prompt formulation provides no structural benefit and the core methodological motivation collapses.

### Deeper Analysis Needed (top 3-5 only)
1. Provide rigorous statistical validation of the causal mediation assumptions, as the Appendix derivation relies on proportionality and mutually exclusive events that do not hold in CLIP's continuous, overlapping embedding space. Without proving the subtracted term actually isolates the indirect effect, the causal framework is merely post-hoc justification masking a simple arithmetic sum.
2. Add a robustness study quantifying performance degradation when injecting random, unrelated, or negatively correlated label pairs into the co-occurrence set. Without characterizing tolerance to prompt noise, the claim that DualPrompt reliably neutralizes the "negative effect" of suspicious co-occurrence is unsupported and the method's practical reliability is unclear.
3. Stratify the per-class results by label frequency (head vs. tail) to isolate whether improvements are driven by common objects with strong dataset biases rather than genuine semantic co-occurrence understanding. If gains vanish on rare or long-tail classes, the claim to robust open-vocabulary zero-shot generalization is significantly weakened.

### Visualizations & Case Studies
1. Plot the empirical distributions of cosine similarity scores for true positives, false positives, and hallucinated predictions across all three prompting strategies. This distributional view is necessary to verify whether the additive combination actually increases separability between real objects and hallucinations or merely shifts the entire score distribution.
2. Generate patch-level attention maps or activation visualizations for ViT backbones when using CoP versus DualPrompt on images with occluded or minor objects. Without showing that DualPrompt preserves discriminative focus on the target while utilizing context, it remains unclear if the method actually improves local recognition or just reinforces confounding global scene priors.
3. Provide a curated gallery of images where CoP severely hallucinates but DualPrompt fails to correct it, alongside a semantic analysis of the failure conditions. Identifying specific visual or contextual scenarios where the additive heuristic breaks down is critical to exposing the method's actual operational boundaries beyond selective success cases.

### Obvious Next Steps
1. Integrate a fully unsupervised co-occurrence extraction mechanism (e.g., mining CLIP's own zero-shot predictions on unlabeled data or leveraging external knowledge graphs). Relying on 1% labeled annotations for estimation compromises the strict "training-free" narrative and severely limits applicability to truly annotation-free deployment scenarios.
2. Quantify the computational inference overhead in terms of additional text encoder passes, memory footprint, and latency per image. Evaluating whether the marginal mAP gain justifies the multiplied forward pass cost is essential for establishing the method's practical utility over efficient single-prompt or lightweight ensemble alternatives.
3. Evaluate the interaction of DualPrompt with continuous prompt tuning or larger template ensembles to determine if the structural modification generalizes beyond discrete prompt engineering. Proving compatibility with adaptive textual representations would significantly strengthen the contribution's relevance to modern VLM adaptation pipelines expected at ICLR.

# Final Consolidated Review
## Summary
The paper proposes DualPrompt, a training-free inference strategy for zero-shot multi-label classification with CLIP that injects label co-occurrence priors via a correlative prompt. Observing that correlative prompts induce object hallucination through over-reliance on contextual co-occurrence, the authors introduce a dual-prompt calibration that additively combines correlative and discriminative prompt probabilities, motivated by a causal mediation framework. Extensive experiments across MS-COCO, VG-256, and Objects365 demonstrate consistent improvements over training-free and parameter-efficient baselines, particularly when synergized with local-feature extraction methods.

## Strengths
- **Clear diagnostic and simple, effective remedy:** The stepwise ablation cleanly isolates the dual nature of co-occurrence injection: Correlative Prompts (CoP) recover missed minor objects but induce hallucination, while the proposed DualPrompt consistently mitigates false positives across nearly all classes (Figure 5). This provides a transparent, interpretable validation of the core hypothesis.
- **High data-efficiency without gradient updates:** Using only 1%–2% of downstream labels to estimate dataset-specific co-occurrence probabilities outperforms prompt-tuning methods that consume the same annotation budget for fine-tuning (Figure 6, Table 1/2). This establishes a highly practical, parameter-free alternative for deployment on frozen VLMs.
- **Strong and consistent empirical gains:** The method improves over vanilla CLIP and competes strongly with specialized multi-label adapters across diverse backbones (ResNet-101, ViT-B/16) and dataset scales (80 to 365 classes), demonstrating robust generalization of the prompt-combination principle.

## Weaknesses
- **Causal framing overstates theoretical rigor:** The derivation in Appendix A transforms a proposed causal subtraction (Eq. 1) into an additive form (Eq. 2) by introducing proportionality assumptions and a trade-off parameter $\lambda$ that is ultimately fixed to 1 empirically. This makes the method a heuristic probability ensemble rather than a mathematically grounded Total Direct Effect estimator. For ICLR standards, the causal narrative should be reframed as an interpretive motivation for the ensemble design, not a formal theoretical guarantee, to avoid misalignment with reviewers' expectations for causal claims.
- **Unspecified zero-shot thresholding protocol for F1:** Multi-label F1 scores are highly sensitive to decision thresholds, yet Section 6.1 does not specify how thresholds are chosen (fixed global, per-class oracle, or validation-tuned). In a strict zero-shot/training-free setting, threshold selection methodology must be explicit; otherwise, reported F1 gains could partially reflect implicit calibration on the validation split rather than genuine predictive superiority.
- **Missing efficiency analysis and opaque synergy claims:** DualPrompt doubles the text-encoder similarity computations per class and increases prompt token length, but no latency, FLOP, or memory overhead is reported. This is critical for practical adoption, especially on large-class datasets like Objects365. Additionally, the state-of-the-art claims depend on combining DualPrompt with TagCLIP, which slightly outperforms DualPrompt alone on ViT backbones (Table 1: 68.7 vs. 67.7 mAP). The paper lacks mechanistic analysis of why global correlative prompts and local patch-based features complement each other, leaving the synergy unexplained.

## Nice-to-Haves
- Report performance stability across multiple random seeds or provide bootstrap confidence intervals, particularly since some marginal gains (~1 mAP) could overlap with evaluation variance.
- Include a straightforward baseline that naively averages predictions or logits from individual co-occurring label prompts (e.g., mean of "A photo of {label2}") to verify that bundling co-occurrence into a single structured template provides unique benefits over simple ensembling.
- Discuss handling of mutually exclusive or negatively correlated labels, which are common in real-world multi-label taxonomies but excluded from the positive-only co-occurrence formulation.

## Novel Insights
The work reveals that CLIP's multi-label degradation is not solely a feature representation bottleneck but an inference-time conditioning problem. By treating co-occurrence as a contextual mediator, the paper shows that discriminative grounding and contextual prior injection can be effectively decoupled and recombined at the probability level. This establishes a lightweight, parameter-free mechanism to inject dataset statistics without retraining or fine-tuning, shifting the focus from architectural modifications to prompt-conditioned bias calibration. The additive formulation demonstrates that mitigating indirect contextual bias is empirically equivalent to amplifying direct discriminative signals, offering a practical blueprint for safer, context-aware zero-shot tagging in vision-language models.

## Suggestions
- **Reframe the theoretical narrative:** Explicitly position the causal graph and Appendix A derivation as a conceptual motivation that justifies why additive calibration works, and clarify that $\lambda=1$ is an empirically validated heuristic. Replace claims of "rigorous equivalence" with "causal-inspired calibration."
- **Specify the F1 thresholding protocol:** State clearly whether thresholds are fixed globally (e.g., 0.5), derived per-class from dataset priors, or tuned on the validation split. If validation-tuned, report zero-shot fixed-threshold results as a baseline to isolate true predictive gains.
- **Report inference efficiency and TagCLIP synergy:** Provide a table of inference time, text-encoder FLOPs, and memory usage for DiP, CoP, and DualPrompt. Add a brief analysis or ablation explaining how DualPrompt's global contextual signals complement TagCLIP's local patch features, clarifying why their combination yields SOTA performance.
