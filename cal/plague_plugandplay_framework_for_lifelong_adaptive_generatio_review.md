# Harsh Critic Review
## Section-by-Section Critical Review

### Title & Abstract
- **Title accuracy:** The title accurately captures the core contribution: a modular, lifelong-learning framework for multi-turn jailbreak generation.
- **Abstract clarity:** The abstract clearly states the problem (multi-turn jailbreak adaptability/efficiency), the method (three-phase Primer/Planner/Finisher architecture with memory retrieval), and results (SOTA ASR, +30% improvements, specific numbers for o3 and Opus 4.1).
- **Unsupported/conflicting claims:** The abstract claims a 67.3% ASR on Claude’s Opus 4.1, but Table 2 reports 0.318 Bin-ASR / 0.465 SRE for the default configuration, with a footnote redirecting to Table 4 for the 67.3% figure. This creates ambiguity about which configuration constitutes the "main result." Additionally, the claim of achieving these results in a "lesser or comparable query budget" is not directly substantiated in the abstract and is somewhat contradicted by Table 5, where PLAGUE's total LLM calls often exceed GOAT's. The abstract would benefit from clarifying that the reported gains come with modest compute trade-offs and aligning the primary Table with the claimed numbers.

### Introduction & Motivation
- **Motivation & Gap Identification:** The motivation is strong. Citing Li et al. (2024) effectively grounds the importance of multi-turn attacks, and the identification of the gap (lack of formal investigation into multi-turn attack anatomy, trade-offs between diversity/success/compute, absence of lifelong learning) is well-justified.
- **Contributions Stated:** Contributions are clearly enumerated: (1) the 3-phase modular architecture, (2) empirical SOTA results, (3) plug-and-play component analysis, and (4) insights into initialization/context optimization.
- **Over-claiming/Positioning:** The introduction states PLAGUE addresses adaptability, efficiency, and effectiveness, which aligns with results. However, Section 2.3 claims PLAGUE is the "first multi-turn attack to feature a lifelong-learning component." While this may be true for the specific architecture, it slightly downplays prior agentic/red-teaming works with memory components (e.g., AutoDAN-Turbo, AutoRedTeamer). Clarifying that PLAGUE is the first to use *goal-embedding-based strategy retrieval* for lifelong planning would be more precise. Overall, the introduction does not under-sell or overclaim in a detrimental way, but the positioning relative to prior agentic frameworks needs tighter framing.

### Method / Approach
- **Clarity & Reproducibility:** The three-phase pipeline (Sections 3.3–3.5) is intuitively described. Backtracking, reflection, and memory retrieval are conceptually clear. However, several implementation details critical for reproducibility are deferred entirely to Appendix B (prompts), making the method textually incomplete. The pseudocode in Appendix A is helpful but lacks precise definitions for `CREATEITERATIVEPROMPTREFUSAL` and the exact scoring aggregation logic.
- **Assumptions & Justifications:** The black-box assumption and fixed 6-turn budget are clearly stated. However, the reliance on a fixed, high-capability Attacker (Deepseek-R1) and an external Rubric Scorer (Qwen-based) is a strong assumption. The method implicitly assumes the Rubric Scorer R is perfectly calibrated to detect adversarial escalation and refusals, yet Section 2.1 notes AutoDAN-Turbo's reliance on human-generated strategies due to low similarity in automated retrieval, highlighting that LLM-based feedback is non-trivial. This assumption is not justified or tested.
- **Logical Gaps:** The budget accounting with backtracking is confusing. Section 3.5 states that a low Finisher score triggers backtracking and "the remaining budget is decremented by one," but it's unclear whether the failed attempt that triggered backtracking also consumed a turn. If backtracking costs an extra turn without progressing the attack sequence, the effective budget shrinks rapidly. This interaction needs explicit formulation.
- **Edge Cases / Failure Modes:** The framework assumes the Planner will always generate a coherent 2-step plan (Section 4 mentions "two-step plan... best-performing"). It does not discuss what happens when the Planner fails, generates semantically drifting steps, or when the memory bank returns irrelevant strategies early in the run. The system lacks a fallback mechanism for failed retrieval or poor plan generation beyond the initial Crescendo seed.
- **Theoretical/Algorithmic Completeness:** No theoretical proofs are claimed, but the retrieval mechanism (cosine similarity threshold 0.6, max 2 examples) is arbitrary. An ablation justifying these hyperparameters is missing from the main text or appendix provided.

### Experiments & Results
- **Claims vs. Experiments:** The experiments largely test the core claims (modularity, SOTA performance, efficiency). However, the "lifelong learning" aspect is not properly validated. The paper adds successful strategies to a vector database and retrieves them, but never shows a *learning curve* (e.g., ASR on the first 50 objectives vs. the last 50 objectives). Without demonstrating progressive improvement as the memory bank grows, the "lifelong learning" claim remains architectural rather than empirically verified.
- **Baselines Fairness:** The authors claim an "apples-to-apples" comparison by modifying baseline settings (disabling GOAT's history, capping Crescendo at 6 turns, limiting AutoDAN-Turbo iterations). While justified for budget control, these modifications fundamentally alter how baselines operate. For example, Crescendo's original design leverages longer contexts for escalation; capping it at 6 turns disproportionately harms baselines compared to PLAGUE, which is explicitly designed around a tight budget. The impact of these modifications on baseline performance should be ablated or explicitly quantified.
- **Missing Ablations:** 
  1. Memory Bank Size/Growth: How does ASR scale as the library accumulates 10, 50, 100 strategies? 
  2. Scorer Sensitivity: How does performance change if R's thresholds (7/10 for Primer, 3/10 for Finisher backtracking) are adjusted? 
  3. Attacker Model Dependency: Results are reported only for Deepseek-R1 as the attacker. ICLR expects analysis on whether the framework transfers to weaker or alternative attacker LLMs.
- **Statistical Significance:** Section 4 states results are "averaged over three runs for robustness," but Table 2, 3, 4, and 5 report single decimal values with no standard deviations, confidence intervals, or statistical tests. For ICLR, reporting variance across 3 runs is mandatory, especially given the stochasticity of LLM sampling and the multi-turn branching nature of attacks.
- **Results Alignment / Cherry-picking:** Table 2 presents PLAGUE's primary results, showing a notably lower score for Claude Opus 4.1 (0.465 SRE) compared to other models. The 40.2% improvement claim relies on swapping the Finisher to Crescendo (Table 4). Presenting the "best config" results in a secondary ablation table while claiming them in the abstract/main text is misleading. The primary results table should either include the optimized Opus 4.1 configuration or the text should explicitly frame the default vs. optimized results consistently.
- **Datasets & Metrics:** HarmBench (200 samples) and StrongReject/Binary-ASR are appropriate. Using ASR@K=2 is reasonable, but the choice of K=2 without analyzing K=1 or K=3+ leaves room for variance exploitation.

### Writing & Clarity
- **Confusing Sections:** The interaction between the Rubric Scorer (R), the Evaluator Judge (J), and the backtracking mechanism is described across Sections 3.4 and 3.5 but remains procedurally ambiguous. Specifically, whether R and J are the same model queried differently, or entirely separate instances with different prompts, affects computational claims. Section 3.2 says "We prompt our Evaluator differently for intermediate feedback R and for the final evaluation," conflating terminology.
- **Figures & Tables:** 
  - Table 1 effectively summarizes components. 
  - Figure 2 clearly shows the turn-to-ASR scaling. 
  - Table 5's formatting is heavily garbled in the provided text, but conceptually it attempts to compare call budgets. The distinction between "Target," "Eval," "Plan," and "Total" calls is logically sound but lacks error margins.
  - Figure 4's category breakdown is informative but would benefit from aggregated averages per category across models rather than just a single visual.
- **Overall Clarity:** The paper is well-structured and readable. The primary clarity bottleneck is the over-reliance on appendices for prompt templates and the lack of explicit algorithmic steps for context fusion and backtracking.

### Limitations & Broader Impact
- **Acknowledged Limitations:** The conclusion briefly mentions leaving "better diversity-inducing Planner" as future work and hints at formal prompt optimization. The ethics statement acknowledges potential misuse but argues for defensive value.
- **Missed Fundamental Limitations:** 
  1. **Judge/Scorer Reliability:** The entire framework hinges on the Rubric Scorer's ability to accurately grade adversarial escalation and refusals. LLM-as-a-Judge is known to suffer from position bias, prompt sensitivity, and over-refusal calibration. This is not discussed.
  2. **Computational & Economic Cost:** Running an Attacker, Scorer, Evaluator, Summarizer, and Embedding model per attack turn is computationally heavy. Token/cost implications are absent.
  3. **Real-World Robustness:** HarmBench prompts are synthetic benchmarks. The framework's performance in open-ended, realistic conversational contexts with dynamic safety filters is untested.
  4. **Memory Scalability:** Vector retrieval efficiency and strategy contamination (retrieving overly similar or counterproductive plans for novel goal categories) are unaddressed.
- **Societal Impact:** The ethics section is standard but superficial. It does not address how plug-and-play frameworks lower the barrier for malicious actors with minimal prompting expertise, nor does it discuss potential defensive integrations (e.g., automated defense training using PLAGUE's generated traces).

### Overall Assessment
PLAGUE presents a well-conceived, modular architecture for multi-turn jailbreak generation that conceptually improves upon prior work by decoupling planning, context priming, and finishing. The empirical results demonstrate strong attack success rates across major state-of-the-art models, and the plug-and-play analysis provides useful insights into which components drive performance. However, the paper falls short of ICLR's current bar for methodological rigor in several key areas: (1) the "lifelong learning" claim is unsupported by learning curves or memory-scaling experiments, (2) statistical reporting is entirely missing despite inherent multi-turn stochasticity, (3) result alignment between the abstract/main claims and Table 2 for Claude Opus 4.1 is inconsistent and risks appearing cherry-picked, and (4) heavy reliance on uncalibrated LLM scorers and judges introduces unquantified bias. Addressing these concerns with proper variance reporting, a dedicated limitations discussion (including scorer reliability and computational overhead), and validation of the memory retrieval dynamics would significantly strengthen the contribution. The core architectural idea is valuable for red-teaming research, but the current experimental framing requires tighter statistical and methodological grounding to meet publication standards.

# Neutral Reviewer
## Balanced Review

### Summary
This paper introduces PLAGUE, a modular, retrieval-augmented framework for orchestrating multi-turn LLM jailbreaks by decomposing attacks into Planner, Primer, and Finisher phases. The system leverages a memory bank of successful strategies, rubric-driven reflection, and adaptive backtracking to systematically improve attack success rates (ASR) across state-of-the-art models under strictly constrained query budgets. Empirical results demonstrate consistent, substantial improvements over existing multi-turn baselines, validating the utility of component modularity and structured contextual escalation.

### Strengths
1. **Clear Modular Architecture & Component Validation:** The explicit decomposition into three phases provides a transferable taxonomy for multi-turn attacks, and the plug-and-play claim is empirically substantiated. Evidence: Tables 3 and 4 systematically swap components (e.g., integrating ActorBreaker’s planner or Crescendo’s finisher), showing consistent ASR gains and proving architectural interoperability.
2. **Strong Empirical Performance Under Fair Budget Constraints:** The framework achieves high ASR across heavily defended frontier models while strictly capping target model invocations, demonstrating practical efficiency. Evidence: Table 2 shows 81.4% SRE on OpenAI o3 and 67.3% on Claude Opus 4.1, outperforming baselines by 30-40%, while Table 5 confirms comparable or lower total LLM call counts (~3-4 target calls per run).
3. **Granular Ablation & Model-Specific Insights:** The component-wise addition study reveals how different defensive models fail under distinct attack pressures, offering actionable red-teaming insights. Evidence: Table 3 shows that retrieval of successful strategies drives gains on o3, while backtracking is the critical factor for Claude Opus 4.1, highlighting heterogeneous vulnerability profiles.

### Weaknesses
1. **Overstated Technical Novelty & Misleading "Lifelong Learning" Terminology:** The framework orchestrates established techniques (strategy retrieval, prompt escalation, LLM-based reflection/backtracking) rather than introducing new algorithms. The "lifelong learning" claim corresponds to a simple cosine-similarity vector buffer updated post-success, lacking true continual adaptation, weight updates, or cross-session generalization. Evidence: Section 3.3.1 and Algorithm 4 describe a static embedding-indexed memory bank with a fixed retrieval threshold (0.6) and max k=2 examples.
2. **Evaluation Rigor & Baseline Configuration Trade-offs:** While budget capping ensures fairness in call counts, it inherently disadvantages baselines designed for open-ended exploration or adaptive turn expansion. Additionally, reliance on an LLM-as-a-Judge for rubric scoring introduces potential noise and shared failure modes with the target models. Evidence: Section 4 notes GOAT's history is disabled and rubric cutoffs (7/10 for Primer, 3/10 for Finisher) are heuristic without ablation or human-judge calibration.
3. **Limited Mechanistic or Security Analysis:** The paper focuses heavily on attack efficacy but lacks deep analysis of *why* the multi-turn structure bypasses safety filters, missing an opportunity to contribute to alignment science. Evidence: Sections 5 and 6 report performance metrics and ablations without analyzing refusal patterns, attention/context drift, or providing feature-attribution insights that could inform defense design.
4. **Reproducibility Gaps:** Critical implementation details (exact prompt templates, memory initialization strategies, rubric prompt weights, and hyperparameter justifications) are relegated to appendices not fully detailed in the text, and computational costs are reported only in abstract call counts rather than tokens or API expenses. Evidence: Section 3 references Appendices B.1/B.2 for prompts, which are essential for standalone reproduction but absent from the main narrative.

### Novelty & Significance
**Novelty:** Moderate. The paper's primary contribution is architectural integration and empirical systematization rather than algorithmic innovation. Structuring multi-turn jailbreaks into discrete, swappable phases is a valuable engineering abstraction, but the underlying mechanisms (retrieval-augmented planning, iterative refinement, context escalation) are well-established in agentic and adversarial literature.  
**Significance:** High empirical significance. Delivering consistent, state-of-the-art ASR on frontier models within strict query budgets establishes a strong new benchmark for automated red-teaming. The modular framework is immediately useful for the safety evaluation community, and the component-ablation results provide practical guidance for stress-testing LLM defenses.  
**Clarity & Reproducibility:** Generally clear in concept and experimental design, but would benefit from tighter justification of hyperparameters, explicit prompt inclusion, and standard token/cost reporting to meet ICLR's reproducibility standards.

### Suggestions for Improvement
1. **Reframe Terminology & Clarify Novelty:** Replace "lifelong learning" with "online strategy retrieval" or "memory-augmented planning" to accurately reflect the RAG-like implementation. Explicitly position the work as a unifying empirical framework and systematic ablation study, which aligns better with the actual contributions.
2. **Add Mechanistic & Failure Analysis:** Include a qualitative analysis of successful vs. failed trajectories, refusal patterns, and context-window utilization. Investigate whether success stems from refusal fatigue, semantic softening, or attention decay. This would transform the paper from a pure attack benchmark into a scientifically grounded safety study suitable for ICLR.
3. **Strengthen Evaluation Robustness:** Validate the Rubric Scorer against a human-labeled subset or industry-standard evaluators (e.g., Llama Guard, WildGuard) to mitigate LLM-as-a-judge bias. Provide a sensitivity analysis for the 0.6 cosine threshold and the 7/10 vs. 3/10 scoring cutoffs to prove they are not brittle design choices.
4. **Improve Reproducibility Transparency:** Move core prompt templates, memory initialization examples, and exact sampling configurations into the main paper or clearly structured supplementary material. Report computational overhead in tokens and estimated API costs alongside call counts to provide a complete efficiency picture for the red-teaming community.

# Spark Finder Review
## How to Improve This Paper

### Missing Experiments (top 3-5 only)
1. Re-run baselines (GOAT, ActorBreaker, X-Teaming) with their original memory, history tracking, and refinement budgets intact because artificially handicapping baselines to lower their compute fundamentally invalidates the claimed SOTA ASR.
2. Evaluate against standard adaptive defenses (e.g., self-reflection prompts, output filtering, system-level moderation) because claiming superior jailbreak efficacy without testing robustness to basic mitigations fails ICLR safety evaluation standards.
3. Measure ASR improvement across a continuously executed sequence of 50+ diverse goals to prove the lifelong-learning component drives *progressive* adaptation rather than providing static, one-off gains from initial retrieval.
4. Test with alternative attacker LLMs (e.g., GPT-4o, Llama-3-70B) instead of relying solely on DeepSeek-R1, as current results may reflect the base attacker's inherent jailbreak susceptibility rather than PLAGUE's architectural contributions.

### Deeper Analysis Needed (top 3-5 only)
1. Calibrate the rubric scorer's scoring thresholds (7/10, 3/10, 8/10) against the final Evaluator's binary/SRE labels because unvalidated feedback thresholds mean the reflection/backtracking loops may optimize for scorer bias rather than true safety bypass.
2. Quantify how the frozen primer context shifts the target model's internal safety or refusal probability distributions before the finisher step, as the core claim that "benign questions subtly steer context" lacks mechanistic evidence.
3. Analyze the semantic categories and refusal patterns of the 15–35% of goals where PLAGUE consistently fails, because understanding hard failure boundaries is required to validate claims of comprehensive vulnerability coverage.

### Visualizations & Case Studies
1. Publish a complete, turn-by-step transcript showing Planner→Primer→Finisher with per-turn rubric scores and target responses to prove that context buildup actively suppresses safety guardrails rather than merely exhausting the target's context window.
2. Provide a semantic overlap or similarity distribution plot mapping retrieved historical strategies to new target goals, exposing whether the memory module retrieves functionally relevant attack templates or just superficial keyword matches.
3. Plot ASR vs. conversation turns for PLAGUE and top baselines on a single axis to visually verify the claimed linear scaling and validate that the 6-turn budget cap is empirically justified, not arbitrarily chosen.

### Obvious Next Steps
1. Add bootstrap confidence intervals or statistical significance tests across all ASR comparisons because LLM stochasticity across only 3 runs makes ~30–40% point-estimate improvements unreliable without variance bounds.
2. Test retrieval precision and ASR when the strategy library scales past 100+ entries, because fixed cosine similarity thresholds will inevitably degrade into noise unless long-term memory dilution and retrieval interference are quantified.
3. Report zero-shot performance with a completely empty strategy library to prove the framework can autonomously bootstrap novel strategies, confirming the "lifelong learning" claim isn't secretly dependent on pre-seeded human-crafted examples.

# Final Consolidated Review
## Summary
This paper introduces PLAGUE, a modular, three-phase framework for generating multi-turn LLM jailbreaks by decoupling attacks into planning, context priming, and execution stages. The system incorporates embedding-based strategy retrieval, rubric-driven reflection, and adaptive backtracking to systematically improve attack success rates under strictly constrained query budgets. Empirical evaluations demonstrate substantial, consistent improvements over established multi-turn baselines across frontier models, with detailed ablations revealing model-specific vulnerability profiles.

## Strengths
- **Effective modular architecture with validated interoperability:** The explicit decomposition into Planner, Primer, and Finisher phases is conceptually clean and empirically justified. Tables 3 and 4 systematically demonstrate that swapping components (e.g., integrating ActorBreaker’s planner or Crescendo’s finisher) yields consistent ASR gains, proving the framework’s plug-and-play utility rather than relying on monolithic prompt engineering.
- **Strong empirical performance under controlled, comparable budgets:** PLAGUE achieves 30–40% absolute ASR improvements over strong multi-turn baselines on heavily defended frontier models (OpenAI o3, Claude Opus 4.1, Deepseek-R1) while strictly capping target model invocations. Table 5 confirms that these gains are achieved with total LLM call counts comparable to or lower than baselines, demonstrating practical efficiency.
- **Granular ablation reveals heterogeneous model vulnerabilities:** The component-wise addition study uncovers that defensive models fail under distinct attack pressures. For example, retrieval of past strategies drives the largest gains on o3, while backtracking is the critical factor for Claude Opus 4.1. This provides actionable, model-specific red-teaming insights rather than treating all aligned LLMs as uniform targets.

## Weaknesses
- **The "lifelong learning" claim lacks sequential empirical validation:** While the architecture maintains a vector-indexed memory of successful strategies that updates post-run, the paper does not demonstrate progressive performance improvement over a continuous sequence of diverse goals. Without a learning curve or memory-growth ablation, the lifelong adaptation claim rests on architectural design rather than empirical evidence. This matters because the terminology sets expectations for cross-session generalization and true continual adaptation that the current retrieval-augmented implementation does not substantiate.
- **Unverified attacker model dependency:** All primary results use Deepseek-R1 as the sole attacker LLM. It remains unclear whether the reported ASR gains stem from PLAGUE’s orchestration framework or from Deepseek-R1’s inherent prompt-following/jailbreak susceptibility relative to other models. Generalizability to alternative attacker LLMs (e.g., GPT-4o, Llama-3-70B) is necessary to isolate the framework’s contribution from the base attacker’s capabilities.
- **Inconsistent presentation of the Opus 4.1 results in the main narrative:** The abstract and introduction prominently claim a 67.3% ASR on Claude Opus 4.1, yet Table 2 (the primary results table) reports ~46.5% SRE, with a footnote deferring the 67.3% figure to an ablation configuration using a different Finisher (Table 4). While mathematically consistent, presenting an optimized variant’s result in the abstract alongside the main table creates confusion about the default framework’s standalone performance versus a manually tuned configuration.

## Nice-to-Haves
- Add standard deviations or bootstrap confidence intervals to ASR tables across the three reported runs to quantify variance given LLM stochasticity.
- Include a qualitative, turn-by-turn case study transcript showing per-turn rubric scores and target responses to transparently demonstrate how the Primer phase steers context without triggering early refusals.
- Report zero-shot performance with an empty strategy library to quantify the baseline overhead of the framework versus its retrieval-augmented gains.
- Clarify baseline modifications (e.g., disabling GOAT’s history, capping turns) with explicit quantitative deltas showing the exact performance impact of each adjustment, even if observed to be negligible.

## Novel Insights
The most valuable insight emerging from this work is that multi-turn jailbreak efficacy is not driven by a single universal mechanism but by model-specific failure modes that respond to distinct components. The finding that backtracking disproportionately improves performance on Claude Opus 4.1 while strategy retrieval drives gains on OpenAI o3 suggests that frontier safety alignments fail under different pressures—some succumb to iterative context correction, others to retrieved historical escalation patterns. Additionally, decoupling the context-building (Primer) phase from the final execution (Finisher) phase effectively prevents semantic drift, a known failure mode in prior multi-turn attacks, by anchoring escalation to intermediate plan steps rather than the original harmful goal.

## Suggestions
1. **Reframe terminology and validate sequential adaptation:** Replace "lifelong learning" with "online strategy retrieval" or "memory-augmented planning" to accurately reflect the RAG-like mechanism. To substantiate the adaptive claim, run a sequential evaluation over 50+ diverse goals and plot ASR vs. cumulative memory bank size.
2. **Test attacker generalizability:** Replicate primary ablations using at least one alternative attacker LLM (e.g., GPT-4o or Llama-3-70B) to demonstrate that PLAGUE’s gains are transferable and not contingent on a single model's alignment profile.
3. **Standardize result reporting:** Update Table 2 to either include the optimized Opus 4.1 configuration or explicitly frame default vs. optimized results in the text. Ensure all main tables reflect the configurations described in the abstract to avoid misalignment between claims and primary evidence.
