# Harsh Critic Review
## Section-by-Section Critical Review

### Title & Abstract
- **Accuracy**: The title accurately reflects the core contribution: a self-play RL framework with multi-role interaction for long-context LLMs.
- **Clarity**: The abstract clearly outlines the problem (lack of labels/verifiable rewards for long-context reasoning), the method (three cyclic roles, automated curriculum, Gaussian difficulty reward), and results (consistent gains across 12 models, outperforming RL baselines).
- **Supported Claims**: The claim of "outperforming equally sized models fine-tuned on large-scale annotated data" is supported by Table 1 (e.g., Qwen2.5-7B+SPELL > Qwen2.5-7B-Instruct). However, the abstract states "7.6-point gain in pass@8," while Section 4.2 and Figure 3 emphasize that the model surpasses Gemini-2.5-pro at `pass@4`. This minor metric discrepancy in the abstract should be aligned. Additionally, claiming it "raises the performance ceiling" is qualitative; the abstract would benefit from specifying the exact benchmark or metric scale (average across 6 tasks? specific dataset?).

### Introduction & Motivation
- **Problem Motivation & Gap**: The bottleneck of long-context RLVR—prohibitive annotation costs, declining human accuracy on extra-long tasks, and lack of programmatic verifiers—is well-motivated and grounded in recent empirical findings (e.g., LongBench-V2 human performance drop).
- **Contributions**: Clearly stated and accurate. The shift from external supervision to a closed-loop, self-rewarding system for long contexts addresses a genuine gap in current self-play literature, which largely focuses on short-context code/math with executable verifiers.
- **Claims**: The introduction slightly over-claims by implying the system requires *zero* human intervention. Section B and Appendix G reveal reliance on human-curated prompt templates, document clustering, and a hand-designed grounding filter. For ICLR, it is crucial to calibrate the "label-free/without human supervision" claim to acknowledge that prompt engineering and pipeline design still constitute significant human prior. The comparison setup with Gemini-2.5-pro in Figure 3 also borders on apples-to-oranges (different decoding strategies, proprietary model internals, unspecified pass@k for the baseline), which weakens the positioning.

### Method / Approach
- **Clarity & Reproducibility**: The three-role loop, memory mechanism, and reward formulations are clearly described. Algorithm 1 provides a helpful procedural overview. Hyperparameters (G=8, L=3, m=5, LR, batch size) are specified.
- **Assumptions & Justifications**: The method assumes a single policy can seamlessly switch roles via prompting and that joint optimization won't cause severe gradient interference. The assumption that responder accuracy `r_bar_res` accurately reflects question difficulty assumes the responder is reasonably calibrated, which may not hold early in training.
- **Logical Gaps / Reward Design**: Equation 5 (`r_i,j^ver = I(v_i,j == v_i^ver)`) trains the verifier to match its own majority vote. This is inherently self-reinforcing: if all `G` verifier rollouts hallucinate the same incorrect judgment, they reinforce each other. While the "consistency update" (aligning with rule-based CEM on verifiable tasks) mitigates this for numerical/multiple-choice tasks, **open-ended semantic QA tasks lack rule-based anchors**. Section F.6 acknowledges this risk, but the paper does not provide a robust theoretical or empirical guarantee against verifier collapse on purely semantic questions where no external judge is used during training.
- **Edge Cases / Failure Modes**: The grounding filter (Section 3.1) discards questions answerable *without* context, but does not filter questions that are simply unanswerable given the provided documents. If the questioner generates a hallucinated premise, the responder will fail, the verifier may incorrectly penalize or reward, and these toxic pairs are stored in `H_C`. The dynamic sampling strategy (Section 3.3) helps but doesn't fully prevent curriculum drift into unanswerable regions.

### Experiments & Results
- **Claim Testing**: The experiments extensively test generalization across model sizes, families, and architectures. The comparison to static RLVR directly tests the curriculum advantage claim.
- **Baselines**: The comparison to RLVR using DeepSeek-R1-0528 synthesized data is appropriate and fair. Appendix F.4 adds valuable comparisons to SoLoPO, LongPO, and QwenLong-L1. However, a direct comparison to other self-play RL frameworks adapted to long-context (e.g., R-Zero or AZR with LLM-as-judge) is missing from the main text, limiting the relative positioning within the self-play literature.
- **Missing Ablations & Statistical Reporting**: The paper reports averages over 8 runs (Section 4.1) but **fails to report standard deviations, confidence intervals, or p-values in any table or figure**. For ICLR, this is a notable omission given the high variance inherent in RLVR and self-play training. Ablations in Table 2 show component drops, but an ablation comparing *Unified Single Policy* vs. *Separate Role-Specific Policies* is critical to validate the core architectural claim that one shared model can co-evolve three distinct capabilities without interference.
- **Dataset & Metric Appropriateness**: Benchmarks are standard and well-chosen. Using CEM + LLM-as-judge for open-ended QA is reasonable, but the paper uses `gpt-oss-120b` for evaluation judgment. Potential judge bias or prompt sensitivity isn't analyzed. The `pass@k` scaling claim (Figure 3) is compelling, but the y-axis lacks explicit metric labels, and the Gemimi comparison lacks methodological transparency (e.g., identical sampling temperature, max tokens, and pass@k for the proprietary model).

### Writing & Clarity
- **Clarity**: The narrative flow is logical, and the role separation is easy to follow. Mathematical notation is generally consistent.
- **Figures & Tables**: Table 1 is comprehensive but dense; adding row highlights for significant gains (beyond bold) would improve readability. **Figure 3** y-axis is unlabeled (presumably accuracy % across tasks), and **Figure 4** y-axis values (0.2-0.8) are explained in the caption as "1 - pass@1" but could be explicitly labeled as "Question Difficulty (1-Pass@1)" on the axis for immediate comprehension. **Figure 5a** visualizes reward functions but lacks explicit y-axis scales, making the "steeper penalty" claim purely qualitative.

### Limitations & Broader Impact
- **Acknowledged Limitations**: The authors honestly note the lack of theoretical convergence guarantees, the 16K training context limit, and reliance on prompt templates/corpus preprocessing.
- **Missed Limitations**: The computational cost of generating `G` verifier rollouts for every responder rollout (`G^2` total verifications per question) scales poorly. While Appendix F.1 claims it's only ~50% of responder time, this ratio will worsen as models and contexts scale. The paper does not discuss how this impacts feasibility for 100K/1M+ context or larger MoE models. Furthermore, the method's success heavily depends on the base model's initial long-context attention and retrieval capabilities; weak base models with needle-in-haystack failure modes may not bootstrap successfully, but this boundary isn't empirically mapped.
- **Broader Impact / Failure Modes**: The Ethics statement is standard. Section F.6 thoughtfully discusses reward hacking (questioner stagnation, responder mode collapse, verifier self-delusion), which is a strong point. However, the risk of the self-play loop amplifying subtle biases present in the Ultra-Fineweb corpus or generating plausible but factually hallucinated long-form Q&A that passes the verifier is underexplored.

### Overall Assessment
SPELL presents a compelling, well-engineered framework that addresses a genuine bottleneck in long-context LLM training: the lack of scalable, label-free reward signals. The multi-role self-play design, automated curriculum, and unified policy update are conceptually clean and empirically validated across a wide range of models. The work stands as a solid step toward autonomous long-context alignment and offers practical insights into stabilizing self-rewarding loops. However, for ICLR acceptance, the paper requires two key improvements: (1) rigorous statistical reporting (standard deviations/error bars across the 8 runs) to substantiate the claimed gains, especially against strong RLVR baselines; and (2) a deeper mechanistic analysis of the verifier's reliability on purely semantic tasks where rule-based consistency checks are unavailable, alongside an ablation isolating the unified policy optimization from role-specific training. The Gemini-2.5-pro pass@4 comparison, while intriguing, should be reframed with methodological parity or explicitly caveated as a scaling benchmark rather than a direct performance claim. With these clarifications and added statistical rigor, the contribution is well-positioned for publication.

# Neutral Reviewer
## Balanced Review

### Summary
This paper introduces SPELL, a self-play reinforcement learning framework that enables a single LLM to autonomously evolve its long-context reasoning capabilities by cycling through three roles: questioner, responder, and verifier. By leveraging a history-based curriculum, a Gaussian reward function centered at the model's competency frontier, and a self-calibrated verifier, SPELL generates reliable reward signals without human annotations. Extensive experiments across 12 diverse LLMs demonstrate that the method consistently improves performance on six long-context benchmarks, outperforms static RLVR baselines, and generalizes to 100K context lengths.

### Strengths
1. **Innovative Closed-Loop Architecture for Unverifiable Domains:** The integration of questioning, answering, and verification within a unified policy effectively addresses the scarcity of programmatic reward signals in long-context reasoning. The verifier's self-consistency mechanism (Eq. 4) combined with consistency updates aligned to rule-based checks (Sec 3.2) robustly mitigates false negatives in open-ended tasks, evidenced by a +3.2 point average gain over the verifier-less ablation (Table 2).
2. **Comprehensive Empirical Validation and Generalization:** The experimental scope is rigorous, evaluating 12 models spanning dense/MoE architectures and base/instruct/reasoning variants. SPELL shows consistent gains (+6.9 to +16.2 points on average) and notably achieves zero-shot generalization to 100K tokens despite training solely at 16K (Table 1). The pass@k analysis further demonstrates that SPELL raises the model's test-time exploration ceiling, surpassing strong baselines like *gemini-2.5-pro* at pass@4.
3. **Principled Curriculum and Reward Design:** The Gaussian reward mapping (Eq. 7) targeting a 50% success rate provides a mathematically grounded approach to curriculum learning. Section 4.4 convincingly shows that this design yields more stable training entropy and controlled response length growth compared to peer methods like AZR and R-Zero, preventing the questioner from collapsing into trivial or impossibly hard prompts.
4. **Thorough Analytical Depth:** The paper goes beyond standard benchmarks by including hyperparameter sensitivity (Table 3), computational cost breakdown (Sec F.1), and transfer to short-context reasoning (Sec F.2), where SPELL-trained models show gains of +6.9 to +8.7 points on math benchmarks. This holistic analysis strengthens confidence in the method's robustness and efficiency.

### Weaknesses
1. **Misalignment Between Abstract Claims and Method Details:** The abstract and introduction state that SPELL incorporates a curriculum that "gradually increases document length." However, the methodology (Sec 3.1) fixes the training context to 16K tokens, and the curriculum primarily escalates *question complexity* via history memory and distractor documents rather than raw context length. While generalization to 100K is empirically shown, framing the training loop as expanding context length is inaccurate and overstates the curriculum's scope.
2. **Lack of Analysis on Single-Policy Role Interference:** Training a single model to optimize three distinct objectives (Eq. 10) carries inherent risks of gradient interference or catastrophic forgetting, especially when the verifier and responder have divergent optimal behaviors. The authors acknowledge the absence of a theoretical framework for co-evolutionary dynamics in the limitations, but the paper lacks empirical investigation (e.g., gradient cosine similarity analysis or loss trajectory decoupling) to prove that the unified update does not suffer from destructive gradient conflict.
3. **Verifier Dependence on Rule-Verifiable Tasks for Calibration:** The verifier's consistency update relies on aligning with CEM-based rule rewards. For datasets dominated by highly open-ended or subjective answers where CEM yields zero signal, the self-calibration mechanism has no anchor. The paper does not address how SPELL behaves if rule-verifiable samples are absent or extremely sparse, leaving a potential failure mode for highly generative long-context tasks unexamined.
4. **Insufficient Isolation of RL vs. Data Generation Benefits:** The RLVR baseline synthesizes data using DeepSeek-R1-0528 but does not include a static SFT or DPO baseline trained on the exact same self-generated dataset. Without this comparison, it remains ambiguous whether SPELL's gains stem from the superiority of the RL self-play loop itself or simply from the high quality/diversity of the dynamically generated curriculum data.

### Novelty & Significance
**Novelty:** High. SPELL extends the self-play RL paradigm beyond math and code (domains with external verifiers) to long-context reasoning with a unified policy and an adaptive internal verifier. The Gaussian difficulty-targeting reward and role-specific dynamic sampling offer a fresh, principled approach to multi-role co-evolution.  
**Significance:** High. The method directly tackles the annotation bottleneck in long-context model training, demonstrating that label-free self-play can match or exceed instruction-tuned models trained on human-annotated data. Its strong generalization and test-time scaling make it a highly relevant contribution for the ICLR community interested in scalable alignment and reasoning.

### Suggestions for Improvement
1. **Clarify the Curriculum Description:** Revise the abstract and intro to accurately reflect that the curriculum escalates *question complexity, multi-hop reasoning, and distractor load* rather than raw token length. Emphasize the 100K generalization as an emergent property of robust reasoning rather than direct curriculum training.
2. **Analyze Gradient Dynamics:** Add an empirical analysis of gradient interference between roles, such as computing the cosine similarity between questioner and responder/update gradients or tracking per-role loss trajectories. This would substantiate the claim that the unified policy effectively balances co-evolution without degradation.
3. **Test Verifier Robustness in Absence of CEM:** Conduct a controlled experiment or ablation where the consistency update is disabled or rule-verifiable samples are removed, evaluating how much the verifier drifts on open-ended tasks. Propose a fallback mechanism (e.g., entropy-based confidence filtering) for purely non-verifiable domains.
4. **Add a Static Supervised Baseline on Self-Generated Data:** Include an SFT or DPO baseline trained on the dataset generated by the initial SPELL questioner/responder loop. This will cleanly isolate the marginal benefit of the RL self-play optimization and strengthen the argument for RL over standard fine-tuning in this setting.

# Spark Finder Review
## How to Improve This Paper

### Missing Experiments (top 3-5 only)
1. Add a direct SFT baseline trained on a comparable volume of high-quality human or large-scale synthetic long-context data. The paper claims to surpass instruction-tuned models relying on extensive annotations but omits this direct comparison, leaving the data-efficiency claim unsubstantiated.
2. Report greedy decoding (temperature=0) results for the pass@k scaling analysis. Claiming to surpass gemini-2.5-pro via pass@4 can be achieved merely by inflating output diversity with hallucinations; deterministic evaluation is required to prove the actual reasoning ceiling is raised.
3. Evaluate the framework with training context lengths intermediate to 16K (e.g., 32K, 64K). Relying entirely on 16K training to claim "long-context generalization" to 100K risks conflating genuine multi-hop reasoning improvements with positional encoding extrapolation or the base model's inherent length robustness.

### Deeper Analysis Needed (top 3-5 only)
1. Quantify the verifier's false-positive and calibration rates on non-rule-verifiable tasks (e.g., LongBench-V2, Frames). Since the entire self-play reward signal hinges on internal verification, proving the verifier does not collapse into rewarding plausible hallucinations is essential to trust the optimization loop.
2. Analyze gradient interference and loss trajectories across the three distinct roles. Training a single parameter set to simultaneously act as questioner, responder, and verifier risks negative cross-role transfer; separate loss curves and gradient norm tracking are needed to prove the unified policy update remains stable.
3. Perform explicit decontamination analysis between the Ultra-Fineweb/DocMath training documents and all evaluation benchmarks. Given widespread long-context benchmark leakage in current LLMs, ruling out memorization as a primary driver of the reported gains is critical for ICLR.

### Visualizations & Case Studies
1. Provide qualitative case studies of generated question-response-verification triples across training steps, specifically highlighting failures on open-ended tasks. This directly reveals whether the verifier learns true semantic grounding or degenerates into template matching or blind agreement with incorrect responder outputs.
2. Visualize attention or retrieval attribution (e.g., Integrated Gradients or attention rollouts) over the full 16K context before and after training. Without evidence that the model actually learns to locate and synthesize information across long distractor-heavy documents, "long-context reasoning" gains may simply reflect shortcut learning or parametric recall.
3. Plot the joint trajectory of question generation entropy, responder success rate, and verifier disagreement. A unified chart tracking these metrics will expose whether the Gaussian difficulty targeting successfully maintains the learning frontier or if the curriculum degenerates into mode collapse and reward stagnation.

### Obvious Next Steps
1. Report a rigorous compute budget (total tokens generated, FLOPs, and GPU-hours normalized against standard RLVR/SFT). The stated ~8 hours on 8×A100s for 16K self-play RL seems disproportionately low; transparent efficiency accounting is required to support claims of practical viability and scalability.
2. Benchmark directly against other contemporary zero-data/self-evolution paradigms (e.g., R-Zero, Absolute Zero, DAPO) adapted for long-context. Proving superiority over a single static-data RLVR baseline is insufficient for ICLR; the specific mechanistic value of the 3-role self-play loop must be isolated against modern alternatives.
3. Release the full generated curriculum trajectory (QA pairs per iteration) and raw verifier decision logs. Given the opacity of self-play reward dynamics, providing this data is mandatory for reproducibility and allows rigorous auditing for long-term quality degradation or subtle reward hacking.

# Final Consolidated Review
## Summary
SPELL introduces a unified, three-role self-play reinforcement learning framework (questioner, responder, verifier) that autonomously evolves long-context reasoning capabilities in LLMs without human annotations or programmatic verifiers. By coupling a history-based curriculum with a Gaussian difficulty-targeting reward and a self-consistent verifier calibrated on rule-verifiable signals, the method enables stable co-evolution of task generation, reasoning, and reward assessment. Extensive experiments across 12 models demonstrate consistent gains on long-context benchmarks, strong zero-shot generalization to longer inputs, and superior test-time scaling over static RLVR baselines.

## Strengths
- **Closed-loop reward generation for unverifiable domains:** SPELL effectively bypasses the lack of programmatic verifiers in long-context reasoning by integrating a self-consistent verifier that produces stable, semantically aware rewards. The +3.2 average point gain over the verifier-less ablation (Table 2) empirically validates this design.
- **Principled curriculum design that stabilizes training:** The Gaussian difficulty reward targeting a 0.5 success rate provides a mathematically grounded curriculum that prevents questioner collapse into trivial or impossible prompts. Section 4.4 demonstrates this yields more stable training entropy and controlled response length growth compared to prior self-play methods (AZR, R-Zero).
- **Broad empirical validation and strong generalization:** The framework is rigorously tested across 12 diverse models (dense, MoE, base, instruct, reasoning), showing consistent improvements (+4 to +16 avg points) and remarkable zero-shot generalization from 16K training contexts to 100K evaluations (Table 1), indicating it learns robust grounding and multi-hop reasoning rather than length-specific memorization.

## Weaknesses
- **Misalignment between curriculum description and implementation:** The abstract and introduction state that the curriculum "gradually increases document length," but the training loop explicitly caps the context window at 16K tokens (Sec 3.1, Appendix E.1). Difficulty escalation actually stems from accumulating historical QA pairs and increasing distractor documents, while the 100K results are zero-shot generalization. This textual overstatement misrepresents the mechanism and overclaims the curriculum's direct scope.
- **Verifier calibration dependency on rule-verifiable signals:** The verifier's consistency update relies on aligning with rule-based Cover Exact Match (CEM) rewards to filter self-reinforcing errors. On purely open-ended or highly subjective tasks where CEM yields no signal, the self-consistency mechanism lacks an external anchor. The paper does not empirically quantify verifier drift or failure rates in fully unverifiable regimes, leaving a potential calibration vulnerability unexamined.
- **Lack of analysis on unified policy gradient interference:** Jointly optimizing three distinct roles via a single policy (Eq. 10) carries inherent risks of gradient conflict or capacity dilution, particularly when questioner and responder objectives diverge during early training. While role-specific dynamic sampling mitigates sample imbalance, the paper provides no empirical analysis (e.g., gradient cosine similarity, per-role loss trajectories, or a unified-vs-separate policy ablation) to verify that simultaneous multi-role optimization remains stable and interference-free.

## Nice-to-Haves
- Reporting standard deviations or error bars across the 8 independent evaluation runs would help contextualize the robustness of gains against RLVR baselines, though reporting averages is common practice in this setting.
- Including a supervised fine-tuning (SFT) or DPO baseline trained on a snapshot of the self-generated curriculum data would cleanly isolate the marginal benefit of the RL self-play loop from the intrinsic quality of the dynamically generated QA pairs.
- Clarifying the methodological parity for the Gemini-2.5-pro pass@4 comparison (e.g., decoding temperature, max tokens, and sampling strategy used for the proprietary model) would strengthen the test-time scaling claim.

## Novel Insights
The Gaussian difficulty reward exhibits a latent data-filtering property: incorrectly formatted or hallucinated question-answer pairs naturally cluster at the extremes of success rates (near 0 or 1), causing them to fall into the low-reward tails of the distribution and be suppressed during optimization without explicit filtering. Furthermore, the verifier demonstrates that sparse rule-verifiable signals (like CEM on math or multiple-choice tasks) can successfully bootstrap semantic judgment on non-verifiable, open-ended tasks, suggesting that verifiable and unverifiable reasoning capabilities can co-calibrate within a single self-play loop rather than requiring separate supervision streams.

## Suggestions
- Revise the abstract and introduction to accurately describe the curriculum as escalating *reasoning complexity and distractor load* rather than raw context length, and explicitly frame the 100K results as emergent zero-shot generalization.
- Add a brief empirical analysis of training dynamics, such as per-role loss curves or gradient cosine similarity across the three roles, to substantiate that the unified policy update does not suffer from destructive gradient interference.
- If feasible, conduct a controlled ablation evaluating verifier stability on fully open-ended task subsets when the CEM-consistency update is disabled, and discuss fallback mechanisms (e.g., entropy-based confidence filtering or diversity penalties) for purely non-verifiable domains.
