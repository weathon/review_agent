# Harsh Critic Review
## Section-by-Section Critical Review

### Title & Abstract
The title accurately reflects the core contribution. The abstract clearly outlines the paradigm shift (transcript-only supervision for LTA) and summarizes the technical pipeline (alignment → pseudo-labels → cross-modal grounding → segmentation/anticipation decoding). However, the claim that LTA "has been tackled exclusively in a fully supervised manner" is slightly overstated. As noted in Section 1 and 2, semi/weakly-supervised variants exist (e.g., Zhang et al., 2021). The abstract should be precise: this is the first method relying *exclusively* on transcripts without any frame-level or next-action weak labels. 

### Introduction & Related Work
**Introduction:** The motivation is well-grounded and the contributions are explicitly listed. The logical link between transcripts and LTA ("semantic abstraction captures procedural regularities") is intuitive but could be sharpened. Specifically, how does the model handle activities with high variability in step order or optional substeps? Addressing this early would strengthen the methodological justification.
**Related Work:** The coverage of TAS, LTA, and sequence alignment is comprehensive. However, the differentiation from Kim et al. (2024) ("language-based anticipation without explicit time annotations") and Zhang et al. (2021) needs to be crisper. Kim et al. predicts symbolic sequences, while TbLTA outputs dense frame-level forecasts. This distinction is critical for positioning the paper but is currently underemphasized. Similarly, explicitly contrasting TbLTA with weakly-supervised TAS methods that *could* be adapted for LTA (by training a TAS head on full videos and appending a forecasting head) would better isolate the novelty of your unified alignment+anticipation objective.

### Methodology
This section contains the most substantive technical concerns:

1. **Splitting the Transcript for ATBA (Section 3.1 & Problem Definition):** You state that $k^*$ (the observation boundary) is unknown at inference, and the model must implicitly estimate it. During training, however, ATBA requires partitioning $Y$ into $Y_{obs}$ and $Y_{future}$. How is this partition determined without temporal annotations? If you use a fixed temporal ratio (e.g., $\alpha T$) to split the transcript, you are implicitly training with a strong prior on observation length. If ATBA jointly optimizes the split, the mechanism must be detailed. This is a critical logical gap in reproducing the training procedure.
2. **Differentiability of the Masked Cross-Attention (Section 3.1, Eq 1-2):** You describe a "binary local mask $M \in \{0, 1\}^{N \times T}$" that restricts attention to neighborhoods around predicted action occurrences. If applied as a hard mask on attention logits or values, gradient flow will be zero for masked positions, breaking end-to-end training. Is this mask implemented via a soft relaxation (e.g., Gumbel-Softmax, sigmoid thresholding, or additive bias)? The equations in the provided text are garbled, but the conceptual design needs explicit clarification on how differentiability is maintained.
3. **Duration Loss Assumption (Section 3.2.3, Eq 7):** The duration loss regresses against class-wise priors $\hat{d}_{y_i}$ computed from the *observed* portion. This assumes action durations are stationary across observed and future intervals. In procedural tasks, this often fails (e.g., initial setup steps vs. rapid execution or cleanup phases). While the momentum buffer is creative, this assumption should be explicitly acknowledged as a limitation, or empirically validated (e.g., via an ablation showing correlation between observed and future durations per class).
4. **Stochastic vs Deterministic Contradiction:** Section 3.1 describes a deterministic architecture (parallel transformer decoder + linear-chain CRF, typically solved via Viterbi/argmax). Yet, Section 4.2 and Table 1 caption discuss "stochastic results" and a "probabilistic framework" where TbLTA captures "multiple plausible futures." There is no mention of a generative component (e.g., diffusion, latent sampling, or CRF marginalization) in the methodology. How are diverse futures sampled? If this is deferred to supplementary material, the core mechanism must at least be signposted in Section 3.

### Experiments & Results
1. **Missing Primary Results in Table 1:** In the provided manuscript, Table 1 lists baselines (Cycle Cons., FUTR, ObjectPrompt, ActFusion, WS-DA) but omits the row(s) for TbLTA. While this may be a parser artifact, it obscures the central empirical claim. The comparison must be explicitly tabulated with TbLTA's scores alongside bold/underscore formatting.
2. **Statistical Significance:** Several performance gains over baselines are narrow (e.g., ~0.5-1.0 points on MoC). Given the inherent variance in action anticipation across splits, reporting mean ± standard deviation and statistical significance tests (e.g., paired t-tests across folds) is essential to substantiate claims of superiority, particularly against strong supervised baselines.
3. **Baseline Selection:** The weakly-supervised baseline (Zhang et al., 2021) is appropriate but sparse. To firmly establish the "first transcript-only" claim, it would be valuable to include a strong weakly-supervised TAS baseline (e.g., ECTC, ATBA, or OTAS) trained with your exact protocol (full-video encoding + appended decoder) as a lower-bound comparator. This isolates the gain from your architecture/losses versus the mere use of transcripts.
4. **EGTEA Protocol:** You evaluate mAP on verb predictions only. Given that transcripts include verb-noun compositions, restricting to verbs simplifies the task but reduces comparability to noun-aware or compositional LTA methods. This design choice should be justified in Section 4.1.

### Limitations & Broader Impact
The limitations section is notably brief. ICLR expects a candid discussion of failure modes. Key aspects missing:
- **Transcript Errors/Incompleteness:** Real-world transcripts (e.g., from ASR or imperfect generation) often contain noise, missing steps, or hallucinations. How does TbLTA degrade under transcript corruption?
- **Non-Procedural Tasks:** The method heavily exploits procedural regularity. How does it generalize to tasks with high branching or weak temporal priors?
- **Duration Estimation Challenges:** As noted in Section 4.4, duration prediction remains weak. This limits practical deployment in robotics/HRI where timing is crucial.
- The broader impact is not discussed. Given LTA's use in surveillance, assistive robotics, and human monitoring, a brief note on potential misuse, bias in action datasets, or failure-critical scenarios is expected for ICLR submissions.

### Overall Assessment
TbLTA presents a timely and conceptually compelling direction for Long-Term Action Anticipation by removing the dependency on dense frame-level annotations. The use of transcripts for alignment, cross-modal grounding, and global sequence supervision is well-motivated, and the reported performance is promising. However, the paper currently leaves several technical mechanisms underspecified, particularly the handling of the unknown observation boundary during training, the differentiability of the binary attention mask, and the generation of stochastic forecasts. Experimentally, the missing primary results in Table 1, lack of statistical validation for close margins, and thin limitation discussion weaken the empirical and scholarly rigor expected at ICLR. Addressing these methodological clarifications, providing complete and statistically robust comparisons, and expanding the limitations to cover transcript noise and duration non-stationarity would significantly strengthen the manuscript. The core contribution is novel and valuable, but requires tighter technical formalization and more rigorous experimental reporting to meet the ICLR acceptance bar.

# Neutral Reviewer
## Balanced Review

### Summary
This paper introduces TbLTA, the first framework for dense long-term action anticipation trained exclusively with weak video transcripts, completely removing the need for costly frame-level boundary annotations. The approach integrates a temporal alignment module for pseudo-label generation, CTC and CRF losses for sequence consistency, cross-modal attention for semantic grounding, and a self-supervised duration estimator to forecast future actions. Experiments on Breakfast, 50Salads, and EGTEA demonstrate that transcript-only supervision yields results competitive with, and at times superior to, fully supervised baselines.

### Strengths
1. **Clear and impactful problem formulation:** The authors correctly identify a major bottleneck in LTA: the reliance on dense, frame-level annotations that do not scale. Formulating the first weakly-supervised LTA setting using only ordered transcripts (Sec. 1, Sec. 3) effectively lowers the annotation barrier and expands applicability to real-world, long-horizon video tasks.
2. **Effective integration of complementary learning signals:** The model successfully bridges symbolic transcripts and continuous visual features through a well-designed pipeline. The use of ATBA for soft pseudo-labeling (Sec. 3.1), CTC for frame-to-transcript alignment (Eq. 4), and a conditional random field for anticipating coherent sequences (Eq. 5-6) addresses key challenges in weak supervision, namely boundary uncertainty and error propagation.
3. **Strong empirical validation across multiple benchmarks:** TbLTA achieves competitive or superior performance compared to fully supervised methods at specific observation horizons (e.g., outperforms supervised baselines on Breakfast at 30% observation in Table 1) and notably improves performance on rare/low-shot classes in EGTEA (Table 2). The qualitative results (Fig. 3) also support the claim that the model maintains temporal coherence while anticipating future segments.

### Weaknesses
1. **Incremental methodological novelty:** While the problem setting is novel, the architecture primarily stitches together established techniques from weakly-supervised segmentation (ATBA, CTC, cross-attention, CRF) and adapts them to LTA. ICLR typically expects a principled learning contribution or a new theoretical insight. Here, the novelty lies more in the application and engineering integration than in a new learning paradigm or algorithmic breakthrough.
2. **Incomplete ablation and missing hyperparameter transparency:** The ablation study (Sec. 4.3) only reports Top-1 MoC and lacks breakdowns across anticipation horizons, limiting insight into how each component affects long-horizon forecasting specifically. Furthermore, crucial training details are omitted: the loss weighting coefficients ($\gamma_1, \gamma_2, \gamma_3$), optimizer type, learning rate schedule, and exact batch sizes are not specified, hindering reproducibility.
3. **Questionable generalizability of the duration loss:** The affinity-based duration loss (Eq. 7) relies on a momentum-based buffer of observed class durations to supervise future predictions. This assumes strong temporal stationarity and repetitive action durations within a video, which does not hold in many real-world scenarios (e.g., cooking steps with highly variable timing, or unseen action combinations). The paper acknowledges duration prediction remains a challenge but does not provide a mitigation strategy or failure-mode analysis for this assumption.

### Novelty & Significance
**Novelty:** Moderate. The transcript-only weakly-supervised LTA setting is a clear and timely contribution. However, the methodological components are largely borrowed from prior work on weakly-supervised TAS and sequence modeling, with limited algorithmic innovation beyond their careful integration and adaptation to dense anticipation. **Clarity:** High. The problem setup, architecture, and loss design are logically structured and well-explained, though the progressive 3-stage training schedule could be formalized more clearly. **Reproducibility:** Moderate. While dataset splits and feature extractors are specified, the absence of key hyperparameters, loss balancing coefficients, and full ablation metrics makes faithful replication difficult without supplementary material. **Significance:** High for video understanding and scalable human-machine interaction, as it demonstrates that dense LTA can be decoupled from expensive boundary labels. For the broader ICLR community, the significance is somewhat constrained by the applied nature of the contribution and the lack of a generalized learning framework that could transfer to other weakly-supervised sequence tasks.

### Suggestions for Improvement
1. **Expand ablation rigor:** Include full anticipation horizons (10-50%) for each ablated component, not just average MoC. This will clarify whether, for example, CTC loss stabilizes short-horizon vs. long-horizon forecasting, or if the cross-modal attention primarily benefits semantic accuracy vs. temporal alignment.
2. **Provide complete training specifications:** Explicitly list optimizer type, learning rates, scheduler, batch sizes, and the exact values or scheduling strategy for $\gamma_1, \gamma_2, \gamma_3$ in the main text. A brief pseudocode or algorithmic block for the 3-stage progressive training would significantly improve reproducibility.
3. **Stress-test the duration prior and add failure cases:** Since duration estimation is identified as a remaining challenge, include an analysis of when the momentum buffer succeeds or fails. Compare the proposed duration loss against simpler baselines (e.g., uniform duration assumption or learnable prior per action across the whole dataset) to quantify its actual benefit.
4. **Strengthen baseline comparisons:** Add a direct comparison with recent language/vision-language models adapted to anticipation (e.g., AntGPT, PALM) under a fair zero/few-shot prompt setting. This will better position transcript-based weak supervision against modern foundation model paradigms.

# Spark Finder Review
## How to Improve This Paper

### Missing Experiments (top 3-5 only)
1. Add a minimal transcript-only baseline (e.g., ATBA pseudo-labels + CTC directly feeding the LTA decoder) to prove that the proposed architectural modules contribute beyond simply applying weak supervision to a standard pipeline.
2. Quantify pseudo-label alignment accuracy against ground-truth segment boundaries to verify the temporal alignment module actually learns meaningful correspondences rather than exploiting dataset-specific ordering biases.
3. Evaluate predicted action durations against ground-truth boundaries instead of comparing them to the self-trained momentum buffer, which creates a circular metric that cannot validate true duration estimation capability.
4. Provide an ablation contrasting the 3-stage progressive training with standard end-to-end optimization to rule out training instability or hyperparameter fragility as confounders for the reported gains.

### Deeper Analysis Needed (top 3-5 only)
1. Analyze whether DistilBERT embeddings of action labels provide genuine semantic grounding or merely act as high-dimensional learnable IDs, since transcripts here contain no natural language descriptions beyond class names.
2. Report error analysis stratified by action frequency, observation horizon, and segment variability to pinpoint exactly where transcript-only supervision degrades and whether the claimed robustness is dataset- or class-specific.
3. Investigate model sensitivity to transcript-video mismatches (e.g., skipped, repeated, or out-of-order steps), as strict sequence-to-sequence alignment assumes perfect procedural compliance that breaks in uncurated real-world videos.
4. Quantify the isolated contribution of the CRF refinement versus a standard sequence-level cross-entropy or autoregressive loss to prove it enforces valid temporal transitions rather than masking decoder uncertainty or posterior collapse.

### Visualizations & Case Studies (top 1-3 only)
1. Visualize the learned cross-modal attention weights over training epochs to demonstrate that the model conditions video features on semantically relevant transcript actions rather than attending uniformly or memorizing positional priors.
2. Provide side-by-side visualizations of predicted alignment paths vs. ground-truth boundaries with corresponding anticipated frames to expose whether boundary noise directly propagates into anticipation failures or if the decoder generalizes beyond noisy labels.
3. Show explicit failure cases where anticipation diverges from the future due to branching action possibilities, highlighting the fundamental limits of deterministic transcript alignment in open-ended tasks.

### Obvious Next Steps (top 1-3 only)
1. Harmonize evaluation metrics across all benchmarks (e.g., adopt frame-level MoC/ED for EGTEA instead of verb-only mAP) to enable a valid cross-dataset comparison and substantiate the claim of a unified framework.
2. Compare against recent self-supervised or contrastive video representation baselines (e.g., MAE, DINO on I3D features) to determine if transcript supervision truly provides a unique inductive bias beyond standard unsupervised representation learning.
3. Release standardized transcript-only splits, preprocessing scripts, and exact alignment constraints to establish a reproducible benchmark, which is mandatory for any paper claiming to introduce the "first framework" in a new weakly-supervised setting.

# Final Consolidated Review
## Summary
This paper introduces TbLTA, the first framework for dense long-term action anticipation trained exclusively using video transcripts as weak supervision, eliminating the need for costly frame-level boundary annotations. The method integrates a temporal alignment module to generate pseudo-labels, CTC and CRF losses for sequence consistency, and a cross-modal attention mechanism to ground visual features with transcript semantics. Experiments across three standard benchmarks demonstrate that transcript-only supervision yields competitive performance with fully supervised baselines, with particular robustness on data-imbalanced classes.

## Strengths
- **Novel and impactful problem formulation:** The authors correctly identify and formalize a previously unaddressed gap in LTA: the feasibility of dense future forecasting using only ordered action transcripts. This formulation significantly lowers the annotation barrier and aligns the task with scalable, real-world video analysis pipelines.
- **Effective integration of complementary weak-supervision signals:** The pipeline logically bridges symbolic transcripts and continuous visual streams. The combination of ATBA for soft pseudo-labeling, CTC for global transcript consistency, and cross-modal attention for semantic grounding directly addresses boundary uncertainty and error propagation, with ablations confirming each module's utility.
- **Demonstrated benefit for low-resource generalization:** Results on EGTEA show that high-level semantic supervision mitigates the performance drop on rare/low-shot classes compared to fully supervised counterparts. This empirically validates the core intuition that transcript-level procedural grammar provides a stronger inductive bias than local visual features alone when training data is sparse or imbalanced.

## Weaknesses
- **Methodological novelty is primarily integrative:** The architecture adapts established weak-supervision primitives (ATBA alignment, CTC marginalization, standard cross-attention, and a linear-chain CRF) rather than introducing new learning principles or theoretical insights. While the problem setting is novel, the algorithmic contribution reads as a careful engineering adaptation of known tools to LTA, which falls short of the deeper methodological innovation typically expected at ICLR.
- **Circular evaluation of the duration prediction component:** The affinity-based duration loss (Eq. 7) regresses predictions against a momentum buffer $\hat{d}_{y_i}$ constructed from the model's own segmentation outputs. Because this buffer is self-generated rather than derived from ground-truth segment lengths, the component's effectiveness cannot be independently validated. This circular metric obscures whether duration modeling actually improves anticipation accuracy or merely acts as an opaque regularizer.
- **Incomplete ablation transparency for the core LTA task:** The ablation study reports only averaged Top-1 MoC accuracy, which collapses the temporal horizon dimension. Since LTA's central challenge is forecasting over varying future horizons ($\beta \in \{10\%, 20\%, 30\%, 50\%\}$), failing to decompose component contributions across these horizons makes it impossible to assess whether modules like CTC or cross-attention stabilize long-range forecasting or only improve short-term alignment.

## Nice-to-Haves
- Explicitly clarify how the transcript is partitioned into $Y_{obs}$ and $Y_{future}$ during training (e.g., whether a fixed temporal ratio $\alpha$ dictates the split or if it is dynamically optimized), as this detail is currently underspecified.
- Report standard deviations across dataset splits to contextualize narrow performance margins against strong supervised baselines.
- Analyze the semantic contribution of DistilBERT embeddings versus high-dimensional class IDs to confirm that cross-modal attention leverages genuine linguistic grounding rather than positional memorization.
- Include a robustness analysis evaluating TbLTA under partially corrupted, out-of-order, or incomplete transcripts to assess real-world viability beyond clean benchmark annotations.

## Novel Insights
The work successfully demonstrates that for procedural, goal-directed videos, the sequence grammar encoded in transcripts provides a stronger global prior for future forecasting than local visual boundary cues. By replacing dense frame-level labels with transcript-level alignment, the model effectively learns procedural syntax over pixel-perfect temporal precision, yielding better generalization in low-resource regimes. However, the heavy reliance on established weak-supervision components and the inability to independently validate duration estimation underscore a persistent gap in the field: symbolic sequence alignment remains robust for categorical anticipation, but mapping that alignment to continuous, variable-duration forecasting without ground-truth temporal anchors remains largely heuristic.

## Suggestions
- Replace or supplement the circular duration buffer evaluation with an analysis against available ground-truth segment frame counts, or explicitly reframe duration prediction as an auxiliary heuristic with a clear failure-mode analysis for actions exhibiting high intra-class temporal variance.
- Expand the ablation tables to report performance across all anticipation horizons ($\beta$ values) for each removed component, explicitly showing whether cross-modal attention and CTC primarily stabilize long-range forecasts or short-horizon segmentation.
- Provide full training specifications (loss weights $\gamma_1, \gamma_2, \gamma_3$, optimizer type, learning rate schedule, batch sizes) and a concise pseudocode block for the three-stage progressive training in an appendix or supplementary material to ensure strict reproducibility.
