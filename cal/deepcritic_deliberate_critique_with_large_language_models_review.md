# Harsh Critic Review
## Section-by-Section Critical Review

### Title & Abstract
- **Accuracy:** The title accurately captures the core contribution: a framework for generating deliberate, step-wise critiques.
- **Clarity:** The abstract clearly outlines the problem (shallow critiques in current LLM critics), the method (two-stage SFT + RL pipeline with iterative seed curation), and key results (outperforms strong baselines on benchmarks and improves generator refinement).
- **Supported Claims:** The claim that DeepCritic outperforms DeepSeek-R1-Distill models and GPT-4o is directly supported by Table 1. The claim regarding better refinement feedback is supported by Table 3 and Appendix H. No unsupported overstatements are present in the abstract.

### Introduction & Motivation
- **Motivation & Gap:** The gap is well-motivated. The observation that existing LLM critics produce superficial, echo-chamber reasoning rather than genuine verification aligns with recent literature on process supervision and LLM-as-a-judge. The authors correctly identify that accurate step-level judgment and actionable feedback are bottlenecks for scalable oversight.
- **Contributions:** Clearly stated: (1) iterative seed critique generation pipeline, (2) two-stage training (SFT + RL), (3) strong empirical performance on verification, and (4) effectiveness in test-time scaling/refinement.
- **Claims Calibration:** The introduction avoids over-claiming. Notably, the authors explicitly acknowledge that `Qwen2.5-Math-PRM-7B` achieves higher F1 scores than their model on some subsets but lacks refinement capability (Section 4.2), which is an honest and necessary calibration for ICLR readers.

### Method / Approach
- **Clarity & Reproducibility:** The pipeline is logically structured and reproducible. Section 3.2 and Appendices A-C provide detailed prompts, hyperparameters, and data filtering criteria. Equation formulations (2-5) clearly define the step-wise critique process.
- **Assumptions & Justification:** 
  - The method assumes `Qwen2.5-72B-Instruct` can reliably produce high-quality initial and in-depth critiques. This is standard for teacher models but relies heavily on the teacher's own critique reasoning capabilities, which aren't independently verified.
  - The SFT data filtering criterion (retaining only solutions where *all* in-depth critique judgments match ground truth, Section 3.2.1) is strict and ensures label quality, but may create a distribution skewed toward "easy to verify" solutions. The model might not learn robust behaviors for ambiguous or partially correct steps.
- **Logical Gaps / Edge Cases:** 
  - The RL stage relies on a sparse binary accuracy reward (Section 3.2.1 & 4.1). While Appendix L shows an informativeness reward caused degradation (likely reward hacking), the paper does not discuss whether the binary reward might collapse the "deliberate" CoT behavior during RL. The model could learn to generate stylistically similar long critiques without performing actual verification, as long as the terminal label matches.
  - The task formulation treats steps as strictly correct/incorrect. In mathematical reasoning, valid alternative derivations or suboptimal-but-correct steps are common. The impact of this binary framing on the critic's robustness to non-canonical reasoning paths is not discussed.

### Experiments & Results
- **Testing Claims:** The experiments thoroughly test the main claims. Table 1 validates error identification, Table 3 and Appendix H validate refinement/test-time scaling, and Section 4.3 validates the iterative seed generation design.
- **Baselines & Fairness:** Baselines are strong and appropriately chosen (PRMs, instruction-tuned LLMs of varying scales, reasoning-optimized models, and GPT-4o). Comparisons are mostly at the 7B scale, which is fair and relevant for deployment.
- **Missing Ablations / Analyses:** 
  - **Cost & Latency:** A critical missing dimension is computational cost. Generating 8K-token deliberate critiques per solution is vastly more expensive than evaluating a scalar PRM score. The paper should quantify inference latency/VRAM usage and FLOP cost compared to baseline PRMs/critics.
  - **Causal Role of "Deliberation":** While Table 8 shows the frequency of multi-perspective/meta-critique behaviors, the paper lacks an analysis linking critique length or explicit self-correction frequency to final judgment accuracy. Do solutions requiring more tokens to critique actually see higher accuracy, or is length purely stylistic?
- **Error Bars / Statistical Significance:** Results are reported as point estimates without variance, confidence intervals, or multiple random seeds. Given that RL training (GRPO) can be highly seed-sensitive, reporting mean ± std across at least 3 seeds would significantly strengthen empirical rigor per ICLR standards.
- **Dataset & Metrics:** F1 on ProcessBench/MR-GSM8K/PRM800K is appropriate and standard. The refinement evaluation on MATH500/AIME24-25 is well-suited to demonstrate downstream utility.

### Writing & Clarity
- **Overall Clarity:** The paper is well-organized, technically precise, and easy to follow. The distinction between SFT (teaching format/reasoning structure) and RL (incentivizing accuracy) is clearly articulated.
- **Figures/Tables:** Table 1 and Table 3 are clear and informative. Figure 3's caption and methodology (using GPT-4o to automatically detect self-correction) are clearly explained, though this introduces a secondary LLM-evaluation bias that should be acknowledged in the main text, not just implied. Equation 5 contains a parser-induced formatting artifact but the mathematical intent is recoverable and does not impede understanding.

### Limitations & Broader Impact
- **Acknowledged Limitations:** The authors honestly discuss label noise impact (Appendix K), the risk of reward hacking with auxiliary rewards (Appendix L), and constraints on response length during RL due to compute limits.
- **Missed Fundamental Limitations:** 
  - **Scalability to Non-Verifiable Domains:** The entire pipeline (both for SFT filtering and RL auto-labeling) relies on unambiguous step-level correctness. In open-ended or subjective domains, Monte Carlo rollout correctness cannot be reliably determined. While Appendix M shows promising summarization results using a reward model proxy, generalizing this to complex, non-verifiable reasoning without ground truth remains a major open challenge.
  - **Instruction-Following & Critic-Generator Alignment:** Table 3 notes that `DS-R1-Distill-Qwen-7B` ignores stop instructions and leaks answers during critique. This highlights a critical failure mode in weak-to-strong supervision pipelines: if the generator doesn't adhere to critique formatting/stopping constraints, the refinement loop breaks down. This deserves more explicit discussion in the main limitations section.
- **Broader Impact:** The ethics statement is brief but adequate. The work aligns with positive scalable oversight goals. No significant negative impacts are overlooked.

### Overall Assessment
This paper presents a well-motivated and technically sound two-stage framework for enhancing LLM critique capabilities in mathematical reasoning. The iterative seed generation pipeline (initial + in-depth/meta-critique) effectively injects deliberate reasoning behaviors during SFT, and the subsequent RL stage successfully boosts step-level identification accuracy to state-of-the-art levels among LLM critics. The empirical results across multiple verification benchmarks and refinement tasks are robust and clearly support the main contributions. However, to meet ICLR's high standards for empirical rigor and deployment readiness, the paper should address: (1) the absence of variance/error bars across training seeds, (2) a quantitative analysis of inference cost/latency compared to standard PRMs, and (3) a deeper mechanistic investigation into whether the long-formCoT is causally driving accuracy improvements or merely serving as stylistic priors trained via SFT. The modest refinement gains on the 72B generator (+3.6%) also suggest weak-to-strong supervision remains an open challenge rather than a solved one. Despite these points, the methodological clarity, honest ablation of reward designs, and strong empirical performance make this a solid contribution to process supervision and LLM oversight. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to include cost metrics and statistical variance.

# Neutral Reviewer
## Balanced Review

### Summary
The paper proposes DeepCritic, a two-stage training framework designed to enhance LLM critique capabilities for mathematical reasoning by moving beyond shallow, echo-like step evaluations. It first constructs a 4.5K seed SFT dataset via iterative prompting of a strong teacher model, combining initial critiques with in-depth multi-perspective or meta-critiques, then applies GRPO-based reinforcement learning using either human-labeled or Monte Carlo auto-annotated data. Extensive experiments demonstrate that the resulting 7B critic substantially outperforms existing PRMs and LLM-based critics on standardized error identification benchmarks, exhibits strong test-time scaling, and effectively improves downstream generator performance through verified majority voting and feedback-guided refinement.

### Strengths
1. **Comprehensive and Rigorous Empirical Validation:** The evaluation spans three established benchmarks (MR-GSM8K, PRM800K, ProcessBench) with consistent F1 metrics, strong baselines (including GPT-4o and DeepSeek-R1-Distill), and thorough secondary analyses (test-time scaling via Maj@8, verified majority voting, critique-based refinement). The ablation in Section 4.3 cleanly isolates the value of iterative seed generation over direct distillation.
2. **Well-Motivated and Structured Pipeline:** The two-stage SFT+RL design directly targets the identified failure mode of superficial critiques. The explicit incorporation of multi-perspective verification and meta-critiquing into the seed data (Section 3.2.1) provides a clear, reproducible recipe for injecting deliberate reasoning behavior into smaller models.
3. **Strong Attention to Practical Scalability:** The exploration of fully automated RL data construction via strict Monte Carlo sampling with filtering (Section 3.2.2, Appendix J/K), combined with robustness analyses for label noise and binary-search acceleration, demonstrates thoughtful engineering. The successful cross-model transfer (LLaMA3.1, self-improvement in Section 4.4, and supervision of 72B+ generators in Section 5.2.2/Appendix H) underscores real-world applicability.

### Weaknesses
1. **Limited Algorithmic Novelty:** The core methodology composes well-established techniques: iterative CoT data synthesis, rejection filtering, SFT, and GRPO optimization. While the "deliberate critique" framing and pipeline integration are effective, the paper does not introduce fundamentally new learning objectives, architectural changes, or theoretical insights beyond recent verifier and self-correction literature.
2. **High Teacher Dependency and Compute Intensity:** The initial SFT data generation relies on extensive multi-turn prompting and filtering with Qwen2.5-72B (Appendix C.1: 16 samples per step for in-depth critique). While self-curation is attempted, Table 2 shows a significant performance drop (Avg F1 38.2 vs 54.1), indicating that strong teachers remain critical for high-quality seed data. The auto-annotation for RL also requires massive MC rollouts, incurring substantial compute costs that are not explicitly quantified.
3. **Reward-Design Tension and Refinement Robustness:** The RL stage uses a sparse binary accuracy reward. The authors note in Appendix L that an "informativeness-aware" reward causes reward hacking (longer but unhelpful critiques), revealing an unresolved gap between optimizing judgment accuracy and producing actionable feedback. Furthermore, refinement experiments (Table 3) rely on strict step-by-step formatting prompts; the modest absolute gains and lack of analysis on when feedback succeeds/fails limit claims about real-world generator improvement.

### Novelty & Significance
Novelty is moderate but execution is high-quality. The conceptual components (iterative critique generation, meta-evaluation, SFT, and RL) are familiar in the verifier/reasoning literature, but their careful integration, strict data filtering, and thorough empirical characterization deliver solid value. Significance for ICLR is strong: the work directly tackles scalable oversight and weak-to-strong supervision in mathematical reasoning, provides transparent training recipes, and openly discusses practical trade-offs (reward design, auto-annotation, teacher dependence). It will serve as a reliable baseline and practical guide for researchers building process critics, verifiers, and automated refinement pipelines.

### Suggestions for Improvement
1. **Provide Deeper Mechanistic & Case Analysis:** Beyond reporting self-correction frequency (Figure 3), include a granular error-case breakdown showing which types of reasoning failures (e.g., arithmetic, logical leaps, misapplied formulas) are reliably caught vs. missed by the deliberate critiques. This would clarify *why* the pipeline works and where it still struggles.
2. **Explicitly Quantify Compute & Data Costs:** Report approximate token volume, GPU hours, or FLOPs for the SFT seed generation (72B prompting, multi-sample filtering) and MC auto-annotation. Compare these costs against training baselines like Qwen2.5-Math-PRM-7B to ground claims of "scalability" and help practitioners weigh trade-offs.
3. **Strengthen Refinement Evaluation:** Analyze successful vs. failed refinement cases qualitatively to demonstrate what makes a critique actionable. Consider running refinement without enforcing rigid step-by-step generation prompts to assess real-world robustness, and report per-benchmark refinement deltas to show consistency across difficulty levels.
4. **Sharper Positioning Against Recent Verifiers/PRMs:** Clarify the exact trade-offs between DeepCritic's natural-language critique outputs and scalar PRM scores. Discuss how this approach compares conceptually and empirically to recent process-supervision methods (e.g., Qwen2.5-Math-PRM, Critique-GRPO, RLHF-Reward-Modeling) to better highlight the unique contribution of deliberate, iterative critique generation.

# Spark Finder Review
## How to Improve This Paper

### Missing Experiments (top 3-5 only)
1. Run an ablation where the generator receives only the error index from DeepCritic (masking the critique text) versus receiving the full deliberate critique. Without this, the claim that "detailed feedback" drives generator improvements is confounded; gains may simply stem from more accurate error localization rather than the deliberation itself.
2. Benchmark the inference-time compute, latency, and token cost of generating step-wise deliberate critiques compared to standard PRMs or direct critics. The paper heavily claims "scalable oversight," but without demonstrating that the performance gains justify the significant computational overhead, the scalability claim is unverified.
3. Conduct a direct human or strong-LLM audit on a stratified sample of the auto-labeled Numina RL data to quantify false-positive rates. The Monte Carlo heuristic assumes generator failure implies step incorrectness, which systematically mislabels correct but difficult steps on which the rollout model simply lacks knowledge; validating this noise floor is essential for the RL results to be credible.

### Deeper Analysis Needed (top 3-5 only)
1. Analyze how the RL phase alters critique behavior compared to the SFT model, particularly regarding the observed drop in self-correction frequency. You must demonstrate that the binary accuracy reward isn't causing the model to collapse into shallow pattern-matching or heuristic shortcuts that merely mimic deliberation without genuine reasoning depth.
2. Provide a correlation analysis between critique length (or CoT depth) and judgment accuracy across different benchmarks. This is necessary to prove that the performance gains are driven by actual deliberate reasoning improvements rather than simple verbosity or increased test-time compute acting as a brute-force heuristic.
3. Quantify the systematic biases in your auto-labeling heuristic across problem types (e.g., algebra vs. combinatorics). Different math domains have different roll-out success probabilities even from correct states, and without showing the heuristic is uniformly reliable, the generalizability of the RL-trained model is suspect.

### Visualizations & Case Studies
1. Present explicit failure cases where the model produces long, seemingly deliberate critiques that confidently justify an incorrect judgment. Showing where the deliberation process hallucinates valid-sounding but flawed logic is crucial for reviewers to trust the model's reliability in high-stakes oversight scenarios.
2. Visualize the distribution of judgment accuracy against critique token count or reasoning step count for both DeepCritic and baselines. This plot would directly reveal if there is a diminishing return point where added deliberation becomes noise, validating the efficiency of your method.
3. Provide a side-by-step comparison of a successful refinement versus a failed refinement, highlighting exactly which parts of the deliberate critique were actionable to the generator and which were ignored. This exposes whether the generator truly benefits from the critique structure or just self-corrects upon re-reading the prompt.

### Obvious Next Steps
1. Integrate DeepCritic into a multi-turn iterative refinement loop to evaluate stability over multiple correction cycles. Real-world oversight requires the critic to adapt to evolving solutions, and a single-turn refinement experiment cannot prove the critiques don't degrade into hallucinations or conflict as the generator changes its reasoning path.
2. Open-source the full auto-labeled Numina RL dataset used for training. Given the critical reliance on the Monte Carlo heuristic for the paper's scalability claims, external validation of the label quality by the community is expected at ICLR for methods built on synthetic data curation.

# Final Consolidated Review
## Summary
This paper introduces DeepCritic, a two-stage training pipeline designed to enhance the step-wise mathematical critique capabilities of LLMs. The method first curates a 4.5K seed dataset by iteratively prompting a strong teacher model to produce initial critiques followed by in-depth multi-perspective or meta-critiques, then applies supervised fine-tuning to teach deliberate reasoning formats. A second stage applies GRPO-based reinforcement learning using either human-labeled data or strictly filtered Monte Carlo auto-annotations. The resulting 7B critic achieves state-of-the-art step-level error identification among LLM critics, demonstrates consistent test-time scaling, and improves downstream generator performance via verified majority voting and feedback-guided refinement.

## Strengths
- **Rigorous and well-calibrated empirical evaluation:** The method consistently outperforms strong baselines, including 7B/8B process reward models, reasoning-distilled models (DeepSeek-R1-Distill), and GPT-4o across multiple step-level benchmarks. The authors transparently calibrate their claims against larger PRMs that score higher on identification but lack refinement utility, demonstrating academic honesty and precise contribution positioning.
- **Effective pipeline for injecting deliberate reasoning:** The iterative seed curation strategy (initial + in-depth critique generation with strict label alignment filtering) successfully teaches smaller models multi-perspective verification and self-correction. Ablations (Table 1, Sec 4.3) cleanly isolate the value of this structured curation over naive distillation, proving the quality of the synthetic data recipe.
- **Practical engineering for scalable supervision:** The exploration of fully automated RL data construction via Monte Carlo rollouts, coupled with robustness analyses for label noise (Appendix K), binary-search acceleration (Appendix J), and preliminary self-improvement results (Table 2), demonstrates a thoughtful approach to reducing human annotation dependency while maintaining label quality.
- **Demonstrated downstream utility and weak-to-strong scaling:** Beyond verification metrics, the critic effectively boosts generator performance through critique-based refinement, successfully supervising even a 72B model (Sec 5.2.2). This validates the practical relevance of deliberate natural-language feedback for test-time scaling and oversight.

## Weaknesses
- **Unclear causal link between deliberation depth and judgment accuracy:** While the model generates long-form critiques and initially exhibits self-correction behaviors (Fig 3), the paper lacks analysis correlating critique length, reasoning depth, or explicit self-correction frequency with final accuracy. Without this, it remains ambiguous whether performance gains stem from genuine step-wise re-verification or from the model learning a stylistic CoT prior that mimics deliberation to satisfy the sparse binary accuracy reward. The observed drop in self-correction post-RL could indicate the RL phase is collapsing the model into faster, heuristic-based pattern matching rather than preserving deliberative rigor.
- **Confounded refinement improvements (error localization vs. feedback quality):** The claim that detailed deliberate critiques drive generator refinement (Table 3) is heavily confounded by the improved step-level localization accuracy. Since pinpointing the exact erroneous step improves substantially, the refinement gains could primarily result from the generator receiving precise error coordinates rather than benefiting from the textual deliberation, multi-perspective analysis, or meta-critiquing. Without an ablation providing only the error index (masking the critique text), the unique value of "deliberate" natural-language feedback remains empirically unverified.
- **Unanalyzed domain bias in Monte Carlo auto-labeling:** The heuristic for auto-annotating RL data assumes symmetric generator capability across reasoning steps and domains. However, rollout success probabilities inherently vary by math domain (e.g., algebra vs. combinatorics vs. number theory) due to differing search space structures. The paper does not quantify how mislabeled steps—stemming from generator capability gaps rather than actual step incorrectness—are distributed across problem types. This unexamined noise floor risks training a critic biased toward domains where the rollout model performs well, potentially limiting robustness on harder or out-of-distribution subsets.
- **Missing inference cost and scalability trade-off analysis:** The framework advances "scalable oversight," yet generating ~8K-token deliberate critiques per solution imposes substantially higher latency, VRAM, and token-throughput costs compared to scalar PRMs or standard compact critics. The paper does not quantify this computational overhead or compare FLOP/token budgets against baselines. Without this analysis, the practical deployment viability and true scalability of the pipeline for real-time or large-scale test-time compute allocation remain theoretically appealing but empirically ungrounded.

## Nice-to-Haves
- Reporting variance or confidence intervals across multiple RL training seeds would strengthen empirical rigor, though single-run evaluations are common for compute-intensive GRPO training.
- Providing explicit failure cases where the model produces lengthy, confident critiques that nonetheless yield incorrect judgments would help map the boundaries of the deliberation heuristic.
- Analyzing successful vs. failed refinement cases qualitatively to identify which critique components (e.g., alternative verification, meta-critique, explicit error location) generators actually utilize during correction.

## Novel Insights
The paper formalizes "deliberation" not as an emergent property of model scale, but as a structured data curation and alignment objective. By decomposing critique generation into initial verification followed by explicit multi-perspective re-evaluation or meta-critiquing, the authors identify a reproducible pathway to inject reflective reasoning into 7B-scale models without architectural modifications. A particularly valuable implicit finding is the tension between binary accuracy optimization and deliberative trace preservation: as RL improves terminal judgment accuracy, the explicit self-correction frequency decreases, suggesting that optimizing for correctness may streamline reasoning at the expense of the very analytical traces that initially enabled robust oversight. This highlights a critical design consideration for future critics—they must balance judgment accuracy with the retention of interpretable, multi-step verification processes if they are to serve as scalable, human-alignable oversight tools rather than opaque verifiers.

## Suggestions
- **Add an "index-only" refinement ablation:** Mask the critique text and provide only the localized error index to the generator. This will isolate whether refinement gains come from the deliberate textual feedback or simply from superior error localization.
- **Quantify inference overhead:** Report average critique generation length, latency, and approximate token/FLOP costs compared to scalar PRMs and baseline critics. Frame the performance gains relative to this computational budget to honestly assess "scalability."
- **Analyze auto-labeling noise distribution:** Examine the MC heuristic's false-positive/negative rates stratified by problem domain (e.g., algebra, geometry, combinatorics) and difficulty. This will clarify whether the RL-trained critic generalizes uniformly or inherits rollout-model domain biases.
- **Correlate deliberation metrics with accuracy:** Provide a scatter or binned analysis of critique token count/self-correction frequency against judgment accuracy across benchmarks, and include representative failure cases where long CoTs lead to wrong judgments. This will empirically ground whether "deliberation" causally drives performance.
