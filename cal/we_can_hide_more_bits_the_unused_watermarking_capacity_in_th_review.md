# Harsh Critic Review
## Section-by-Section Critical Review

### Title & Abstract
The title clearly captures the paper's dual focus (theoretical bounds and empirical validation). The abstract accurately summarizes the contributions. However, it claims Chunky Seal preserves "quality and robustness" at 1024 bits relative to VideoSeal at 256 bits without clarifying a critical metric: **bit accuracy vs. message accuracy**. For a 1024-bit message, a bit error rate (BER) of even 1% implies a near-zero probability of recovering the entire message intact. The abstract (and paper at large) relies exclusively on bit accuracy, which significantly overstates the practical viability of the proposed capacities. This should be explicitly framed or corrected with error-correcting code (ECC) overhead considerations.

### Introduction & Motivation
The motivation is strong and well-articulated: identifying a stagnation in watermarking capacity and questioning whether current models have hit a fundamental limit. The three contributions are clearly stated. The introduction successfully bridges classical signal-processing intuition with modern deep learning setups. One minor gap: the framing of "capacity" shifts implicitly between combinatorial (how many discrete images fit in an $\ell_2$ ball) and information-theoretic (how many bits a continuous neural map can reliably encode/decode). Clarifying this distinction early would help readers calibrate expectations for what the bounds actually guarantee for neural systems.

### Method / Theoretical Framework (Sections 2.1–2.6)
The geometric approach to watermarking capacity is novel and a welcome departure from traditional Gaussian-channel assumptions. The derivations for the PSNR-only bounds (Sections 2.2–2.4) are rigorous, and the use of exact lattice counting (Mitchell's algorithm) alongside volume approximations is well-executed.

**Key concerns:**
1. **Combinatorial Upper Bound vs. Learnable Capacity:** The bounds count the maximum number of grid points in an $\ell_2$ ball that can theoretically be mapped to distinct messages. This is a *combinatorial packing limit*, not an *information-theoretic achievable rate* for differentiable, continuous mappings. Neural networks operate as smooth function approximators; they cannot necessarily tile the pixel space as densely as optimal discrete packings without violating local Lipschitz constraints or encountering gradient vanishing/conflict issues. The paper attributes the gap purely to architecture/training, but part of it is fundamentally due to the mismatch between discrete optimal packing and continuous optimization landscapes. This distinction should be explicitly acknowledged in Section 2 or 5.
2. **Blind Decoding Assumptions:** Section 2.6 attempts to account for data distribution collisions in blind watermarking by arguing that the capacity penalty is at most $\log_2 N$ bits, approximated via VQ-VAE latent codebooks. This argument is heuristic. Blind decoders do not know which of the $N$ potential covers a watermarked image originated from, meaning the effective codebook must be globally separable, not just locally packed. The reduction by $\log_2 N$ assumes uniform random placement of covers and ignores the geometric structure of natural image manifolds. A more rigorous treatment of blind capacity (or at least a clearer statement that bounds assume known or highly structured cover distributions) is needed.
3. **Heuristic Robustness Bounds (Section 2.5):** The authors transparently label Bounds 10–12 as heuristics and demonstrate cases where they over/under-approximate true capacity (Figures 8–9). While acceptable for exploratory analysis, these bounds rely heavily on singular values and ignore spatial structure, interpolation artifacts, and boundary clipping effects that dominate real robustness. The conservative bound (Bound 13) is mathematically sound but extremely loose (Table 2), limiting its practical utility. The theoretical section would be stronger if it clearly stated that these robustness estimates are *empirical proxies* rather than formal guarantees.

### Empirical Gap Analysis (Section 3)
The controlled experiments effectively isolate the source of the capacity gap. Retraining VideoSeal on a single gray image with only an MSE constraint (Section 3.1) cleanly rules out hypotheses A–C. The demonstration that a linear embedder/decoder and a handcrafted hypercube mapping achieve high capacities on the same task (Section 3.2) convincingly supports hypothesis E: current architectures are underutilizing the available degrees of freedom.

**Key concerns:**
1. **Tiling Without Synchronization:** The 32×32px tiling experiment achieving ~32k bits is clever, but it is only evaluated under PSNR constraints (Table 1). Tiling inherently assumes perfect patch alignment. Under global transformations (cropping, non-integer resizing, rotation), tile boundaries shift, breaking independent decoding unless synchronization or spatial alignment mechanisms are added. The robustness of the tiled approach is untested, which weakens the claim that simple architectural tweaks can bridge the gap in realistic settings.
2. **Learning Dynamics:** The paper notes that VideoSeal "fails to use the full resolution" and that linear models succeed quickly. This points to implicit inductive biases in U-Nets and ConvNeXts that may suppress high-frequency, globally spread perturbations. A brief discussion on *why* these architectures struggle (e.g., skip connections favoring low-frequency residuals, pooling operations destroying high-Capacity bits) would strengthen the analysis.

### Chunky Seal & Experiments (Section 4 & Tables 3–5)
Chunky Seal demonstrates that scaling VideoSeal increases capacity to 1024 bits with modest quality drops. The results are comprehensive across multiple datasets and augmentations.

**Key concerns:**
1. **Bit Accuracy vs. Message Error Rate (Critical):** This is the most significant oversight. Table 3 reports 99.15% overall bit accuracy for Chunky Seal. For 1024 independent bits, the probability of *exactly zero* bit errors is $0.9915^{1024} \approx 0.00016$ (0.016%). For JPEG (Q=50–80), bit accuracy drops to ~98.79%, yielding a message success rate of $\approx 3.6 \times 10^{-6}$. In practical watermarking, **message-level accuracy** matters, not bit accuracy. The paper either needs to: (a) incorporate and report ECC overhead (e.g., BCH/Reed-Solomon) to show *usable* capacity, or (b) explicitly state that the reported capacities are raw bit rates and discuss the massive error-correction penalty required for reliable decoding. Without this, the claim of "1024-bit capacity" is practically misleading.
2. **Scaling Efficiency:** Chunky Seal achieves 4× capacity with a ~90× larger embedder and ~23× larger extractor. The paper acknowledges this in the limitations but doesn't analyze whether the marginal gain is due to true representational improvement or simply memorization of the augmented attack distribution. An ablation comparing parameter scaling to training schedule optimization or loss reweighting would help validate that architectural scaling is the primary driver.
3. **JPEG Robustness Degradation:** Table 5 shows Chunky Seal's JPEG 40–90 bit accuracy ranges from 65.86% to 93.21%, significantly lower than VideoSeal's 97.79–99.87%. The main text downplays this by focusing on average or higher-Q values. For a method claiming to preserve robustness at higher capacities, this drop should be explicitly analyzed. It suggests that high-frequency capacity (which carries the extra bits) is disproportionately vulnerable to frequency-domain compression.

### Writing & Clarity
The writing is generally clear and mathematically precise. The heavy derivations in the appendices are well-organized. However, the core narrative occasionally gets buried in lattice-counting technicalities that, while correct, are not central to the ML-focused ICLR audience. Condensing Appendices C–F and emphasizing the geometric intuition in the main text would improve accessibility.

### Limitations & Broader Impact
The limitations section honestly addresses heuristic bounds, model size, and image-only scope. However, it misses two critical practical aspects:
1. **Adversarial/Watermark Removal Attacks:** The bounds assume fixed, known augmentations. Modern watermark removal attacks (e.g., diffusion-based inversion, GAN purification, or learned scrubbers) can strip watermarks with minimal perceptual distortion. How do these bounds hold against adaptive, learning-based attacks?
2. **Synchronization & Real-World Decoding:** As noted, tiling and dense packing require precise alignment. Real-world pipelines involve cropping, recompression, and format conversion that break synchronization. The paper should acknowledge that achieving theoretical capacities in practice will require new synchronization and alignment mechanisms, not just capacity-dense encoders.

### Overall Assessment
This paper makes a valuable conceptual contribution by reframing watermarking capacity through a geometric, combinatorial lens and convincingly demonstrating that modern neural watermarks operate orders of magnitude below theoretical limits. The controlled experiments isolating architectural limitations are rigorous and insightful, and Chunky Seal provides strong empirical proof that scaling can push the capacity frontier. However, the contribution is significantly weakened by the exclusive reliance on bit accuracy without addressing message error rates or ECC overhead, which are essential for practical high-capacity watermarking. Additionally, the theoretical bounds, while mathematically sound, describe discrete packing limits that continuous neural maps cannot trivially achieve, and the robustness analysis relies on heuristics that diverge from real-world degradation patterns. Addressing the BER/MER disconnect, discussing ECC implications, and clarifying the distinction between combinatorial limits and learnable rates would substantially strengthen the paper. With these revisions, the work would meet ICLR's standards for rigorous, impactful research at the intersection of representation learning and signal processing.

# Neutral Reviewer
## Balanced Review

### Summary
This paper investigates whether current deep learning-based image watermarking methods have reached fundamental performance limits, demonstrating theoretically and empirically that they fall orders of magnitude short of achievable capacity. By modeling watermarking as a discrete geometric problem, the authors derive novel upper bounds for capacity under PSNR and linear robustness constraints, and systematically rule out data, quality, and robustness complexity as explanations for current performance plateaus. Through controlled ablations and a heavily scaled baseline ("Chunky Seal"), the work proves that architectural limitations, not theoretical ceilings, restrict current models, achieving 4× capacity with comparable quality and robustness.

### Strengths
1. **Rigorous geometric capacity analysis:** The paper successfully reframes watermarking capacity away from classical Gaussian channel assumptions toward a discrete grid/ball intersection model that respects quantized pixel spaces. Bounds 1–9 provide explicit, mathematically grounded capacity estimates under PSNR constraints, with clear validity regimes (low/medium/high PSNR, center vs. corner covers).
2. **Well-designed empirical ablation strategy:** Section 3 systematically tests five hypotheses (A–E) to explain the theory-practice gap. By stripping Video Seal down to a single gray image and PSNR-only loss, the authors convincingly show that Video Seal fails at 1024 bits (Fig. 5, Table 1) while linear/handcrafted baselines succeed, providing strong evidence of architectural bottlenecks rather than fundamental limits.
3. **High reproducibility and transparency:** The paper includes explicit derivations, pseudo-code for lattice counting (Algorithms 1–4), detailed constructions of LinJPEG and affine transforms (Appendix H), and complete training hyperparameters with sweeps. Promising code/checkpoint release aligns well with ICLR's reproducibility standards.
4. **Clear empirical proof-of-concept:** Chunky Seal (Section 4) demonstrates that scaling architectural dimensions and capacity targets yields 1024-bit watermarks with PSNR ~45.3 dB and robustness matching 256-bit Video Seal across standard transforms (Table 3). The proposed "sanity checks" for future models are actionable and community-beneficial.

### Weaknesses
1. **Heuristic and loose robustness bounds:** Bounds 10–12 rely on a singular-value product heuristic that the authors explicitly acknowledge can over- or under-estimate true capacity (Figures 8–9). While Bound 13 provides a formal lower bound, it is extremely conservative. The lack of tighter, theoretically guaranteed bounds for quantized linear transforms leaves uncertainty about how close the heuristic estimates are to actual capacity.
2. **Impractical model scale and lack of efficiency analysis:** Chunky Seal requires an embedder with ~1.02B parameters and an extractor with ~774M parameters (Table 3). The authors concede that naive scaling is impractical but do not explore or benchmark parameter-efficient alternatives, distillation strategies, or compute trade-offs, limiting immediate practical utility.
3. **LinJPEG may not reflect real-world compression bottlenecks:** LinJPEG replaces JPEG's non-linear quantization matrix rounding with a hard frequency cutoff to maintain linearity (Appendix G.4). While analytically tractable, real JPEG/WebP codecs exhibit non-linear rate-distortion behaviors and entropy coding interactions that may impose stricter capacity limits not captured by this approximation.
4. **Approximate treatment of natural image distributions:** Section 2.6 estimates the capacity penalty from dataset diversity (~0.05 bpp) using VQ-VAE codebook sizes as a proxy for distinct perceptual images. This is a coarse upper bound that ignores manifold structure, semantic redundancy, and adversarial collision risks, potentially understating the blind decoding challenge on diverse, real-world covers.

### Novelty & Significance
The novelty is high. The paper departs from the incremental architecture-tuning trend in neural watermarking by introducing a first-principles geometric capacity framework tailored to discrete, quantized image spaces. The empirical demonstration that current SOTA models severely underutilize available capacity is a provocative and timely contribution. For the ICLR community, this work is highly significant: it reopens a mature subfield by providing theoretical headroom, concrete empirical baselines, and actionable diagnostic checks. It also bridges information geometry and deep learning robustness, offering a foundation for future high-capacity, offline AI provenance systems.

### Suggestions for Improvement
1. **Tighten robustness bounds or empirically validate them:** Provide empirical capacity measurements for simple 2D/low-dimensional toy settings to quantify the error margin of Bounds 10–12, or derive formal correction terms for the quantization step in linear transforms.
2. **Explore parameter-efficient pathways to high capacity:** Since Chunky Seal is computationally heavy, evaluate knowledge distillation from Chunky Seal to a lightweight student, or test low-rank adaptation (LoRA) / adapter modules to determine if 4× capacity gains can be retained with <10% of the parameters.
3. **Benchmark against real non-linear codecs:** Add robustness evaluations using standard JPEG (with varying quantization tables), WebP, and AVIF. Discuss discrepancies between LinJPEG theoretical bounds and empirical performance on these codecs to ground the capacity gap in real-world deployment conditions.
4. **Clarify assumptions and limitations in distribution modeling:** Explicitly state that the VQ-VAE proxy is a rough estimate, and consider supplementing it with a manifold packing analysis or collision rate simulation across a diverse image set (e.g., SA-1B vs. COCO) to better bound the blind decoder penalty.

# Spark Finder Review
## How to Improve This Paper

### Missing Experiments (top 3-5 only)
1. Evaluate Chunky Seal against active adversarial removal attacks (e.g., neural scrubbers, gradient-based steganalysis) and complex real-world pipelines (e.g., Instagram/TikTok compression). Without this, the claimed capacity applies only to benign linear transforms, which ignores the fundamental security requirement of watermarking.
2. Scale competing SOTA architectures (e.g., WAM, TrustMark, CIN) to 1024 bits for a direct comparison. Relying exclusively on scaling a single baseline cannot isolate whether the capacity gap is an architectural universality versus a video-seal-specific training or hyperparameter flaw.
3. Provide a parameter-count and resolution-controlled ablation study for Chunky Seal. The paper increases U-Net channels, ConvNeXt depth, and embedding dimension simultaneously; disentangling which scaling factor actually unlocks the extra bits is required to validate the call for architectural innovation.

### Deeper Analysis Needed (top 3-5 only)
1. Diagnose *why* VideoSeal structurally fails at high capacity. The paper claims architectural limitations but provides no analysis of gradient flow, latent space collapse, or spectral bias. Characterizing the exact optimization bottleneck would prove the gap is learnable rather than a fundamental expressivity limit.
2. Quantify the information loss from non-linear real-world encoders versus your LinJPEG proxy. Real JPEG uses adaptive quantization matrices and entropy coding, and neural codecs introduce heavy non-linearities; bounding this empirical gap is needed to trust your theoretical packing density on actual pipelines.
3. Analyze bit-interdependence and error covariance at high message lengths. As capacity approaches 1024 bits, bit errors should exhibit spatial or spectral correlation; showing this distribution would validate whether the model encodes robustly distributed information or merely memorizes fragile local patterns.

### Visualizations & Case Studies
1. Provide Fourier/spectral heatmaps comparing the embedded residuals of Chunky Seal, the handcrafted baseline, and VideoSeal. This would directly reveal whether deep models actually utilize the high-frequency bands predicted by your capacity bounds or remain low-frequency limited.
2. Include localized bit-error maps across a spectrum of crop/rotation/JPEG strengths. Showing exactly which bits fail and under which distortions would prove whether robustness degrades gracefully or suffers catastrophic phase transitions as capacity increases.
3. Present a composite-attack failure case study (e.g., 50% crop + 15° rotation + JPEG 70). Visualizing the exact failure boundary would expose whether Chunky Seal's high capacity genuinely trades off with robustness or artificially inflates accuracy via redundant, easily stripped encoding.

### Obvious Next Steps
1. Deliver a formal security and key-sensitivity analysis. Without measuring resistance to brute-force extraction, cover-image ambiguity, and unauthorized decoding, high capacity risks producing trivially breakable or forgeable watermarks, undermining practical deployment claims.
2. Benchmark computational overhead (FLOPs, latency, memory footprint) against lightweight baselines. A 90× parameter increase is prohibitive for real-world use; quantifying the efficiency cost is necessary to argue that the capacity-robustness frontier was meaningfully advanced rather than just bought with brute-force scaling.
3. Experiment with multi-watermark coexistence and collision rates. Since you argue capacity is vastly underutilized, demonstrating empirically that multiple independent watermarks can be packed into a single image without mutual interference would directly validate the practical utility of your theoretical bounds.

# Final Consolidated Review
## Summary
This paper challenges the prevailing assumption that deep learning-based image watermarking has reached a fundamental capacity ceiling. By reframing capacity as a discrete geometric packing problem in quantized pixel space, the authors derive rigorous upper bounds under PSNR and linear robustness constraints that reveal orders-of-magnitude headroom over current methods. Through controlled ablations showing that simple linear models outperform state-of-the-art architectures in simplified settings, and by introducing "Chunky Seal" (a scaled-up VideoSeal achieving 1024 bits with comparable quality and robustness), the work demonstrates that the capacity gap is driven by architectural and optimization bottlenecks, not theoretical limits.

## Strengths
- **Novel geometric capacity framework:** Departing from classical Gaussian channel assumptions, the paper models watermarking capacity as discrete lattice packing within $\ell_2$ balls intersecting quantized pixel grids. The derivation of explicit bounds (Bounds 1–9) with clear validity regimes across low/medium/high PSNR and arbitrary cover images provides a mathematically grounded ceiling that current methods drastically underutilize.
- **Highly effective empirical isolation of architectural bottlenecks:** Section 3 cleanly rules out data distribution, perceptual complexity, and robustness constraints as explanations for the theory-practice gap. By reducing VideoSeal to a single gray image with only an MSE constraint, the authors convincingly show it fails at 1024 bits, while linear and handcrafted hypercube mappings succeed. This cleanly attributes the gap to inductive biases and optimization limitations in current neural architectures.
- **Conclusive feasibility proof via Chunky Seal:** Scaling VideoSeal’s embedder and extractor successfully pushes raw capacity to 1024 bits while maintaining PSNR $\sim$45 dB and strong robustness across standard transformations (Table 3). The paper explicitly frames this as a feasibility study rather than a deployment solution, and the resulting "sanity checks" (linear scaling with resolution, predictable augmentation drops) provide actionable diagnostic tools for future work.
- **Transparency and reproducibility:** The paper includes exhaustive derivations, explicit algorithms for exact lattice counting, detailed linear transform constructions (LinJPEG), complete hyperparameter sweeps, and commitments to release code/checkpoints.

## Weaknesses
- **Bit accuracy vs. reliable message recovery at scale:** The paper reports raw bit accuracy (~99.15% overall for Chunky Seal), but does not translate this into message-level accuracy or account for error-correcting code (ECC) overhead. For a 1024-bit payload, even a ~1% average bit error rate compounds to a near-zero probability of perfect message recovery without robust ECC. While reporting BER is standard in preliminary watermarking work, high-capacity claims require explicit discussion of how many raw bits are needed to guarantee a target number of reliable payload bits.
- **Capacity-robustness trade-off under aggressive compression:** Table 5 reveals that Chunky Seal’s bit accuracy drops to ~65% at JPEG Q=40, significantly underperforming VideoSeal’s ~97%. This indicates the additional capacity is likely encoded in higher-frequency bands that are disproportionately vulnerable to non-linear DCT quantization. The linearized JPEG model (LinJPEG) captures broad capacity trends but misses this non-linear fragility, meaning the claimed "comparable robustness" holds primarily for moderate distortions.

## Nice-to-Haves
- Explicitly simulate or discuss standard ECC schemes (e.g., BCH, Reed-Solomon, or neural error correction) to translate the 1024-bit raw capacity into actionable reliable payload sizes.
- Provide spectral/Fourier visualizations of the embedded residuals for Chunky Seal, VideoSeal, and the handcrafted baseline to empirically verify that the capacity gains utilize higher-frequency bands as theory predicts.
- Conduct a parameter-controlled ablation (depth vs. width vs. embedding dimension) in Chunky Seal to disentangle which architectural factor most effectively reduces the optimization bottleneck.
- Validate the heuristic robustness bounds (Bounds 10–12) in low-dimensional toy settings to quantify their deviation from exact capacities under quantization.

## Novel Insights
The paper fundamentally shifts the watermarking capacity paradigm from continuous information-theoretic assumptions to discrete geometric packing in quantized pixel spaces. By demonstrating that simple linear models and handcrafted hypercube mappings easily exceed the capacity of heavily optimized deep networks in controlled settings, it reveals that current architectures suffer from implicit inductive biases (e.g., spectral bias, poor resolution utilization) that prevent them from approaching the true limits of the medium. This reframing—from a mature, plateauing field to one with vast untapped geometric headroom—provides a clear theoretical foundation and actionable diagnostic checklist for the next generation of high-capacity, AI-driven content provenance systems.

## Suggestions
- Add a supplementary analysis translating the reported bit accuracy curves into expected message-level success rates using standard ECC parameters, clarifying the practical payload capacity after error correction.
- Include a dedicated short discussion or appendix analyzing the performance drop at JPEG Q=40, explicitly linking it to high-frequency component truncation and contrasting LinJPEG’s linear approximation with real codec behavior.
- When discussing future architectural directions, explicitly reference the spectral bias observation (U-Nets/ConvNeXts favoring low-frequency residuals) as a concrete hypothesis for why current models fail to utilize full resolution, guiding researchers toward specific inductive bias modifications rather than generic "better architectures."
