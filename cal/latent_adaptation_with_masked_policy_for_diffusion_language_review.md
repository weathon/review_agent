# Harsh Critic Review
## Section-by-Section Critical Review

### Title & Abstract
The title accurately reflects the core contribution, and the abstract clearly outlines the pipeline. However, the claim that LAMP is a "training-free framework that performs instance-level, reward-guided policy-gradient updates" needs precise scoring regarding the reward source. The abstract highlights "modest compute" and "consistent improvements," but the main results in Section 3.2 rely heavily on PSRM, which is an oracle reward requiring ground-truth answers at test time. For ICLR, distinguishing between oracle-guided simulation and practically deployable test-time methods (e.g., verifier or self-reward) is critical. The abstract should explicitly acknowledge that the primary performance gains depend on oracle supervision, or reframe the contribution as a controlled study of reward-guided latent search rather than a broadly applicable inference-time technique.

### Introduction & Motivation
The motivation is well-articulated: identifying the gap between mature AR test-time reasoning methods and the underexplored bidirectional, revisable nature of masked dLLMs. The contributions are clearly enumerated. 
**Concern:** The introduction positions LAMP as complementary to inference-time scaling but does not define what "modest compute" means relative to standard baselines. ICLR reviewers expect explicit positioning against compute-matched alternatives (e.g., Best-of-K sampling, self-consistency, or diffusion-specific search like particle Gibbs) to establish whether the gains stem from algorithmic efficiency or simply trading additional forward passes for accuracy. Without this, it is difficult to assess the true value of the proposed adaptation loop versus scaling candidate generation.

### Methods (Sections 2.1–2.4 & Algorithm 1)
**2.1–2.2:** The preliminaries and high-level overview are clear. The exploitation of parallel scoring and constrained infilling is well-motivated.
**2.3:** The reward formulation is standard. However, calling PSRM a "Perfect Sparse Reward Model" while using it as the *default* reward for main experiments requires careful framing. It is effectively an answer oracle. The paper acknowledges its sparsity but does not adequately discuss the practical limitation of requiring ground-truth availability during test-time adaptation. A more realistic evaluation would use a trained verifier or process reward, which are absent from the main claims.
**2.4 & Algorithm 1:** 
- **Gradient Flow & Overhead:** The pseudo-code clarifies that gradients are computed via REINFORCE, where the reward `r` is treated as a scalar constant and gradients flow only through `log q_i(z_i)`. This is mathematically sound, but the computational cost is understated. Each policy-gradient step calls `model.diffuse(prompt, fixed={S: y_tilde})`. If the base sampler uses 10 diffusion steps (per Appendix D), running 2 PG steps adds ~20 forward passes, plus a final constrained decode. The paper's claim of "negligible overhead" (Sec 2.2, Abstract) is not supported by the architecture described. A precise FLOPs or wall-clock comparison against vanilla decoding is required.
- **Manifold Validity:** Editing hidden states `z_i` with a learning rate of 0.3 risks pushing representations off the pretrained manifold. While KL and L2 regularization are included, no ablation or sensitivity analysis is provided for `η` or regularization strength. If `z_i` is perturbed significantly, the local categorical policies `q_i(z_i)` may become poorly calibrated, which could destabilize sampling.
- **Conceptual Clarification:** Despite the term "latent adaptation," the final decode (Line 11 of Algorithm 1) ignores the updated `z` entirely. It only uses the sampled tokens `y_tilde` that pass confidence gating to clamp the final diffusion pass. Thus, `z` functions purely as a parameterization for local token search, not as an improved representation injected into the model. The title and terminology slightly overstate the role of latent-space editing; reframing it as "gradient-guided token search via soft latent policies" would be more precise.

### Experiments & Results (Section 3)
**3.1 & 3.2:** The experimental setup is transparent. Table 1 shows substantial gains with PSRM (+5.8 to +20.2 points) and marginal gains with self-reward (+0.8 to +3.2 points). 
**Major Concern 1: Missing Compute-Matched Baselines.** The most critical omission is the lack of comparison against baselines that consume equivalent compute. If LAMP uses ~2–3× the inference budget of vanilla decoding, how does it compare to generating 2–3 candidates and selecting the best via PSRM? Without this, the observed gains cannot be confidently attributed to the adaptation mechanism rather than increased compute budget.
**Major Concern 2: Statistical Fragility on AIME.** AIME 2024 contains only 30 questions. A 3.3% change corresponds to exactly one problem. Reporting single-run point estimates without confidence intervals, error bars, or evaluation across multiple seeds/seeds is statistically insufficient for ICLR, especially given the high variance inherent in math reasoning at this difficulty level.
**3.3 (Scaling):** Figure 2 demonstrates concave scaling with PSRM and early plateauing with self-reward. The analysis is consistent with expectations but again lacks compute baselines. The caption notes a baseline of 71.8 for LLaDA-8B, while Table 1 reports 71.3. This minor discrepancy should be clarified.
**3.4 (Self-Reward Transitions):** The transition matrix analysis is a strong analytical addition. It honestly reveals that self-reward primarily preserves correct states (True→True) and rarely recovers incorrect ones (False→True). This effectively explains the weak empirical performance but also highlights that the method, as currently paired with self-reward, has limited practical utility for hard reasoning.
**3.5 (Qualitative):** The TF/FT examples are instructive and align with the transition analysis. However, they reinforce that without strong reward signals or process-level constraints, local edits can easily break global arithmetic consistency.

### Writing, Limitations & Broader Impact
The paper is generally well-written and logically structured. The pseudocode and appendix tables provide useful implementation details. 
**Limitations:** The paper should explicitly acknowledge the oracle dependency, the lack of compute equivalence in baselines, and the statistical uncertainty on small datasets. The claim that environmental impact is limited because overhead is "modest" directly contradicts the iterative diffusion calls documented in the appendix. Environmental/compute impact should be quantified honestly (e.g., tokens/sec, FLOPs, or relative wall-clock).
**Broader Impact:** The ethics statement is standard and appropriate. The reproducibility statement mentions anonymized supplementary material, which is acceptable for double-blind review.

---

### Overall Assessment
LAMP presents a clear and conceptually interesting idea: leveraging the bidirectional, revisable nature of masked dLLMs to perform reward-guided token search via soft latent policy updates. The method is well-specified, and the empirical results demonstrate that oracle-guided adaptation can substantially boost math reasoning accuracy across multiple diffusion backbones. However, the paper in its current form falls short of the ICLR acceptance bar due to two fundamental gaps: (1) **lack of compute-matched baselines**, making it impossible to distinguish algorithmic efficiency from brute-force compute scaling, and (2) **heavy reliance on an oracle reward (PSRM)** for the primary claims, while the practically relevant self-reward variant yields only marginal improvements. Additionally, the computational overhead is understated, and the AIME results lack statistical rigor. The core insight that diffusion models can be adapted at test-time via gradient-based token selection is valuable and aligns with current ICLR interests in test-time compute. To strengthen the submission, the authors should add compute-matched comparisons, quantify true overhead, temper claims regarding self-reward efficacy, and report variance/confidence intervals on small benchmarks. With these revisions, the work would be a strong, rigorous contribution to dLLM inference strategies.

# Neutral Reviewer
## Balanced Review

### Summary
This paper introduces LAMP, a training-free, instance-level adaptation framework for masked diffusion language models that applies reward-guided policy-gradient updates to a sparse set of token latents and restores sequence coherence via a novel clamp-and-inpaint decoding step. Experiments across multiple diffusion backbones (LLaDA, LLaDA-1.5, Dream) on GSM8K, MATH-500, and AIME demonstrate substantial accuracy improvements when paired with a perfect sparse reward model, while simultaneously revealing that heuristic self-reward signals yield marginal or unstable gains. The work establishes latent-space editing as a viable inference-time scaling axis for non-autoregressive models, contingent on high-quality reward supervision.

### Strengths
1. **Domain-Aware Design for Diffusion Paradigms**: The method thoughtfully adapts latent policy optimization to masked diffusion's bidirectional, non-causal decoding. The `clamp-and-inpaint` mechanism (Sec 2.2) directly exploits diffusion's ability to re-sample masked positions while fixing confident edits, which is a principled departure from autoregressive latent-seeking methods.
2. **Comprehensive Empirical Analysis & Honest Reporting**: The paper evaluates scaling behavior (Fig 2), reward transition dynamics (Fig 3, Sec 3.4), and qualitative success/failure cases (Table 2/9). The authors transparently report that self-reward yields only +1 to +3 point gains and can regress reasoning paths, providing valuable negative results that prevent overclaiming.
3. **Strong Reproducibility Artifacts**: The submission meets or exceeds ICLR reproducibility standards. Appendix C provides clear PyTorch-style pseudocode, and Appendix D supplies exact run configurations (GPUs, dtype, optimizer, LR, diffusion steps) per benchmark and model variant (Tables 3–8). Answer extraction, normalization pipelines, and prompt templates are also explicitly documented (Appendix B).

### Weaknesses
1. **Over-Reliance on Oracle Supervision Limits Practical Impact**: The headline improvements (+10 to +20 absolute points) exclusively depend on the Perfect Sparse Reward Model (PSRM), which uses ground-truth answers. In contrast, the lightweight self-reward produces inconsistent +1–3 point gains (Table 1). Without a deployed, accurate verifier or process reward, the method's real-world utility remains constrained to settings with oracle feedback.
2. **Incomplete Compute & Latency Characterization**: The authors claim "modest compute" and "negligible overhead" (Sec 2.2), but do not report concrete inference-time metrics. ICLR reviews on test-time scaling typically require wall-clock latency, peak memory, token throughput, and exact forward-pass counts relative to vanilla baseline decoding.
3. **Ambiguity in Gradient Flow & Graph Connectivity**: Section 2.4 and the pseudocode (Appendix C) do not clearly explain how gradients backpropagate to the editable latents $z_i$ given that `CONSTRAINEDDIFFUSE` (Line 6) involves a multi-step denoising process that typically breaks the computational graph. Without detailing whether gradients are approximated, unrolled, or detached-and-reinjected, the training-free policy gradient step lacks full technical transparency.

### Novelty & Significance
**Novelty**: Medium-High. Adapting per-instance latent policy gradients to masked diffusion is novel, as prior work (e.g., LatentSeek) focused on causal autoregressive models. The introduction of diffusion-specific components (sparse low-confidence token selection, clamp-and-inpaint re-decoding, trust-region regularization on discrete token heads) constitutes a meaningful conceptual shift.
**Clarity**: High. The method is logically structured, the algorithm is well-pseudocoded, and figures effectively communicate scaling and reward dynamics. Despite minor parser artifacts in tables, the technical narrative remains accessible and mathematically sound.
**Reproducibility**: High. Deterministic settings, exhaustive hyperparameter tables, dataset preprocessing code, and modular pseudocode provide everything needed to replicate the results on the stated backbones.
**Significance**: Medium-High. As diffusion LLMs scale, demonstrating a viable test-time adaptation pathway is timely and impactful. The paper successfully reframes inference-time scaling for dLLMs around latent budget allocation rather than candidate sampling. However, the significance is currently capped by the oracle reward dependency; unlocking practical, high-quality self-verification remains the critical bottleneck for broader adoption.

### Suggestions for Improvement
1. **Quantify Inference Overhead Rigorously**: Report exact wall-clock time per example, peak VRAM usage, and total forward-pass multiply-accumulate (FLOP) overhead for $K=1$ to $10$ iterations compared to vanilla decoding. A compute-vs-accuracy Pareto plot would align this work with ICLR's test-time scaling expectations.
2. **Clarify Computational Graph Handling**: Explicitly detail how gradients flow from the reward signal back to the editable latents $z_i$ across the diffusion scheduler. State whether the denoising steps are truncated, replaced with a single forward pass, or approximated via the straight-through estimator. This is essential for methodological completeness.
3. **Introduce a Realistic Verifier/Process Reward Baseline**: To bridge the gap between oracle PSRM and noisy self-reward, evaluate a lightweight, trainable verifier (e.g., a small reward model fine-tuned on step-level consistency or best-of-N self-consistency reranking). This would demonstrate LAMP's applicability in practical, non-oracle settings.
4. **Expand Task Diversity & Hyperparameter Ablations**: Validate generalization on at least one non-mathematical domain (e.g., HumanEval code generation or GSM-Hard) to prove the latent editing mechanism isn't overfitted to arithmetic reasoning. Additionally, provide an ablation table varying the edit budget ($k$), policy gradient steps ($K$), and step size ($\eta$) to justify the chosen defaults and show robustness.

# Spark Finder Review
## How to Improve This Paper

### Missing Experiments (top 3-5 only)
1. **Comparison to standard test-time scaling baselines (Self-Consistency/Best-of-N) using the exact same PSRM reward.** Add this baseline because without it, the massive accuracy gains could simply result from leveraging the perfect oracle signal on multiple random samples, rather than from the unique diffusion latent adaptation mechanism.
2. **Evaluation with a realistic Reward Model or Process Verifier (PRM) rather than an oracle.** Replace or supplement PSRM with a trained verifier or LLM-as-a-judge, because relying exclusively on ground-truth answers invalidates the core claim that LAMP is a "practical" test-time method for real-world deployment where answers are unknown.
3. **Explicit compute and latency benchmarking against vanilla decoding and search baselines.** Report wall-clock time, FLOPs, and the exact number of forward/backward passes per problem, because unverified claims of "negligible overhead" and "modest compute" are meaningless without empirical efficiency metrics.
4. **Statistical significance testing via multiple random seeds.** Report mean accuracy and standard deviations across at least 3–5 seeds instead of single-run point estimates, because the stochastic nature of policy gradients and diffusion decoding means current results may simply reflect lucky initialization rather than reliable method improvements.

### Deeper Analysis Needed (top 3-5 only)
1. **Distribution analysis of edit locations (reasoning chain vs. final answer box).** Quantify exactly which tokens are modified by the policy-gradient updates, because if edits only occur in the final answer span, the gains represent trivial post-hoc calibration rather than actual structured reasoning enhancement as claimed.
2. **Hyperparameter sensitivity analysis for edit budget ($k$), iteration steps ($K$), and step size ($\eta$).** Show ablation curves for these values, because noting that "no broad sweeps" were performed implies the method may rely on highly brittle, fine-tuned settings that will fail on out-of-distribution data.
3. **Policy gradient stability and KL divergence tracking over update steps.** Analyze the gradient norms and KL penalties during the $K$ adaptation steps, because without demonstrating stable updates, it remains unknown whether the REINFORCE estimator actually guides the policy or simply causes high-variance collapse in discrete latent space.

### Visualizations & Case Studies
1. **Attention/confidence heatmap showing clamp-and-inpaint propagation.** Visualize a sample sequence before and after the clamping step to show how a single sparse edit globally realigns the remaining masked tokens, proving the claimed diffusion-specific "global coherence" actually occurs.
2. **Token-level probability shift visualization for success vs. regression cases.** Show the exact token distributions before and after adaptation for a `True→False` versus a `False→True` transition, exposing whether the edits are semantically meaningful shifts or just random noise that accidentally triggered the reward.
3. **Projected latent trajectory of editable states ($z_i$) during PG updates.** Plot the movement of selected hidden states in a reduced-dimension space (e.g., PCA) relative to correct/incorrect token embeddings, verifying that gradients are steering latents toward valid semantic regions rather than drifting aimlessly.

### Obvious Next Steps
1. **Direct quantitative comparison to LatentSeek or adapted autoregressive search methods.** The paper claims LatentSeek fails to transfer to diffusion but does not present a rigorous, adapted baseline; this comparison is strictly required to prove LAMP's novelty and justify its existence beyond standard search techniques.
2. **Integration of an adaptive compute budgeting strategy.** Replace the static edit budget and iteration count with a dynamic stopping criterion based on intermediate confidence or reward deltas, because a fixed compute budget ignores varying problem difficulty and destroys any claim of inference efficiency.
3. **Extension to code generation (e.g., HumanEval) or symbolic logic benchmarks.** The exclusive use of math datasets with easily extractable numerical answers makes the oracle reward trivially simple, leaving the method's generalization capabilities to unstructured reasoning and code entirely unproven.

# Final Consolidated Review
## Summary
This paper introduces LAMP, a training-free framework that applies reward-guided policy-gradient updates to a sparse set of token latents in masked diffusion language models, followed by a clamp-and-inpaint decode to maintain global coherence. Experiments across multiple diffusion backbones demonstrate that iteration-based latent scaling yields substantial accuracy gains on mathematical reasoning benchmarks when paired with an oracle reward, while heuristic self-reward provides marginal improvements. The work establishes latent-space editing as a viable inference-time scaling axis for non-autoregressive models, contingent on high-quality supervision.

## Strengths
- **Domain-aware architectural design**: The method correctly leverages masked diffusion's bidirectional, revisable decoding. The `clamp-and-inpaint` mechanism (Sec. 2.2) is a principled departure from autoregressive latent-search, allowing local gradient-guided edits to propagate globally through constrained diffusion without requiring sequential recomputation.
- **Transparent empirical analysis & honest reporting**: The paper provides a rigorous breakdown of reward transition dynamics (Fig. 3, Sec. 3.4) and explicitly acknowledges that lightweight self-reward primarily preserves already-correct trajectories (True→True) rather than recovering errors. This prevents overclaiming and clearly isolates the bottleneck in current self-verification methods.
- **Strong reproducibility artifacts**: Appendix C-D provides clear PyTorch-style pseudocode, exact run configurations (hardware, dtype, optimizer, diffusion steps per split), and fully documented prompt templates and answer normalization pipelines, meeting high standards for independent replication.

## Weaknesses
- **Unverified compute claims & missing compute-matched baselines**: The abstract and Sec. 2.2 claim "negligible overhead" and "modest compute," but each policy-gradient iteration triggers a full constrained diffusion pass (Appendix D notes 10 steps per pass). With $K=2$, overhead exceeds $2\times$ vanilla decoding. Without reporting exact forward-pass counts, wall-clock latency, or comparing against compute-equivalent baselines (e.g., Best-of-$N$ sampling with the same PSRM budget), it is impossible to determine whether gains stem from algorithmic efficiency or simply trading additional inference budget for accuracy.
- **Statistical fragility on small benchmarks**: AIME 2024 contains only 30 questions, making each 3.3% accuracy point correspond to exactly one problem. Reporting single-run point estimates without variance, error bars, or multi-seed evaluations undermines confidence in the reported gains, particularly given the inherent stochasticity of REINFORCE updates and discrete diffusion sampling.
- **Practical deployment is bottlenecked by oracle dependency**: The headline improvements (+10 to +20 absolute points) exclusively depend on PSRM, which requires ground-truth answers at test time. While the authors transparently diagnose the failure of self-reward signals, the method's current utility is effectively limited to settings where a highly accurate external verifier or oracle is already available. This significantly narrows the immediate applicability of LAMP as a standalone test-time inference technique.

## Nice-to-Haves
- **Analysis of edit locations**: Quantify whether policy-gradient updates primarily modify the final answer span versus intermediate reasoning steps, to verify that gains stem from genuine reasoning correction rather than superficial post-hoc calibration.
- **Hyperparameter sensitivity curves**: Provide ablations for the edit budget ($k$) and learning rate ($\eta$) beyond the default values to demonstrate robustness across problem difficulties and reduce reliance on fixed, untuned hyperparameters.
- **Token-level probability shift visualizations**: Illustrate how the `clamp-and-inpaint` step realigns masked tokens in successful (False→True) versus regressive (True→False) cases to empirically ground the claimed "global coherence" effect.
- **Extension to open-ended or code-generation tasks**: Validate generalization beyond parseable math answers (e.g., HumanEval) to prove the latent editing mechanism handles unstructured reasoning and syntax constraints.

## Novel Insights
The paper effectively reframes inference-time scaling for discrete diffusion from candidate generation to **latent-space budget allocation**. By treating hidden states as optimized sampling distributions rather than static representations, LAMP exploits diffusion's bidirectional self-attention to inject local corrections late in the decoding chain and propagate them globally via constrained inpainting. However, the transition analysis reveals a fundamental insight: test-time adaptation in dLLMs is severely bottlenecked not by compute or search topology, but by **reward signal fidelity**. Sparse, accurate outcome supervision scales predictably with latent iterations, whereas noisy self-reward exhibits high stability for correct paths but fails to drive recovery from errors, highlighting a critical gap in current verification mechanisms for non-autoregressive reasoning.

## Suggestions
1. **Quantify true inference cost and add a compute-matched baseline**: Report exact wall-clock time, token throughput, and total forward passes for LAMP vs. vanilla decoding. Include a Best-of-$K$ (or self-consistency) baseline with PSRM at matched compute budgets to isolate algorithmic gains from raw scaling.
2. **Report statistical variance across seeds**: Evaluate AIME and MATH-500 across at least 3–5 random seeds, reporting mean accuracy and standard deviations, to address the statistical fragility of single-run estimates on small benchmarks.
3. **Adjust terminology and overhead claims**: Replace "negligible overhead" with an accurate statement of the $1+K$ diffusion pass requirement, and clarify that "latent adaptation" functionally operates as gradient-guided token search via soft policy optimization for clamping, rather than direct hidden-state injection into the model's forward pass.
