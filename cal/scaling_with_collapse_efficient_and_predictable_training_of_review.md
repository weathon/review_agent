# Harsh Critic Review
## Section-by-Section Critical Review

**Title & Abstract**
The title accurately captures the paper's dual focus on scaling phenomena and practical training applications. The abstract clearly delineates the gap left by prior work (Qiu et al., 2025 tested small models, no weight decay, co-scaled dimensions) and states the two main practical contributions (early diagnostics, early stopping in HPO). The claim that collapse "emerges as a signature of compute-efficient training" is bold but well-motivated by the subsequent empirical evidence. No major issues.

**Introduction & Background**
The motivation is well-grounded in the practical needs of frontier-scale training where direct experimentation is prohibitively expensive. The paper correctly identifies that prior collapse studies lacked practical scaling ladders (co-scaling width, depth, batch size, weight decay). The background section cleanly defines TPP, $\mu$P, and the AdamW timescale $\tau = B/(\eta\lambda D)$. 
*Concern:* The background cites Hoffmann et al. (2022) for $\approx 20$ TPP being compute-optimal, but recent replication studies (e.g., Porian et al., 2024) show optimal TPP can vary significantly with architecture, data quality, and sequence length. The paper treats TPP=20 as a fixed reference point. While acceptable for framing, a brief acknowledgment of how data quality or architecture shifts the compute-optimal TPP would strengthen the motivation for the chosen scaling bands.

**Section 3: What Factors Modulate Training Curve Shape?**
This section provides the core empirical grounding. Sweeping $\eta$, $\lambda$, and $B$ while tracking $\tau$ (Fig. 3) convincingly shows $\tau$ is the primary knob for TLC shape. The bias-variance explanation via the noisy quadratic model (Eq. 3, Appendix B.3) is intuitive and aligns with established optimization theory.
*Concern 1 (Normalization Dependency):* The collapse demonstrations in this section normalize by the *final* training loss $L(T)$ (setting $\hat{L}=0$ in Eq. 1). This is inherently retrospective. While Sec. 4 and Sec. 5 later introduce real-time alignment strategies, Sec. 3 risks giving the impression that this normalization is trivially available during training. The authors should explicitly frame Sec. 3 as a retrospective analysis tool that motivates the forward-looking methods in later sections.
*Concern 2 (Sensitivity to $\alpha \approx \beta$ assumption):* Appendix B.2 derives scale-invariance under the assumption that model and data scaling exponents are equal ($\alpha = \beta$). Empirically, these often diverge ($\alpha \approx 0.35, \beta \approx 0.25$ in modern setups). How sensitive is the collapse phenomenon to deviations from $\alpha = \beta$? If $\alpha \neq \beta$, the normalized curves should theoretically drift slightly with scale. The paper shows tight collapse empirically, but a brief theoretical note or empirical ablation showing robustness to $\alpha \neq \beta$ would preempt reviewer concerns about the derivation's strictness.

**Section 4: Celerity: A Compute-Efficient Model Family with Collapse**
The training of the Celerity family serves as a strong real-world validation. The choice of TPP=234 is justified via a compute-compression trade-off analysis (Eq. 23, Appendix C.1). The diagnostic example (1.8B numerical instability detected at $\hat{t} \approx 0.6$) is highly compelling and demonstrates immediate practical utility.
*Concern 1 (High-TPP Divergence):* The authors note that at 234 TPP, "divergences appear late in training for larger models (Fig. 1, middle)" and attribute this to train loss improving disproportionately while held-out loss remains aligned. This is a critical observation that partially undermines the claim that collapse is a universal signature of efficient training. High TPP crosses into the over-memorization regime where train/val dynamics decouple. The paper should explicitly quantify this decoupling threshold and clarify that collapse primarily tracks *train* dynamics, which may diverge from validation quality when TPP >> 20.
*Concern 2 (Reference Curve Robustness):* The diagnostic method relies on comparing a large run to a smaller-scale reference curve. Fig. 12 shows approximate collapse across architectures (CompleteP vs $\mu$P, different vocabularies/context lengths), but the curves are visibly looser. What is the tolerance window for architectural mismatch before collapse residuals produce false positives? Practitioners training novel architectures (e.g., MoE, hybrid attention) need to know how much shape deformation is "normal" vs "pathological."
*Concern 3 (Reproducibility of HP Transfer):* The paper links CompleteP code but does not explicitly share the scripts or exact power-law coefficients used to transfer $\tau$ and optimal batch size $B \propto D^{0.5}$ across scales. Given that precise $\tau$ matching is critical for the claimed collapse (Sec. 3), providing the exact scaling recipe used for Celerity would significantly enhance reproducibility.

**Section 5: Collapse Enables Early Stopping in Hyperparameter Tuning**
This is arguably the most practically impactful section. The insight that fixing $\lambda$ during batch size sweeps inadvertently varies $\tau$ and breaks curve ordering (Fig. 7) is a valuable community correction. The predictive surrogate model (Eq. 4) and alignment-based early stopping procedure work well in $\lambda$ sweeps, correctly identifying winners by 10-30% of training (Fig. 9).
*Concern 1 (Predictor Optimization Stability):* The parameters $b$ and $q$ are fitted via an "alternating greedy optimization" to reduce complexity from $O(g^4)$ to $O(g^2)$. While practical, greedy coordinate descent can converge to local optima, especially if $b$ and $q$ are correlated. Table 12 shows the joint fit doesn't improve over the alternating approach, but no guarantee of convergence is provided. A brief discussion on the sensitivity of the final early-stopping accuracy to this optimization routine would be helpful.
*Concern 2 (Metric Clarification & Variance):* Fig. 9 reports "Loss gap" on the y-axis, but does not specify whether this is absolute cross-entropy loss or normalized logit difference. Assuming absolute nats, the gaps are very small (~0.01-0.02), which is excellent. However, the plots lack error bars or seed variance. Given that collapse is claimed to be robust to inter-run variation, showing variance across 3-5 seeds for the early-stopping predictions would strengthen confidence that the method isn't benefiting from a favorable random initialization.
*Concern 3 (Noise Sensitivity at High Batch Size):* Appendix D.2 (Fig. 23/25) correctly notes that high batch size sweeps introduce noise that degrades alignment accuracy. The paper acknowledges this, but does not provide a practical threshold or smoothing guideline for practitioners to determine when curve alignment becomes unreliable. A rule-of-thumb (e.g., max $B$ before empirical noise exceeds the alignment tolerance) would make the method more robustly deployable.

**Limitations & Broader Impact (Appendix A & Main Text)**
Appendix A is unusually thorough for a conference submission. The authors honestly address optimizer limitations (AdamW focus), data curricula, train-vs-val decoupling, and scheduler dependencies. The discussion of making collapse an operational invariant (monitoring residuals, logging fraction-of-data) is highly actionable.
*Concern:* The appendix notes that collapse is "impaired by loss spikes," framing it as a feature for diagnostics. While true, this creates a catch-22 for the early-stopping application in Sec. 5: if a run spikes naturally (not due to a kernel bug), the predictor fails, causing early termination to recommend a suboptimal HP. The paper should explicitly discuss how to distinguish "diagnostic" spikes from "stochastic" spikes in the alignment pipeline.

---

### Overall Assessment
This is a strong, highly practical paper that successfully bridges theoretical scaling insights with real-world LLM pre-training workflows. The empirical foundation is robust ($\approx 600$ training runs), the identification of $\tau$ as the dominant shape modulator is clear and actionable, and the demonstration of collapse-based diagnostics and early stopping is compelling. The Celerity model family provides credible evidence that these principles translate to competitive compute-efficiency at scale.

The main concerns are methodological rather than fundamental: (1) the normalization by final loss in Sec. 3 needs clearer framing as a retrospective tool; (2) the high-TPP (234) band reveals train/val decoupling that slightly weakens the "collapse = efficiency" narrative at extreme overtraining; (3) the early-stopping predictor's sensitivity to noise/local optima and lack of seed variance reporting leave minor reproducibility questions. These do not detract from the paper's core contributions. The work is well-aligned with ICLR's emphasis on rigorous scaling analysis, practical training insights, and transparent methodology. With minor clarifications regarding robustness thresholds and variance reporting, this paper would make a valuable addition to the proceedings.

# Neutral Reviewer
## Balanced Review

### Summary
This paper demonstrates that normalized training loss curves (TLCs) for LLMs collapse onto a universal trajectory when the tokens-per-parameter ratio (TPP) and the AdamW exponential moving average timescale ($\tau$) are fixed across model scales. Establishing collapse as a signature of compute-efficient training, the authors introduce the competitive Celerity model family and leverage the phenomenon for two practical applications: early detection of training pathologies via collapse residuals, and principled early stopping in hyperparameter tuning using a lightweight parametric surrogate. The work bridges empirical scaling laws with actionable, large-scale pre-training workflows.

### Strengths
1. **Rigorous empirical characterization of TLC controls:** The paper systematically isolates the effects of $\tau$ and TPP through $\sim$600 controlled runs, sweeping learning rate, weight decay, and batch size (Fig. 3, Fig. 4). This provides strong, reproducible evidence that $\tau$ acts as a unifying control knob for curve shape, resolving ambiguities from prior work that varied these factors independently.
2. **High practical utility with clear demonstrations:** The collapse framework is immediately actionable. The authors show how collapse residuals detect a numerical instability at 60% of training that only manifests at 90% in raw loss (Fig. 1, right), and demonstrate that a simple predictive model (Eq. 4) enables accurate early stopping by 10–30% of training, substantially reducing HPO compute costs (Fig. 9).
3. **Sound analytical grounding:** Appendix B.3 derives a noisy quadratic model that cleanly decomposes the bias-variance trade-off governed by $\tau$, explaining why constant vs. decaying LR schedules invert the ordering of normalized curves. This provides intuitive, theoretically motivated backing for empirical observations without overclaiming.
4. **Strong transparency and reproducibility:** The paper provides exhaustive documentation: full architecture tables (Table 2, Table 3), data composition (Table 6), hyperparameter transfer rules (CompleteP under $\mu$P), FLOP counting methodology (Table 9), and complete downstream evaluation scores (Table 10). This level of detail meets and exceeds ICLR's reproducibility standards.

### Weaknesses
1. **Modest parameter and compute scale:** The Celerity family and controlled experiments cap at $\sim$3.9B parameters and 900B tokens. While methodologically sound, claims of "LLM scale" and frontier applicability are somewhat overstated for a 2026 submission, where production models routinely operate at 70B+ parameters and multi-trillion token budgets. The paper does not empirically verify whether collapse holds or requires adjustment in regimes where multi-epoch training, data limitations, or extreme batch sizes dominate.
2. **Limited applicability to modern training pipelines:** The framework assumes single-epoch training on stationary data distributions. The authors acknowledge (Appendix A) that collapse degrades with late-stage data annealing, curriculum learning, or benchmark-specific mid-training—practices now standard in open and closed LLM development. The absence of a modified normalization or analysis for these regimes reduces immediate real-world relevance.
3. **Narrow baselines for early stopping:** The HPO evaluation in Section 5 compares the collapse-based predictor only against "random" and "current best" heuristics. It lacks comparison to established learning-curve extrapolation or Bayesian optimization methods (e.g., Domhan et al., 2015; Swersky et al., 2014), leaving it unclear whether the observed compute savings represent a methodological advance or simply a convenient proxy.
4. **Predictive sensitivity to noise and spikes:** The surrogate model relies on post-hoc smoothing and aligns partial curves to a reference trajectory. As noted in Appendix A, loss spikes or numerical instabilities distort alignment and can cause false early-stopping decisions or inaccurate $L(T)$ projections. The paper does not provide quantitative robustness thresholds or uncertainty estimates, which are critical for operational deployment.

### Novelty & Significance
- **Novelty:** Incremental but highly focused. It extends Qiu et al. (2025) from small-scale, vanilla Adam setups to practical, co-scaled LLM training ladders, introducing $\tau$ as a critical scaling invariant and demonstrating novel applications (diagnostic residuals, predictive early stopping). The theoretical framing via EMA timescale is a valuable conceptual shift.
- **Clarity:** Excellent. Definitions are precise (Table 1), derivations are transparent, and figures effectively communicate curve alignment and diagnostic utility. The narrative logically progresses from phenomenon identification to application.
- **Reproducibility:** Very high. Complete experimental configurations, scaling rules, data recipes, and evaluation protocols are provided. The main constraint is hardware dependency (Cerebras CS-3) and data availability, but the methodology is fully specified for replication on other clusters.
- **Significance:** High for the ML training community. Collapse provides a low-overhead, principled reference trajectory for monitoring expensive runs and a computationally cheap alternative to brute-force HPO. While not a theoretical breakthrough, it offers tangible efficiency gains aligned with ICLR's emphasis on scalable, predictable, and resource-conscious deep learning.

### Suggestions for Improvement
1. **Address the scale extrapolation explicitly:** Add a discussion or preliminary analysis on how $\tau$ and TPP scaling might behave beyond $\sim$10B parameters, particularly in data-constrained or multi-epoch regimes. If $\tau$ power-law scaling deviates at larger scales, propose how practitioners should adapt the framework.
2. **Strengthen early-stopping baselines:** Compare the collapse-based predictor against standard learning-curve extrapolation techniques (e.g., power-law fitting, Bayesian LC predictors) on identical sweeps. Reporting compute saved, ranking accuracy, and MAE relative to these methods will clarify the method's practical standing.
3. **Propose extensions for modern training pipelines:** Since late-stage annealing curricula break collapse, suggest a practical workaround (e.g., segment-wise collapse normalized per data phase, or a dynamic offset term in Eq. 1) and provide a small ablation showing how residuals remain useful after data distribution shifts.
4. **Quantify robustness and operational thresholds:** Provide concrete guidelines on required smoothing windows, acceptable noise variance, and residual alert thresholds to prevent false positives from standard training stochasticity. Adding confidence intervals or bootstrap uncertainty to the predicted $L(T)$ would greatly enhance deployment readiness.

# Spark Finder Review
## How to Improve This Paper

### Missing Experiments (top 3-5 only)
1. Test collapse and predictive HPO on models $\ge$7B parameters because without validation at frontier-adjacent scales, the claim that collapse bridges the gap where "direct experimentation disappears" remains unproven at the regime the paper targets.
2. Add a controlled baseline applying Celerity's compute-optimal recipe to the exact data mixes used by comparator models because Table 7 proves data curation drastically shifts downstream accuracy, making it impossible to disentangle whether compute gains stem from $\tau$ scaling or superior datasets.
3. Evaluate early-stopping predictions by their ability to correctly rank models according to downstream benchmark scores, not just final training loss, because the HPO contribution is practically irrelevant if train-loss predictability does not generalize to test-time performance rankings.
4. Systematically sweep single-control deviations (e.g., fixed TPP but varying $\tau$, or matched $\tau$ but different schedules) to quantify collapse residuals because the current plots show joint variations, leaving practitioners without explicit tolerance bounds for operational monitoring.

### Deeper Analysis Needed (top 3-5 only)
1. Rigorously quantify the train-to-validation/generalization divergence during collapse because high TPP and specific schedules can produce excellent train-loss alignment while memorizing training noise, which would invalidate collapse as a proxy for compute efficiency.
2. Analyze the predictive surrogate's (Eq. 4) robustness to modern learning-rate schedules (warmup-stable-decay, cosine, inverse sqrt) because the model was fit exclusively on decay-to-zero schedules, severely limiting its applicability to current LLM training pipelines.
3. Bridge the noisy quadratic model (NQM) analysis with empirical transformer loss landscape properties because the current theoretical derivation is highly idealized; without demonstrating how actual curvature/spectrum evolution in transformers maps to the NQM's bias-variance tradeoff, the mechanistic explanation remains heuristic.

### Visualizations & Case Studies
1. Plot the temporal alignment of collapse residuals, training loss, and validation/downstream accuracy during the 1.8B numerical instability to prove the diagnostic actually predicted generalization degradation rather than merely tracking an isolated training artifact.
2. Visualize predictive error bands (not just point estimates of the "best" run) across the full hyperparameter sweep because HPO decision-making requires uncertainty quantification to determine when partial runs are sufficiently converged to safely terminate.
3. Place normalized trajectories for fixed-$\lambda$ sweep (crossing curves) and fixed-$\tau$ sweep (monotonic curves) side-by-side at both 1.7B and 3.3B scales because demonstrating preserved loss ordering requires multi-scale visual proof that the effect isn't a scale-specific artifact.

### Obvious Next Steps
1. Report the exact computational overhead (FLOPs and wall-clock) of training the proxy models and fitting the surrogate subtracted from the tuning savings claimed because asserting efficiency requires a net-ROI calculation; without it, the proposed method may cost more than the compute it saves.
2. Extend the collapse monitoring framework to handle dynamic batch sizes or gradient clipping because these are standard in large-scale pre-training and directly alter $\tau$ mid-run; failing to address schedule adaptability renders the collapse invariant inapplicable to real pipelines.
3. Include a formal ablation showing the degradation in collapse quality when $\alpha, \beta$ Adam parameters deviate from standard defaults because Appendix B.4 hints at instability spikes with non-standard momentum, and the main text claims robustness without quantifying the failure thresholds.

# Final Consolidated Review
## Summary
This paper demonstrates that normalized training loss curves (TLCs) for LLMs collapse onto a universal trajectory across model scales when the tokens-per-parameter ratio (TPP) and the AdamW exponential moving average timescale ($\tau$) are fixed. Framing collapse as a signature of compute-efficient training, the authors introduce the Celerity model family and leverage the phenomenon for two practical applications: early detection of training pathologies via collapse residuals, and predictive early stopping in hyperparameter tuning using a lightweight parametric surrogate.

## Strengths
- Systematic empirical isolation of optimization controls across ~600 runs convincingly establishes $\tau$ as the dominant knob for TLC shape. The paper correctly identifies that common practices (e.g., fixing weight decay $\lambda$ during batch size sweeps) inadvertently vary $\tau$, breaking curve ordering and predictability (Fig. 3, Fig. 7).
- High practical utility with rigorous, transparent demonstrations. Collapse residuals successfully pinpoint a numerical instability at 60% of training that raw loss only reveals at 90% (Fig. 1, Fig. 6). Furthermore, the predictive surrogate enables accurate hyperparameter selection by just 10–30% of training, offering a computationally cheap alternative to brute-force sweeps (Fig. 9, App. D).
- Exceptional transparency and reproducibility. The paper provides exhaustive documentation including complete architectural specifications, data composition, FLOP accounting methodology, hyperparameter transfer rules, and full downstream evaluation tables. This level of detail meets and exceeds standard reproducibility expectations for large-scale training papers.

## Weaknesses
- Confounding of data quality and scaling methodology in efficiency claims. Celerity’s strong downstream performance relative to baselines in Fig. 2 likely stems partly from curated data mixes (Table 7 shows data changes yield ~5–8% accuracy gains), but the paper does not disentangle this from the benefits of optimal $\tau$/TPP scaling. Consequently, the pure marginal gain attributable to the collapse methodology remains ambiguous.
- Early-stopping validity relies on an unverified assumption that train-loss reduction monotonically tracks downstream performance. Section 5 selects hyperparameters by minimizing extrapolated *training* loss. The paper does not validate whether this ranking reliably correlates with validation or benchmark scores, risking optimization for train-loss artifacts rather than generalization, particularly in high-TPP regimes where train/val dynamics begin to decouple.
- Lack of comparison to established learning-curve extrapolation and HPO baselines. The evaluation in Sec. 5 compares predictions only to naive heuristics (random selection, current best loss). Without benchmarking against standard power-law fitting or Bayesian LC predictors on identical sweeps, the relative magnitude of the claimed compute savings and the method's practical advantage over existing tools remain unquantified.
- Scale claims outpace empirical validation. While the proposed invariances are theoretically scale-stable, all experiments cap at ~3.9B parameters. Framing the methodology as ready for "frontier" training where direct experimentation disappears is premature; empirical validation on larger, data-constrained regimes is necessary to confirm that $\tau$ scaling rules and collapse diagnostics hold without degradation at the scales where the method is supposedly most critical.

## Nice-to-Haves
- Quantify operational tolerance thresholds for collapse residuals (e.g., acceptable smoothing windows, noise variance limits, or architectural mismatch bounds) to help practitioners distinguish pathological divergence from standard training stochasticity.
- Extend the predictive surrogate analysis to modern learning-rate schedules (e.g., warmup-stable-decay, cosine) and incorporate uncertainty quantification (e.g., bootstrap confidence intervals) for the predicted final loss to support risk-aware early termination.

## Novel Insights
The paper successfully reframes the AdamW timescale $\tau$ from a mere optimizer hyperparameter into a scale-invariant control mechanism that implicitly governs the bias-variance trade-off throughout training. By demonstrating that fixing $\tau$ transforms chaotic, crossing loss curves into ordered, predictable trajectories, the work turns opaque optimizer tuning into a principled, transferable invariant. This shift provides practitioners with a low-overhead, scale-agnostic reference frame for monitoring expensive runs, diagnosing instabilities early, and pruning hyperparameter searches long before convergence—a practical operationalization of scaling laws that was previously absent.

## Suggestions
- Add an ablation training a baseline model with standard (non-optimal $\tau$) scaling on Celerity's exact data mix to isolate the marginal downstream and compute-efficiency gains attributable solely to the collapse methodology.
- Validate the early-stopping predictor by showing that minimizing extrapolated train loss reliably selects configurations that rank highest on held-out validation or downstream benchmarks, rather than optimizing purely for training loss reduction.
- Include a direct empirical comparison against standard learning-curve extrapolation or Bayesian optimization baselines on identical hyperparameter sweeps to quantify relative ranking accuracy, prediction error (MAE), and net compute savings.
