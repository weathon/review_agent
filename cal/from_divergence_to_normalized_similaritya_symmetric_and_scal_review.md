# Harsh Critic Review
## Section-by-Section Critical Review

### Title & Abstract
- **Title Accuracy:** The title accurately reflects the two-pronged contribution: extending the RTD framework to symmetry (SRTD) and introducing a normalized similarity measure (NTS).
- **Abstract Clarity:** The abstract clearly identifies the limitations of RTD (asymmetry, lack of normalization) and CKA (geometric bias), introduces SRTD/SRTD-lite and NTS, and summarizes key outcomes (efficiency, LLM discriminability).
- **Claim Assessment:** The claim that NTS is "robust to representation scale" is potentially misleading given the later findings. The results section (Appendix K.1) indicates NTS performs poorly without Z-score normalization on LLM representations, suggesting it is *not* invariant to feature-wise scale variations, which are common across different model families. This contradiction between the abstract's claim and the empirical requirements needs reconciliation.

### Introduction & Motivation
- **Motivation & Gap:** The motivation is strong. The critique of RTD's asymmetry and unbounded nature is well-founded, and the positioning of topological methods as complementary to geometric methods like CKA addresses a recognized niche in representation analysis.
- **Contributions:** Clearly stated. The introduction effectively sets up SRTD as a theoretical completion and NTS as a practical tool for normalized comparison.
- **Claim Calibration:** The introduction claims RTD asymmetry exists "without a clear theoretical explanation." While the paper provides a rigorous explanation via the mapping cone and min/max filtrations, prior works on RTD likely intuited this through the properties of min/max distances. The paper's contribution is the formalization and proof, which is valuable, but the claim that it was entirely unexplained previously is slightly overstated.

### Method / Approach
- **Description & Reproducibility:** Algorithms 1-4 are clear. The definition of SRTD via the auxiliary matrix $M_{sym}$ and the connection to the mapping cone of $A \cap B \to A \cup B$ is topologically sound. The reduction to SRTD-lite via MST weight differences is elegant and reproducible.
- **Assumptions & Justification:** The method assumes that the MST uniquely captures the hierarchical structure relevant for comparison. In high-dimensional neural representations, distance concentration and noise can cause MST instability; small perturbations in activations may change the MST edges. NTS relies on $E_{core} = E_w \cup \tilde{E}_w$; if $E_w$ is highly sensitive to noise, the set of core pairs may fluctuate significantly, impacting the rank correlation. This robustness to high-dimensional noise is not discussed.
- **Logical Gaps:** The relationship between SRTD and RTD Max-Min components (Theorem 3.3) is proven via integration of Betti numbers, which is standard in persistence. The proofs for NTS Theorems 4.1 and 4.2 are correct. However, there is a subtle gap: NTS uses Spearman's $\rho$ on $E_{core}$. If $E_w$ and $\tilde{E}_w$ are disjoint, $|E_{core}| = 2|E_{MST}|$. The algorithm evaluates distances from $w$ on edges in $\tilde{E}_w$ and vice versa. These edges may correspond to local structures in one model that are global distances in the other. The interpretability of correlating these cross-structure values needs more justification. Are we comparing "apples to apples" when the topologies differ?
- **Edge Cases:** The method does not address tied distances in the MST construction. While a deterministic tie-breaking rule is mentioned, in practice, floating-point activations rarely have exact ties, but near-ties are common. The stability of NTS under small perturbations (which change the order of near-ties) is an open question.

### Experiments & Results
- **Claim Testing:** The Clusters and UMAP experiments effectively demonstrate the sensitivity of NTS/SRTD to structural changes where CKA fails. The autoencoder experiments validate SRTD-lite's utility as a loss.
- **Baselines:** The comparison relies heavily on CKA. While CKA is the de facto standard, the LLM representation analysis lacks comparison to other robust baselines used in literature, such as Procrustes similarity, Cosine Similarity of layer means, or Representation Similarity Analysis (RSA) on the full distance matrix (mentioned briefly in Appendix I, but not compared directly in the LLM section).
- **Missing Ablations/Significance:**
    *   **LLM Sampling Variance:** The LLM analysis uses 1,000 QA pairs but reports single heatmap results (Figure 5, 6) without error bars or confidence intervals across different samples. Given the stochastic sampling, the stability of the NTS scores across different data subsets should be reported to support the claim of "reliable" identification.
    *   **Layer Selection Bias:** Section 5.4 states that Layer 6 was selected because it "empirically yielded the most discriminative results." While supplementary heatmaps for other layers are provided in Appendix K.2, the risk of cherry-picking a layer that favors the proposed method over CKA should be mitigated by a quantitative metric averaging across layers or a principled selection criterion.
- **Datasets/Metrics:** TruthfulQA and ToxiGen are appropriate for eliciting diverse features. The use of Spearman rank correlation is appropriate for NTS, but the claim that NTS is "more discriminative" than CKA based on Figure 6 requires quantitative validation (e.g., correlation with known model lineage distances or benchmark performance differences) rather than purely visual inspection of saturation.

### Writing & Clarity
- **Clarity of Explanation:** The theoretical development in Section 3 is dense but well-structured. The connection between the auxiliary matrices and the filtrations is explained clearly.
- **Figures/Tables:** Figure 4 and Figure 5 are informative and support the text. Figure 6 effectively illustrates CKA saturation.
- **Ambiguities:** As noted, the "scale invariance" terminology is confusing. If NTS requires Z-score normalization to work on LLMs, it is not invariant to the scale of the representations as implied. The text should clarify that NTS is invariant to *global monotonic rescaling* of distances, but sensitive to feature-wise covariance structures, necessitating normalization in cross-model comparisons.

### Limitations & Broader Impact
- **Acknowledged Limitations:** The authors correctly highlight that NTS is non-differentiable and thus unsuitable for direct optimization losses.
- **Missing Limitations:**
    *   **Computational Scalability:** The paper claims scalability, but the $O(n^2)$ complexity of distance matrices and MST construction is explicitly quadratic. For large $n$, this remains a bottleneck. The "efficiency" is relative to higher-order homology, but absolute scalability for large datasets is limited by the quadratic pairwise computation.
    *   **Interpretability of Similarity:** High NTS indicates similar merge orders, but it does not imply the models have similar decision boundaries or safety properties. Two models could have identical hierarchical clustering of inputs but vastly different outputs on critical samples. Limiting the interpretation of high similarity to structural analogy is crucial to prevent over-conclusion by practitioners.
    *   **Z-Score Dependency:** As discussed, the necessity of Z-scoring for LLMs is a limitation that should be foregrounded. It implies NTS measures a normalized topological structure that depends on the alignment of feature variances, rather than an absolute topological property.

### Overall Assessment
This paper makes a meaningful contribution to the representation analysis toolkit by formally symmetrizing the RTD family with SRTD and proposing NTS, a rank-based normalized similarity measure that addresses the saturation and scale-dependence issues of existing methods. The theoretical framework linking RTD, Max-RTD, and SRTD via persistent homology sequences and mapping cones is rigorous and well-derived. Empirically, NTS demonstrates superior sensitivity to hierarchical structures in LLM representations compared to CKA, which is a timely and valuable finding given the growth of model families.

However, the paper requires clarification on several substantive points before full acceptance. First, the contradiction between the claim of "scale invariance" and the empirical requirement for Z-score normalization on LLMs must be resolved. Second, the robustness of NTS to high-dimensional noise and MST instability needs discussion. Third, the LLM experiments would benefit from statistical validation via sampling variance reporting and a more quantitative assessment of "discriminative power" beyond visual inspection of heatmaps. Addressing these concerns will significantly strengthen the paper's reliability and impact.

# Neutral Reviewer
## Balanced Review

### Summary
This paper addresses two key limitations of Representation Topology Divergence (RTD) in neural network representation analysis: directional asymmetry and lack of scale normalization. The authors theoretically complete the RTD framework by introducing Symmetric RTD (SRTD) and SRTD-lite, proving exact mathematical relationships that decompose RTD and Max-RTD into shared symmetric and private asymmetric components. They then propose Normalized Topological Similarity (NTS), a rank-based, scale-invariant metric capturing hierarchical merge orders via MSTs, and demonstrate its superior interpretability, efficiency, and discriminative power over CKA across synthetic, convolutional, and LLM representation tasks.

### Strengths
1. **Theoretical Framework Completion**: The paper rigorously closes a theoretical gap in topological representation analysis. Theorem 3.3 and Corollaries 3.4/3.5 elegantly unify RTD, Max-RTD, and SRTD, providing an interpretable decomposition of asymmetry and a strict hierarchical ordering for the lite variants that matches empirical observations.
2. **Innovative Metric Design (NTS)**: Shifting from magnitude-based barcode summation to Spearman’s rank correlation on MST merge structures directly solves the normalization and outlier-sensitivity problems inherent in divergence metrics. The distinction and theoretical proofs for NTS-M (Theorem 4.1) and NTS-E (Theorem 4.2) are well-motivated and practically useful.
3. **Comprehensive & Targeted Empirical Validation**: Experiments effectively cover multiple regimes: structural sensitivity (synthetic/UMAP), optimization utility (autoencoder loss), layer-wise consistency (TinyCNN), and modern LLM fingerprinting. Results convincingly show NTS captures graded layer similarity like CKA while retaining topological sensitivity to functional shifts (e.g., pooling layers) and avoiding CKA’s score saturation across model families.
4. **Clear Efficiency Analysis**: The paper provides formal $O(N^2)$ complexity bounds and empirical runtime benchmarks demonstrating that SRTD-lite and especially NTS-E avoid the heavy auxiliary matrix constructions of full RTD, making them scalable for contemporary representation analysis.

### Weaknesses
1. **0D Topological Scope Limitation**: Despite being framed as a general "topological toolkit," NTS and SRTD-lite exclusively characterize 0-dimensional homology (connected components/MSTs). Higher-order features are acknowledged but dropped for scalability. This should be explicitly framed as a clustering/merge-order similarity method to avoid overstating topological generality.
2. **Critical Normalization Dependency Buried**: Section 5.4 and Appendix K.1 reveal that NTS-E requires Z-score normalization of features prior to computation; without it, scores degrade severely (e.g., for Llama models). This crucial preprocessing step is omitted from the main methodology, hindering immediate reproducibility and fair comparison with baselines that operate on raw features.
3. **Qualitative Over Quantitative LLM Claims**: Assertions about CKA’s "unreliability" and "severe score saturation" are primarily supported by heatmaps and a single lineage counterexample (DeepSeek vs. Qwen). Lacking quantitative metrics (e.g., correlation with known architectural/lineage distances, variance across seeds, or cluster quality scores) weakens the empirical rigor expected at ICLR.
4. **Baseline & Layer Selection Scope**: The empirical section contrasts almost exclusively with CKA, omitting other widely used alignment metrics (e.g., Linear CKA, dCKA, or Procrustes). Additionally, the recommendation to use Layer 6 for LLM analysis is described as purely empirical ("yielded the most discriminative results") without sensitivity analysis or principled justification.

### Novelty & Significance
The paper delivers strong theoretical novelty by formally unifying the RTD family and deriving exact relationships that resolve prior asymmetry ambiguities. Methodologically, NTS introduces a principled shift from scale-dependent topological divergence to scale-invariant structural similarity, directly addressing a major bottleneck that has limited TDA adoption in representation learning. For the ICLR community, this work is highly relevant: it provides a mathematically grounded, computationally efficient alternative to CKA that preserves interpretability while gaining topological sensitivity. The toolkit is directly applicable to mechanistic interpretability, representation regularization, and LLM ecosystem mapping, aligning well with ICLR’s emphasis on theoretically motivated and practically useful representation analysis.

### Suggestions for Improvement
1. **Clarify 0D Scope in Main Text**: Explicitly state in Sections 3 and 4 that NTS and SRTD-lite operate on 0-dimensional persistent homology. Discuss why merge-order clustering suffices for representation similarity and how (or if) higher-order topological features could be incorporated in future work.
2. **Promote Z-Score Normalization Protocol**: Move the Z-score normalization requirement to Section 4.3 and explicitly detail it in the experimental pipeline (Algorithm 1/2 footnotes or methodology). Include a main-text ablation table quantifying NTS performance with/without normalization across at least two LLM families.
3. **Add Quantitative Validation to Similarity Claims**: Supplement heatmap visualizations with quantitative summaries. For example, report Spearman/Pearson correlations between NTS/CKA scores and established model lineage/structural distances, include variance across random seeds, or apply cluster validity indices to inter-model similarity matrices to statistically substantiate claims of saturation and degradation.
4. **Broaden Baselines & Justify Layer Selection**: Compare NTS and SRTD-lite against at least one additional alignment baseline (e.g., Linear CKA or dCKA) to contextualize gains. Provide a layer-sensitivity plot or ablation justifying the Layer 6 choice for LLMs, or report how inter-model rankings shift when using median vs. layer-specific similarities.
5. **Strengthen Reproducibility Details**: While code release is planned, add fixed random seeds, exact software/library versions (e.g., persistent homology backend), and clarify hardware parity for runtime benchmarks (e.g., Appendix G notes RTX 5090; specify if other experiments matched this or provide hardware-agnostic scaling trends).

# Spark Finder Review
## How to Improve This Paper

### Missing Experiments (top 3-5 only)
1. **Add statistical robustness analysis via bootstrap resampling across varying sample sizes.** Representation similarity metrics are notoriously sample-dependent; without confidence intervals showing NTS stabilizes faster or more reliably than CKA at smaller $N$, the claim of "robust" LLM fingerprinting is statistically unsupported.
2. **Ablate NTS design choices (Z-score normalization, core edge union vs. intersection, Spearman vs. Pearson).** The paper relies on ad-hoc engineering to make NTS work; quantifying how each component independently affects correlation and saturation is mandatory to prove the architecture is optimal rather than a lucky configuration.
3. **Compare against additional representation similarity baselines beyond CKA (e.g., SVCCA, linear probing transfer, or modern geometric metrics).** Claiming NTS "outperforms CKA" is insufficient for ICLR; demonstrating it captures unique, non-redundant information compared to a standard suite of baselines is critical to establish its distinct contribution.
4. **Validate SRTD-lite as an optimization loss on standard representation learning benchmarks (e.g., CIFAR-100, contrastive learning).** Claiming SRTD-lite "matches top performance" based solely on small-dataset dimensionality reduction is weak; results on established vision/NLP tasks are needed to prove utility as a scalable training objective.

### Deeper Analysis Needed (top 3-5 only)
1. **Analyze NTS's behavior under distance metric variation (Euclidean vs. Cosine) and high-dimensional distance concentration.** TDA methods degrade when distances concentrate in high dimensions; without proving NTS remains discriminative on raw LLM embeddings or identifying necessary preconditioning, the method's reliability is questionable.
2. **Investigate the mechanistic cause of the detected "functional shift" at the pooling layer.** A topological break could trivially result from dimensionality collapse rather than a meaningful architectural shift; correlating this signal with layer-wise task importance or gradient norms is required to validate the interpretation.
3. **Characterize the theoretical gap between SRTD and the combined asymmetric RTD/Max-RTD measures.** Theorem 3.3 shows equality only up to a non-negative homological kernel integral; providing a bound or analyzing when this discrepancy becomes significant is necessary to justify SRTD as the definitive correction to the RTD framework.

### Visualizations & Case Studies
1. **Provide score distribution histograms (not just heatmaps) to quantitatively validate "score saturation."** Visual heatmaps in Figure 6 are subjective; histograms of pairwise similarity scores for all 17 models would rigorously demonstrate NTS's superior dynamic range and lack of saturation compared to CKA.
2. **Include a concrete failure case where NTS yields high similarity for semantically distinct representations.** Showing an example where matched merge orders correspond to different functional clusters would expose the method's limits and prove the authors understand where topological similarity diverges from semantic similarity.
3. **Visualize the core MST edge structures for high and low NTS pairs.** Mapping the specific edges driving the rank correlation would prove the method captures meaningful topology rather than trivial metric artifacts or dataset biases.

### Obvious Next Steps
1. **Develop a differentiable approximation or continuous relaxation of NTS.** Since the paper establishes NTS as a superior similarity measure, the glaring omission is a tractable formulation that enables its use for direct representation learning and optimization, rather than remaining an "analysis-only" metric.
2. **Validate LLM fingerprint consistency across hold-out datasets.** Testing only on TruthfulQA and ToxiGen risks capturing dataset-specific artifacts; applying the protocol to distinct domains (e.g., math reasoning or code generation) is mandatory to claim NTS reveals stable, intrinsic model architectures.
3. **Theoretically map exactly which higher-order topological features are discarded by the SRTD-lite approximation.** While efficiency gains are shown, explicitly identifying the trade-off in terms of lost 1D+ homological information relative to full SRTD is needed to properly scope the method's capabilities for representation analysis.

# Final Consolidated Review
## Summary
This paper addresses two prominent limitations in Representation Topology Divergence (RTD): directional asymmetry and unbounded, scale-dependent divergence scores. It introduces Symmetric RTD (SRTD), theoretically unifying RTD, Max-RTD, and SRTD via persistent homology mapping cones and proving exact decomposition properties. It then proposes Normalized Topological Similarity (NTS), a rank-based similarity metric operating on 0-dimensional persistent homology (MST merge orders/edge distances) that yields a bounded $[-1, 1]$ score. Empirically, SRTD-lite matches RTD-based methods as an optimization loss, while NTS demonstrates superior interpretability for layer-wise consistency and avoids CKA's score saturation in cross-family LLM comparisons.

## Strengths
- **Theoretical Unification of the RTD Family:** Theorems 3.3 and Corollaries 3.4/3.5 rigorously close prior theoretical gaps. The proof that $RTD + Max$-$RTD = SRTD + \text{residual}$ (via mapping cone long exact sequences) elegantly decomposes directional asymmetry into a dominant shared symmetric component and minor "private" topological features, providing a mathematically sound foundation for symmetrization.
- **Principled Shift from Magnitude to Rank Similarity:** NTS directly tackles the unbounded, outlier-sensitive nature of persistent homology divergence by leveraging Spearman's rank correlation over MST merge events. The proofs (Thms 4.1 & 4.2) correctly establish conditions for perfect similarity, and the $O(n^2)$ MST-based implementation is computationally tractable and avoids expensive higher-dimensional homology computations.
- **Reveals Structural Signals Missed by Geometric Baselines:** The empirical results convincingly show that NTS captures graded inter-layer similarity (matching CKA's interpretability) while simultaneously detecting functional architectural shifts (e.g., global pooling layers) and providing richer dynamic range across LLM families, addressing a well-documented niche where geometric alignment fails.

## Weaknesses
- **Misaligned Claims vs. Empirical Preprocessing Dependencies:** The abstract and main text repeatedly claim NTS is "scale-invariant" and "robust to representation scale," yet Appendix K.1 demonstrates that NTS scores collapse on raw LLM embeddings without feature-wise Z-score normalization. NTS is invariant to *monotonic distance scaling*, but highly sensitive to feature variance/covariance misalignment. Burying this critical preconditioning requirement obscures the metric's true operating domain and directly contradicts the central robustness claim.
- **Lack of Quantitative Rigor in LLM Analysis:** The core experimental claims (CKA "severe saturation," NTS "more discriminative," reliable fingerprinting) rest entirely on qualitative heatmap inspection and a single lineage counterexample. The paper reports no statistical validation: there are no confidence intervals across data subsamples/seeds, no quantitative correlation with known architectural distances or benchmark hierarchies, and no cluster validity scores. Furthermore, selecting Layer 6 because it "empirically yielded the most discriminative results" introduces unquantified cherry-picking risk without layer-averaged metrics or principled selection criteria.
- **High-Dimensional Robustness & Metric Scope are Under-specified:** The method is explicitly restricted to 0D homology, effectively functioning as a hierarchical clustering alignment tool. More critically, transformer embeddings suffer from severe distance concentration, where Euclidean distances lose discriminative power and small activation noise can abruptly rewire MST edges. The paper does not analyze how the union-based core edge set ($E_{core} = E_w \cup \tilde{E}_w$) behaves under metric substitution (e.g., cosine distance), noise perturbation, or dimensionality scaling, leaving the reliability of the rank correlation on high-dimensional manifolds unverified.

## Nice-to-Haves
- **Design Choice Ablations:** Quantify the impact of key engineering decisions: Spearman vs. Pearson correlation, $E_{core}$ union vs. intersection, and the exact variance explained by the Z-score preprocessing step across model families.
- **Score Distribution Analysis:** Supplement heatmap visualizations with score distribution histograms or boxplots across model pairs to objectively substantiate claims of "dynamic range" and prevent reader bias from sequential color mapping.
- **Optimization Benchmark Expansion:** Validate SRTD-lite as a regularization term on standard representation learning tasks (e.g., SimCLR/MoCo on CIFAR-100 or subset ImageNet) to move beyond small-scale autoencoder dimensionality reduction and prove broader training utility.

## Novel Insights
The paper's most valuable conceptual contribution is reframing topological similarity from a *persistent barcode magnitude summation* problem to an *order-preserving ranking* problem over Minimum Spanning Trees. This shift elegantly sidesteps the intractable normalization bottleneck of traditional persistence diagrams while naturally filtering out global scale variations and single-outlier barcode dominance. The theoretical decomposition of asymmetric RTD into a symmetric core plus residual directional components reveals that prior asymmetry was largely driven by a small subset of model-specific filtration artifacts, not fundamental topological disagreement. By treating merge-order as the primary structural invariant, NTS successfully isolates the functional clustering hierarchy of neural representations, demonstrating that structural alignment in deep networks is better captured by topological sequencing than by linear or kernelized geometric alignment alone.

## Potentially Missed Related Work
- **Gromov-Wasserstein / Sinkhorn Distances** (e.g., *Peyré et al., 2019*; *Vayer et al., 2020*) — Relevant for comparing metric spaces without requiring strict point-to-point correspondence; discussing how NTS trades OT's global optimality for MST-based computational efficiency would contextualize the design choice.
- **dCKA & Kernel Alignment Variants** (e.g., *Kornblith et al.* extended analyses, or *Ding et al., 2022* on CKA biases) — Comparing NTS against modern robust alignment baselines (Procrustes, HSIC-based, or distance correlation) would strengthen claims of superiority over CKA.

## Suggestions
1. **Relocate & Justify Z-Score Normalization:** Move the Z-score preprocessing step from Appendix K.1 into Section 4.3 and explicitly state that NTS requires feature-wise variance alignment to operate correctly. Clarify the theoretical boundary: NTS normalizes *distance ranks*, but relies on aligned feature scales to produce stable cross-model comparisons. Include a brief ablation table in Sec 5.4 showing score degradation without normalization.
2. **Quantify LLM Fingerprinting Claims:** Replace purely qualitative saturation assertions with quantitative evidence. Report bootstrap confidence intervals over multiple 1k-sample subsets, compute Spearman correlations between NTS similarity matrices and known architectural/lineage distances (parameter count, training data overlap, or distillation graphs), and provide a layer-sensitivity analysis to justify or replace the Layer-6 selection.
3. **Analyze MST Stability under High-Dimensional Metrics:** Add a short discussion or experiment examining how NTS behaves when using cosine distance vs. Euclidean distance on LLM embeddings, and test sensitivity to Gaussian noise injection. If distances concentrate or edges flip frequently, address how the $E_{core}$ rank aggregation remains stable, or propose a robust edge filtering mechanism.
