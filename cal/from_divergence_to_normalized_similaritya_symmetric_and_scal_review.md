# Harsh Critic Review
## Section-by-Section Critical Review

### Title & Abstract
- **Title Accuracy:** The title accurately reflects the paper's dual contribution: completing the theoretical framework of RTD with a symmetric variant (SRTD) and introducing a normalized similarity metric (NTS).
- **Abstract Clarity:** The abstract clearly outlines the limitations of RTD (asymmetry, lack of normalization), introduces the proposed methods, and summarizes key empirical findings. The claim that SRTD "matches the top performance of existing RTD-based methods in optimization tasks" is consistent with the experimental setup, though it implies equivalence rather than superiority, which is appropriate.
- **Claim Support:** The claim that NTS "provides a more discriminative score than CKA" for LLMs is supported by the inter-model similarity experiments (Figure 6), where CKA saturates. However, the abstract could briefly acknowledge that NTS's discriminative power here is contingent on a specific preprocessing step (Z-score normalization), which is detailed later but relevant to the scope of the claim.

### Introduction & Motivation
- **Problem Motivation & Gap:** The motivation is strong. The dichotomy between geometric methods like CKA (normalized but insensitive to topological shifts) and topological methods like RTD (sensitive but asymmetric/unnormalized) is a well-motivated gap in representation analysis.
- **Contributions:** Clearly stated. The theoretical completion of the RTD family and the introduction of a normalized, rank-based topological similarity measure are distinct and valuable contributions.
- **Over-claiming/Under-selling:** The introduction does not over-claim. The framing of SRTD as "completing" the framework is reasonable given Corollary 3.4 and 3.5, which elegantly relate the symmetric measure to the asymmetric variants. The distinction between the "full" and "lite" families is well-handled.

### Method / Approach
- **Clarity & Reproducibility:** The core definitions (3.1 for SRTD, 3.2 for SRTD-lite) and Algorithms 1/2 for NTS are generally clear. However, there is a discrepancy between Definition 3.2 and Algorithm 4 regarding SRTD-lite.
    - *Definition 3.2* states that SRTD-lite is obtained by comparing MST weights of `min(w, w̃)` and `max(w, w̃)`, implying a simple algebraic calculation: `MST(max) - MST(min)`.
    - *Algorithm 4*, however, describes a procedure that constructs a `TemporaryGraph` and checks connectivity in a nested loop. This algorithmic description suggests a simulation of 0-dimensional persistence pairing that would incur higher computational complexity (potentially $O(n^3)$ due to graph copying inside the loop) and contradicts the claimed "lightweight" and "efficient" nature of SRTD-lite. If SRTD-lite is indeed just the MST weight difference (as implied by Corollary 3.4 and the efficiency claims), Algorithm 4 is either misleading or should be explicitly labeled as a pedagogical illustration of the barcode construction rather than the implementation used in experiments.
- **Key Assumptions:** Assumptions are standard for representation analysis (one-to-one correspondence, valid distance metrics). The proofs rely on standard algebraic topology and graph theory results.
- **Logical Gaps / Theoretical Claims:**
    - *Theorem 3.3:* The relationship between Betti numbers and the integral of kernel dimensions is sound. The proof correctly integrates the rank-nullity result over the filtration parameter $\alpha$, which aligns with the fundamental theorem of persistent homology (sum of barcode lengths equals the integral of Betti numbers).
    - *Corollary 3.5:* The proof utilizing the supermodularity of the component number function $\kappa(S)$ is elegant and correct.
- **Edge Cases & Failure Modes:** The method assumes stable MST construction. In high-dimensional spaces or with significant noise, MSTs can be unstable (small perturbations can swap edge selections). The paper does not discuss the potential sensitivity of NTS and SRTD-lite to MST instability, which is a known failure mode for tree-based topological summaries.

### Experiments & Results
- **Testing Claims:** The experiments address the main claims. Synthetic data (Clusters, UMAP) shows sensitivity to structural changes. Autoencoder training demonstrates SRTD's utility as a loss. LLM analysis compares NTS against CKA.
- **Baselines:** CKA is the appropriate primary baseline. Justification for omitting SVCCA is standard and accepted.
- **Metric Utility & Saturation:**
    - In the autoencoder experiments, Tables 1 and 2 show NTS-E scores saturating at ~0.97 for *all* RTD-family optimized methods (RTD, Max-RTD, SRTD, and their lite variants). While NTS-E successfully distinguishes the baseline AE (0.71/0.78) from topological optimizations, it fails to discriminate *between* the topological methods. Using NTS-E as an optimization metric in this context does not add discriminatory value among the proposed variants. The authors should clarify that external metrics (e.g., Triplet Acc, Dist Corr) are necessary to differentiate optimization quality.
    - *Statistical Rigor:* The paper reports average accuracy over 45 model pairs (e.g., 97.22% in Figure 4b) and average runtimes, but lacks error bars, standard deviations, or confidence intervals. Given the variance inherent in training 10 random seeds, reporting variance for these key results is necessary for a top-tier venue like ICLR.
- **NTS Normalization Dependency:**
    - The main text presents NTS as "scale-invariant" and "normalized." However, Appendix K.1 and Section 5.4 state that Z-score normalization across the feature dimension is "crucial" for NTS to work on LLM representations. This is a significant practical limitation. Z-scoring is an affine transformation of the representation space, not a scale-invariant operation on the distance matrix. If NTS requires Z-scoring to avoid degenerate scores, this contradicts the claim of inherent robustness to representation scale and suggests sensitivity to the anisotropy of the feature distribution. This dependency should be discussed in the main text and justified in the context of "scale-invariance."
- **Runtime Claims:** Figure 7 clearly shows NTS-E's superior scalability. The complexity claim "$O(n^2(\alpha(n) + d))$" uses non-standard notation (likely mixing dimension $d$, sample size $n$, and inverse Ackermann $\alpha(n)$). While the empirical results validate scalability, the theoretical bound notation is imprecise.

### Writing & Clarity
- **Conceptual Flow:** The progression from asymmetric divergence to a symmetric framework, then to a normalized rank-based similarity, is logical and well-structured.
- **Figures & Tables:** 
    - Figure 4 effectively visualizes the functional shift detection. 
    - Figure 6 is compelling in showing CKA saturation.
    - Algorithm 4, as noted, causes confusion regarding implementation vs. theoretical construction. Clarifying that SRTD-lite is computed via the MST difference formula (not the nested graph construction) would significantly improve clarity.
    - The matrix notations for $M_{sym}$, $M_{min}$, and $M_{max}$ suffer from parser artifacts in the provided text, but the block structure is inferable. In the final version, ensuring these are clearly rendered is important for reproducibility.

### Limitations & Broader Impact
- **Acknowledged Limitations:** The authors correctly note that NTS is non-differentiable and thus analysis-only, pointing to future work on differentiable approximations. The computational cost of full SRTD is also acknowledged.
- **Missed Limitations:**
    - **MST Instability:** As mentioned, the reliance on MST structure makes NTS and SRTD-lite potentially brittle to metric perturbations or high-dimensional concentration of measure. A brief discussion on this would strengthen the paper.
    - **Preprocessing Dependency:** The necessity of Z-score normalization for LLMs is a limitation that impacts the generalizability of the "scale-invariant" claim. It should be explicitly stated as a limitation rather than buried in the appendix.
- **Broader Impact:** The paper focuses on analytical tools for model interpretability and debugging. These tools are benign and generally positive for the field, helping practitioners understand training dynamics and model hierarchies. No negative societal impacts are apparent.

### Overall Assessment
This paper presents a solid theoretical extension of Representation Topology Divergence (RTD) by introducing a symmetric formulation (SRTD) with elegant properties (Theorems 3.3, Corollaries 3.4/3.5) and proposing Normalized Topological Similarity (NTS) to address the interpretability and scale-dependency of divergence measures. The empirical evaluation is comprehensive, covering synthetic data, optimization tasks, and LLM representation analysis. The identification of CKA score saturation in LLM comparisons is a genuinely useful insight for the community. 

However, for ICLR acceptance, the authors must address several substantive concerns: (1) the discrepancy between the efficient MST-difference definition of SRTD-lite and the computationally expensive Algorithm 4 description; (2) the saturation of NTS-E in the autoencoder tables, which limits its utility as a discriminative metric among topological methods; (3) the lack of statistical reporting (error bars/variance) for the layer accuracy and runtime claims; and (4) the implicit dependency of NTS on Z-score normalization for LLMs, which challenges the "scale-invariant" claim and warrants clearer discussion. If these issues are resolved, particularly by clarifying the method implementation and adding statistical rigor, the contribution stands as a valuable addition to the toolkit for representation analysis. The theoretical framework is sound, and the practical motivation is well-justified.

# Neutral Reviewer
## Balanced Review

### Summary
This paper addresses the asymmetry and lack of scale invariance in Representation Topology Divergence (RTD) by introducing Symmetric RTD (SRTD) and a computationally efficient variant, SRTD-lite, backed by a complete theoretical framework using mapping cones and long exact sequences. It further proposes Normalized Topological Similarity (NTS), a rank-based metric that captures hierarchical clustering structure to provide a bounded, interpretable similarity score robust to representation scale and size. Comprehensive experiments across synthetic data, CNN layer analysis, and LLM representation comparison demonstrate that the proposed toolkit bridges topological sensitivity with the practical interpretability previously dominated by geometric measures like CKA.

### Strengths
1. **Rigorous Theoretical Unification:** The paper formally connects RTD, Max-RTD, and the proposed SRTD through a long exact sequence of relative homology groups and mapping cones (Theorem 3.3, Definition 3.1). For the MST-based variants, it proves exact relationships and strict hierarchical inequalities (Corollaries 3.4–3.5), transforming previously ad-hoc symmetrization into a mathematically elegant framework.
2. **Practical & Well-Motivated NTS Metric:** Recognizing that summing barcode lengths is unbounded and sensitive to outliers (Fig. 19a, App. J), NTS shifts focus to the rank order of merge events. By using Spearman’s correlation on a unified MST edge set (Algorithms 1 & 2), it yields a normalized `[−1, 1]` score that successfully reveals graded inter-layer similarities and avoids the severe saturation observed in CKA heatmaps for LLMs (Fig. 6b vs 6a).
3. **Strong Empirical Diagnostic Value:** The TinyCNN experiments convincingly show that NTS matches CKA’s ability to map adjacent layer similarity while uniquely detecting the functional shift at the global pooling layer (Fig. 4). The LLM analysis further demonstrates that NTS consistently recovers family-specific hierarchical fingerprints and correctly identifies parent-child distillation lineages where CKA produces counter-intuitive, low-similarity scores (Sec. 5.4).
4. **Clear Algorithms & Complexity Analysis:** The step-by-step presentation of SRTD/SRTD-lite and NTS, accompanied by explicit time complexity breakdowns `O(n^2(α(n)+d))` and memory footprint comparisons, makes the methods highly accessible and directly implementable, aligning with ICLR’s emphasis on practical, reproducible toolkits.

### Weaknesses
1. **Residual O(N^2) Bottleneck & Scaling Claims:** While SRTD-lite and NTS-E are more efficient than full RTD, they still require computing and storing full pairwise distance matrices `O(N^2)`. The scalability claims are therefore bounded to moderate sample sizes (~30k as tested). The paper does not explore or benchmark approximate strategies (e.g., landmark sampling, k-NN graph sparsification, or out-of-core computation) that would be necessary for larger-scale representation analysis.
2. **Tension Between Scale Invariance Claim & Z-Score Sensitivity:** NTS is presented as scale-invariant due to rank correlation, yet Appendix K.1 reveals it is highly sensitive to Z-score normalization of input features, with scores becoming "surprisingly low" without it (Fig. 21). The precise point of normalization (pre-distance vs. post-distance) and its impact on the underlying metric geometry are under-specified, raising questions about true invariance and requiring clearer justification.
3. **Limited Baseline Scope in Modern Context:** The evaluation primarily compares against CKA, RTD, and RTD-lite. Given rapid advances in representation analysis, the absence of comparisons to recent baselines (e.g., Optimal Transport-based metrics, attention-aware similarity scores, or differentiable topological losses) weakens the claim of superior discriminative power in isolation, particularly for the LLM experiments.
4. **NTS Non-Differentiability Restricts Optimization Use:** The authors correctly note NTS cannot serve as an optimization loss due to non-differentiable MST and ranking operations (Sec. 6). While acknowledged as future work, the paper does not explore or propose relaxations (e.g., soft sorting, Gumbel-Sinkhorn, or continuous MST approximations) that would bridge the gap between analysis-only metrics and trainable objectives.

### Novelty & Significance
**Novelty:** High. SRTD completes an active research line on topological divergence with novel topological proofs, while NTS introduces a fundamentally different approach to topological similarity by moving from persistence magnitude to merge-order correlation. The unification of directional RTD variants into a single symmetric theoretical structure is a meaningful contribution to TDA in ML.
**Clarity:** Very Good. The logical flow from limitation diagnosis → theoretical resolution → algorithmic design → empirical validation is coherent. Mathematical derivations and algorithms are well-paced and adequately referenced.
**Reproducibility:** Good. Standard datasets, explicit algorithms, hyperparameter tables, and a promise to release code support replication. Minor clarifications on tie-breaking rules for MST/ranking and exact normalization placement would ensure strict reproducibility.
**Significance:** High for representation analysis and model interpretability. The toolkit directly addresses practical pain points (CKA saturation, divergence scaling, asymmetry) and demonstrates timely value in LLM ecosystem mapping and architectural analysis, meeting ICLR’s standard for methods that advance both theoretical understanding and practical tooling.

### Suggestions for Improvement
1. **Clarify Normalization Protocol:** Explicitly state whether Z-score normalization is applied per-feature before distance computation or as a rank transform on distances. Justify why it's necessary despite the scale-invariant nature of rank correlation, and consider evaluating a purely distance-based normalization (e.g., min-max scaling of the core edge set) to maintain strict metric compatibility.
2. **Address Large-N Scalability:** Acknowledge the `O(N^2)` ceiling and discuss practical workarounds. Even a brief discussion or ablation on landmark-based distance approximation or sparse k-NN MST construction would strengthen the "scalable" claim and demonstrate readiness for larger representation corpora.
3. **Disentangle "Functional Shift" from Architectural Bottlenecks:** To firmly attribute pooling layer sensitivity to topological hierarchy rather than representational collapse or dimensionality reduction, add a control experiment using an architecture that replaces pooling with strided convolutions or global average pooling earlier. This would isolate the topological signal from geometric compression artifacts.
4. **Explore Differentiable Relaxations of NTS:** Propose or benchmark a differentiable surrogate (e.g., soft rank correlation via sorting networks, continuous approximations of MST via graph neural potentials, or entropic regularization) that approximates NTS-E/M. Even initial experiments showing gradient flow or loss curve comparison would significantly broaden the paper's impact for representation learning.
5. **Expand Baseline Comparisons:** Include at least one recent, non-geometric similarity metric (e.g., Wasserstein distance of representation distributions, or shape-matching scores) in the LLM inter-model analysis. This would contextualize NTS's discriminative advantage beyond the RTD/CKA family and strengthen conclusions about its robustness in the modern model landscape.

# Final Consolidated Review
## Summary
This paper addresses two critical limitations of Representation Topology Divergence (RTD)—its asymmetry and lack of a normalized scale—by introducing Symmetric RTD (SRTD/SRTD-lite) with a complete theoretical framework, and Normalized Topological Similarity (NTS), a rank-based metric that compares hierarchical clustering structures. Through experiments on synthetic data, autoencoder optimization, CNN layer analysis, and LLM representations, the authors demonstrate that NTS avoids the severe score saturation of Centered Kernel Alignment (CKA), reveals graded inter-layer similarity patterns, and correctly identifies architectural shifts and model lineages that geometric measures miss.

## Strengths
- **Mathematically rigorous theoretical unification:** The paper elegantly completes the RTD framework by connecting directional RTD, Max-RTD, and SRTD through mapping cones and a long exact sequence of relative homology groups (Theorem 3.3). For the efficient lite variants, it proves exact algebraic relationships and strict hierarchical inequalities (Corollaries 3.4–3.5), replacing ad-hoc symmetrization with a principled topological structure.
- **NTS successfully bridges interpretability and topological sensitivity:** By shifting from persistence magnitudes to Spearman's rank correlation of MST merge orders, NTS yields a normalized `[-1, 1]` score that directly addresses the interpretability gap of unbounded divergences. The empirical results compellingly show NTS matching CKA's ability to map graded layer similarity while uniquely detecting the functional shift at CNN pooling layers.
- **Practical value for LLM ecosystem analysis:** The inter-model similarity experiments (Figure 6) convincingly demonstrate that NTS avoids the severe score saturation that renders CKA heatmaps uninformative across different LLM families. NTS preserves family-specific hierarchical patterns and correctly identifies parent-child distillation relationships (e.g., DeepSeek-R1 → Qwen2.5), offering a more discriminative diagnostic tool for large-scale representation comparison.

## Weaknesses
- **Contradiction between "scale-invariance" claims and Z-score dependency:** The paper repeatedly frames NTS as inherently "scale-invariant" and robust to representation scale due to its rank-based formulation. However, Appendix K.1 and Section 5.4 admit that Z-score normalization across the feature dimension is "crucial" for LLM representations, and scores degrade ("become surprisingly low") without it. Z-scoring is an affine transformation on the representation vectors, not a scale-invariant operation on pairwise distances. If the metric's reliability hinges on this preprocessing step, the core claim of intrinsic scale-invariance is overstated and requires explicit qualification or a mechanistic explanation of why rank correlation fails without feature-space standardization.
- **Algorithmic presentation contradicts efficiency claims for SRTD-lite:** Definition 3.2 and Corollary 3.4 establish that SRTD-lite reduces to a simple difference of MST weights (`MST(max) - MST(min)`), which is computationally lightweight. Yet Algorithm 4 describes a nested graph-construction loop with explicit connectivity checks inside a `foreach` block, which suggests a significantly higher complexity than the claimed $O(n^2)$ implementation. This creates ambiguity about whether the algorithm is a pedagogical illustration of barcode pairing or the actual implementation used in benchmarks, hindering reproducibility and casting doubt on the "scalable" framing if misinterpreted.
- **Lack of stability analysis for finite-sample MST dependence:** Both NTS and SRTD-lite rely entirely on the Minimum Spanning Tree topology constructed from finite sample sizes (e.g., $N=1000$ for LLMs). MSTs in high-dimensional neural activation spaces are notoriously sensitive to small metric perturbations and sampling variance. The paper asserts that NTS provides a "stable similarity score" but provides no empirical ablation on how sample size or injected noise affects the rank correlation. Without demonstrating robustness at finite $N$, the reliability of the proposed scores for diagnostic use remains unverified.
- **Limited discriminative utility in optimization diagnostics:** While NTS excels as an analysis tool, its saturation at `~0.97` across all topological optimization baselines in Tables 1 and 2 shows it cannot differentiate between the RTD-family variants when used as a quality metric. The paper acknowledges NTS as "analysis-only," but presenting it alongside SRTD-lite as a top-tier diagnostic creates an expectation of fine-grained discriminative power that the current formulation does not support in training contexts.

## Nice-to-Haves
- Report standard deviations or confidence intervals for the 10-seed TinyCNN layer identification accuracy (e.g., $97.22\% \pm ?$) to quantify variance inherent to random initialization.
- Clarify in the main text or a brief appendix why Z-score normalization is necessary despite the rank-based formulation (e.g., does it mitigate anisotropic covariance structures that distort distance ranks?).
- Briefly discuss the $O(N^2)$ distance matrix bottleneck in the context of modern LLM hidden states, and note whether approximate strategies (e.g., landmark sampling or k-NN sparse MST) could extend scalability beyond 30k samples without compromising topological signal.
- Justify the choice of Spearman’s rank correlation over alternatives like Kendall’s tau or cosine similarity on normalized merge vectors, even if empirically equivalent, to solidify the methodological grounding.

## Novel Insights
The work successfully reframes topological similarity analysis by demonstrating that the *order* of hierarchical cluster merges, rather than their persistence magnitudes, carries a more robust and interpretable signal for neural representation comparison. This rank-shift elegantly bypasses both the unbounded scaling of divergence measures and the geometric saturation of kernel-based alignment, revealing that topological sensitivity to architectural bottlenecks (like pooling layers) and lineage relationships is preserved even when absolute distance scales vary drastically.

## Suggestions
- **Reframe the scale-invariance claim:** Explicitly state that NTS achieves score normalization via rank correlation, but requires feature-space standardization (Z-scoring) to mitigate anisotropy in high-dimensional representations. Provide a brief empirical or theoretical justification for why unnormalized distances break the monotonic merge-order assumption in LLM spaces.
- **Clarify the SRTD-lite implementation:** Add a note in Section 3 or Algorithm 4 explicitly stating that the practical, efficient computation uses the closed-form MST weight difference (`SRTD-lite = MST(max(w, w̃)) - MST(min(w, w̃))`), and label Algorithm 4 as a conceptual construction for understanding barcode pairing rather than the implementation used in runtime benchmarks.
- **Add a robustness ablation:** Include a concise experiment or discussion on NTS stability as a function of sample size $N$ and/or Gaussian noise injection on the distance matrix. Quantify where rank correlation begins to degrade to establish trustworthy operating regimes for practitioners.
