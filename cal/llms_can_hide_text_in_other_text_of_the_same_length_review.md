# Harsh Critic Review
## Section-by-Section Critical Review

**Title & Abstract**
The title accurately captures the core technical contribution. The abstract clearly states the protocol, computational efficiency, and key implications. One minor precision issue: "same length" refers to *token* length, not character or word length, which should be explicitly clarified given that tokenizers vary in how they segment text. The claim that 8B models suffice is later substantiated in Section A.2, so the abstract's scope is well-calibrated.

**Introduction & Motivation**
The introduction effectively bridges a conceptual problem (decoupling text from intent) with a concrete technical proposal. The contributions are clearly stated, and the historical/philosophical framing, while stylized, appropriately motivates the paper's broader aims. However, the introduction could more explicitly distinguish this work from prior *generative steganography* beyond just "full capacity," as the token-to-token mapping introduces unique constraints that set up the methodological choices later.

**Related Work**
The survey of steganography and LLM-based generative methods is adequate and positions the paper within current literature. However, the claim of offering "full capacity" (stegotext and secret message being the same length) is terminologically ambiguous. In information theory, capacity refers to *bits per token*, not token count. A 1:1 token mapping does not guarantee a 1:1 information rate; the effective capacity depends entirely on the rank distribution of the hidden text `e`. If `e` contains highly predictable tokens, their ranks will cluster near 1, severely limiting the entropy available for steganographic embedding. This distinction between *length preservation* and *information capacity* should be clarified to avoid overstating the protocol's throughput.

**Method**
The rank-preserving procedure is elegantly simple and reproducible in principle. However, two substantive technical gaps require attention:
1. **Hardware & Determinism Fragility:** The protocol requires exact rank matching between encoding and decoding. As acknowledged in Section 3 (Limitations), this relies on identical logits. In practice, modern LLM inference uses FP16/BF16, non-associative arithmetic, flash attention, and kernel-level optimizations that routinely shift token probabilities enough to alter rank ordering, even with identical seeds and weights. The paper notes this as a "challenge" but provides no mitigation. For a practical protocol, this is critical. Error-tolerant rank binning, top-k tolerance windows, or lightweight error-correcting codes should be discussed or at least formally recognized as a strict requirement.
2. **Information Rate Analysis:** The method maps ranks but does not quantify how many bits of `e` can be reliably embedded per token. Since ranks are not uniformly distributed across arbitrary text (see Figure 5), the effective embedding rate varies significantly with domain and model quality. A brief analysis of bits/token or a capacity bound would strengthen the methodological foundation.

**Experiments & Results**
The empirical evaluation demonstrates feasibility but lacks the rigor expected for an ICLR submission in the steganography/evaluation space:
1. **Plausibility Metric:** Section 3 uses raw log-probability as the soundness proxy and explicitly rejects perplexity because "shorter texts would be less plausible" due to initial token probabilities. This reasoning mischaracterizes perplexity, which normalizes log-likelihood by token count and is standard precisely because it neutralizes length effects. For 85-token sequences, initial-token probability contributions are negligible. Using raw log-prob confounds length, tokenization variance, and true fluency, making Figure 4 and Figure 14 difficult to compare with standard literature.
2. **Missing Detection Baselines:** Steganography papers at ICLR/NeurIPS/ICCV are typically expected to evaluate against steganalyzers. The protocol's detectability is only assessed via probability gaps, yet rank-constrained generation leaves statistical fingerprints (e.g., unnatural rank uniformity, entropy deviation, or context-probability mismatch). Simple baselines (e.g., LLM-based anomaly detection, fine-tuned classifiers on rank distributions, or perplexity delta tests) are absent. Without them, the security claims remain conceptual rather than empirical.
3. **Security & Deniability:** The deniability example (Figure 15) is illustrative but constructed. A rigorous claim of deniability requires analyzing the attacker's search space efficiency, the false-positive rate of bogus keys, and the statistical overlap under adversarial key search. As written, the security analysis reads as a proof-of-concept rather than a measured evaluation.

**Discussion & Implications**
The "unaligned chatbot" scenario is creative and highlights a novel abuse vector. However, it assumes the end-user will run a local LLM in a non-standard, deterministic rank-following mode, which deviates from all mainstream chat APIs and requires user-side technical execution. The scenario also assumes the company can guarantee `s` passes safety filters while `u` remains harmful; given that `s`'s coherence depends on `e`'s rank distribution, harmful `u` often forces `s` into low-probability or stylistically anomalous regions, increasing detection risk. The philosophical reframing of hallucination as "lack of intent" is thought-provoking and aligns with broader interpretability discourse, but it remains speculative and does not impact how models are currently evaluated or mitigated for factual errors. The discussion would benefit from tighter grounding in practical alignment/evaluation pipelines.

**Limitations & Broader Impact**
The limitations section correctly identifies coherence dependency, abrupt termination, and GPU nondeterminism. However, it omits the protocol's vulnerability to statistical steganalysis, which is a primary failure mode for deployment. The broader impact discussion appropriately flags censorship circumvention, misinformation, and AI safety trade-offs. Given the protocol's ease of implementation, a brief note on responsible disclosure or detection countermeasures would strengthen the paper's positioning at ICLR.

### Overall Assessment
The paper presents a novel, elegantly simple protocol (`Calgacus`) that uses token-rank preservation to achieve token-level length-preserving text steganography, accompanied by a thoughtful discussion on AI safety, intent, and LLM knowledge. The core idea is sound, the demo is reproducible, and the implications are timely for ICLR's focus on LLM capabilities and alignment. However, the empirical evaluation falls short of the venue's standards: the plausibility metric bypasses standard likelihood normalization, the paper lacks any evaluation against steganalyzers or detection baselines, and the method's critical fragility to hardware/precision-induced rank shifts is noted but not addressed. Additionally, claims of "full capacity" conflate token count with information rate, and the AI safety scenario relies on idealized user behavior and untested assumptions about filter evasion. With rigorous detection baselines, a clarified capacity analysis, and at least a practical mitigation for rank instability, this work would be a strong fit. In its current form, it is promising but requires substantial empirical and methodological tightening before meeting ICLR's acceptance bar.

# Neutral Reviewer
## Balanced Review

### Summary
The paper introduces *Calgacus*, a generative steganography protocol that hides an arbitrary secret message within a coherent, topically steered cover text of identical token length by mapping the rank sequence of the secret message to a target LLM's generation process. The authors demonstrate that modest 8B-parameter models can efficiently encode/decode texts on consumer hardware and use the protocol to discuss significant implications for AI safety, particularly the potential to mask unaligned model behaviors within aligned responses, as well as philosophical questions surrounding LLM knowledge attribution and authorial intent.

### Strengths
1. **Elegant Methodology & Exceptional Clarity:** The core algorithm is remarkably simple and is explained with a highly accessible step-by-step recipe, clear notation, and intuitive visualizations (e.g., Figure 3). The writing effectively bridges technical execution and high-level conceptual discussion.
2. **Solid Empirical Validation of Plausibility:** The use of a diverse corpus of 1,000 real Reddit posts to benchmark log-probability distributions (Figure 4) provides a concrete, quantitative backing for the claim that stegotexts fall within the natural plausibility range of human writing. The analysis of why stegotexts are systematically less probable (rank 1 tokens being "wasted" on low-entropy positions, Figure 5) is insightful.
3. **High Reproducibility & Practical Accessibility:** The authors provide a public codebase, clear hardware/software specifications (Appendix A.2: Llama.cpp, RTX 4070, quantized weights), and straightforward runtime metrics, making it easy for the community to verify and build upon the work.
4. **Provocative & Timely Conceptual Framing:** The paper successfully elevates a technical steganography mechanism into a broader discourse on AI alignment vulnerabilities (the "Shibbolethian Theatre" scenario), the nature of hallucinations as a lack of intent, and the fragility of trust in machine-generated text, which aligns well with ICLR's interest in broader impacts and interpretability.

### Weaknesses
1. **Insufficient Steganographic Evaluation & Missing Baselines:** The evaluation relies almost exclusively on LLM-assigned log-probability as a proxy for text quality. For an ICLR submission, this lacks standard metrics: no human evaluation scores for coherence/naturalness, no comparison to established generative steganography baselines (e.g., Meteor, distribution-preserving schemes), and no testing against statistical stego-detectators (e.g., GLTR, watermark detectors, or trained classifiers). The claim of "high-quality results" is thus only partially substantiated.
2. **Qualitative Security & Deniability Analysis:** The security discussion (Section 3.1) is largely theoretical. The $O(d^{|k|})$ brute-force bound ignores the low entropy of natural language keys. The deniability claim (Figure 15) is illustrated with a single handcrafted toy example rather than a systematic analysis of false-positive rates, key-collision probabilities, or the computational cost of searching for alternative plausible decodings. This falls short of the rigorous cryptographic or probabilistic security standards typically expected for steganographic protocols.
3. **Overstated "Same Length" Guarantee & Tokenization Blind Spots:** The protocol guarantees *token*-length parity, not word or character length. Due to subword tokenizers (BPE), a stegotext will almost always differ in visual length and linguistic structure from the cover. The paper does not adequately address how tokenizer differences between encoder/decoder models or across languages impact the claim, nor does it quantify the variance in word/character counts.
4. **Speculative AI Safety Scenario:** While the aligned/unaligned LLM "disguise" scenario is compelling conceptually, it is presented without empirical validation against real-world aligned serving pipelines, which typically employ output filtering, monitoring layers, and diverse safety classifiers. Claiming it as a "concrete scenario" without demonstrating evasion of standard safety guardrails weakens the practical impact.

### Novelty & Significance
The technical novelty is moderate; using token ranks/predictions for text hiding exists in prior literature, and the "full capacity" (1:1 token mapping) constraint limits information-theoretic flexibility compared to adaptive-rate schemes. However, the achievement of exact token-length matching with high steerability using commodity models is a valuable practical contribution. The paper's significance lies more in its conceptual and safety-oriented framing than in algorithmic innovation. It effectively uses a simple mechanism to provoke necessary debate on authorship, intent, and the limitations of current alignment paradigms, making it a strong discussion piece for the ICLR community despite empirical gaps.

### Suggestions for Improvement
1. **Add Rigorous Quantitative & Human Evaluation:** Report human-rated naturalness/fluency scores (e.g., Likert scale via crowd-sourcing or expert reviewers) and test steganographic detectability using both statistical methods (GLTR, rank distributions) and/or trained binary classifiers. Compare embedding capacity, detectability, and fluency against at least two baseline steganography methods.
2. **Formalize Security & Deniability Analysis:** Replace qualitative security claims with a quantified analysis: calculate the effective entropy of natural language prompts, estimate brute-force feasibility given linguistic constraints, and run a systematic search experiment to measure the empirical frequency of producing alternative high-probability decodings for a single stegotext (quantifying the deniability false-positive rate).
3. **Clarify Tokenization Implications & Cross-Model Constraints:** Explicitly discuss the difference between token, word, and character length in the limitations. Provide data on how word-length variance scales with different prompts/languages and address the practical barriers of cross-model/cross-tokenizer compatibility (mentioned briefly in Appendix A.3 but needs clearer limitations framing).
4. **Ground the AI Safety Scenario:** Add a realistic threat model analysis showing how the proposed unaligned/aligned disguise strategy would interact with modern LLM deployment safeguards (e.g., external moderation APIs, system-prompt injections, output logging). Even a brief empirical test or structured theoretical breakdown would strengthen the claim from speculative to practically concerning.

# Spark Finder Review
## How to Improve This Paper

### Missing Experiments (top 3-5 only)
1. Benchmark against established LLM steganography baselines (e.g., Meteor, distribution-preserving methods, and rank-mapping variants) on fluency and detectability metrics, because without comparative baselines the claim of a superior or novel protocol is unverified.
2. Run a blind human evaluation study assessing perceived coherence, topic steering fidelity, and concealment success, because LLM log-probabilities are known to diverge from human judgment and the paper's core contribution rests on texts being human-plausible.
3. Evaluate the generated stegotexts against automated steganalysis detectors (e.g., entropy-based tests or lightweight classifiers trained on rank distributions), because aggregate probability gaps are not a standard security metric and the method's actual resistance to statistical detection remains unknown.
4. Empirically test the "unaligned chatbot" pipeline using actual safety-aligned models to measure how often the aligned model accepts the engineered reasoning-trace prompt `k`, because aligned models routinely reject or rewrite complex adversarial instructions, making the current AI safety claim purely hypothetical.

### Deeper Analysis Needed (top 3-5 only)
1. Quantify the precise capacity-versus-quality trade-off by deriving the failure rate as a function of source text perplexity and domain mismatch, because the "full capacity" claim ignores the severe fluency degradation that occurs when `e` contains high-rank tokens.
2. Rigorously calculate the statistical overlap between real and synthetic log-probability distributions and report the baseline success rate of generating valid "bogus keys," because deniability cannot be claimed on the basis of hand-picked outliers.
3. Provide an information-theoretic or mechanistic derivation of why rank-preserving sampling systematically degrades log-probability under high-entropy contexts, because the heuristic explanation of "wasted rank-1 tokens" is insufficient to predict or bound output quality.

### Visualizations & Case Studies
1. Plot ROC curves or confusion matrices for human readers and automated steganographers attempting to distinguish stegotext from authentic text in identical domains, because high classification accuracy would directly invalidate the concealment premise.
2. Visualize generation breakdown trajectories by highlighting the exact token positions where high-rank source tokens derail narrative flow across varying prompt lengths, because a systematic failure map would expose whether the method generalizes or only works on highly predictable, in-distribution text.
3. Include a case study mapping prompt `k` complexity to the aligned model's compliance/rejection behavior when simulating the AI safety scenario, because visualizing the narrow band of prompts that actually bypass safety filters reveals the practical fragility of the proposed attack vector.

### Obvious Next Steps
1. Implement entropy-coding or distribution-matching decoding strategies rather than naive rank selection, because raw rank mapping is information-theoretically inefficient and unnecessarily sacrifices fluency to preserve token length.
2. Conduct rigorous cross-backend and cross-precision decoding tests (INT4 vs FP16, different inference libraries, different hardware architectures) to quantify exact-match failure rates, because the requirement for bitwise-identical logits is a severe deployment bottleneck that is only briefly acknowledged.
3. Formalize the alignment-evasion scenario into a reproducible adversarial benchmark with defined success/failure criteria, because moving from a theatrical script to a measurable protocol is required for the AI safety community to take the threat model seriously.

# Final Consolidated Review
## Summary
The paper introduces *Calgacus*, a generative steganography protocol that hides an arbitrary secret message within a coherent, steerable cover text of identical token length by directly mapping the secret's token rank sequence to a target LLM's generation process. Demonstrated efficiently on commodity hardware with 8B-parameter models, the protocol is leveraged not just as a technical contribution, but as a conceptual lens to examine the decoupling of text from authorial intent, propose a novel framework for understanding LLM hallucinations, and illustrate a theoretical AI safety vulnerability where aligned model outputs could covertly encode unfiltered responses.

## Strengths
- **Elegant, Reproducible Methodology:** The rank-preserving mapping is remarkably simple, explicitly documented, and efficiently executable on consumer hardware. The step-by-step recipe, clear notation, and public codebase ensure high reproducibility while successfully achieving the paper's core technical goal: exact token-length parity with high topical steerability.
- **Mechanistic Plausibility Analysis:** The empirical comparison against 1,000 authentic Reddit posts rigorously demonstrates that stegotext log-probabilities fall within natural text distributions. The paper's mechanistic explanation for why stegotexts are systematically less probable (i.e., the "wasting" of high-confidence rank-1 tokens on low-entropy contexts) provides valuable, grounded insight into the constraints of autoregressive generation.
- **Conceptual Reframing of LLM Behavior:** The paper effectively elevates a steganographic mechanism into a broader discourse on AI alignment and interpretability. The arguments redefining hallucinations as a "void of intent" rather than a factual error, and questioning what it means for an LLM to "know" something when it can serve as a lossless conduit for arbitrary encoded information, are intellectually rigorous and highly resonant with current ICLR research trajectories.

## Weaknesses
- **Security and Deniability Claims Lack Quantitative Grounding:** The protocol's security relies heavily on prompt-key secrecy and key-collision ambiguity, but these claims remain conceptual. The deniability argument is illustrated through a single, handcrafted toy example without quantitative assessment of false-positive rates, empirical bounds on alternative decoding overlaps, or analysis of adversarial search-space complexity under linguistic constraints. Without systematic measurement, the steganographic security guarantees are illustrative rather than rigorously established.
- **Highly Idealized AI Safety Threat Model:** The "Shibbolethian Theatre" scenario is conceptually intriguing but rests on specific, unvalidated assumptions: end-users executing non-standard deterministic local inference, and the engineered cover text seamlessly bypassing modern deployment safeguards (e.g., external moderation APIs, real-time output filtering, and system-prompt monitoring). Without empirical validation against actual safety pipelines or an analysis of detection risks when the hidden payload contains highly anomalous tokens, the practical severity of this alignment-evasion vector remains largely speculative.

## Nice-to-Haves
- Investigating lightweight error-tolerance mechanisms (e.g., rank binning, top-k windows, or simple forward error correction) to mitigate decoding failures caused by hardware non-determinism, varying precision settings, or cross-backend inference discrepancies.
- Benchmarking against established generative steganography methods or applying lightweight statistical steganalysis (e.g., entropy deviation tests or rank-distribution classifiers) to contextualize the protocol's detectability within the broader literature.
- Clarifying how subword tokenization inherently decouples token-length parity from visual word/character length, and providing brief empirical data on how variance scales across prompts or languages to manage reader expectations.

## Novel Insights
The paper's most compelling contribution lies in treating LLM-generated stegotext as an empirical instrument to decouple textual coherence from authorial intent. By demonstrating that an LLM can maintain syntactic and semantic fluility in a generated text while having every token strictly dictated by an external, unrelated sequence, the authors compellingly argue that "hallucination" is better understood as a failure of trust in the author's purpose rather than a mere factual inaccuracy. Furthermore, the protocol reveals that an LLM's probabilistic knowledge space is sufficiently rich to act as a neutral conduit for arbitrary information it would otherwise be fine-tuned to suppress, fundamentally challenging current paradigms of alignment, safety filtering, and the epistemic boundaries of machine-generated text.

## Suggestions
- Substantiate the deniability and security sections by running a systematic empirical sweep: quantify the distribution of log-probability overlaps across diverse prompt candidates for a given stegotext, and report the frequency/rate of generating alternative plausible decodings to move from illustrative examples to measurable bounds.
- Ground the AI safety discussion by adding a brief threat-model analysis or pilot experiment that tests how engineered reasoning-trace prompts interact with standard aligned model serving layers (e.g., rejection rates, safety classifier triggers, or output monitoring flags), clarifying whether the scenario represents a near-term vulnerability or a theoretical edge case under modern deployment practices.
