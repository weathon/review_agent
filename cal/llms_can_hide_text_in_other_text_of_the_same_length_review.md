# Harsh Critic Review
## Section-by-Section Critical Review

### Title & Abstract
- **Title Accuracy:** The title accurately captures the core technical claim: hiding text in text of identical length using LLMs.
- **Abstract Clarity:** The abstract clearly states the problem (concealing meaningful text), the method (`Calgacus`, rank-based mapping), and key results (works on 8B models, runs locally in seconds, steerable output). It also correctly foreshadows the philosophical/AI safety implications.
- **Unsupported Claims:** The claim that `Calgacus` obtains "high-quality results" is not quantitatively defined in the abstract. While the paper later uses LLM log-probability and qualitative human inspection as proxies, the abstract presents quality as a given without specifying the evaluation paradigm. Additionally, stating that the method demonstrates a "radical decoupling of text from authorial intent" crosses into interpretive territory; while well-argued in Section 4, it is less a result and more a framing device. The abstract would be stronger by briefly noting the empirical proxy for quality (e.g., distributional alignment with human text log-probabilities) and scoping the decoding constraint (requires exact model matching).

### Introduction & Motivation
- **Problem & Gap:** The motivation is clear and well-positioned. The authors correctly identify that prior generative steganography methods either modify a pre-existing covertext, operate at limited capacity, or lack the exact 1:1 token-length symmetry. Framing the contribution around preserving exact length while maintaining steerability addresses a meaningful gap.
- **Contributions:** Clearly stated: (1) the `Calgacus` protocol, (2) demonstration on consumer hardware, (3) quantitative plausibility analysis, and (4) discussion of AI safety/intent implications.
- **Over/Under-claiming:** There is a slight over-claim regarding steerability. Section 3 ("When the stegotext s sounds like a real text") and Appendix A.1 acknowledge that if the secret text $e$ is out-of-distribution (e.g., Romanesco dialect) or contains high-rank tokens, the stegotext becomes incoherent regardless of $k$. The introduction should explicitly bound the steerability claim to cases where $e$ falls within the LLM's learned distribution manifold, to avoid setting unrealistic expectations.

### Method / Approach
- **Clarity & Reproducibility:** The 3-step encoding and 2-step decoding procedure is elegantly described. The requirement for exact model reproduction is noted in the limitations. Given the simplicity, reproducibility is straightforward assuming fixed random seeds, identical floating-point environments, and matching tokenizer/LLM versions.
- **Key Assumptions:** The method implicitly assumes that rank indices map perfectly to token probabilities across contexts, which holds for a single fixed model. A more critical unstated assumption is that the decoding process works autoregressively: to recover token $e_i$, the receiver must correctly decode tokens $e_1 \dots e_{i-1}$ first. If a single rank maps to a token that drastically shifts the conditional distribution, an error could cascade. The method implicitly relies on the fact that rank $r_i$ is applied independently, so cascading errors are contained to the extent that $r$ values are fixed in advance. This should be clarified.
- **Logical Gaps & Analysis:** The explanation of why stegotexts exhibit lower log-probability than originals (Section 3, "Low entropy token choices") contains a minor modeling leap. You model ranks as a random process and approximate the frequency of rank 1s as uniform over the sequence. In practice, ranks are highly correlated with local entropy and syntactic constraints. While the qualitative intuition is correct (preserving rank does not preserve probability mass, especially at peaky distributions), a tighter formulation using the expected probability mass of the $r$-th token $\sum_{x} p(x|c) \cdot \mathbb{I}(\text{rank}(x)=r)$ would formalize the drop. This does not invalidate the method but weakens the theoretical grounding of Figure 5's claim.
- **Security:** Section 3.1 conflates cryptographic security (key secrecy) with steganographic security (undetectability). The method is explicitly statistically detectable (average log-probability shift in Fig 4 & 14). In steganography literature, this means the scheme is *insecure* against likelihood-ratio or neural steganalysis detectors. The "deniability" argument relies on finding prompt keys that produce outlier stegotexts with log-probs matching or exceeding the original. The feasibility of systematically finding such keys is not quantified, leaving a gap between the claim and practical deployment.

### Experiments & Results
- **Testing Claims:** The experiments successfully demonstrate that rank-mapping preserves text length, that output log-probabilities fall within the human-text distribution (Fig 4), and that the method runs efficiently. Figures 11-12 effectively illustrate the AI safety threat model.
- **Baselines & Comparisons:** A major weakness for an ICLR submission is the absence of comparison against established LLM steganography baselines (e.g., Meteor, KGW-A/G, or recent distribution-preserving schemes). Comparing embedding capacity, detectability scores (e.g., using n-gram tests or modern NN steganalysis models), and fluency metrics would contextualize `Calgacus`'s trade-offs. Currently, the paper operates largely in a vacuum.
- **Ablations & Metrics:** Quality is evaluated almost exclusively via cumulative log-probability against 1,000 Reddit posts. Log-probability is a weak proxy for semantic coherence, tone preservation, and steerability. There are no automated readability scores, LLM-as-a-judge evaluations, or human studies. Figure 4 shows distributions but no error bars or significance tests; Figure 5 analyzes only one Economist article. The claim of "steerable tone/style" relies entirely on cherry-picked examples (Fig 1, 11, 13) rather than systematic prompt-conditioned evaluations across topics.
- **Cherry-picking vs. Support:** While failure modes are shown (Fig 7, Fig 13 code errors), the success rate is never quantified. What percentage of randomly sampled 85-token pairs yield a coherent stegotext across different $k$ complexities? Without a quantitative success rate or failure threshold, the empirical foundation remains anecdotal.
- **Datasets/Metrics:** The Reddit dataset is appropriate for plausibility bounds, but narrow. Evaluating on more structured or domain-specific texts (news, code, dialogue) would strengthen the generality claims.

### Writing & Clarity
- The paper is exceptionally well-written, with a clear, accessible narrative. Section 3's method description is easy to follow.
- **Potential Confusion:** The decoding step in Section 3 states: *"regenerate $e$ step by step using the LLM without $k$ by selecting every time the $r_i$-th token."* This omits an explicit statement that generation is autoregressive, using the previously recovered tokens of $e$ as context. Clarifying this prevents misinterpretation that ranks are decoded in parallel.
- The transition from technical steganography to the philosophical discussion of intentionality, hallucination, and Oulipo in Section 4 is conceptually rich but stylistically abrupt for a machine learning conference. While not a flaw per se, framing these thoughts as "Implications for LLM Evaluation" rather than broad historical discourse would align better with ICLR norms.
- Figures and tables are highly informative. Figure 13 effectively demonstrates domain limitations, and Figure 15 clearly operationalizes deniability.

### Limitations & Broader Impact
- **Acknowledged Limitations:** The authors correctly identify GPU floating-point non-determinism, domain dependence of $e$, sensitivity to short prompts $k$, and statistical detectability by LLMs.
- **Missed Limitations:** 
  1. **Capacity Rigidity:** The 1:1 token mapping means hiding a 100-token message requires generating a 100-token covertext. If the cover domain has strict length conventions (e.g., a 280-character tweet, ~50-70 tokens), longer messages cannot be hidden without chunking, which compounds detectability risk.
  2. **Cross-Architecture Determinism:** The limitation mentions GPU architectures, but modern quantization, speculative decoding, and compiler differences (e.g., vLLM vs llama.cpp logits rounding) can shift token rankings even with the same float16 weights. This is a significant reproducibility hurdle for real-world deployment.
  3. **Deniability Guarantees:** The protocol assumes an attacker will accept a bogus message if its probability exceeds the stegotext. However, an attacker could simply run a classifier trained on natural vs. rank-mapped distributions (which Fig 14 shows is feasible). The paper does not address adversarial deniability against detection models.
- **Societal Impact:** Well-addressed. The AI safety scenario (Section 4, "Unaligned chatbots disguised as aligned chatbots") is concrete and pressing. The discussion of censorship evasion is balanced. No negative impacts are omitted.

### Overall Assessment
This paper introduces `Calgacus`, a conceptually elegant and surprisingly simple method for exact-length generative steganography using LLM rank mapping. The contribution is clear, the AI safety implications are timely and well-articulated, and the writing is excellent. However, by ICLR standards, the empirical validation is underdeveloped. The absence of quantitative text quality metrics, systematic success/failure rates across diverse prompts-domains, and comparisons to existing steganographic baselines weakens the claim of "high-quality results." Furthermore, the security analysis inadvertently proves the method is statistically detectable, and the deniability mechanism lacks formal bounds or adversarial robustness testing. The method stands as a compelling proof-of-concept that highlights genuine vulnerabilities in text authenticity and alignment paradigms. With rigorous quantitative evaluation (success rates, baseline comparisons, steganalysis testing) and clearer scoping of steering/reproducibility constraints, this work would be a strong fit for the venue. In its current form, it requires substantial experimental strengthening to meet the acceptance bar.

# Neutral Reviewer
## Balanced Review

### Summary
This paper introduces _Calgacus_, a rank-based generative steganography protocol that embeds an arbitrary secret message into an LLM-generated cover text of exactly the same token length. By extracting the rank distribution of the secret text during autoregressive generation and re-applying those ranks to guide generation from a secret prompt, the method achieves full 1:1 embedding capacity with coherent, steerable outputs using modest 8B-parameter models. Beyond the technical contribution, the paper explores practical implications for AI safety (e.g., covertly encoding unaligned responses inside aligned-looking outputs) and discusses broader conceptual questions regarding authorial intent, deniability, and the definition of hallucination in LLMs.

### Strengths
1. **Elegant, accessible, and highly reproducible methodology.** The rank-mapping mechanism is conceptually clean, requires only standard logit access, and runs efficiently on consumer hardware (e.g., ~5 seconds encoding/decoding on an RTX 4070). The authors provide clear pseudocode, discuss practical variations (e.g., rank inversion, vocabulary mismatch handling), and accompany the paper with an open implementation, strongly aligning with ICLR's reproducibility standards.
2. **Transparent empirical characterization of plausibility.** The authors quantitatively analyze stegotext quality by comparing log-probability distributions against 1,000 real Reddit posts, demonstrating that generated texts fall within the natural plausibility range. The paper also provides a principled explanation for the aggregate probability deficit (Section _Low entropy token choices_), correctly attributing it to the mismatch between rank frequency and intrinsic token probability.
3. **Thoughtful exploration of concrete deployment scenarios and security properties.** The proposed AI safety application (encoding unfiltered answers within censored responses) is clearly framed with a step-by-step script, and the analysis of deniability, key secrecy, and hardware-dependent logits constraints reflects a grounded understanding of real-world limitations.

### Weaknesses
1. **Insufficient evaluation of steganographic detectability and perceptual quality.** The security analysis focuses primarily on key secrecy and brute-force complexity, conflating cryptographic security with steganographic undetectability. Modern steganography requires empirical evaluation against steganalysis (e.g., GLTR-style detectors, fine-tuned classifiers, or LLM-based perplexity/anomaly detectors), which are absent. Additionally, relying solely on aggregate log-probability is a weak proxy for human-perceived coherence, style consistency, or domain alignment; no human studies or modern LLM-as-a-judge evaluations are provided.
2. **Limited engagement with recent generative steganography and distribution-matching baselines.** While prior work is cited, the paper lacks direct comparison to recent entropy-coding or distribution-preserving schemes (e.g., arithmetic coding integrated into LLM generation, Meteor, or recent watermarking-inspired embedding methods). Without benchmarking embedding capacity vs. detectability vs. text quality against SOTA, the relative novelty and practical advantages of Calgacus remain under-validated.
3. **Narrow and partially unrealized AI safety threat model.** The alignment-bypass scenario assumes both sender and receiver can run the exact same LLM with identical logits/hardware conditions, which is often infeasible in production API settings. The requirement that the user must locally run a specific open LLM and manually apply the decoding protocol limits the attack's scalability and real-world exploitability compared to the severe safety implications claimed. Cross-model robustness is not empirically tested beyond log-prob shifts.

### Novelty & Significance
The core technical novelty is a clean, mathematically straightforward formulation of full-capacity (1:1 token length) generative steganography via deterministic rank preservation. While conceptually elegant and practically useful, the mechanism builds directly on well-understood autoregressive generation properties, making it incrementally novel rather than foundational. The significance of the work is primarily conceptual and safety-oriented: it effectively demonstrates a concrete vulnerability in text-based trust, alignment verification, and content moderation pipelines, raising important questions about covert communication and deniability. The philosophical discussion on intent and hallucination, while intellectually engaging, leans more toward humanities/sci-tech ethics than empirical ML, which may limit its alignment with ICLR's typical technical focus unless tightly coupled to measurable model behaviors in future work.

### Suggestions for Improvement
1. **Add rigorous steganalysis and perceptual evaluation.** Integrate experiments with modern detectors (e.g., rank-based anomaly detectors, fine-tuned classifiers, or GLTR-style visualizations) to quantify detectability under realistic attack assumptions. Supplement log-prob metrics with human evaluations or LLM-as-a-judge assessments measuring coherence, topic adherence, and style consistency across diverse prompt domains and message types.
2. **Benchmark against contemporary generative steganography methods.** Compare Calgacus against recent distribution-preserving, entropy-coding, or watermarking-inspired steganography approaches across standardized metrics: embedding rate, detectability (ROC/AUC), and text quality (perplexity, human judgment). This will clearly delineate the trade-offs where Calgacus excels (full capacity, determinism) versus where it lags (detectability, robustness to sampling variations).
3. **Strengthen the AI safety threat model and discuss mitigations.** Empirically evaluate cross-model decoding robustness (e.g., does decoding succeed if the receiver uses a different 8B LLM or a different quantization?) to clarify practical constraints. Additionally, propose and evaluate potential defenses (e.g., API-enforced randomized sampling policies, server-side steganology filters, or output format constraints) to provide a balanced safety analysis that guides practitioners on mitigation pathways.
4. **Condense or empirically ground the philosophical discussion.** The reflections on authorial intent, Oulipo constraints, and hallucination as "lack of intent" are compelling but currently read as speculative. Consider reframing these as testable hypotheses for future work or grounding them in measurable properties (e.g., quantifying "intent" via prompt-relevance scores, attribution studies, or intervention experiments) to better match ICLR's empirical and technical expectations.

# Spark Finder Review
## How to Improve This Paper

### Missing Experiments (top 3-5 only)
1. Add evaluation against established text-steganography detectors and simple likelihood classifiers (reporting AUC, accuracy, and false-positive rates); without quantitative detectability metrics, the core claim of human-perceptual stealth versus LLM-detectability is purely anecdotal.
2. Benchmark Calgacus directly against recent LLM steganography baselines (e.g., Meteor, distribution-preserving, or coding-based schemes) using standardized metrics like bits-per-token, embedding rate, and BERTScore; without this, the claim of novelty or practical superiority cannot be validated.
3. Expand the Reddit plausibility test from 3 seed texts to a statistically powered dataset spanning diverse domains, lengths, and difficulty levels; the current sampling is too narrow to support the generalized claim that the protocol consistently yields human-plausible stegotexts.
4. Evaluate robustness to common text-channel perturbations (paraphrasing, translation, formatting changes, or light summarization); practical steganography claims require proof that the hidden message survives real-world transmission, which is completely absent.

### Deeper Analysis Needed (top 3-5 only)
1. Quantify the effective entropy of the secret prompt `k` and model the information leakage from the observed stegotext `s` to potential key-recovery attacks; without this, the security and "deniability" arguments are handwavy and ignore how much `s` structurally constrains the search space for `k`.
2. Rigorously analyze the cross-model tokenizer mismatch problem and the exact conditions required for bitwise reproducibility (e.g., floating-point determinism, attention kernel variations); ICLR requires clear reproducibility boundaries for any method that claims identical logits across hardware.
3. Provide a formal or empirical analysis of why rank inversion improves steering (e.g., modeling context-entropy decay or prefix probability concentration); the current heuristic observation lacks mechanistic grounding needed to trust the method across arbitrary prompts.

### Visualizations & Case Studies
1. Produce token-rank alignment heatmaps showing the exact mapping from `e` to `s`, highlighting where high-rank injections cause syntactic drift or semantic breaks; this visually verifies the proposed mechanism rather than relying on cherry-picked success cases.
2. Plot the trade-off curve between prompt length/detail and the probability gap (detectability); without showing how steerability quantitatively impacts stealth, readers cannot assess the practical operating regime of the protocol.
3. Include a systematic catalog of alignment-evasion failures in the unaligned-chatbot scenario; a single successful gas-meter example does not support the broader AI-safety claim, and showing where the encoding breaks under real safety filters is essential for credibility.

### Obvious Next Steps
1. Conduct a controlled human evaluation (e.g., forced-choice A/B test with crowd workers) to empirically verify the claim that stegotexts are "opaque to humans"; top conferences will reject perceptual claims lacking human study data.
2. Release a deterministic inference pipeline (exact temperature, sampler, and hardware config) alongside a cross-platform reproducibility check; the protocol is functionally unfalsifiable if identical logits cannot be guaranteed across sender/receiver systems.
3. Frame the discussion on AI safety and hallucination within a measurable boundary (e.g., quantify how often rank-based encoding bypasses alignment fine-tuning); philosophical speculation without empirical failure/success rates dilutes the technical contribution.

# Final Consolidated Review
## Summary
This paper introduces _Calgacus_, a deterministic rank-preservation protocol for generative steganography that hides an arbitrary secret message inside an LLM-generated cover text of identical token length. By extracting per-token probability ranks from the secret text and replaying them during autoregressive generation from a steerable prompt, the method achieves exact 1:1 embedding capacity while maintaining plausible, topic-aligned output using modest open-weight models. The authors use this capability to illustrate concrete AI safety vulnerabilities and discuss broader conceptual implications for LLM knowledge and authorial intent.

## Strengths
- *Elegance and practical feasibility:* The core mechanism is conceptually clean, requires only standard logit access, and executes efficiently on consumer hardware. The method achieves exact length symmetry and full topic/style steerability in a way that prior limited-capacity steganographic schemes do not.
- *Clear conceptual framing of safety and intent:* The paper effectively demonstrates a plausible covert channel that could bypass aligned chatbot filters, and the operationalization of deniability under coercion is well-motivated. The connection to LLM "knowledge" and the reframing of hallucination as an absence of authorial intent provides a compelling theoretical hook.

## Weaknesses
- *Absence of rigorous steganographic benchmarking and success quantification:* The empirical evaluation operates largely in isolation. There are no comparisons against established generative steganography or distribution-preserving baselines (e.g., Meteor, arithmetic coding schemes, or KGW-based embedding methods) to contextualize trade-offs in fluency vs. detectability. Claims of "high-quality" and "human-opaque" outputs rely on cherry-picked qualitative examples and aggregate log-probability histograms, lacking quantified success/failure rates across diverse domains, prompt complexities, and secret text types.
- *Inadequate detection analysis and real-world robustness:* Relying on LLM self-log-probability as the sole proxy for undetectability is insufficient for a steganography paper. The work omits evaluation against dedicated steganalysis detectors (e.g., rank-distribution classifiers, neural steganalysts, or GLTR-style analyses) and completely ignores robustness to common text-channel perturbations (paraphrasing, translation, summarization), which would deterministically collapse the rank-mapping mechanism in practice.
- *Operationally fragile safety threat model:* The alignment-bypass application is presented as an urgent, scalable vulnerability, yet it requires perfect bitwise reproducibility of LLM logits across sender and receiver hardware/software stacks. The paper notes non-determinism as a limitation but does not quantify how minor quantization shifts, framework differences, or speculative decoding break the protocol, severely limiting its practical exploitability compared to the claimed impact.
- *Speculative discussion dilutes technical focus:* The lengthy philosophical treatment of historical intent, Oulipo constraints, and redefining hallucination is intellectually interesting but lacks measurable grounding or testable hypotheses. At ICLR, this narrative overshadows methodological limitations and empirical gaps without translating into falsifiable claims or quantitative analysis of model behaviors.

## Nice-to-Haves
- Incorporate LLM-as-a-judge or controlled human evaluations to corroborate claims of perceptual indistinguishability from natural text across stratified domains.
- Formalize or empirically characterize the information leakage of the secret prompt $k$ given the observable stegotext $s$, and quantify the computational cost of systematically discovering the "outlier" prompts required for formal deniability.

## Novel Insights
The paper’s strongest conceptual contribution is reframing generative steganography around exact symmetry rather than capacity optimization, using rank preservation to expose the fundamental disconnect between LLM autoregressive sampling and surface-level communicative intent. By demonstrating that identical token ranks yield divergent probabilities across original and covered contexts due to local entropy mismatches, _Calgacus_ provides a concrete mathematical lens for why LLM-generated text can remain syntactically plausible while being semantically decoupled from its visible prompt. This insight offers a novel diagnostic for probing how language models compress, constrain, and reconstruct meaning under rigid structural conditions.

## Potentially Missed Related Work
- *Modern steganalysis and watermarking inversion literature:* Work on LLM watermarking (Kirchenbauer et al.), rank-based detection (GLTR, Gehrmann et al.), and cryptographic text steganography (e.g., Kaptchuk et al.) would provide necessary baselines for discussing detectability and adversarial robustness in contemporary LLM security research.

## Suggestions
1. Benchmark _Calgacus_ against 2–3 established generative steganography baselines using standardized metrics: detectability AUC against rank-anomaly classifiers, bits-per-token embedding efficiency, and automated fluency scores (e.g., perplexity, BERTScore).
2. Quantify the protocol's empirical reliability by evaluating across a stratified dataset (varying $e$ domain complexity, $k$ length, and linguistic register) to report explicit success rates, coherence thresholds, and failure modes instead of qualitative case studies.
3. Stress-test decoding under realistic operational conditions: simulate cross-framework non-determinism, 4/8-bit quantization drift, and light paraphrasing of the stegotext to definitively map the reproducibility boundaries required for the claimed AI safety application.
4. Ground the AI safety threat model in measurable terms by empirically testing the covert channel's success rate in bypassing standard RLHF filters across a diverse set of restricted prompts, transforming the alignment discussion into a verifiable security assessment.
