# Harsh Critic Review
## Section-by-Section Critical Review

### Title & Abstract
- The title accurately reflects the core contribution, though "Operator Flow Matching" slightly undersells the latent autoencoder and conditioning mechanisms central to TempO.
- The abstract concisely states the problem (long-horizon PDE forecasting), method (latent flow matching with FNO), and key results (outperforms baselines, spectral fidelity, parameter efficiency). 
- The claim "We prove an upper bound on FNO approximation error" in the abstract requires caution. Upon reading Theorem 3.1 and Appendix A, this bound is a direct adaptation of existing FNO universal approximation theory (Kovachki et al., 2021). While correctly applied, it characterizes architectural expressivity rather than proving a novel bound specific to flow matching or ODE integration error. The abstract would benefit from tempering this claim to avoid implying a new theoretical guarantee for the flow matching framework itself.

### Introduction & Motivation
- The problem is well-motivated. The critique of autoregressive compounding errors and diffusion stochasticity/inefficiency for deterministic PDE rollouts is accurate and aligns with current community challenges. The gap (lack of stable, conditionable, deterministic flow matching models for long-horizon PDE forecasting) is clearly identified.
- Contributions are explicitly listed and mostly accurate. Point 2 ("time-conditioned latent spectral embeddings") and Point 3 ("decouple spatial and temporal processing") are key methodological innovations.
- The introduction slightly over-claims in the framing of related work by suggesting LLM/tokenization approaches "offer little practical benefit," which feels like a strawman for the PDE domain. While acceptable for contrast, it could be more narrowly scoped. More importantly, the distinction between TempO and prior Functional Flow Matching (Kerrigan et al., 2023) is stated but the *mechanistic* difference could be sharper upfront to avoid reviewers conflating the two.

### Method / Approach
- **Reproducibility & Clarity Gap (Critical):** Section 3.1 describes "sparse conditioning" and "time-conditioned FNO" but lacks precise mathematical implementation details. The notation mixes flow time $t$ (ODE integration variable), PDE time indices $T, \tau$, and temporal offset $\Delta$. Specifically, Equation (1) defines $\dot{z} = v_\theta(z, t)$, yet the text states $v_\theta(z, t) = G_\theta(z)$ and then introduces conditioning via $z_{\hat{T}+1} = f_\theta(z_T, z_\tau, \Delta)$. How exactly are $t$, $z_\tau$, and $\Delta$ injected into the spectral FNO regressor? FNOs are inherently spatial operators; conditioning on scalar offsets and past latent states requires an explicit architectural mapping (e.g., concatenation in channel dimension, FiLM modulation, or temporal tokens) that is not described. This is a significant omission for reproducibility.
- **Theoretical Claims:** Theorem 3.1 and Proposition 3.2 correctly bound FNO approximation and sampler complexity, but these rely heavily on existing Sobolev space approximation theory. Crucially, these bounds do not account for the *flow matching regression error* or the *ODE integration error* propagation. The bound characterizes what the FNO *can* represent, not how well the flow matching objective learns the velocity field. Corollary 3.3's conclusion depends on $\beta > 1$ for "samplers," which is architectural and asymptotic; stating FNOs achieve accuracy with "asymptotically fewer parameters" is theoretically sound but practically nuanced (e.g., attention scales differently, and constant factors matter at ICLR scales).
- **Channel Folding:** The claim that channel folding "disentangles $t$ from $\tau$" is hand-wavy. Collapsing batch and channel axes ($u' \in \mathbb{R}^{B\cdot C \times T \times H \times W}$) and applying FNO identically per channel suggests the FNO sees channels as independent spatial maps. If so, how does the model learn cross-channel/latent temporal correlations necessary for PDE evolution? This mechanism needs rigorous mathematical clarification or an architectural diagram.
- **Edge Cases:** The method assumes smooth latent trajectories for ODE integration. For stiff or highly chaotic regimes with discontinuities or shock formation (common in SWE or high-Reynolds NS), adaptive ODE solvers may fail or require prohibitive NFEs. This is not discussed.

### Experiments & Results
- **Experimental Design:** The evaluation on SWE, RD-2D, and NS-$\omega$ is appropriate and aligns with standard scientific ML benchmarks. Baselines (RIVER, Affine-OT, FNO-2D/3D, WNO, U-Net) provide a fair comparison across generative and operator-learning families.
- **Fairness & Inference Protocol:** Table 2 shows FNO-2D vastly outperforms on single-step MSE ($6.09\times10^{-4}$ vs $5.61\times10^{-2}$), which is expected. The paper correctly argues TempO wins on long-horizon stability. However, the comparison of computational efficiency requires nuance. Table 4 reports TempO uses 560 NFEs, ViT 942, U-Net 728. If these are *total* NFEs for a 40-step rollout, that's ~14 NFEs per PDE step. Autoregressive FNO-2D takes 1 evaluation per step. While TempO is more memory-efficient in parameters, it is computationally heavier per forecast step than a recursive FNO. The claim of "efficiency" should explicitly distinguish parameter efficiency from inference wall-clock cost/NFEs.
- **Ablations:** The Fourier mode truncation ablation (Figure 3, Table 12/13) is excellent and validates the spectral saturation hypothesis. However, missing ablations reduce confidence in design choices:
    - No ablation on the autoencoder latent dimension/compression rate.
    - No ablation on probability path choice (Affine-OT is primary; is TempO robust to RIVER or SLP?). The results in Table 9/10 suggest path sensitivity exists.
    - No statistical significance or confidence intervals reported in Tables 2/3. Figure 1 includes bands for Pearson, but core MSE/SpectralMSE metrics lack variance estimates given the small dataset sizes (1000-5000 trajectories).
- **Results Support:** Results strongly support the claim of spectral fidelity and long-horizon stability. Pearson >0.95 at 40 steps (Figure 1) is a compelling demonstration of flow matching's deterministic advantage over autoregressive blur.

### Writing & Clarity
- The writing is generally technical and accessible, but Section 3.1 suffers from heavy notation overlap between ODE time and sequence time, impeding clarity. The transition from abstract flow matching equations to "sparse conditioning" logic is abrupt.
- Figures 1 and 2 are informative. Figure 3 and Table 12/13 are slightly redundant; consolidating them would improve readability. 
- The claim in Section 5.2 that "spectral convolution scales as $O(N^2 \log N)$ in contrast to naive $O(N^3 \log N)$" due to folding is misleading without context. Folding typically avoids a full 3D FFT by doing independent 2D FFTs over the folded dimension, which is standard practice in video FNOs, not a novel scaling benefit unique to this paper.

### Limitations & Broader Impact
- The limitations section correctly identifies data sparsity and horizon constraints (>40 steps).
- **Critical Missing Limitation:** The "sparse conditioning" protocol mentions "pinning the generation against a known correct solution." If during rollout the model conditions on ground-truth frames from the past (teacher forcing / data assimilation setup), this significantly limits applicability to true *forecasting* where future ground truth is unavailable. If the authors instead only pin the *initial* condition and recursively feed back predictions, the error accumulation risk returns. The paper must clarify the inference procedure and explicitly discuss the limitation that optimal performance assumes access to past ground truth (assimilation) rather than pure autoregressive forecasting.
- Another missed limitation is sensitivity to the choice of ODE solver and tolerance. Real-world deployment requires fixed-step solvers for predictable latency; the reliance on adaptive `dopri5` may not generalize to strict compute budgets.
- Broader impact is appropriately scoped to scientific computing; no negative societal impacts are overlooked given the PDE focus.

### Overall Assessment
This paper presents a promising integration of latent flow matching with Fourier neural operators for long-horizon PDE forecasting, demonstrating clear empirical advantages in spectral fidelity and rollout stability compared to autoregressive and diffusion-based baselines. The methodological intuition of using deterministic ODE flows for operator learning is well-founded, and the spectral ablations strongly support the design. However, to meet ICLR's standards for methodological rigor and reproducibility, the paper requires significant clarification on how temporal conditioning ($z_\tau, \Delta$) is mathematically and architecturally integrated into the FNO, and the theoretical bounds, while correct, are adaptations of existing approximation theory rather than novel flow matching guarantees. Additionally, the reliance on ground-truth pinning during rollout must be transparently discussed as an operational limitation for pure forecasting, and computational efficiency claims should carefully disentangle parameter count from NFE/inference cost. With these clarifications and minor additional ablations (latent dimension, path robustness), this work would make a strong contribution to the scientific machine learning community at ICLR.

# Neutral Reviewer
## Balanced Review

### Summary
This paper introduces TempO, a time-conditioned latent flow matching framework for long-horizon forecasting of PDE-governed spatiotemporal dynamics. The method combines a multi-scale attention autoencoder, a Fourier Neural Operator (FNO) parameterized vector field regressor, and two engineering choices: sparse conditioning on two reference frames and "channel folding" to decouple spatial operator application from temporal evolution. The authors provide theoretical approximation bounds for the FNO regressor, compare them to sampler-based architectures, and empirically demonstrate superior stability, spectral fidelity, and parameter efficiency over diffusion and autoregressive baselines across three standard PDE benchmarks.

### Strengths
1. **Strong empirical stability and spectral fidelity:** TempO maintains Pearson correlations >0.95 across 40 forecast steps on NS-ω, while ViT and U-Net baselines visibly degrade (Section 5, Figure 1). Spectral analysis explicitly quantifies TempO's advantage in recovering high-wavenumber energy, which is critical for turbulent/vorticity dynamics (Section 5.1, Figure 2).
2. **Parameter and compute efficiency:** The FNO-based regressor achieves competitive or superior results with ~0.49M parameters and 560 NFEs, outperforming a 3.39M-parameter ViT and 14M-parameter U-Net in both memory and sampling cost (Table 4). The O(N² log N) scaling analysis for folded FNOs vs O(N⁴) for ViTs is well-justified.
3. **Practical and effective conditioning strategy:** Sparse conditioning combined with pinning a ground-truth reference frame directly addresses error compounding, a known failure mode in autoregressive forecasting. The ablation across sequence lengths demonstrates that TempO efficiently trades off context length against long-horizon stability (Table 5).
4. **Clear experimental protocol and benchmark selection:** The use of standard, well-documented PDE datasets (SWE, RD-2D, NS-ω) and comprehensive metric suites (MSE, SpectralMSE, RFNE, PSNR, Pearson, SSIM) facilitates fair comparison and aligns with community standards in scientific machine learning.

### Weaknesses
1. **Theoretical bounds are largely adapted rather than novel:** Theorem 3.1 and Proposition 3.2 closely follow existing universal approximation and parameter-complexity results for FNOs (Kovachki et al., 2021). The bounds do not explicitly account for the flow-matching probability path, ODE integration error, or the latent compression from the autoencoder, making the connection to the proposed generative setup somewhat indirect (Appendix A, Corollary 3.3).
2. **Incomplete ablation of key architectural claims:** The "channel folding" mechanism is highlighted as a core innovation for preventing temporal blurring, but the paper lacks an ablation comparing it to a standard 2D FNO with cross-channel mixing or a native 3D FNO. Without this, it's unclear whether stability gains stem from folding, sparse conditioning, or simply the ODE integration itself (Section 3.1, Table 5).
3. **Baseline fairness and comparison scope:** While autoregressive FNO-2D and FNO-3D are included, they are well-known to struggle with long-horizon rollouts. A more direct comparison to modern Neural PDE/ODE operators (e.g., ANO, Graph Neural ODEs, or recent state-augmented diffusion solvers) would better isolate the contribution of latent flow matching. Additionally, the NFE advantage depends heavily on ODE solver tolerances, which are not consistently aligned across baselines in the main text.
4. **Reproducibility gaps:** The paper does not specify whether the autoencoder and FNO regressor are co-trained end-to-end or trained sequentially/frozen. Hardware specifications, wall-clock training time, and a commitment to code/data release are absent, which hampers independent verification given the multi-stage optimization involved (Appendices D–F).

### Novelty & Significance
**Novelty:** Moderate. The paper effectively integrates established components (latent flow matching, FNOs, sparse conditioning) into a cohesive pipeline tailored for deterministic PDE forecasting. The channel folding trick and the explicit spectral error analysis are practical contributions, but the theoretical bounds repackage known FNO approximation theory rather than deriving new flow-matching-specific guarantees.
**Significance:** High practical value for SciML. The empirical demonstration of stable, spectrally accurate, and parameter-efficient long-horizon rollouts addresses a critical bottleneck in generative surrogate modeling. The results are reproducible on standard benchmarks and the efficiency gains could lower the barrier for deploying neural forecasters in resource-constrained scientific workflows.
**Clarity:** Generally strong narrative structure, though theoretical notation occasionally lacks consistency (e.g., overlapping definitions of `p`, `α`, and Sobolev indices) and the interaction between channel folding, latent ODE time, and PDE time could be formalized more rigorously.
**Reproducibility:** Adequate hyperparameter reporting, but limited by missing training dynamics (co-training vs. freezing), hardware/timing details, and lack of publicly released code. ODE tolerance specifications could be standardized across methods.

### Suggestions for Improvement
1. **Add a targeted ablation for channel folding:** Compare TempO with and without channel folding (e.g., allowing full cross-channel FNO attention/mixing) to isolate its impact on temporal blurring and gradient flow.
2. **Clarify training dynamics and reproducibility details:** Explicitly state whether the autoencoder and vector field regressor are optimized jointly or sequentially. Provide hardware specs, wall-clock training times, learning rate schedules for each stage, and commit to open-sourcing the code/configuration files.
3. **Strengthen theoretical framing:** Either formally extend Theorem 3.1 to bound the *rollout* error of the learned ODE flow (accounting for path choice and integration stability) or reframe it as an architectural efficiency guarantee rather than a generative modeling bound. Cite how the flow path affects the effective Lipschitz constant of `v_θ`.
4. **Expand baseline comparisons:** Include at least one modern Neural PDE/ODE baseline (e.g., ANO, Neural Graph ODE, or a recent latent diffusion operator) to contextualize whether stability stems from flow matching specifically or from the combination of ODE integration + sparse conditioning. Standardize ODE solver tolerances across all methods for a fair NFE comparison.
5. **Improve mathematical notation consistency:** Unify symbol definitions in Section 3 (e.g., clearly define Sobolev indices `s, s'`, decay exponent `p`, and `α` in a single consolidated definition block) and add a concise pseudocode algorithm for the training and rollout phases to enhance readability.

# Spark Finder Review
## How to Improve This Paper

### Missing Experiments (top 3-5 only)
1. Add a pure open-loop autoregressive baseline and a "no-pinned-frame" ablation because conditioning on a fixed ground-truth timestep during rollout effectively turns forecasting into conditional interpolation, making the claimed long-horizon stability statistically artificial and unsupported.
2. Add wall-clock runtime and throughput benchmarks (samples/sec) because reporting only NFEs and FLOPs ignores the high integration overhead of adaptive solvers like `dopri5`, leaving the paper's parameter-and memory-efficiency claims practically unverified.
3. Compare against modern stabilized PDE forecasting baselines (e.g., PDE-Refiner, U-FNO, or AFNO) because benchmarking primarily against basic 2D/3D FNOs and video-adapted flow models creates a weak evaluation that fails to prove TempO advances the actual state-of-the-art in neural operators.

### Deeper Analysis Needed (top 3-5 only)
1. Connect Theorem 3.1 and Proposition 3.2 directly to temporal error propagation bounds because the current derivations only restate static spatial approximation rates, leaving the central claim that FNO flow matching prevents exponential rollout error mathematically unsubstantiated.
2. Quantify physical invariant drift (e.g., enstrophy, energy, or mass conservation over the 40 steps) because high Pearson/MSE scores on vorticity fields do not guarantee physical consistency, which ICLR reviewers require for credible scientific forecasting claims.
3. Isolate the contribution of the "channel folding" mechanism via targeted ablation because the paper attributes temporal stability to this decoupling of spatial and temporal processing but provides zero experimental evidence separating its effect from standard spectral or attention layers.

### Visualizations & Case Studies
1. Plot phase space projections or low-dimensional latent trajectories over time to visually verify that the ODE integration follows a smooth continuous manifold rather than accumulating micro-noise masked by spatial smoothing.
2. Display spatial error heatmaps (prediction minus ground truth) across key timesteps to expose whether spectral fidelity degrades uniformly or catastrophically at physical boundaries and vortex cores where generative models typically fail.
3. Show the distribution of adapter ODE solver step sizes and Lipschitz estimates per timestep to reveal whether observed stability is a genuine property of the learned vector field or merely an artifact of the solver forcing excessive micro-steps in unstable regions.

### Obvious Next Steps
1. Implement and evaluate fully joint training of the autoencoder and flow regressor because cascading a frozen, pretrained compressor introduces irreversible reconstruction error that inevitably compounds during ODE integration, directly undermining the claimed end-to-end long-horizon accuracy.
2. Test forecasting performance under varying physical regimes (e.g., sweeping Reynolds numbers or forcing amplitudes) to demonstrate out-of-distribution generalization, as evaluating only on fixed-parameter datasets masks the model's inability to adapt to the stiffness or chaos shifts common in real-world PDEs.

# Final Consolidated Review
## Summary
The paper introduces TempO, a latent flow matching framework for long-horizon PDE forecasting that combines a multiscale attention autoencoder, a Fourier Neural Operator (FNO) parameterized vector field, sparse conditioning, and a "channel folding" technique. Theoretical bounds characterize the parameter-scaling advantage of FNOs over sampler-based architectures, while empirical results on Navier-Stokes, shallow water, and reaction-diffusion datasets demonstrate stable 40-step rollouts, superior spectral fidelity, and strong parameter efficiency compared to diffusion, U-Net, and ViT baselines.

## Strengths
- **Robust long-horizon stability on chaotic dynamics:** TempO maintains Pearson correlations >0.95 over 40 forecasted timesteps on NS-$\omega$, significantly outperforming autoregressive FNO-3D and attention-based flow matchers that exhibit error accumulation or spatial blurring (Section 5, Figure 1). This empirically validates the advantage of deterministic ODE-based transport for PDE evolution.
- **Parameter and memory efficiency with spectral inductive bias:** The method achieves competitive results with ~0.49M parameters and ~50MB memory usage, leveraging FNO spectral convolutions and channel folding to outperform heavier ViT (3.39M) and U-Net (14M) baselines while requiring fewer NFEs (Table 4).
- **Rigorous spectral analysis and truncation ablations:** The paper explicitly quantifies scale-resolved error, showing TempO closely tracks true energy spectra where ViT baselines systematically underestimate high-wavenumber components (Section 5.1, Figure 2). Ablations on Fourier mode cutoffs confirm performance stability down to 8 modes, aligning with theoretical expectations and justifying architectural compression (Figure 3, Table 12/13).

## Weaknesses
- **Rollout protocol relies on continuous ground-truth anchoring, limiting open-loop forecasting claims:** The inference procedure pins a historical ground-truth frame ($z_\tau$) and continuously increments the conditioning tuple while updating the reference frame (Section 5: "conditioning frame is then pinned... effectively pinning the generation against a known correct solution"). This setup acts as continuous data assimilation or teacher forcing, artificially suppressing error accumulation and diverging from true open-loop forecasting where no future ground truth is accessible. The paper's strong stability claims may not generalize to pure extrapolation settings.
- **Theoretical bounds characterize static expressivity, not temporal integration error:** Theorem 3.1 and Proposition 3.2 correctly adapt existing FNO approximation theory (Kovachki et al., 2021) to bound parameter complexity against samplers. However, these bounds apply to static function approximation and do not account for the flow matching regression objective, ODE solver error, or drift propagation over time. Corollary 3.3's conclusion that FNOs enable asymptotically fewer parameters is valid, but the paper overstates its relevance to *rollout stability*, as the bounds make no claims about temporal error accumulation.
- **Architectural opacity regarding conditioning injection and channel folding:** Section 3.1 states conditioning is provided via $C = \{z_\tau, \Delta\}$ and implemented through channel folding, but lacks precise mathematical or architectural details on how the scalar offset $\Delta$ and past latent state $z_\tau$ are mapped into the spectral FNO (e.g., FiLM modulation, channel concatenation, or additive biases). Furthermore, folding collapses the batch and channel axes and applies the FNO identically per channel without cross-channel mixing; it remains unclear how latent temporal dependencies are propagated if channels are processed independently. The absence of an ablation isolating channel folding from sparse conditioning or the autoencoder prevents attribution of the stability gains to this specific mechanism.
- **Training protocol ambiguity and missing practical efficiency metrics:** Section 4 states the autoencoder is pretrained, while Section 3.1 describes TempO as "end-to-end trainable." It is unclear whether the compressor and flow regressor are optimized jointly or sequentially; a frozen autoencoder introduces irreversible reconstruction error that compounds during ODE integration. Additionally, Table 4 reports NFEs and parameter counts but omits wall-clock training/inference time and hardware specifications. Given adaptive solver overhead (dopri5), parameter efficiency does not automatically translate to practical runtime advantages.

## Nice-to-Haves
- Physical invariant drift analysis (e.g., enstrophy, energy conservation over rollout) to complement spectral/MSE metrics and strengthen the SciML rigor.
- Standardized confidence intervals or variance estimates across multiple random seeds for Tables 2/3, given dataset sizes of 1k-5k trajectories.
- Comparison against modern stabilized PDE operators (e.g., PDE-Refiner, U-FNO, or latent diffusion operators) to contextualize whether stability stems specifically from the flow matching formulation or from the ODE + sparse conditioning pipeline.
- Evaluation under varying physical parameters (e.g., Reynolds number sweeps) to test out-of-distribution generalization and stiffness robustness.
- Fixed-step solver evaluation and reporting of per-step solver tolerances/Lipschitz estimates to assess deployment viability under strict compute budgets.
- Consolidation of redundant figures/tables (e.g., Figure 3 vs. Table 12/13) and addition of pseudocode for the training and sparse-conditioned rollout phases.

## Novel Insights
The work effectively bridges deterministic flow matching with neural operator theory by recognizing that PDE evolution operators inherently describe continuous, differentiable state transports. By replacing the stochastic, iterative denoising of diffusion models or the discrete, compounding steps of autoregressive architectures with a latent-space ODE driven by a spectrally inductive vector field, TempO aligns the generative process with the semigroup structure of classical PDE solvers. The empirical saturation of spectral truncation and the parameter-efficiency bounds jointly suggest that capturing the dominant low-to-mid frequency manifold is sufficient for stable latent transport, while high-frequency details can be safely compressed without inducing rollout divergence. This positions flow matching not merely as another generative prior, but as a deterministic, continuous analogue to operator regression that naturally mitigates discretization artifacts.

## Suggestions
- Explicitly distinguish between data-assimilation-style (pinned) forecasting and pure open-loop extrapolation in the limitations section, and consider adding a purely autoregressive rollout ablation (no pinned frame) to quantify the true error accumulation rate of the learned ODE flow.
- Clarify the exact architectural mapping of the conditioning tuple $\{z_\tau, \Delta\}$ into the FNO (provide dimensional flow or a diagram) and add an ablation comparing channel folding against standard cross-channel mixing to isolate its contribution to temporal stability.
- Reframe Theorem 3.1 and Corollary 3.3 strictly as architectural expressivity and parameter-efficiency guarantees, and avoid implying they mathematically explain temporal stability. A brief discussion linking the learned velocity field's Lipschitz constant to ODE stability would better bridge theory and practice.
- Resolve the "pretrained" vs "end-to-end" ambiguity by specifying the exact training schedule (stages, freezing policy, loss functions). Report wall-clock training/inference times on standardized hardware to ground the efficiency claims in practical deployment costs.
