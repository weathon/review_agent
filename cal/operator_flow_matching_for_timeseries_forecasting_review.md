# Harsh Critic Review
## Section-by-Section Critical Review

### Title & Abstract
- The title accurately reflects the core contribution (operator-inspired flow matching for forecasting), though "Timeseries" undersells the spatiotemporal/PDE nature of the work.
- The abstract clearly states the problem, method, and key empirical results. However, the claim "We prove an upper bound on FNO approximation error" overstates novelty. The derivation in Section 3 explicitly states it is "in line with the estimates and arguments made in (Kovachki et al., 2021)" and closely mirrors existing FNO approximation theory without novel adaptation to the flow matching objective. ICLR reviewers typically expect theoretical claims to either extend prior theory in a meaningful way or directly justify the proposed architecture.
- Empirical claims (16% lower error, Pearson >0.95 over 40 steps) are supported by Tables 2 and 3, so they are not unsupported.

### Introduction & Motivation
- The problem is well-motivated. The critique of autoregressive compounding errors and diffusion stochasticity for deterministic forecasting is clear, and positioning flow matching as a natural fit for deterministic operator learning is insightful.
- The gap in prior work is identified, but the claim that TempO is the "first principled integration of time-conditioned latent flow matching with neural operators mimicking classical PDE evolution operators" is slightly overreaching. Kerrigan et al. (2023) and Li et al. (2025) already explored FNOs and latent flow matching for PDE fields. The true novelty here is the combination of *sparse temporal conditioning*, *channel folding*, and *spectral latent embeddings*. The introduction should explicitly distinguish against these prior works before making "first" claims.
- Contributions are clearly enumerated and accurate relative to the experimental section.

### Method / Approach
- **Theoretical Claims:** Proposition 3.2 contains a critical logical gap. It assumes sampler-based learners (ViT/U-Net) "observe each \(u \in U\) only through \(n\) fixed point evaluations." This is fundamentally incorrect for grid-based architectures like ViTs or U-Nets, which process full spatial fields simultaneously and do not rely on point-wise sampling to retain global Fourier information. The resulting lower bound \(P_{sampler}(\varepsilon) \gtrsim \varepsilon^{-\beta d/\alpha}\) relies on a sampling model that does not match how these architectures operate. This weakens the theoretical justification for Corollary 3.3 and should either be rigorously reformulated to account for grid/token-based processing, or framed as a comparative capacity analysis under an explicit sampling assumption.
- **Time Conditioning & Flow Matching Objective:** The method conflates two distinct time variables without clear formalism. Flow matching integrates over normalized time \(t \in [0,1]\), while physical PDE time is handled via \(\Delta = T - \tau\). Equation (1) defines \(v_\theta(z, t)\), but Section 3.1 later writes \(z_{T+1} = f_\theta(z_T, z_\tau, \Delta)\). It is mathematically unclear how the physical offset \(\Delta\) and reference frame \(z_T\) are injected into the vector field \(v_\theta(z, t)\). Is \(\Delta\) concatenated as a static conditioning vector? Does it modulate the path coefficients \(a_t, b_t, c_t\)? A precise mathematical definition of the conditioned FM loss is missing.
- **Channel Folding:** Collapsing batch and channel axes to apply 2D FNO layers independently is a standard technique for multi-channel or temporal data. The paper claims this "disentangles \(t\) (FM integration step) from \(\tau\) (PDE timestep)" and "mitigates temporal blurring," but provides no ablation or theoretical argument showing why independent channel processing outperforms cross-channel mixing (e.g., a 3D FNO in latent space). Without empirical or theoretical justification, this reads more as a computational trick than a principled architectural innovation.
- **Lipschitz Stability Argument:** The statement "global forecasting error grows no faster than \(\exp(\int_0^1 L_t dt) - \varepsilon\) yielding polynomial accumulation" is syntactically garbled and mathematically imprecise. Standard ODE Grönwall bounds show exponential error growth w.r.t. Lipschitz constants over the *integration* horizon, not the *rollout* horizon. Long-horizon PDE rollouts accumulate error across multiple ODE solves; the paper does not formalize how sparse conditioning actually bounds rollout error beyond heuristic claims.

### Experiments & Results
- **Baselines:** Appropriate and fairly compared across multiple FM paths and regressor backbones. Including FNO-2D/3D and WNO baselines contextualizes performance well.
- **Missing Ablations:** Several critical ablations are absent that would materially change conclusions:
  1. **Conservation/Physics Metrics:** For PDE forecasting, Pearson/SSIM/MSE are insufficient. Metrics tracking enstrophy drift, energy conservation, or divergence penalties over 40 steps are standard in scientific ML and would validate "physical consistency" claims.
  2. **Conditioning Strategy:** Why exactly two frames (\(z_T, z_\tau\))? An ablation varying conditioning window size or pinning strategy is needed to justify sparse conditioning.
  3. **Channel Folding vs. Full Mixing:** Compare to a variant with cross-channel/temporal attention or 3D FNO in latent space to isolate the effect of folding.
  4. **Latent Dimension Sensitivity:** How does performance scale with compression ratio? The autoencoder depth is fixed at 2 (4x compression) for all datasets.
- **Metrics & Statistical Significance:** Figure 1 reports means and standard deviations, which is good. However, Tables 2 and 3 lack standard deviations or confidence intervals across random seeds. Given the stochasticity of training and ODE solver tolerances, reporting only point estimates makes it hard to assess whether 11–30% improvements are statistically significant.
- **Dataset Scale:** NS-ω is evaluated at \(64 \times 64\) resolution with 5000 trajectories. While acceptable for prototyping, ICLR's neural operator/PDE community routinely benchmarks at \(256 \times 512\) or larger. Claims about modeling "high-dimensional" dynamics and "multi-scale features" are somewhat weakened by the modest resolution and short physical trajectories (50 steps total, 40 predicted).
- **Results vs Claims:** Empirical results support the stability claims, but the efficiency comparison in Table 4 compares NFEs, parameters, and FLOPs across different architectures without reporting actual wall-clock training/inference time. ODE solvers with 560 NFEs can sometimes underperform single-pass models in throughput, which matters for practical forecasting.

### Writing & Clarity
- The most significant clarity issue is the ambiguous distinction between flow matching time (\(t \in [0,1]\)) and physical PDE time (\(\tau, T, \Delta\)). The method section blends these concepts, making it difficult to reproduce the exact conditioning mechanism.
- Notation inconsistencies: Section 3.1 introduces \(v_\theta(z,t)\), then later refers to \(G_\theta(z)\), then writes the predictor as \(f_\theta(z_T, z_\tau, \Delta)\). These should be unified under a single conditional vector field formulation, e.g., \(v_\theta(z, t \mid z_T, z_\tau, \Delta)\).
- Figures and tables are conceptually clear despite parser artifacts. Figure 1 effectively demonstrates rollout stability. Table 2's formatting makes metric comparisons slightly tedious, but the key takeaways remain accessible.

### Limitations & Broader Impact
- The authors acknowledge data sparsity challenges, the need for architectural modifications for foundation models, and the 40-step horizon limit.
- **Missed Limitations:**
  1. **Physical Conservation Drift:** The model does not enforce or report conservation of mass, momentum, or enstrophy. In chaotic regimes (high Reynolds number), even visually accurate rollouts can violate conservation laws, leading to unphysical downstream use.
  2. **Stiffness/Chaos Sensitivity:** The paper does not discuss performance degradation under higher viscosity/Reynolds variations or stiff reaction-diffusion regimes, which are common failure modes for neural operators.
  3. **Solver Dependency:** The method relies on an adaptive ODE solver (dopri5). Failure modes like solver stiffness, step rejection, or tolerance sensitivity are not discussed, which impacts robustness in production deployment.
- No negative societal impacts are discussed, but given the scientific context, this is appropriately scoped.

### Overall Assessment
TempO presents a promising direction for deterministic, long-horizon PDE forecasting by combining latent flow matching with spectral operator inductive biases and sparse temporal conditioning. The empirical results demonstrate clear stability advantages over autoregressive and alternative flow-matching baselines across multiple PDE types, and the spectral analysis provides useful insight into frequency-domain behavior. However, the paper requires significant revision to meet ICLR's technical rigor standards. The theoretical bounds largely repackaging existing FNO theory and rely on an unrealistic sampling assumption for the lower bound (Proposition 3.2). The mathematical formulation of how physical time conditioning integrates into the flow matching objective is ambiguous, and critical ablations (physical conservation metrics, conditioning strategy, channel folding justification, latent dimension sensitivity) are missing. Dataset resolution is modest for claims of "high-dimensional" modeling. If the authors clarify the conditioning mechanism, rigorously reformulate or contextualize the theoretical claims, add conservation/physics-aware metrics, and provide missing architectural ablations, the contribution would be much stronger and better aligned with ICLR expectations for scientific generative modeling. As is, the empirical promise is notable, but the theoretical-methodological bridge and evaluation depth need strengthening.

# Neutral Reviewer
## Balanced Review

### Summary
This paper introduces TempO, a latent flow matching framework for long-horizon PDE forecasting that combines a multi-headed attention autoencoder with a time-conditioned Fourier Neural Operator (FNO) vector field regressor. The method incorporates sparse conditioning and channel folding to decouple spatial spectral processing from temporal ODE integration, aiming to stabilize deterministic rollouts while reducing parameter and memory overhead. The authors provide theoretical scaling bounds favoring FNO-based parameterization over dense samplers and demonstrate empirical advantages in accuracy, spectral fidelity, and computational efficiency across three standard PDE benchmarks.

### Strengths
1. **Strong and Comprehensive Empirical Evaluation:** The paper evaluates TempO across three distinct PDE regimes (Navier-Stokes, Shallow Water, Reaction-Diffusion) using a rich suite of metrics (MSE, SpectralMSE, RFNE, PSNR, Pearson, SSIM). The consistent outperformance over multiple flow-matching paths and regressor architectures (ViT, U-Net) demonstrates robustness rather than overfitting to a single dataset.
2. **Notable Computational Efficiency:** TempO requires only ~0.49M parameters (~7× fewer than ViT, ~28× fewer than U-Net) and exhibits lower ODE solver function evaluations (NFEs) and memory footprint (Table 4). This aligns well with ICLR’s emphasis on efficient, scalable architectures and makes the method practical for resource-constrained scientific computing.
3. **Targeted Architectural Innovations for PDEs:** The introduction of sparse conditioning (pinning to two prior timesteps) and channel folding directly addresses known failure modes in neural PDE forecasting: compounding autoregressive error and temporal blurring. The empirical rollout stability (Pearson >0.95 over 40 steps) validates these design choices.
4. **Spectral Analysis Integration:** The paper goes beyond pixel-space errors to analyze energy spectra across wavenumbers, demonstrating that TempO preserves essential multi-scale dynamics up to $k=8$ while aggressive ViT baselines underestimate high-frequency content. This is highly relevant for physics-informed machine learning.

### Weaknesses
1. **Theoretical Bounds Are Derivative and Weakly Linked to Practice:** Theorem 3.1 and Proposition 3.2 largely adapt existing FNO approximation theory (e.g., Kovachki et al., 2021) to a flow-matching setting. While Corollary 3.3 elegantly shows parameter efficiency for spectral vs. dense operators, it does not theoretically guarantee the observed *rollout stability* or *lower NFEs*. The claim that FNO error accumulation is "polynomial" vs. autoregressive "exponential" is stated without rigorous derivation or citation, making the theoretical contribution feel more like motivation than proof.
2. **Incomplete Baseline Coverage and Context:** Section 2 discusses several highly relevant recent works (e.g., Kerrigan et al. 2023 Functional Flow Matching, Li et al. 2025 Generative Latent Neural PDE Solver, Lippe et al. 2023 PDE-Refiner), yet these are absent from the main comparison tables. Additionally, while FNO-2D achieves orders-of-magnitude better 1-step MSE, the paper dismisses it due to long-horizon failure without deeply analyzing how TempO’s flow-matching formulation inherently mitigates this, beyond empirical observation.
3. **Underspecified Algorithmic Details:** The precise interaction between the flow matching time $t \in [0,1]$ and the physical PDE timestep $\tau$ (and offset $\Delta$) during training and inference is described textually but lacks a formal algorithm or clear mathematical mapping. It remains ambiguous how the FNO regressor ingests both the ODE integration step and the sparse physical conditioning vector simultaneously. The "MSE/time" metric is frequently cited but never formally defined.
4. **Limited Discussion of Stochasticity vs. Determinism Trade-offs:** The paper positions deterministic flow matching as superior to diffusion, yet overlooks that stochasticity is often desirable in chaotic PDE regimes for ensemble forecasting and uncertainty quantification. The limitations on extreme sparsity and lack of uncertainty calibration are acknowledged in Section 6, but the practical implications for scientific workflows are underexplored.

### Novelty & Significance
The novelty is moderate to high. Applying flow matching to long-horizon neural PDE forecasting is timely, and the specific combination of latent FNO parameterization, sparse conditioning, and channel folding represents a meaningful methodological step. However, the core idea builds directly on recent functional flow matching and neural operator literature. The significance to the ICLR community is solid: TempO demonstrates that aligning generative modeling inductive biases (deterministic ODE transport, spectral compression) with PDE structure yields efficient, stable forecasters that outperform heavy vision transformers and unstable autoregressive baselines. It is particularly valuable for applications requiring deterministic, compute-light surrogate modeling, though it does not yet address foundation-model scaling or probabilistic forecasting. Reproducibility appears strong given the detailed hyperparameter tables (Appendices D-G), standard dataset splits, and explicit solver tolerances, though a public code release would solidify this.

### Suggestions for Improvement
1. **Provide a Formal Inference Algorithm:** Include a concise algorithm box detailing the training loop, sparse conditioning update rule, ODE integration step (explicitly showing inputs $[z_\tau, z_{\tau-\kappa}, \Delta]$, flow time $t$, and FNO forward pass), and decoding step. This will resolve ambiguities regarding temporal alignment.
2. **Define Custom Metrics and Expand Baselines:** Clearly define "MSE/time" and "RFNE" in the main text. Include direct comparisons or discussions of the omitted but highly relevant baselines from Related Work (e.g., PDE-Refiner, DiffusionPDE, or recent operator flow matching methods) to better position TempO in the landscape.
3. **Strengthen the Theory-to-Practice Bridge:** If possible, derive or empirically justify a bound on rollout error accumulation for latent flow ODEs versus autoregressive stepping, or explicitly frame the existing bounds as parameter-efficiency motivators rather than stability guarantees. Add an ablation showing how NFEs correlate with the proven $\epsilon$-scaling.
4. **Clarify the Deterministic vs. Probabilistic Trade-off:** Discuss when deterministic rollouts are strictly preferred over stochastic diffusion sampling in PDE forecasting, and outline how TempO could be extended to support uncertainty quantification (e.g., via conditional initial priors or ensemble NFE sampling) to increase its scientific utility.

## How to Improve This Paper

### Missing Experiments (top 3-5 only)
1. Re-evaluate non-flow baselines (FNO, U-Net) using the exact same latent autoencoder and sparse 2-frame conditioning. Mixing architectural backbones with different input modalities and conditioning lengths makes it impossible to isolate whether the gains come from flow matching or simply the compressed representation.
2. Report results across 3-5 random seeds with confidence intervals on all tabled metrics. Claiming a 16-30% error reduction over baselines without statistical significance metrics or variance estimates falls below ICLR's empirical rigor standards.
3. Benchmark TempO against modern latent neural PDE solvers (e.g., LatentODE, Neural SDEs, or physics-informed neural operators). Comparing primarily to video prediction flow models misaligns the method with the most competitive forecasting architectures in the scientific domain.

### Deeper Analysis Needed (top 3-5 only)
1. Empirically verify the error accumulation claim by tracking the learned vector field's local Lipschitz constants and stepwise Jacobian products over the 40-step rollout. Without quantified ODE stability, the assertion that TempO avoids the exponential error compounding of autoregressive models remains unsubstantiated.
2. Reformulate or defend Proposition 3.2's lower bound assumption that samplers only observe data via fixed point evaluations. Grid-based CNNs and ViTs process complete spatial patches, making the pointwise sampling premise a flawed strawman that weakens the claimed theoretical parameter advantage.
3. Disentangle the spectral gains of the autoencoder from the flow matching regressor via an ablation that fixes the regressor and varies the latent truncation levels or bottleneck sizes. The current attribution of spectral fidelity to the FNO regressor is confounded by the autoencoder's pre-processing.

### Visualizations & Case Studies
1. Plot phase-space trajectories of the latent states over the forecast horizon for TempO vs. FNO-3D. This will directly expose whether TempO preserves the true PDE attractor dynamics or merely generates spatially smooth but dynamically divergent paths.
2. Provide spatial error heatmaps at intermediate timesteps (e.g., t=10, t=20, t=40). Visualizing exactly where and when errors accumulate is necessary to prove the model delays compounding drift rather than masking it with low-resolution compression.
3. Show reconstructions after high-pass filtering the ground truth and predictions. This tests the explicit claim of "superior recovery of high-frequency dynamics," revealing if the model genuinely captures fine scales or simply oversmooths.

### Obvious Next Steps
1. Implement and report physically consistent conservation checks (e.g., mass, energy, or divergence-free errors) over long rollouts. For ICLR scientific applications, spectral and MSE metrics are insufficient without demonstrating adherence to fundamental physical invariants.
2. Replace the fixed-step Euler/dopri5 solver reporting with a systematic analysis of solver tolerance vs. NFE vs. forecasting accuracy. The paper claims ODE efficiency but only reports raw NFE counts without linking computational cost directly to temporal accuracy.
3. Provide a pseudo-code algorithm or clear flowchart detailing the exact rollout procedure, conditioning update, and channel folding mechanics. The current textual description is ambiguous, making the core method unreproducible and obscuring whether the "pinning" strategy effectively reduces to a modified autoregressive loop.

# Final Consolidated Review
## Summary
The paper proposes TempO, a latent flow matching framework that combines a multi-headed attention autoencoder with a time-conditioned Fourier Neural Operator (FNO) regressor for deterministic, long-horizon PDE forecasting. By introducing sparse temporal conditioning and channel folding, the method aims to decouple spatial spectral processing from temporal ODE integration, mitigating autoregressive error accumulation. Empirical evaluations on three PDE benchmarks demonstrate reduced spectral error, stable 40-step rollouts, and notable parameter/memory efficiency compared to attention-based and diffusion baselines.

## Strengths
- **Demonstrated long-horizon stability and spectral fidelity:** TempO consistently outperforms ViT, U-Net, and path-conditioned FM baselines across NS-ω, SWE, and RD-2D datasets. Figures 1–2 and Tables 2–3 show Pearson correlations >0.95 over 40 timesteps and tight alignment with ground-truth energy spectra up to $k=8$, directly addressing the high-frequency degradation common in attention-based forecasters.
- **Strong computational efficiency without sacrificing accuracy:** The parameter-light design (~0.49M, ~7–28× fewer parameters than ViT/U-Net) and reduced ODE solver function evaluations (NFEs) are well-documented in Table 4, making the approach practically viable for resource-constrained scientific workflows.

## Weaknesses
- **Theoretical justification is fundamentally flawed and overclaimed:** Proposition 3.2 asserts a parameter lower bound for sampler-based architectures by assuming they observe fields "only through $n$ fixed point evaluations." This premise is a strawman for grid/tensor-based architectures (ViTs, U-Nets) that process full spatial patches simultaneously, not pointwise samples. Consequently, Corollary 3.3's claim that FNOs achieve superior scaling relies on a mischaracterized comparison model. Theorem 3.1 furthermore closely mirrors existing FNO approximation bounds (Kovachki et al., 2021) without novel adaptation to the flow matching objective, rendering the theoretical section more motivational than contributory.
- **Underspecified conditioning and temporal alignment:** The mathematical interface between normalized flow matching time $t \in [0,1]$ and physical PDE timestep offset $\Delta$ is ambiguous. The paper defines a conditional FM objective $v_\theta(z, t)$ but implements prediction as $\hat{z}_{T+1} = f_\theta(z_T, z_\tau, \Delta)$ without formally specifying how $\Delta$ and the reference frames are injected into the vector field regression loss, or how the ODE solver handles discrete physical steps versus continuous transport time. This lack of formalism obscures reproducibility and prevents rigorous stability analysis.
- **Confounded architectural contributions and insufficient evaluation rigor:** The claimed gains from flow matching and channel folding cannot be disentangled from the pre-trained autoencoder's inductive bias. There are no ablations isolating (a) the FNO regressor vs. a matched regressor using the same latent space, (b) sparse conditioning window size/pinning strategy, or (c) channel folding vs. cross-channel/temporal mixing. Additionally, Tables 2–3 report only point estimates without variance across seeds, and custom metrics (MSE/time, RFNE) are used but never formally defined. For a paper claiming "physically consistent" forecasting, the complete absence of conservation/invariant metrics (e.g., enstrophy drift, divergence-free errors) over the 40-step horizon is a critical omission for ICLR standards.
- **Modest experimental scale relative to claims:** Benchmarks operate at 64×64 or 128×128 resolutions with 50-step total trajectories. Claims of modeling "high-dimensional" dynamics and "multi-scale features" are overstated given current PDE-ML standards, which routinely benchmark at 256×256+ with longer horizons. Furthermore, efficiency claims compare raw NFEs rather than wall-clock inference time or solver failure rates, ignoring the practical overhead of adaptive ODE integration versus single-pass baselines.

## Nice-to-Haves
- Provide a systematic ablation of ODE solver tolerances vs. NFE vs. forecasting error to justify the choice of fixed tolerances and clarify computational trade-offs.
- Include high-pass filtered error heatmaps at intermediate timesteps to visually isolate where and how high-frequency spectral errors accumulate during rollout.

## Novel Insights
TempO conceptually reframes latent flow matching as a continuous operator-learning task rather than a stochastic sampling procedure. By folding the channel/batch dimensions, the model forces a clean separation between spatial spectral transport (handled by the 2D FNO) and temporal dynamics (handled by the ODE integrator with sparse physical anchoring). This architecture directly targets the compounding Jacobian instability of discrete autoregressive steppers and the high-frequency spectral bias of attention mechanisms, yielding a deterministic rollout that tracks true PDE attractors over extended horizons. However, the theoretical scaffolding used to justify this advantage misrepresents competitor architectures, weakening the otherwise compelling empirical design.

## Potentially Missed Related Work
- PDE-Refiner (Lippe et al., 2023) and DiffusionPDE (Huang et al., 2024) — both present competitive diffusion/operator hybrids for long-horizon or function-space PDE tasks; inclusion would better contextualize TempO's deterministic vs. stochastic trade-offs.
- Latent Neural ODEs / Neural SDEs for spatiotemporal forecasting — relevant baselines that also learn continuous latent dynamics but often incorporate variational or stochastic priors for chaotic regimes.
- Frequency-debiased or anti-oversmoothing ViTs (e.g., Piao et al., 2024; Wang et al., 2022) — useful for disentangling whether observed spectral gains stem from the FNO regressor specifically or merely avoid known transformer spectral biases.

## Suggestions
1. **Formalize the conditional FM objective and rollout algorithm:** Provide a concise pseudo-code algorithm detailing training inputs, exact injection of $(z_T, z_\tau, \Delta)$ into $v_\theta(z, t)$, the conditioning update rule during inference, and the ODE integration loop.
2. **Correct or reframe Proposition 3.2 & Corollary 3.3:** Either rigorously adapt the lower bound to account for grid/token-based processing in modern architectures, or explicitly reposition the theoretical section as a parameter-efficiency motivation rather than a provable superiority claim over competitors.
3. **Decentralize conflated components with targeted ablations:** Evaluate the FNO regressor against a structurally matched non-FNO regressor using the identical autoencoder and sparse conditioning scheme. Add ablations for conditioning window size, channel folding vs. full cross-channel attention, and latent bottleneck dimensions.
4. **Incorporate physics-consistency metrics and statistical rigor:** Report enstrophy/mass conservation errors and divergence penalties over the 40-step horizon. Replace point estimates with results aggregated over 3–5 random seeds, and formally define MSE/time and RFNE in the main text to ensure evaluation transparency.
