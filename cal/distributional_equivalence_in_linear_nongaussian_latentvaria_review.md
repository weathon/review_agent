# Harsh Critic Review
## Section-by-Section Critical Review

### Title & Abstract / Introduction & Motivation (§1)
The motivation is clearly articulated and addresses a genuine gap in causal discovery: the absence of a general equivalence characterization for latent-variable models with arbitrary structure and cycles. The historical analogy to CPDAGs and MAGs is effective and grounds the contribution well.
* **Scope & "First" Claims:** The claim of being the "first structural-assumption-free discovery method" is strong but largely justified given the literature reviewed. However, it warrants slight qualification. Recent works on flexible latent clustering, generalized measurement models, and score-based latent discovery (e.g., Dong et al., 2024; Jin et al., 2024; Ng et al., 2024) relax strict hierarchical/measurement assumptions, even if not fully arbitrary. Clarifying that your result is the first to impose *zero* constraints on latent connectivity, directionality, or indicator patterns would prevent overstatement.
* **Missing Assumptions in Intro:** The introduction sets up distributional equivalence cleanly but omits any mention of the core statistical/algebraic assumptions required for the main theorems (e.g., generic parameters, faithfulness to path ranks, invertibility of $I-B$). Even brief signposting here would help readers calibrate the theoretical scope before diving into §2.

### Problem Setup (§2)
The formalization of LiNG models, mixing matrices, and distributional equivalence (Def 1) is standard and precise. The irreducibility condition (Prop 1) and reduction procedure (Prop 2) are well-motivated and correctly leverage OICA identifiability.
* **Genericity & Zariski Closure:** The setup implicitly assumes generic edge weights to avoid singularities in $(I-B)^{-1}$ and coincidental rank cancellations. While standard in algebraic statistics, this should be explicitly stated as part of the model class early on, as it directly impacts the validity of Lemma 2 and the subsequent rank-based characterizations.
* **Latent Identifiability vs. Reducibility:** Prop 1 ties irreducibility to OICA column non-proportionality. This is correct, but note that OICA identifiability itself requires the true number of latents to be known or correctly selected via model selection. The reduction procedure assumes we start with a candidate $G$, which is fine theoretically, but creates a circularity in practice if the number of latents is misspecified. This is acknowledged later but should be noted here as a theoretical boundary condition.

### Developing Graphical Tools (§3)
This section successfully bridges algebraic constraints (mixing matrix ranks) and graphical structures (path/edge ranks). The duality theorem (Thm 1) is elegant and correctly positioned as a translation between strict gammoids and transversal matroids.
* **Novelty Framing for Edge Ranks:** Edge ranks and matching ranks are well-known in combinatorics/matroid theory (as cited). The paper's novelty lies not in inventing them, but in recognizing their duality to path ranks *in the context of causal equivalence* and exploiting it for decomposition. I recommend slightly tempering the phrasing from "a new tool" to "a newly applied tool in causal discovery" to avoid matroid community friction, while still highlighting its original utility here.
* **Permutation Handling in Lemma 5:** Lemma 3 uses $\rho_G(Z, Y) = \rho_H(Z, \pi(Y))$, while Lemma 5 uses $r_G(Z, Y) = r_H(\pi(Z), Y)$. Given the duality, this swap is mathematically consistent, but the text should explicitly state that $\pi$ operates on the latent/observed vertex labeling and that equivalence is inherently modulo latent relabeling. Readers may wonder how $\pi$ is resolved without ground-truth labels; clarifying that latent relabeling is absorbed into the equivalence relation would help.

### The Graphical Characterization (§4)
The theoretical core of the paper. Theorem 2's decomposition of global rank checks into singleton "children bases" checks is a significant advance. Theorem 3's transformational characterization (cycle reversals + admissible edge additions/deletions) is well-structured and analogous to Meek's conjecture.
* **Intuition for Lemma 7:** The condition for admissible edge additions (Eq. 20) relies on checking whether $V_j$ is a coloop in a specific transversal matroid. The matroid terminology is precise, but the causal intuition is buried. Why does a "coloop" status guarantee distributional equivalence is preserved? A brief sentence explaining that such an edge does not create new bottleneck dependencies among latents would greatly improve accessibility.
* **Cycle Reversal Bound:** Theorem 3 claims "at most one cycle reversal is needed." This is a strong structural result. I recommend adding a short remark or reference explaining why multiple nested cycle reversals collapse to a single permutation step, as this is non-obvious to readers unfamiliar with cycle group properties in support matrices.
* **Proof Dependencies:** The singleton decomposition (Thm 2) relies heavily on Lemma 9, which is deferred to Appendix B.3.3. For a central result, a 2-3 line intuition in the main text (e.g., "because transversal matroids satisfy a local augmentation property, global basis consistency reduces to singleton checks") would improve flow.

### Algorithm & Evaluation (§5 & Appendices A, D)
The `glvLiNG` pipeline is logically sound: OICA → rank realization via matroid construction → equivalence traversal. The theoretical reduction to bipartite graph realization is a nice algorithmic contribution.
* **OICA Dependence & Finite Samples:** The algorithm explicitly requires an oracle or estimated mixing matrix from OICA. As acknowledged in §5, OICA is notoriously unstable in finite samples, especially with $|L| > 3$ or dense graphs. The practical heuristic for empirical ranks (sigmoid thresholding in Appendix D.4) is ad-hoc and lacks theoretical guarantees on matroid axiom violations. For ICLR, this is the primary weakness: the theoretical guarantees collapse if OICA fails, and no robust, assumption-free rank estimation alternative is provided. The "proof of concept" framing is appropriate but should be stronger in the limitations.
* **Baselines & SHD Metric:** Comparing against PO-LiNGAM and LaHiCaSl is fair for demonstrating robustness to misspecification, but these methods are known to assume hierarchical/leaf-observed structures, so failure on dense/arbitrary graphs is expected. A stronger evaluation would compare to a method that also targets fewer assumptions (e.g., rank-based tetrad/pentad search or recent score-based latent methods) or explicitly frame the experiment as "robustness to structural violation" rather than "superior discovery." Additionally, computing SHD against the *best* graph in the equivalence class is generous; reporting average SHD or precision/recall on invariant edges (solid edges in CP-graph) would provide a more rigorous stability metric.
* **Real-World Data:** The stock market analysis yields interpretable patterns and highlights large equivalence classes, which aligns with the paper's message about inherent uncertainty. However, without interventional validation or strong domain priors, causal claims remain exploratory. The paper handles this responsibly, but should explicitly caution against overinterpreting latent variables in financial data due to confounding and time-aggregation effects.

### Limitations, Theoretical Foundations, & Writing
The paper is mathematically rigorous and well-organized. Appendix C.4's discussion of non-rank constraints is insightful but underplayed.
* **Why Ranks Suffice:** The paper correctly notes that non-rank polynomial constraints exist in mixing matrices but argues rank constraints alone determine equivalence in LiNG. This relies on the algebraic closure/Zariski topology argument. This is a profound theoretical point and should be elevated from a appendix remark to a clear theorem corollary or main-text discussion. Explicitly stating that "in LiNG, the algebraic variety of observationally equivalent models is fully captured by the rank stratification" would significantly strengthen the theoretical contribution.
* **Matroid Terminology:** The heavy use of transversal matroids, coloops, and fundamental cocircuits will appeal to algebraic graph theorists but may alienate applied causal discovery researchers. The intuitive anchors ("pillars," "bottlenecks," "maximal matching") help, but a unified notation glossary or a brief primer in Appendix A would improve accessibility.

### Overall Assessment
This paper makes a substantial theoretical contribution to causal discovery by providing the first general equivalence characterization for linear non-Gaussian models with arbitrary latent structures and cycles. The introduction of edge rank constraints, their duality to path ranks, and the resulting decomposition of global checks into local singleton bases represent a significant conceptual advance. The transformational characterization (Theorem 3) and the CP-graph presentation are rigorous and well-positioned within the history of equivalence research. However, the practical utility of the proposed `glvLiNG` algorithm is currently constrained by its reliance on OICA, which remains unstable in finite samples and high dimensions. The empirical evaluation, while thorough, primarily demonstrates robustness to misspecification rather than outperforming realistic baselines under finite data, and the ad-hoc rank thresholding lacks theoretical guarantees. Overall, the paper is theoretically strong, clearly written, and fits ICLR's emphasis on foundational causal machine learning. The contribution stands firmly on its theoretical merits, provided the authors acknowledge the OICA bottleneck and frame the algorithmic component as a principled blueprint pending more robust rank estimation methods.

# Neutral Reviewer
## Balanced Review

### Summary
This paper establishes the first structural-assumption-free characterization of distributional equivalence for linear non-Gaussian causal models with arbitrary latent variables and cycles, introducing "edge rank" constraints and a novel duality theorem with traditional path ranks to render equivalence checking tractable. Building on this theoretical foundation, it derives a transformational characterization for traversing entire equivalence classes via admissible edge operations and cycle reversals, and proposes the `glvLiNG` algorithm to recover latent-variable models from observational data up to equivalence. The work bridges algebraic statistics, matroid theory, and graphical models, providing both a rigorous identifiability framework and a practical constraint-based learning pipeline.

### Strengths
1. **High Theoretical Novelty & Gap Filling:** The paper successfully removes long-standing restrictive assumptions (e.g., pure measurement models, hierarchical latents, acyclicity) and delivers a complete equivalence characterization for a broad parametric class. The introduction of edge ranks (Definition 4) and Theorem 1 (duality with path ranks) is a genuine conceptual advance, transforming a global, combinatorially complex path-rank condition into a local, edge-level criterion that enables singleton decomposition (Theorem 2).
2. **Rigorous Mathematical Synthesis:** Authors skillfully integrate matroid theory with causal graphical models. The matroid-preserving column augmentation framework (Lemmas 8–10, Corollaries 1–2) and the Hasse diagram traversal proof (Lemma 12) provide a clean, constructive machinery for equivalence class navigation, offering a meaningful generalization of Meek's conjecture to latent/cyclic settings.
3. **Clear Algorithmic Translation & Reproducibility:** The `glvLiNG` pipeline directly operationalizes the theoretical results into a constraint-based recovery procedure. The provision of open-source code, an interactive equivalence traversal demo, and detailed proofs/algorithmic formulations in the appendix strongly align with ICLR's reproducibility expectations.
4. **Well-Designed empirical Evaluation:** The experiments comprehensively benchmark the method across multiple dimensions: quantifying equivalence class sizes, demonstrating significant speedups over MILP baselines (Table 4), isolating structural misspecification errors in existing methods via oracle inputs (Table 5), and validating finite-sample robustness and real-world plausibility.

### Weaknesses
1. **Practical Dependency on OICA:** The pipeline's first step requires estimating an overcomplete mixing matrix via OICA, which is known to be computationally intensive and sensitive to finite-sample noise. While the authors acknowledge this (§5, Appendix D.4), the heuristic thresholding used to approximate empirical ranks under noise lacks theoretical robustness guarantees, and rank estimation errors can easily violate matroid axioms in practice.
2. **Scalability of Equivalence Traversal:** Although `glvLiNG`'s constraint-based rank realization is efficient, the exhaustive BFS/DFS traversal mandated by Theorem 3 remains combinatorially expensive. Table D.1 shows equivalence classes can contain hundreds of thousands of graphs even for $n=5$, indicating that full class enumeration will quickly become intractable for moderate $n$ without further search-space pruning or sampling approximations.
3. **Limited Ground-Truth Validation on Real Data:** The Hong Kong stock market application (Appendix D.5) yields interpretable patterns but lacks domain-validated ground truth or interventional/longitudinal data to confirm the recovered latent structures and cycles. This is typical in causal discovery but weakens the empirical claim of practical deployability.
4. **Narrow Parametric Scope:** The results are strictly confined to the linear non-Gaussian setting. While Appendix C.5 discusses potential extensions to Gaussian, discrete, or nonlinear models, the current framework does not address these broader settings, limiting immediate applicability where distributional assumptions are uncertain.

### Novelty & Significance
The novelty is high and aligns well with ICLR's preference for principled theoretical advances with algorithmic implications. By establishing the first assumption-free equivalence framework for latent-variable LiNG models with cycles, the paper resolves a foundational gap in causal identifiability theory. The edge-rank duality and matroid-based traversal characterization are substantial contributions that will likely serve as a reference point for future work in parametric and nonparametric causal discovery. Its significance lies in clarifying fundamental identifiability limits, providing practitioners with a theoretically sound baseline free from opaque structural constraints, and opening new directions at the intersection of algebraic combinatorics and representation learning. The work comfortably meets ICLR's acceptance bar for theoretical machine learning, though broader empirical validation and scalability considerations would further strengthen its impact.

### Suggestions for Improvement
1. **Strengthen Finite-Sample Robustness Discussion:** Provide a formal or empirical analysis of how OICA estimation noise propagates to matroid realization errors. Consider integrating or comparing with OICA-free rank estimation techniques (e.g., generalized independent noise tests, higher-order cumulants) and explicitly detail how `glvLiNG` could be adapted to use these more robust estimators.
2. **Introduce Practical Search-Space Reductions:** Given the exponential growth of equivalence classes, propose and evaluate heuristic pruning strategies (e.g., leveraging Theorem 4's maximal graph presentation to bound admissible edges early, or using MCMC/beam search over the transformational graph instead of exhaustive BFS/DFS). Report scaling trends and wall-clock times for $n \geq 10$.
3. **Enhance Real-World or Semi-Synthetic Validation:** If domain ground truth is unavailable, construct semi-synthetic benchmarks by embedding known latent-variable causal graphs into real-world observed datasets (e.g., adding controlled latent confounders to financial or genomics data). This would provide quantifiable recovery metrics while preserving realistic marginal distributions.
4. **Clarify Assumption Scope Early:** Explicitly state the faithfulness/genericity assumption and its practical implications in the main text (not just Appendix A). Add a brief ablation or discussion on performance degradation when noise deviates from strict non-Gaussianity (e.g., heavy-tailed or near-Gaussian), helping practitioners understand the method's operational boundaries.

3. Clarify the practical relevance of the non-rank algebraic constraints (Appendix C.4) because ignoring them may artificially inflate the recovered equivalence class when finite-sample data cannot perfectly distinguish rank-equivalent models.

### Visualizations & Case Studies
1. Visualize failure cases where noisy OICA ranks produce invalid matroids, because this exposes whether the method degrades gracefully into interpretable ambiguity or outputs structurally meaningless graphs.
2. Plot sample-complexity curves mapping dataset size to SHD and equivalence class reduction, to demonstrate whether the method actually converges to useful structures within practical sample budgets.
3. Show concrete traversal paths for medium-sized graphs where multiple admissible operations apply, because without visualizing how the algorithm navigates structural ambiguity, the transformational characterization remains unverified in practice.

### Obvious Next Steps
1. Replace the ad-hoc rank thresholding with a formal hypothesis testing procedure or concentration-bound wrapper, because the paper’s theoretical guarantees require exact rank patterns that empirical estimates cannot satisfy without statistical rigor.
2. Develop a principled scoring or selection mechanism within the recovered equivalence class, because outputting tens of thousands of equivalent graphs provides no actionable causal insight to practitioners and defeats the purpose of discovery.
3. Provide asymptotic computational complexity bounds beyond empirical timing, because claiming an "efficient constraint-based design" without formal complexity analysis leaves scalability and worst-case behavior unproven.

# Final Consolidated Review
## Summary
This paper establishes the first structural-assumption-free characterization of distributional equivalence for linear non-Gaussian (LiNG) causal models with arbitrary latent variables and cycles. By introducing "edge rank" constraints and proving a novel duality with traditional path ranks, the authors decompose a globally intractable equivalence condition into a local, singleton-checkable criterion. They further derive a transformational characterization (via admissible edge additions/deletions and cycle reversals) that fully traverses the equivalence class, and propose the `glvLiNG` algorithm to recover models from data up to this equivalence.

## Strengths
- **Foundational theoretical contribution resolving a long-standing gap:** The paper successfully removes decades of restrictive structural assumptions (e.g., pure measurement models, hierarchical latents, acyclicity) and delivers a complete graphical characterization of distributional equivalence for LiNG models. This directly answers the theoretical prerequisite ("what can be identified") needed to design general latent-variable discovery methods.
- **Elegant technical synthesis via matroid-theoretic duality:** The proof of duality between path ranks (strict gammoids) and edge ranks (transversal matroids) is both conceptually clean and practically powerful. It transforms a global, bottleneck-dependent condition into an edge-local matching property, enabling the crucial decomposition in Theorem 2 where global equivalence reduces to independent checks over singleton children bases.
- **Rigorous transformational characterization and algorithmic translation:** The proof that the equivalence class is fully connected via admissible edge operations and at most one cycle reversal (Theorem 3) provides a constructive, Meek-conjecture-like blueprint for latent-cyclic settings. The `glvLiNG` pipeline directly operationalizes this theory, demonstrating substantial efficiency gains over brute-force MILP baselines for the rank-realization step, and is supported by open code, proofs, and an interactive traversal demo.

## Weaknesses
- **Oracle-dependent practical implementation without finite-sample guarantees:** The authors correctly frame `glvLiNG` as a proof-of-concept for their theoretical results. However, the practical pipeline relies entirely on OICA to estimate the mixing matrix, which is notoriously unstable in finite samples, sensitive to hyperparameters, and computationally prohibitive as latent dimensionality grows. More critically, the empirical implementation uses an ad-hoc singular-value thresholding heuristic to extract discrete ranks. This step lacks statistical concentration bounds, meaning estimation errors can violate matroid axioms and break the theoretical guarantees, potentially yielding structurally invalid graphs or failure to converge to the true equivalence class.
- **Combinatorial explosion in equivalence traversal limits scalability:** While the constraint-based rank realization in `glvLiNG` is efficient (Phase 1/2 scale linearly/well in practice), the exact BFS/DFS traversal mandated by Theorem 3 remains combinatorially explosive. Quantification in the appendix shows equivalence classes containing hundreds of thousands of graphs even for $n=5$. The paper does not propose or evaluate practical search-space reduction strategies (e.g., leveraging the maximal graph presentation for early pruning, or stochastic sampling over the transformational space), severely limiting its utility for real-world systems with moderate-to-large variable counts.
- **Evaluation metric obscures structural ambiguity:** The simulation results report Structural Hamming Distance (SHD) against the single *best-matching* graph in the ground-truth equivalence class. This is highly generous and inflates apparent performance, as it credits the method for finding any structurally plausible realization rather than assessing its ability to recover the *actually identifiable* features (i.e., the invariant edges common to all equivalent models). This makes it difficult to gauge how informative the output is for downstream causal reasoning or experimental design.

## Nice-to-Haves
- Explicitly state the faithfulness/genericity assumption in the main text rather than deferring to Appendix A, ensuring readers immediately calibrate the theoretical scope regarding parameter cancellations.
- Report invariant-edge precision/recall (identifying "solid" edges) alongside minimum SHD to better reflect the practical informativeness of the recovered equivalence class.
- Explore or formalize integration with OICA-free or more robust rank estimation techniques (e.g., GIN tests, higher-order cumulant methods) to decouple the algorithmic pipeline from mixing matrix estimation bottlenecks.

## Novel Insights
The paper's core conceptual advance lies in recognizing that distributional equivalence in latent-variable settings is not fundamentally about searching over global graphical Markov properties, but about maintaining consistency in transversal matroids induced by local edge matchings. By establishing the duality between path ranks and edge ranks, the authors reframe a notoriously intractable, globally dependent bottleneck problem into a locally decomposable combinatorial consistency check. This matroid-augmentation view naturally yields a transformational characterization analogous to Meek's conjecture, demonstrating that the space of observationally indistinguishable latent-cyclic models can be navigated purely through localized, rank-preserving edge operations.

## Suggestions
- **Formalize or empirically bound the impact of rank estimation errors:** Provide an analysis (theoretical or simulation-based) of how deviations from true ranks in the OICA output propagate to matroid axiom violations, and clarify whether the algorithm degrades gracefully into a larger but valid equivalence class, or outputs structurally nonsensical graphs.
- **Implement and evaluate a pruning strategy for equivalence traversal:** Leverage Theorem 4's maximal graph presentation to derive hard bounds on admissible edge operations early in the search, or replace exhaustive BFS/DFS with a beam search/MCMC sampler over the transformational space. Report wall-clock times and traversal completion rates for $n \geq 10$.
- **Clarify the role of non-rank polynomial constraints:** Briefly address in the main text why non-rank equality constraints (e.g., those in Appendix C.4) do not partition the distributional equivalence classes further in the LiNG setting, reinforcing that the rank-based characterization is informationally complete for this parametric family.
