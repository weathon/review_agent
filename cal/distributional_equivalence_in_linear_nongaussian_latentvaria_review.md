# Harsh Critic Review
## Section-by-Section Critical Review

### Title & Abstract
- **Does the title accurately reflect the contribution?** Yes. The title explicitly names the setting (linear non-Gaussian, latent-variable, cyclic causal models) and the two main deliverables (characterization of distributional equivalence and learning).
- **Does the abstract clearly state the problem, method, and key results?** Yes. It identifies the core obstacle (lack of a general equivalence characterization), introduces the key tool (edge rank constraints), states the graphical criterion and traversal procedure, and summarizes the algorithm (`glvLiNG`).
- **Are any claims in the abstract unsupported by the paper?** The claim "first structural-assumption-free discovery method" is strong but appropriately scoped to the linear non-Gaussian setting in the main text. The abstract could briefly qualify this to avoid misinterpretation by readers outside parametric causal discovery, but it is not misleading given the paper's framing.

### Introduction & Motivation
- **Is the problem well-motivated? Is the gap in prior work clearly identified?** Yes. The motivation is compelling: the field has fragmented into methods requiring rigid structural priors (pure indicators, hierarchical layouts, acyclicity), which are hard to validate in practice. The authors correctly identify the absence of a general equivalence characterization as the theoretical bottleneck preventing structural-assumption-free learning.
- **Are the contributions clearly stated and accurate?** Yes. The four contributions are enumerated clearly and map directly to Sections 3–5. The progression from algebraic equivalence (Lemma 1/3) to local graphical criteria (Theorem 2) to transformational traversal (Theorem 3) and finally to an algorithm is logical.
- **Does the introduction over-claim or under-sell?** It does not over-claim. The authors carefully distinguish distributional equivalence from Markov equivalence, acknowledge prior work on equivalence in cyclic-only or latent-only settings, and explicitly position their result as filling the gap for *combined* latent variables and cycles under LiNG assumptions. The analogy to CPDAGs and Meek's conjecture is well-chosen to orient readers.

### Method / Approach
- **Is the method clearly described and reproducible?** The theoretical framework is rigorous and well-structured. The transition from path ranks (global) to edge ranks (local) via Theorem 1's duality is elegant. The matroid-theoretic proofs in the appendix are detailed, leveraging transversal matroids and the fundamental lemma of Ingleton & Piff to justify the local decomposition (Lemma 9 → Theorem 2) and traversal (Lemma 12 → Theorem 3). The algorithm pipeline (OICA → rank realization → class traversal) is clearly laid out.
- **Are key assumptions stated and justified?** Yes. Irreducibility (Def 2) is introduced and justified by OICA identifiability results to remove trivially unidentifiable latent vertices. The faithfulness assumption (Assumption 1, Appendix A) is standard in this literature and explicitly defined as ruling out coincidental rank cancellations.
- **Are there logical gaps in the derivation or reasoning?** No major gaps detected. The proof that path rank equivalence implies distributional equivalence (Lemma 3) correctly handles the Zariski closure and generic parameter choices to sidestep the pathological locus where `I - B` becomes singular. The leap from checking all subsets `𝐱 ⊆ X` to singleton checks `Xi ∈ X` is non-trivial but rigorously proven via Lemma 9 using matroid basis properties.
- **Are there edge cases or failure modes not discussed?** The method assumes the exact number of latent variables is known or correctly selected (e.g., via held-out loss as noted in Appendix D.4). While theoretically identifiable under LiNG, finite-sample OICA rank estimation can mis-specify latent dimensionality. Additionally, the approach assumes *exact* rank recovery from data. In practice, distinguishing full-rank from rank-deficient blocks under finite samples and noise lacks rigorous statistical power guarantees. The authors address this heuristically in Appendix D.4 with a sigmoidal confidence score, but a formal discussion of finite-sample rank testing consistency would strengthen the learning component.
- **For theoretical claims: are proofs correct and complete?** Yes. The appendix provides complete proofs. The matroid augmentation results (Lemmas 8–10, Corollaries 1–2) are solid and correctly applied to show how edge additions/deletions preserve equivalence. The transformational characterization (Theorem 3) correctly bounds the number of cycle reversals needed to one, which is a non-trivial and useful result for traversal.

### Experiments & Results
- **Do the experiments actually test the paper's claims?** Yes. The evaluation is structured to test both the theoretical framework (equivalence class sizes, transformational traversal runtime) and the practical algorithm (finite-sample recovery, robustness to structural misspecification, real-world application).
- **Are baselines appropriate and fairly compared?** The comparison with PO-LiNGAM and LaHiCaSl in Table 5 is insightful: by giving baselines *oracle* access to their required independence/rank tests, the authors correctly isolate the failure mode as *structural misspecification* rather than statistical estimation error. This directly supports the claim that structural assumptions limit applicability. The MILP baseline in Table 4 appropriately highlights the computational advantage of the matroid-based constraint approach.
- **Are there missing ablations that would materially change conclusions?** An ablation on the sensitivity of Phase 2 (observed-to-all edge recovery) to different rank-thresholding strategies could be useful, but given the clear theoretical guarantees, it is not strictly required. The real-world experiment lacks ground truth, which is unavoidable in observational causal discovery, but the interpretability analysis (e.g., banks as sources, utilities in cycles) is reasonable.
- **Are error bars / statistical significance reported?** Yes. Tables 3–5 report mean ± standard deviation over 50 runs. Figure 7 includes error bars across multiple sample sizes, latent counts, and graph densities. This meets standard reporting practices.
- **Do the results support the claims made, or are they cherry-picked?** Results are consistent with claims. `glvLiNG` scales well computationally and shows clear advantages in denser, more complex latent structures where baseline assumptions fail. In sparser regimes, baselines perform comparably or slightly better (correctly attributed to their structural priors acting as regularizers). The real-world output aligns with domain intuition without over-interpreting.
- **Are datasets and evaluation metrics appropriate?** Synthetic Erdős–Rényi graphs with varying latent fractions and densities standardly stress-test latent discovery methods. Using minimum SHD over the true equivalence class is the correct metric for this setting, as point estimates are inherently unidentifiable beyond the class. The Hong Kong stock dataset is well-established in causal discovery literature.

### Writing & Clarity
- **Are there sections that are confusing or poorly explained?** The material is dense but logically sequenced. Readers unfamiliar with matroid theory may find Appendix A challenging, but the main text successfully abstracts the necessary concepts (bases, circuits, cocircuits, coloops) into the graphical language of "children bases" and "pillar" vertices. The analogy to CPDAGs and Meek's conjecture in §4 aids intuition significantly.
- **Are figures and tables clear and informative?** Yes. Figure 1 effectively illustrates irreducibility reduction. Figures 3 and 5 clearly show equivalence class structures and transformations. Table 2 provides an excellent unified overview of equivalence characterization literature. The parser introduced some table formatting artifacts, but the underlying data and structure remain clear and interpretable.

### Limitations & Broader Impact
- **Do the authors acknowledge the key limitations?** Yes. They explicitly identify OICA's computational and statistical limitations as the main bottleneck for practical scaling, positioning `glvLiNG` in this work primarily as a proof-of-concept that structural-assumption-free recovery is *possible* and well-defined.
- **Are there fundamental limitations they missed?** The faithfulness assumption (no coincidental rank cancellations) is critical but untestable in practice. Additionally, the linear non-Gaussian assumption, while standard in this subfield, restricts applicability to domains with strongly non-Gaussian exogenous noise. The authors briefly mention extensions to Gaussian and discrete settings in Appendix C.5 but note technical hurdles (e.g., additional algebraic constraints like tetrads/pentads, lack of exact trek-separation duality in discrete cases). A deeper discussion on handling near-faithfulness violations (e.g., edges with very small coefficients that empirically drop ranks) would be valuable for robustness.
- **Are there failure modes or negative societal impacts not discussed?** Standard limitations of observational causal discovery apply: equivalence classes do not imply true causality without interventions, and model misspecification beyond the LiNG framework (e.g., heavy-tailed mixtures, nonlinearities) could yield spurious structures. No significant negative societal impacts are anticipated, and the ethics statement is appropriate.

### Overall Assessment
This paper makes a significant theoretical contribution to causal discovery by establishing the first complete characterization of distributional equivalence for linear non-Gaussian models with arbitrary latent variables and cycles. The introduction of edge rank constraints and their duality with path ranks is elegant and enables a clean local graphical criterion (Theorem 2) and a transformational traversal method (Theorem 3). The algorithm `glvLiNG` is well-motivated, theoretically sound, and empirically validated across synthetic and real-world settings. The primary limitation is reliance on OICA and exact rank recovery, which poses practical challenges in high-dimensional, finite-sample regimes; however, the authors transparently acknowledge this and position the work appropriately. The theoretical framework is rigorous, the proofs are complete, and the experiments directly support the claims while honestly contextualizing baseline performance. This work meets and exceeds ICLR's standards for novelty, technical rigor, and clarity. With minor clarifications on finite-sample rank testing robustness and faithfulness violations, it stands as a strong candidate for acceptance.

# Neutral Reviewer
## Balanced Review

### Summary
This paper establishes the first complete characterization of distributional equivalence for linear non-Gaussian (LiNG) causal models with arbitrary latent variables and feedback cycles, eliminating the need for restrictive structural assumptions like pure indicators or hierarchical layouts. By introducing "edge rank" constraints and proving their duality with traditional path ranks via matroid theory, the authors derive a tractable graphical criterion and a transformational characterization to efficiently traverse equivalence classes. They also present glvLiNG, a constraint-based algorithm that recovers the full equivalence class from observational data using OICA-estimated mixing matrices, demonstrating strong theoretical guarantees and robust empirical performance under structural misspecification.

### Strengths
1. **Foundational Theoretical Contribution:** The paper successfully resolves a long-standing gap in causal discovery by characterizing distributional equivalence without structural assumptions on latent-variable interactions or acyclicity. The introduction of edge ranks (Definition 4) and the elegant duality theorem with path ranks (Theorem 1) provide a powerful new lens for bottleneck analysis in graphs, while Theorems 2 and 3 deliver both a practical decision criterion and a complete transformational characterization (analogous to the Meek conjecture for Markov equivalence).
2. **Algorithmic Efficiency & Clean Mathematical Decomposition:** Despite the combinatorial appearance of rank realization, the authors leverage matroid theory to prove that global rank constraints decompose into independent singleton checks (Lemma 9). This enables glvLiNG to bypass brute-force integer programming, yielding substantial empirical speedups over MILP baselines (Table 4) and enabling traversal of large equivalence classes (e.g., 19,008 graphs for the stock dataset).
3. **Comprehensive & Reproducible Evaluation:** The empirical section thoroughly quantifies equivalence class sizes (Table 3), benchmarks against structural misspecification (Table 5 shows PO-LiNGAM/LaHiCaSl degrade significantly on dense/complex graphs), and validates finite-sample performance across varying sparsity and latent dimensions (Figure 7). The provision of open-source code, precise simulation protocols, and an interactive web demo significantly strengthens reproducibility and community adoption.

### Weaknesses
1. **Practical Bottleneck from OICA Dependency:** The glvLiNG pipeline relies on Overcomplete ICA to estimate the mixing matrix before rank realization. OICA is notoriously sensitive to noise, scales poorly with dimensionality, and requires careful initialization/hyperparameter tuning. The authors acknowledge this in §5 but relegate mitigation to "future directions," leaving a substantial gap between the theoretical guarantee and robust, scalable finite-sample performance.
2. **Heuristic Handling of Finite-Sample Rank Violations:** When empirical OICA estimates violate matroid axioms due to sampling noise, Appendix D.4 employs an ad-hoc confidence scoring function (`1+exp(−α(σ_min−ε))`) and hard thresholding. This heuristic lacks theoretical justification, sensitivity analysis, or ablation studies on threshold selection, raising concerns about the stability of recovered graphs in low-sample or high-noise regimes.
3. **Overstated "Assumption-Free" Scope & Limited Real-World Validation:** While structurally assumption-free, the method still fundamentally relies on linearity and non-Gaussianity, which should be explicitly scoped to avoid misleading practitioners. Furthermore, the real-world stock market application (§5 & Appendix D.5) claims "meaningful patterns" and "plausible interpretations" for latent variables without domain-expert validation, interventional ground truth, or quantitative interpretability metrics, limiting the empirical impact of the case study.

### Novelty & Significance
**Novelty:** Exceptionally high. The edge rank framework and the complete distributional equivalence characterization for latent-variable cyclic LiNG models represent a major theoretical leap. The matroid-based decomposition and transformational characterization (Theorems 2–3) are original and fill a critical void in the literature.
**Clarity:** Strong for a deep theoretical paper. The logical progression from algebraic equivalence to graphical ranks, then to matroid duality and algorithmic design, is well-structured. However, the heavy reliance on transversal matroids, gammoids, and algebraic geometry prerequisites may limit accessibility to broader ML audiences without additional intuitive exposition.
**Reproducibility:** High. The authors provide full implementation details, pseudocode (Appendix A), exhaustive simulation setups, and a public interactive demo. The only reproducibility friction point is the third-party OICA solver dependency and the unvalidated singular-value heuristic.
**Significance:** High for the causal discovery and representation learning communities. By establishing exactly what is identifiable from observational data alone, the work shifts the paradigm from imposing untestable structural priors to rigorously enumerating equivalence classes. It provides a rigorous foundation for future score-based optimization, active intervention design, and causal representation learning under latent confounding.

### Suggestions for Improvement
1. **Develop or Benchmark OICA-Free Rank Estimation:** Since OICA is the primary practical bottleneck, integrate or at least systematically benchmark alternative rank-estimation strategies (e.g., pairwise GIN tests, continuous optimization proxies, or bootstrap aggregation of sub-matrices) to demonstrate how glvLiNG could scale beyond ~15 variables and remain robust to noise.
2. **Formalize the Finite-Sample Rank Thresholding:** Provide a theoretical analysis or extensive empirical ablation of the singular-value-based confidence scoring in Appendix D.4. Establish how $\alpha$ and $\epsilon$ should scale with sample size $N$ and noise variance, or consider replacing the heuristic with a matroid-closure projection or minimum-description-length criterion to ensure axiom consistency.
3. **Clarify Assumption Scope & Strengthen Real-World Validation:** Explicitly state in the abstract and introduction that the method is "structurally assumption-free" but remains parametric (requiring linearity and non-Gaussianity). For the stock market experiment, either validate latent variable interpretations against external macroeconomic factors/market interventions, or replace the case study with a benchmark dataset containing known intervention targets to provide quantitative grounding for the interpretability claims.

# Final Consolidated Review
## Summary
This paper establishes the first complete characterization of distributional equivalence for linear non-Gaussian causal models with arbitrary latent variables and feedback cycles. By introducing edge rank constraints and proving their strict duality with path ranks via matroid theory, the authors derive a local graphical criterion, a transformational characterization for equivalence class traversal, and a constraint-based discovery algorithm (`glvLiNG`) that bypasses restrictive structural priors. The theoretical framework is mathematically elegant and rigorously proven, though the practical learning component currently hinges on an imperfect subroutine and lacks formal complexity guarantees.

## Strengths
- **Foundational theoretical contribution:** The resolution of a long-standing identifiability gap through edge rank/path rank duality (Theorem 1) and subsequent local decomposition (Theorem 2) is mathematically rigorous. It successfully generalizes classical Markov equivalence results to a much broader setting with arbitrary latents and cycles, providing both a practical decision criterion and a transformational characterization analogous to Meek's conjecture.
- **Algorithmic efficiency via matroid theory:** The reduction of a global rank-compatibility check into independent singleton augmentations (Lemma 9) transforms an exponentially hard satisfiability problem into a tractable, constraint-based procedure. Empirical results confirm substantial speedups over MILP baselines and robust performance under structural misspecification.
- **Rigorous exposition and reproducibility:** The manuscript provides complete, logically sequenced proofs, carefully scopes parametric assumptions, and supplies open-source code alongside an interactive web demo. The experimental design correctly isolates structural misspecification as the primary failure mode of existing baselines.

## Weaknesses
- **Practical viability bottleneck due to OICA dependency and heuristic rank testing — why it matters:** The `glvLiNG` pipeline relies entirely on Overcomplete ICA to estimate the mixing matrix before rank realization. OICA is statistically fragile, sensitive to initialization, and computationally prohibitive in higher dimensions. More critically, the finite-sample implementation depends on an ad-hoc singular-value confidence threshold (Appendix D.4) lacking theoretical justification, sensitivity analysis, or convergence guarantees. Without a principled rank-testing procedure, the algorithm's statistical reliability degrades unpredictably in noisy, moderate-sample regimes, reducing it to a theoretical proof-of-concept rather than a deployable estimator.
- **Absence of formal computational complexity analysis — why it matters:** While empirical benchmarks demonstrate clear efficiency gains over integer programming, the paper provides no worst-case time or space complexity bounds for the matroid realization (Phase 1) and singleton augmentation (Phase 2) steps. Without theoretical scaling guarantees as a function of $n$, $|L|$, and graph density, claims of algorithmic efficiency remain purely empirical, leaving uncertainty about how the method scales to realistic, high-dimensional systems where equivalence classes grow combinatorially.

## Nice-to-Haves
- Extend the real-world validation beyond a single financial dataset to benchmarks with interventional ground truth or known causal structures, moving latent variable interpretations from plausible storytelling to empirically verified results.
- Provide a brief ablation on near-faithfulness violations (e.g., empirically vanishing ranks due to weak but nonzero coefficients) and discuss how the irreducibility reduction (Proposition 2) might be regularized for noisy rank estimates.
- Clarify the mapping between edge rank duality and trek-separation in linear Gaussian models to better position the framework for future cross-parametric generalizations.

## Novel Insights
The core intellectual breakthrough lies in recognizing that distributional equivalence in latent-variable LiNG models is fundamentally governed by the combinatorial structure of transversal matroids rather than raw algebraic constraints. By establishing a strict duality between path ranks (dictated by mixing matrices) and edge ranks (dictated by graph adjacencies), the authors bypass the intractability of global bottleneck analysis. This reveals that equivalence class traversal can be localized to independent column-wise matroid augmentations, transforming what appeared to be a combinatorial explosion of latent structures into a sequence of deterministic, graph-level operations. This matroid-theoretic lens not only simplifies identifiability proofs but also structurally decouples causal discovery from specific parametric estimators, providing a clean architectural blueprint for future OICA-free implementations.

## Suggestions
1. **Formalize finite-sample rank testing:** Replace the heuristic sigmoidal threshold in Appendix D.4 with a principled rank-determination procedure (e.g., bootstrap-based eigenvalue gap testing, Tracy-Widom edge statistics, or a minimum description length criterion). Provide an ablation showing recovery performance as a function of sample size, noise variance, and threshold hyperparameters.
2. **Derive explicit complexity bounds:** State worst-case time and space complexity for Phases 1 and 2 of `glvLiNG`. Explicitly detail the polynomial dependence on $n$, latent dimensionality, and edge density to substantiate scalability claims beyond empirical MILP comparisons.
3. **Quantify OICA error propagation:** Run a systematic simulation measuring how deviations in the estimated mixing matrix (e.g., injected Gaussian noise or structural sparsity violations) propagate to final graph recovery SHD. This will clearly delineate the finite-sample regime where `glvLiNG` remains theoretically grounded versus practically degraded.
