# Harsh Critic Review
## Section-by-Section Critical Review

### Title & Abstract
- **Title vs. Contribution:** The title claims alignment of the "Full Diffusion Trajectory," but the method in Section 3.1 explicitly bypasses multi-step sampling in favor of a single-step reconstruction from an intermediate state with injected ground-truth noise. This is a pragmatic approximation, not a trajectory-level alignment. Consider revising the title or abstract to accurately reflect that the method *decouples* reward computation from the full trajectory rather than aligning it.
- **Efficiency Claims:** The abstract states convergence in "10 minutes with 32 NVIDIA H20 GPUs." While striking, this lacks context required for scientific credibility: number of training steps, batch size, and total GPU-hours. For a ~12B parameter model like FLUX.1.dev, 10 minutes of wall-clock time suggests an extremely large effective batch or very few update steps. ICLR reviewers will question whether this reflects meaningful optimization or rapid reward overfitting. Please report exact steps, throughput, and compare compute in standardized GPU-hours rather than cluster-dependent wall-clock time.

### Introduction & Motivation
- **Problem Framing:** The motivation around late-timestep restriction and reward hacking is well-grounded in prior work. However, the claim that early-timestep backpropagation causes "severe optimization instability, such as gradient explosion" needs empirical or theoretical backing. In practice, early-timestep gradients in diffusion models often suffer from *high variance* or *signal dilution* rather than explosion. Clarifying the exact failure mode (variance, vanishing gradients, or compute bottlenecks) would strengthen the rationale for Direct-Align.
- **Scope of Reward Hacking:** The introduction correctly identifies biases in popular reward models (e.g., HPSv2 favoring reddish tones). However, it conflates model bias with the optimization process itself. Reward hacking typically arises from maximizing a flawed proxy, not just from restricting timesteps. The paper should clarify how Single-Step Image Recovery inherently reduces hacking, rather than just changing the point at which the reward is evaluated.

### Method / Approach
- **Direct-Align Formulation (Eqs. 3-5):** The reconstruction logic relies on injecting ground-truth noise $\epsilon_{gt}$ into $x_0$ and analytically recovering $x_0$. Equation 5 mixes model-predicted noise $\epsilon_\theta$ and ground-truth noise $\epsilon_{gt}$ via a convex combination. The gradient w.r.t. $\theta$ flows only through $\epsilon_\theta$, but the reward is evaluated on a *hybrid* image that explicitly contains ground-truth information. This raises a critical question: how does the gradient signal properly credit the model for improving realism when the reward is partially evaluated on a reconstruction that already contains the "correct" structure via $\epsilon_{gt}$? A brief sensitivity analysis on $\Delta \sigma$ or a theoretical discussion on gradient bias/vanishing would strengthen this.
- **SRPO Linearity Assumption (Eqs. 7-8):** The method assumes the reward can be decomposed as $r_{SRP}(x) = f_{img}(x)^T \cdot (C_1 - C_2)$. This holds strictly only for linear similarity metrics (e.g., raw CLIP cosine similarity). Modern reward models like HPSv2 and ImageReward use non-linear projection heads and cross-attention over the text embedding. If the reward function is non-linear, the subtraction $r(x, C_1) - r(x, C_2)$ does not cleanly isolate semantic preferences from image-encoder biases. Please either (1) empirically verify that the relative reward formulation holds for the non-linear heads of your chosen reward models, or (2) clarify that SRPO is an approximation that works empirically despite theoretical non-linearity.
- **Inversion-Based Regularization (Eq. 9):** The description of optimizing in the "inversion direction" to penalize high-frequency reward hacking is conceptually interesting but under-specified. Is this implemented by flipping the sign of the gradient during backpropagation on specific timesteps? How is $\lambda(t)$ scheduled relative to the inversion step? A clearer algorithmic description or pseudocode would improve reproducibility.

### Experiments & Results
- **Dataset & RL Loop Clarification:** The paper states training on HPDv2 (a pairwise preference dataset used for reward model training). How are these pairs converted into a generative RL loop? Do you sample one image per prompt as $x_0$, inject noise, and optimize? Clarifying the data pipeline and how preference pairs inform the single-sample updates is essential.
- **Table 1 Discrepancies:** The HPSv2 score for SRPO (0.289) is identical to the vanilla FLUX baseline, while ImageReward drops slightly (1.118 vs 1.115). The paper claims "immunity to HPS score inflation," which is consistent with the static HPS score, but the primary gains appear in Aesthetic and PickScore. This suggests the method may be shifting the optimization landscape toward aesthetics rather than strictly aligning with the training reward. Please discuss why SRPO improves aesthetic metrics while leaving the training reward (HPS) unchanged, and how this relates to the claimed online reward adjustment.
- **Human Evaluation Rigor:** The reported jump in realism "Excellent" rate from 8.2% to 38.9% is substantial. However, the paper does not report statistical significance (e.g., 95% CIs, p-values, or inter-rater reliability like Fleiss' kappa), nor does it specify whether results are reported as win-rates or absolute ordinal scores. For ICLR, such strong human-eval claims require proper statistical treatment. Additionally, the 4-level ordinal scale analysis would benefit from reporting overall distribution percentages or using appropriate ordinal aggregation methods rather than single-point "Excellent" rates.
- **Baseline Fairness:** ReFL and DRaFT-LV are marked with `[$\star$]` as re-implementations. Given the sensitivity of direct-gradient RL to hyperparameters (step size, gradient clipping, sampling steps), were these baselines extensively tuned for FLUX.1.dev, or run with default settings from their original papers? If under-tuned, the efficiency and quality gaps may be inflated. Clarify the tuning budget allocated to baselines.

### Writing & Clarity
- The mathematical progression from Eq. 5 to Eq. 6 (reward aggregation) to Eq. 9 (inversion regularization) lacks a unifying algorithmic summary. Providing a concise training loop pseudocode (e.g., 1. generate $x_0$, 2. inject noise at $t$, 3. compute hybrid $\hat{x}_0$, 4. evaluate $r_{SRP}$, 5. update $\theta$ with $\lambda(t)$ weighting) would significantly improve readability and reproducibility.
- Several figure references in the text (e.g., "see fig. 5", "see fig. 7 (d)") point to multi-panel ablations without clearly mapping the specific claim to the specific panel. Explicitly tying statements to subfigure labels (e.g., "as shown in Fig. 5, left panel") would help.

### Limitations & Broader Impact
- The authors appropriately acknowledge that control token effectiveness depends on reward model vocabulary coverage and that interpretability is limited. However, the reliance on a 32-GPU H20 cluster is a substantial reproducibility barrier for the broader community. A brief discussion on scaling behavior (e.g., can meaningful gains be achieved on 4-8 GPUs with gradient accumulation over more steps?) would make the work more accessible.
- The ethical statement is generic. Given the focus on realism and style control, a brief note on potential misuse (e.g., deepfake generation, bypassing safety filters through prompt augmentation) and any safeguards implemented during training would align with ICLR's broader impact expectations.

### Overall Assessment
This paper presents a practical and computationally efficient approach to mitigating reward hacking in diffusion model alignment by combining single-step reconstruction with prompt-augmented relative reward optimization. The core insight—decoupling reward evaluation from iterative sampling to stabilize gradients across timesteps—is well-motivated and empirically promising. However, several aspects require clarification before the work meets ICLR's standards for methodological rigor and reproducibility. The mathematical assumption underlying SRPO relies on reward model linearity, which conflicts with the non-linear heads of modern rewards like HPSv2. The training efficiency claim (10 mins on 32 H20s) lacks essential details (steps, batch size, GPU-hours) needed to validate the 75x efficiency gain. Additionally, the human evaluation results, while strong, lack statistical reporting, and the baseline tuning budget is unclear. If the authors can clarify the gradient credit assignment in Direct-Align, address the SRPO linearity approximation empirically or theoretically, provide rigorous human-eval statistics, and fully document compute budgets, the paper will be a strong contribution to efficient diffusion model alignment.

# Neutral Reviewer
## Balanced Review

### Summary
This paper proposes SRPO, an online reinforcement learning framework for aligning text-to-image diffusion models, addressing reward hacking from restricted late-timestep optimization and the static nature of conventional reward models. It introduces Direct-Align, which leverages a closed-form interpolation using predefined noise priors to enable stable single-step recovery across the full generation trajectory, and Semantic Relative Preference Optimization, which computes rewards as the difference between positively and negatively prompted text embeddings to allow dynamic, online reward adjustment. Empirical results on FLUX.1.dev demonstrate substantial gains in human-evaluated realism and training efficiency compared to existing direct-gradient RL baselines.

### Strengths
1. **Practically Motivated Solutions to Known Bottlenecks:** The paper directly tackles two recognized limitations in diffusion RL: computational instability that restricts gradient flow to late timesteps, and static reward models that misalign with real-world aesthetic goals. The proposed relative preference mechanism (Sec 3.2, Eq. 7-8) offers a lightweight, training-free way to steer optimization and filter out reward model biases.
2. **High Training Efficiency & Strong Human-Perceived Gains:** SRPO converges in approximately 10 minutes on 32 H20 GPUs, representing a >75× speedup over DanceGRPO (Table 1). Human evaluations report a notable increase in "Excellent" ratings for realism (8.2% to 38.9%) and aesthetic quality, with qualitative samples (Fig. 3, 7) showing reduced artifacts like oversaturation and edge glossiness.
3. **Effective Cross-Reward Generalization:** Appendix A demonstrates that the relative preference formulation successfully mitigates reward hacking across multiple reward models (HPS, PickScore, CLIP), consistently improving realism without inducing color casts or detail loss (Fig. 8). This suggests the method is robust to the specific biases of individual reward functions.

### Weaknesses
1. **Theoretical Mismatch with Flow Matching Architecture:** FLUX.1.dev is built on Flow Matching / Rectified Flow, not standard variance-preserving diffusion. Equations 3-4 assume $x_t = \alpha_t x_0 + \sigma_t \epsilon$, which does not hold for flow ODE trajectories. The paper does not adapt the interpolation or gradient derivation to the flow matching framework, raising concerns about the mathematical correctness of the single-step recovery and its gradient properties.
2. **Ambiguous Evaluation Claims & Metric Discrepancies:** The claim of a "3.7-fold increase in realism" is poorly justified. Human evaluation uses a 4-level ordinal scale (Table 2), but the conversion to a "fold increase" is undefined. Additionally, Table 1 shows SRPO underperforms Direct-Align on ImageReward (1.118 vs 1.223), GenEval, and DeQA, which contradicts the "SOTA across metrics" narrative and warrants transparent discussion.
3. **Under-Specified Key Components & Shallow Ablations:** The inversion-based regularization (Sec 3.2, Eq. 9) introduces a constant $\mathbf{K}$ and a parameter $\Delta \sigma$ without clear definitions, derivation, or gradient flow explanation. The timestep ablation relies on a small-scale human judgment task (n=200, "mark worst as hacked") rather than automated metrics or statistical testing, weakening the empirical proof against late-timestep overoptimization.
4. **Missing Implementation Details & Baseline Fairness:** Critical hyperparameters (discount schedule $\lambda(t)$, number of noise injections $k$, exact control word pairs, $\Delta \sigma$ values, and learning rates) are absent from the main text and relegated to unreferenced supplementary material. It is also unclear if ReFL and DRaFT baselines were optimized to convergence or matched for compute budget, making the 10-minute efficiency claim potentially misleading.

### Novelty & Significance
The paper offers moderate-to-high novelty in its reward design. Formulating preference optimization as a relative difference between prompt-conditioned text embeddings is an elegant and practical approach to online reward steering, avoiding costly reward model fine-tuning. Direct-Align's single-step trajectory recovery is conceptually appealing, though its mathematical grounding requires architectural adaptation to gain full credibility. The work's significance is high for applied generative alignment, as it provides a fast, compute-efficient alternative to policy-gradient methods (e.g., PPO/GRPO) with tangible improvements in human-perceived quality. However, for ICLR acceptance, the theoretical rigor and evaluation transparency currently fall short of the conference's standards for mathematical soundness and comprehensive benchmarking.

### Suggestions for Improvement
1. **Align Mathematical Formulation with Flow Matching:** Explicitly adapt Eqs. 3-4 to the Flow Matching trajectory ($x_t = (1-t)x_0 + t \epsilon$ or equivalent). Provide the correct Jacobian/gradient derivation for the single-step recovery under FLUX's ODE solver, or empirically validate the approximation's accuracy with gradient norm analysis across timesteps.
2. **Clarify Metrics & Address Discrepancies:** Define exactly how the "fold increase" is calculated from the ordinal human evaluation scale (e.g., odds ratio or weighted score conversion). Add confidence intervals to human evaluation bars (Fig. 4) and openly discuss the drops in ImageReward and GenEval/DeQA, framing SRPO's trade-offs honestly.
3. **Fully Define & Justify Regularization Components:** Rewrite Sec 3.2 to clearly define $\mathbf{K}$, $\Delta \sigma$, and $\lambda(t)$. Provide a step-by-step gradient derivation for the inversion-based regularization (Eq. 9) and include pseudocode for the full training loop to ensure reproducibility.
4. **Strengthen Baselines & Run Quantitative Ablations:** Train ReFL and DRaFT to convergence under identical compute budgets before claiming efficiency gains. Replace the qualitative timestep ablation with a quantitative plot (e.g., reward score vs. overexposure/smoothness metric across Early/Late/All intervals) and test Direct-Align on a standard diffusion backbone (e.g., SDXL or DiT) to prove generalizability beyond flow models.

### Deeper Analysis Needed (top 3-5 only)
1. Rigorously analyze the gradient flow of $\epsilon_{gt}$ in Eqs. 4–5 and prove it does not collapse the RL objective into supervised image reconstruction, because if the injected noise is static or bypasses the model's learned parameters, the method ceases to be reinforcement learning.
2. Provide statistical validation (bootstrapped confidence intervals, significance tests, or pairwise p-values) for the human evaluation results, because claiming a 3.7× realism improvement without variance estimates or inter-annotator agreement scores leaves the core quantitative result highly suspect.
3. Quantify performance degradation when user prompts already contain style/aesthetic descriptors that conflict with the hardcoded $C_1/C_2$ prefixes, because the heavy reliance on prompt augmentation severely limits general utility and must be explicitly scoped.

### Visualizations & Case Studies
1. Plot the training curves for *all* tracked metrics (HPS, Aesthetic, PickScore, and a saturation/edge-artifact heuristic) side-by-side to visually demonstrate that the relative reward actually prevents hacking rather than just trading off competing score dimensions.
2. Visualize gradient norms or training instability across early, middle, and late timesteps to empirically prove that Direct-Align resolves the gradient explosion/collapse that supposedly restricts prior methods to late-stage sampling.
3. Show explicit trios of images where only the semantic pair shifts (e.g., $C_1$="Realistic", $C_1$="Painting", $C_2$="CG Render"), directly illustrating which visual attributes are preserved versus penalized by the embedding difference mechanism.

### Obvious Next Steps
1. Integrate SRPO into a standard policy-gradient framework (PPO or vanilla GRPO), because demonstrating that your reward formulation improves alignment beyond your proprietary Direct-Align recovery pipeline is essential for broader methodological contribution.
2. Convert the qualitative claim about the decaying discount $\lambda(t)$ into a controlled ablation table, as the paper currently asserts its importance for mitigating hacking but provides no quantitative evidence that removing or altering it degrades performance.
3. Formally derive why optimizing $f_{img}(x)^\top(C_1 - C_2)$ neutralizes reward model biases better than standard KL regularization or multi-reward averaging, because the current presentation lacks the theoretical grounding required to justify SRPO as a principled RL objective rather than a prompt-hacking heuristic.

# Final Consolidated Review
## Summary
This paper introduces an online reinforcement learning framework for aligning text-to-image models with human preferences. It proposes Direct-Align, which bypasses costly multistep backpropagation by analytically recovering clean images from injected noise priors across the generation trajectory, and Semantic Relative Preference Optimization (SRPO), which formulates rewards as text-conditioned differences between positive and negative prompt embeddings. Applied to FLUX.1.dev, the method demonstrates substantial improvements in human-evaluated realism and aesthetic quality while achieving high training efficiency and robustness across standard reward models.

## Strengths
- **Practical & Compute-Efficient Alignment Pipeline:** By analytically recovering images from intermediate states rather than unrolling full samplers, Direct-Align stabilizes gradient flow across early timesteps. The reported training budget (~5.3 total H20 GPU-hours) yields a >75× speedup over policy-gradient baselines like DanceGRPO while avoiding late-timestep reward overfitting.
- **Effective Relative Preference Formulation:** The semantic-relational mechanism ($C_1 - C_2$) elegantly steers optimization direction without costly reward model fine-tuning. Cross-reward experiments (Appendix A) confirm that this contrastive signal mitigates static reward biases (e.g., oversaturation, edge glossiness) and generalizes across HPSv2, PickScore, and CLIP-based evaluators.
- **Tangible Gains in Human-Perceived Quality:** Human evaluations report a substantial jump in realism and aesthetic quality, with qualitative comparisons showing marked reductions in common online-RL artifacts. The prompt-augmented control mechanism also demonstrates flexible, training-free style steering at inference.

## Weaknesses
- **Metric Discrepancies & Ambiguous "Fold Increase" Claims:** Table 1 shows SRPO underperforming Direct-Align on ImageReward (1.118 vs 1.223), GenEval, and DeQA, contradicting the narrative of uniform SOTA performance. Furthermore, the claim of a "3.7-fold increase in realism" is derived from a 4-level ordinal scale (8.2% to 38.9% Excellent), but the conversion methodology (e.g., weighted scoring vs. odds ratio) is undefined. These gaps obscure the true trade-offs between aesthetic steering and prompt/object fidelity.
- **Theoretical Mismatch & Under-Specified Optimization Mechanics:** FLUX.1.dev utilizes Flow Matching / Rectified Flow trajectories, yet the single-step recovery relies on DDPM-style notation ($x_t = \alpha_t x_0 + \sigma_t \epsilon_{gt}$) without explicit mapping to flow ODE solvers. Additionally, key components ($\Delta \sigma$, the decaying discount $\lambda(t)$, and constant $\mathbf{K}$ in Eq. 9) lack precise definitions, scheduling rules, or gradient derivations in the main text, hindering reproducibility and theoretical verification of gradient flow.
- **SRPO Linearity Assumption for Non-Linear Reward Heads:** The method assumes $r_{SRP}(x) \propto f_{img}(x)^T \cdot (C_1 - C_2)$, which strictly holds only for linear similarity metrics. Modern preference models like HPSv2 and ImageReward employ non-linear projection heads and cross-attention layers, meaning $r(x, C_1) - r(x, C_2)$ does not cleanly decouple semantic preference from image-encoder biases. While the approach works empirically, treating it as a strict algebraic isolation overstates its theoretical grounding and requires clarification as a working heuristic.

## Nice-to-Haves
- **Statistical Treatment of Human Evaluations:** Reporting confidence intervals, inter-annotator agreement (e.g., Fleiss’ kappa), or bootstrapped significance tests would strengthen the claimed realism improvements.
- **Ablation of the Negative Prompt Term ($C_2$):** Isolating the contribution of the explicit contrastive term against a static/null penalty would clarify whether the relative formulation actively regularizes optimization or primarily scales gradient magnitudes.
- **Full Training Curves:** Plotting reward, aesthetic, and artifact-detection metrics over optimization steps would visually confirm that hacking is prevented throughout training rather than traded off late in the process.
- **Explicit Baseline Compute Matching:** Clarifying the exact step counts, batch sizes, and learning rate schedules used for the ReFL/DRaFT re-implementations would fully validate the 75× efficiency claim under identical compute budgets.

## Novel Insights
The paper reframes reward hacking mitigation from an optimization-stability or heavy-regulation problem into a semantic contrast problem. By constructing reward signals as the difference between positively and negatively augmented prompt embeddings, SRPO effectively factors out static image-encoder biases and isolates the optimization direction toward targeted attributes. This decouples alignment from the need to fine-tune reward models or rely on fragile KL penalties against a reference policy, offering a lightweight, online-controllable steering mechanism that scales naturally with prompt vocabulary.

## Suggestions
- Provide a concise training pseudocode summarizing the forward pass, noise injection schedule, hybrid reconstruction, reward aggregation with $\lambda(t)$, and backward pass. This will clarify gradient flow, resolve ambiguity around $\Delta \sigma$ and $\mathbf{K}$, and improve reproducibility.
- Explicitly map the interpolation/recovery equations to FLUX's Flow Matching formulation (or empirically validate gradient stability across timesteps under the FM solver). Briefly discuss the linearity assumption for SRPO, framing it as an empirically effective contrastive heuristic rather than a strict mathematical decomposition for non-linear reward heads.
- Report the exact methodology used to compute the "3.7x/3.1x fold increase" from the ordinal human evaluation scale. Add a transparent discussion of why ImageReward, GenEval, and DeQA plateau or slightly regress, and clarify what specific trade-offs SRPO introduces relative to Direct-Align.
