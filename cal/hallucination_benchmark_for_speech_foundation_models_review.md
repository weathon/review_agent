# Harsh Critic Review
## Section-by-Section Critical Review

### Title & Abstract
- **Title accuracy:** The title (implied as SHALLOW) and the framing accurately reflect a benchmark for diagnosing ASR hallucination types beyond WER.
- **Abstract clarity:** Clearly states the problem (WER's inability to distinguish semantic/phonetic distortions), the method (four-dimensional taxonomy with weighted metrics), and key results (metrics co-vary with WER at low error rates but decouple under degradation, revealing architecture-specific trade-offs).
- **Unsupported claims:** The claim that this is the "first comprehensive benchmark" is marginally strong given recent concurrent work (e.g., Atwany et al., 2025; AssemblyAI, 2024), though the authors do cite them. The abstract adequately reflects the paper's content without overpromising, but it slightly oversimplifies the metric design by implying a fully independent decomposition, which the later correlation analysis (Figure 3) complicates.

### Introduction & Motivation
- **Problem motivation & gap:** Strong and well-articulated. The distinction between text/image hallucinations (external factuality) and ASR hallucinations (signal fidelity vs. linguistic priors) is a necessary and valuable framing. The critique of WER's equal penalty for semantically benign vs. harmful errors is well-motivated with the medical example.
- **Contributions accuracy:** Clearly listed and map directly to Sections 3–5. However, contribution (3) claims SHALLOW reveals error structure that WER cannot "particularly in acoustically challenging environments." While shown empirically, this is somewhat expected: any metric sensitive to semantic/phonetic distortion will naturally diverge from WER as signal quality drops. The deeper contribution (diagnostic granularity across architectures) is better realized but slightly under-emphasized in the intro.
- **Over/under-claiming:** The introduction over-claims architectural attribution by stating "Hallucination behaviors are not uniformly distributed but rather reflect fundamental architectural design choices." The models compared differ drastically in training data scale, parameter counts (300M to 10.7B), and optimization objectives. Attributing score differences primarily to "architecture" rather than "training regime/scale/data" is confounded and should be tempered.

### Method / Approach
- **Clarity & reproducibility:** The four dimensions are clearly defined. Equations 1, 2, 3, 4, 5, 6, and 7, along with the appendices, provide implementable formulas. The decision to report four separate scores instead of a single aggregate is scientifically sound for diagnostic purposes.
- **Assumptions & justification:** The most significant weakness lies in the weighting schemes (e.g., `0.5·ri + 0.3·rs + 0.2·rd` for LF; `0.4·SD + 0.6·GE` for ME; `0.25·LS + 0.75·GS` for SE). The paper states these weights are "empirically validated" or reflect "preliminary experiments" (Appendix B), but provides no sensitivity analysis, correlation with human severity ratings, or theoretical grounding. These constants directly dictate benchmark outcomes and risk introducing evaluation bias.
- **Logical gaps & edge cases:**
  - **Paraphrase vs. Hallucination:** The semantic (SE) and morphological (ME) metrics are heavily reference-dependent. In real speech, multiple valid paraphrases exist. If a model outputs a semantically equivalent but structurally different sentence, SHALLOW's dependency-parsing (ME) and embedding-distance (SE) will penalize it, potentially flagging valid transcripts as hallucinations. The synthetic "WER-only" category (Table 1) minimizes this via controlled generation, but real-world speech contains natural variation that these metrics do not robustly handle.
  - **Phonetic modeling (Eq 2):** Using Metaphone + averaged Hamming/Levenshtein/Jaro-Winkler distances is computationally efficient but phonologically shallow. Metaphone was designed for English name indexing and often collapses distinct phonemes or treats dialectal variations as identical. It may not reliably capture the nuanced acoustic-phonetic hallucinations the benchmark aims to measure.
  - **NLI penalty in SE (Eq 6 & Sec 3.4):** Assigning 0.0 to "contradiction" and 0.5 to "neutral" heavily skews SE scores towards NLI model behavior. Pretrained NLI models (e.g., BART-large-mnli) are optimized on SNLI/MNLI datasets, which do not align well with clinical or conversational speech. A hypothesis that is tangentially related (neutral) but factually consistent with the audio will receive a 50% penalty, which may not reflect true hallucination severity.
- **Theoretical soundness:** The derivations are operational rather than theoretical, which is acceptable for engineering benchmarks, but the lack of human-validation alignment for the composite scores leaves a gap in construct validity.

### Experiments & Results
- **Claim testing:** The experiments directly test the claim that SHALLOW provides orthogonal diagnostics across architectures and domains. The medical case study (Table 3) effectively demonstrates practical utility for high-stakes errors masked by WER.
- **Baselines & fairness:** WER is appropriately used as the primary baseline. However, the paper lacks a human correlation study. For a novel evaluation framework, ICLR expects at least a moderate-scale human annotation study showing that SHALLOW scores correlate more strongly with human judgments of "hallucination severity" or "harm" than WER does. Relying solely on synthetic isolation and real-world demonstration is insufficient to validate the metric's alignment with human perception.
- **Missing ablations/statistics:** 
  - No error bars, confidence intervals, or statistical significance tests (e.g., bootstrap resampling) are reported for Table 2 or the dataset breakdowns. Given the high variance in speech datasets, it is unclear if differences (e.g., SE of 14.37 vs 12.71 for Q2A vs Q2.5O) are statistically meaningful.
  - Table 2 presents corpus-averaged scores. This obscures dataset-specific variance. While Appendix D provides breakdowns, the main table should reflect variance or be supplemented with statistical rigor.
- **Cherry-picking vs. support:** The correlation analysis (Figure 3 / Appendix E) supports the claim that SHALLOW metrics decouple from WER at high error rates. However, at low WER (10–30%), correlations exceed 0.80, raising the question: does SHALLEL offer meaningful diagnostic value for state-of-the-art models operating in their intended domains, or only in failure modes? This nuance should be addressed.
- **Dataset & metric appropriateness:** Dataset selection is excellent (covers noise, accents, children, medical). However, applying English-specific NLI, dependency parsing, and Metaphone to heavily accented datasets (e.g., CORAAL, SpeechOcean) introduces evaluation bias. The metrics may penalize dialectal grammar or pronunciation variants that are linguistically valid but mismatch the tool's training distribution.

### Writing & Clarity
- **Clarity of explanations:** The paper is well-structured and generally easy to follow. The distinction between local and global semantic errors is clearly explained. Appendix B's synthetic dataset construction methodology is transparent.
- **Figures & tables:** Table 1 effectively illustrates metric behavior on synthetic cases. Figure 2 (t-SNE) clearly shows metric separability. However, Figure 3 (correlation line plot) and Figure 7/Appendix E (correlation heatmap) present nearly identical analyses. In the parsed text, they appear duplicated or fragmented. I will disregard parser fragmentation but note that the paper should consolidate the correlation visualization to avoid redundancy.
- **Confusing elements:** The labeling of the "Phonetic Fabrication" metric is conceptually inverted relative to typical evaluation intuition: lower scores indicate better phonetic matching, while higher scores indicate divergence. This is correct mathematically but requires explicit guidance for practitioners to interpret correctly, which is briefly mentioned in B.5 but could be clearer in Section 3.2. Grammar and formatting are not an issue; the mathematical notation is readable despite minor OCR artifacts.

### Limitations & Broader Impact
- **Acknowledged limitations:** The authors correctly note the English-only constraint, the subjectivity of weight assignments, and the dependency on language-specific NLP tools.
- **Missed limitations:**
  - **Computational overhead & dependency chain:** SHALLOW requires dependency parsing (spaCy), grammar checking (LanguageTool API), sentence embeddings, and NLI models. Appendix F acknowledges runtime cost (~90 min for LibriSpeech), but does not discuss the fragility of relying on third-party APIs and models that may change, be deprecated, or exhibit version-dependent behavior. This impacts long-term reproducibility and benchmark stability.
  - **Paraphrase tolerance:** As noted in the Method section, the inability to distinguish acceptable paraphrases from semantic hallucinations is a fundamental limitation of reference-dependent embedding/NLI metrics that is not fully discussed.
  - **Dialectal bias in NLP pipelines:** The use of standard English NLI and morphological parsers on AAVE or non-native speech (CORAAL, SpeechOcean) may artificially inflate ME and SE scores due to linguistic mismatch rather than true ASR failure. This directly intersects with the ethics statement about fair benchmarking.
- **Broader impact & ethics:** The ethics statement is thoughtful, particularly the warning against misinterpreting high error rates on dialectal speech as "deficiencies in speech communities." The focus on high-stakes domains is appropriate. The paper avoids overclaiming model capabilities and emphasizes diagnostic use.

### Overall Assessment
This paper addresses a genuinely important gap in ASR evaluation: WER's inability to distinguish between acoustically motivated errors and dangerous semantic/phonetic hallucinations. The proposed four-dimensional taxonomy is intuitive, the experimental scope across architectures and domains is comprehensive, and the medical case study convincingly demonstrates real-world relevance. The work is well-positioned for ICLR as a diagnostic benchmark paper. However, to meet the venue's rigor standards, the authors must address three core concerns: (1) the arbitrary nature of the weighting schemes without sensitivity analysis or human-correlation validation to establish construct validity; (2) the conflation of architectural paradigms with disparate training scales and data regimes in the analysis, which weakens claims about architectural trade-offs; and (3) the lack of robust mechanisms to handle valid paraphrases vs. semantic distortions in reference-dependent metrics, alongside the potential bias introduced by applying standard English NLP pipelines to accented/dialectal data. With a human validation study, weight justification/ablation, and tempered architectural claims, this would be a strong, lasting contribution to the speech evaluation literature. As is, the methodology is useful but lacks the empirical grounding expected for a novel evaluation framework at this venue.

# Neutral Reviewer
## Balanced Review

### Summary
This paper introduces SHALLOW, a multi-dimensional benchmark framework for diagnosing and quantifying hallucinations in Automatic Speech Recognition (ASR) systems. Unlike Word Error Rate (WER), which aggregates all transcription errors, SHALLOW decomposes ASR failures into four linguistically grounded dimensions: Lexical, Phonetic, Morphological, and Semantic errors, each computed via targeted, compositionally weighted metrics. The authors validate the framework using a GPT-4o-curated synthetic dataset, analyze twelve state-of-the-art ASR models across ten diverse speech corpora, and demonstrate that SHALLOW metrics reveal architectural trade-offs and high-severity errors that WER obscures, particularly in noisy, accented, and medical contexts.

### Strengths
1. **Clear Conceptual Differentiation & Taxonomy:** The paper correctly identifies that ASR hallucinations are fundamentally about *acoustic fidelity* rather than external factuality, distinguishing them from LLM or vision-language hallucinations. The four-dimension taxonomy (lexical, phonetic, morphological, semantic) is well-motivated, linguistically grounded, and directly addresses known failure modes like polarity flips or phonetically plausible substitutions (Table 3, Sec 1-3).
2. **Comprehensive Empirical Validation:** The evaluation covers a broad model family (encoder-only, encoder-decoder, transducer, SpeechLLMs) and ten datasets spanning standard, noisy, accented, child, and clinical speech. The correlation analysis showing that SHALLOW metrics decouple from WER as recognition degrades (Fig 3, Sec 5) provides strong empirical support for the framework's diagnostic value beyond high-performance regimes.
3. **Strong Reproducibility & Practical Utility:** The authors release code, a synthetic validation dataset, and provide detailed implementation specifics (libraries, edge-case handling, parsing/embs used). The medical case study concretely demonstrates how SHALLOW flags clinically critical errors (e.g., "cannot rotate my neck" → "can rotate my neck") that yield deceptively low WER, aligning well with ICLR's emphasis on AI safety and real-world impact.

### Weaknesses
1. **Heuristic Weight Selection Without Rigorous Ablation:** The composite metrics rely on fixed weights (e.g., LF: 0.5/0.3/0.2; ME: 0.4/0.6; SE: 0.25/0.75) that the authors state reflect "empirical observations" but are only validated against the synthetic dataset. There is no ablation study or sensitivity analysis showing how alternative weight configurations affect downstream model ranking or human alignment, making the absolute scores somewhat arbitrary.
2. **Limited Direct Human Correlation & Real-World Error Fidelity:** While the paper claims the balanced weighting "correlates more strongly with human judgments" (Sec 3.4), no human study or correlation coefficient is reported. Furthermore, the synthetic dataset (1,050 pairs) is GPT-4o-generated and may not fully capture the entangled, context-dependent nature of real ASR hallucinations, limiting claims about metric orthogonality in production settings.
3. **Dependency-Heavy Implementation & Scope Constraints:** The framework requires multiple external NLP models and parsers (spaCy dependency graphs, LanguageTool API, BERT/RoBERTa embeddings, NLI models for coherence). This creates a non-trivial dependency stack that may hinder adoption in lightweight or non-English pipelines. The English-only limitation and the explicit lack of a single aggregate score (Sec 3.5), while defensible, reduce immediate comparability to standard benchmarking practices.

### Novelty & Significance
The novelty is high for the speech and multimodal ML communities. While WER alternatives and semantic metrics exist, SHALLOW is the first to systematically unify lexical, phonetic, morphological, and semantic hallucination channels into a single diagnostic benchmark tailored to ASR's signal-to-text nature. The significance is substantial: as SpeechLLMs proliferate, the trade-off between linguistic fluency and acoustic grounding becomes acute, and WER is increasingly inadequate for safety-critical deployment. This work directly aligns with ICLR's focus on robust evaluation, model transparency, and AI alignment, offering researchers a practical lens to dissect failure modes, guide architecture selection, and potentially inform reward modeling or fine-tuning objectives.

### Suggestions for Improvement
1. **Add Weight Sensitivity & Human Alignment Analysis:** Include an ablation table showing how metric rankings shift under alternative weight schemes. Additionally, conduct a small-scale human evaluation (e.g., rating semantic severity or acoustic fidelity on 200-300 samples) and report Spearman/Pearson correlations between human scores and each SHALLOW dimension to empirically ground the weighting choices.
2. **Bridge Evaluation to Model Improvement:** ICLR reviewers highly value metrics that inform training. Discuss or experiment with how SHALLOW components could be integrated into ASR development (e.g., as a reward signal for DPO/RLHF, a filtering criterion for training data, or a diagnostic early-stopping metric). Even a preliminary fine-tuning or data-selection experiment would greatly strengthen the practical impact claims.
3. **Clarify Synthetic-to-Real Distribution Alignment:** Analyze how the metric distributions on the synthetic dataset compare to real ASR outputs across the 10 corpora. Including a density plot or statistical test (e.g., KL divergence or Wasserstein distance) between synthetic and real error profiles would strengthen confidence that the framework generalizes beyond controlled perturbations.
4. **Streamline Dependency Documentation & Multilingual Pathway:** Provide a minimal, containerized setup script in the repository to ease reproduction. Briefly outline the exact steps required to adapt SE/ME to other languages (e.g., which parsers, language-specific embeddings, or NLI models would replace the current stack) to clarify the framework's extensibility beyond English.

# Spark Finder Review
## How to Improve This Paper

### Missing Experiments (top 3-5 only)
1. **Human Correlation Study on Hallucination Severity:** Evaluate SHALLOW scores against a dataset with human-annotated hallucination types and severity ratings. Without human correlation, claims that metrics capture "dangerous semantic flips" or "harm" remain speculative and lack empirical grounding.
2. **Weight Sensitivity & Component Ablation:** Systematically vary the fixed weights in composite scores (LF, ME, SE) to demonstrate robustness. If scores fluctuate wildly with minor weight changes, the dimensional interpretation is an artifact of arbitrary tuning rather than a stable metric property.
3. **Direct Comparison to Prior Hallucination Baselines:** Run SHALLOW head-to-head against existing metrics like Semantic Distance, Bag of Hallucinations, or LLM-based classifiers on identical model/dataset pairs. The paper cannot claim to be the first comprehensive framework without empirically proving superior or distinct diagnostic capability over recent specialized baselines.
4. **Control for Training Data Scale vs. Architecture:** Re-evaluate or statistically control for model parameter counts and training data size across architectural families. The observed trade-offs between encoder-decoders and SpeechLLMs may stem from massive data scale differences rather than architectural design, undermining the core architectural analysis.

### Deeper Analysis Needed (top 3-5 only)
1. **Impact of Decoding Hyperparameters and Prompting:** Analyze how temperature, repetition penalties, and system prompts shift SHALLOW scores in decoder-only models. Without controlling for these factors, claimed architectural behaviors may simply reflect prompt engineering artifacts or sampling strategies.
2. **False-Positive Rate on Valid Paraphrasing:** Quantify how often SHALLOW penalizes semantically equivalent paraphrases or stylistic variations common in natural speech. If the Semantic Error score flags acceptable linguistic variation as hallucination, the metric’s diagnostic utility collapses in real-world deployment.
3. **Mechanistic Analysis of WER-Hallucination Decoupling:** Investigate the internal model states (e.g., token entropy, attention collapse, or confidence scores) when metrics decouple from WER at high thresholds. Understanding whether decoupling reflects unconstrained generation or feature degradation is necessary to validate the failure-mode claims.

### Visualizations & Case Studies
1. **Real-Data Distribution Plots (Violin/Box):** Replace aggregate mean tables with violin or box plots of SHALLOW scores across all real datasets. Averages hide variance and multimodality; distributions are required to prove the metrics capture meaningful diagnostic spread rather than just mean shifts.
2. **Synthetic-to-Real Metric Projection:** Project SHALLOW score vectors from actual ASR outputs onto the synthetic t-SNE space to demonstrate that real-world errors map cleanly to the isolated dimensions. This validates that the synthetic benchmark dimensions transfer meaningfully to chaotic, real model outputs.
3. **Counter-Example Case Studies:** Present 3–5 examples where SHALLOW metrics produce counter-intuitive or "wrong" scores compared to human judgment. Exposing boundary conditions and failure modes builds credibility and prevents overclaiming metric infallibility.

### Obvious Next Steps
1. **Actionability Demonstration (Intervention Study):** Show that SHALLOW scores can drive improvements by demonstrating a specific intervention (e.g., prompt adjustment, temperature tuning, or targeted fine-tuning) that reduces a targeted dimension like SE or PF without degrading overall performance. Diagnostic claims require evidence of utility beyond observation.
2. **Standardized Evaluation Harness Documentation:** Include a concrete CLI/API specification in the main text that takes audio, reference, and hypothesis to return the four scores. For a benchmark to be adopted in the ICLR ecosystem, the execution barrier must be explicit and minimal beyond a repository link.
3. **Vocabulary/Domain Constraint Quantification:** Evaluate how domain shift (e.g., legal or biomedical jargon) affects the NLP-dependent metrics (dependency parsing, semantic embeddings). Quantifying score degradation due to out-of-vocabulary terms is essential to define the framework’s operational boundaries.

# Final Consolidated Review
## Summary
SHALLOW introduces a four-dimensional benchmark framework (Lexical, Phonetic, Morphological, Semantic) to diagnose hallucination types in Automatic Speech Recognition systems, moving beyond the aggregate limitations of Word Error Rate. It combines computable linguistic metrics with a synthetic validation dataset and evaluates twelve models across ten diverse speech corpora, demonstrating that hallucination patterns vary by architectural design and systematically decouple from WER as acoustic recognition degrades.

## Strengths
- **Precise problem framing and taxonomy:** The paper correctly distinguishes ASR hallucinations (deviation from acoustic ground truth) from LLM/VLM hallucinations (factual or prompt inconsistencies). The four-dimension taxonomy directly targets high-risk failure modes like semantic polarity flips and phonetically plausible substitutions, which are critical in deployment.
- **Comprehensive, reproducible evaluation:** The empirical study spans a wide spectrum of architectures and challenging acoustic/linguistic domains, backed by fully open-sourced code, explicit implementation details, and a synthetic stress-testing dataset. The modular design and thorough documentation lower the barrier for community adoption.
- **Strong practical validation in high-stakes contexts:** The medical case study concretely demonstrates how SHALLOW flags clinically dangerous errors that yield deceptively low WER. The correlation analysis further validates the framework's diagnostic value by showing how error dimensions fracture from aggregate accuracy precisely in low-quality, out-of-distribution settings where reliability matters most.

## Weaknesses
- **Fixed metric weights lack sensitivity analysis or human alignment:** The composite scores rely on hand-tuned weights (e.g., 0.5/0.3/0.2 for LF, 0.4/0.6 for ME) justified by synthetic dataset observations and "preliminary experiments." Without ablation studies or correlation with human judgments of error severity, the absolute scores and relative dimensional prioritization lack empirical construct validity. Rankings could shift substantially under alternative weighting schemes.
- **Conflation of architecture and training scale in diagnostic claims:** The analysis attributes distinct hallucination profiles primarily to "fundamental architectural design choices," yet the compared models vary drastically in parameter counts, training data volume, and optimization objectives (e.g., weakly supervised encoders vs. instruction-tuned SpeechLLMs). This confounding variable weakens causal claims about architectural trade-offs versus scale/data effects.
- **Evaluation pipeline bias in diverse linguistic domains:** Morphological and Semantic metrics depend heavily on standard English NLP tools (dependency parsers, grammar checkers, NLI models). When applied to dialectal (e.g., CORAAL) or non-native speech, these tools may penalize linguistically valid grammatical structures or pronunciation variants as hallucinations, conflating ASR failures with evaluation-tool mismatch.
- **Missing empirical comparison to prior specialized baselines:** While positioned as a comprehensive benchmark, the paper does not empirically compare SHALLOW's diagnostic outputs against existing hallucination or semantic metrics (e.g., semantic distance, LLM-based error classifiers) on identical model-dataset pairs. This leaves the incremental diagnostic value over recent baselines partially unverified.

## Nice-to-Haves
- Report confidence intervals or bootstrap statistical tests for aggregate scores across datasets to clarify whether differences between similarly scoring models (e.g., competing SpeechLLMs) are robust to sample variance.
- Provide a minimal, containerized implementation environment to mitigate dependency brittleness (e.g., API drift, parser version updates) and explicitly document the substitution pathway for adapting ME/SE components to non-English languages.
- Analyze how decoding hyperparameters (temperature, repetition penalties, system prompts) influence SHALLOW scores in decoder-only models to disentangle architectural behavior from sampling artifacts.

## Novel Insights
None beyond the paper's own contributions.

## Suggestions
- Conduct a weight sensitivity analysis and validate the composite scores against a human-annotated dataset (rating hallucination severity/acoustic fidelity) to establish construct validity and ensure metric rankings are not artifacts of arbitrary parameter choices.
- Control for model scale and training data when making architectural claims, either by grouping comparisons within similar size regimes or explicitly acknowledging scale as a confounding variable in the diagnostic analysis.
- Quantify the false-positive rate on valid paraphrases and dialectal variations by running controlled tests against linguistically diverse subsets of CORAAL or GLOBE-v2, ensuring the NLP pipelines measure ASR failures rather than penalizing structural or grammatical diversity.
- Benchmark SHALLOW head-to-head against recent LLM-based and semantic-distance hallucination detectors to empirically demonstrate its comparative diagnostic granularity and justify its claim as a comprehensive, unified framework.
