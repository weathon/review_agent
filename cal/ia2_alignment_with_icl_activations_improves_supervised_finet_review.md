# Harsh Critic Review
## Section-by-Section Critical Review

### Title & Abstract
- Does the title accurately reflect the contribution? Yes, it clearly states the method (IA2: alignment with ICL activations) and the claimed outcome (improves SFT).
- Does the abstract clearly state the problem, method, and key results? Yes. It contrasts SFT and ICL, identifies the gap (distinct activation patterns), introduces IA2 as a self-distillation priming step, and reports broad empirical gains.
- Are any claims in the abstract unsupported by the paper? The claim "ICL can offer better generalizability and more calibrated responses compared to SFT in data scarce settings" is slightly generalized; the paper later shows ICL does not consistently beat trained models (e.g., on math datasets with Qwen), and calibration comparisons are omitted for multi-token tasks. However, the abstract frames this as a general motivation rather than a universal rule, so it is acceptable but could be nuanced.

### Introduction & Motivation
- Is the problem well-motivated? Is the gap in prior work clearly identified? Yes. The practical tension between ICL (data-efficient, calibrated, but compute-heavy) and SFT (parameter-efficient, but prone to overfitting/miscalibration in low-data) is well-established. The gap is precisely identified: prior distillation methods rely on output tokens, ignoring the rich functional signals in ICL internal computations.
- Are the contributions clearly stated and accurate? Yes, the three bullet points map directly to the paper's sections (§3, §4, §5) and accurately reflect the work.
- Does the introduction over-claim or under-sell? The introduction is reasonably calibrated. It avoids claiming IA2 replaces ICL or solves catastrophic forgetting, framing IA2 as a "priming step" to improve SFT quality. It correctly notes that ICL activations contain "rich information," though the introduction would benefit from a clearer definition of what "rich" means in this context (e.g., calibration signals, reasoning circuits, or mere output likelihood smoothing).

### Method / Approach
- Is the method clearly described and reproducible? The pipeline is clearly laid out: collect ICL activations, train LoRA to minimize MSE between current activations and ICL targets, then switch to standard SFT. Appendix details LoRA settings, LR sweeps, and stopping criteria. Reproducibility appears feasible.
- Are key assumptions stated and justified? The core assumption is that aligning activation norms via MSE will transfer ICL's "functional behavior" (calibration, generalization) to the SFT model. This is justified empirically but lacks theoretical grounding. It assumes that hidden state similarity correlates with functional similarity across contexts, which is not guaranteed in non-linear transformers.
- Are there logical gaps in the derivation or reasoning? 
  1. **Context Mismatch:** During ICL, the self-attention mechanism processes demonstrations $[I \circ X]$. During IA2 training, the model processes only $[X \circ \hat{Y}]$. The method hopes the model will learn to "simulate" the demo influence purely through weight changes so that $SA(X; \tilde{W}) \approx SA(I \circ X; W)$. This is a massive compression objective. Aligning activations generated from completely different attention distributions (with vs. without demos) may be unstable or capture superficial statistical regularities rather than true computation.
  2. **Token Mismatch & Shape Handling:** Equation 4 applies MSE over activation tensors. If the model's self-generated response $\hat{Y}$ differs in length or content from the ICL response used to collect $A_{ICL}$, how are shapes matched? The paper mentions fixing $G=200$ for multi-token, but does not clarify padding/truncation strategies or whether the loss is masked. Misaligned tokens could introduce noisy gradients that harm rather than help.
- Are there edge cases or failure modes not discussed? The paper notes that extreme alignment can hurt if ICL overfits. However, it does not discuss the case where the base model generates highly hallucinated or format-violating responses during the IA2 collection/generation phase. If $\hat{Y}$ is poor, the target activations $A_{ICL}$ (which conditioned on good demos) and the source activations $A$ (conditioned on a bad $\hat{Y}$) may misalign catastrophically, potentially pushing weights into degenerate regions.

### Experiments & Results
- Do the experiments actually test the paper's claims? Yes. The authors test IA2 vs. SFT vs. ICL across 12 datasets, few-shot regimes ($N=2$ to $128$), and two model families. They measure both accuracy and calibration where appropriate.
- Are baselines appropriate and fairly compared? SFT-only and ICL are standard baselines. The addition of IA2-only, IA2+SFT, and Knowledge Distillation (soft labels) strengthens the comparison. All methods use the exact same data splits and random seeds, which is strong practice.
- Are there missing ablations that would materially change conclusions?
  1. **Compute/Step Parity:** IA2→SFT involves two sequential convergence phases. SFT-only involves one. It is unclear if the gains come from the *activation signal* or simply a more favorable optimization trajectory / longer effective training. A control matching the total number of update steps or epochs between IA2→SFT and SFT-only is necessary to rule out optimization artifacts.
  2. **Model Scale:** The use of Llama-3.2-1B for multi-token reasoning tasks (GSM8K, SciQ) yields very low absolute performance (e.g., ~14-37% accuracy). Gains observed on such weak baselines may reflect learning output formatting rather than genuine reasoning improvement. Scaling to 7B/14B+ models is critical to demonstrate that IA2's benefits persist when the base model has strong inherent capabilities.
  3. **Layer/Module Selectivity:** Aligning all attention layers may be suboptimal or noisy. The paper briefly mentions this in "Potential improvements" but lacks an ablation showing that full-layer alignment is indeed best or identifying which layers contribute most to the calibration gains.
- Are error bars / statistical significance reported? Yes. Results average over 5 random seeds with standard deviations. Table 8 provides paired t-tests showing most improvements are statistically significant. This meets ICLR standards.
- Do the results support the claims made, or are they cherry-picked? Tables 1-6 and Appendix tables show consistent trends favoring IA2→SFT. The paper honestly reports cases where IA2 does not beat ICL (Table 5) and where performance is sensitive to shot count. No cherry-picking detected.
- Are datasets and evaluation metrics appropriate? Datasets are standard for few-shot evaluation. Accuracy and ECE are appropriate for classification. The reliance on exact string matching for math/scientific QA is standard but can be brittle; however, the authors note this and use common parsing tricks.

### Writing & Clarity
- Are there sections that are confusing or poorly explained? Section 4's derivation of the alignment goal is clear, but the justification for using MSE on non-linear transformer activations is hand-wavy. The transition from "we want functional similarity" to "we minimize MSE of hidden states" happens quickly. A discussion of why MSE is chosen over other divergence metrics (e.g., KL, CKA, or subspace alignment losses) would improve clarity.
- Are figures and tables clear and informative? Figures 1-4 effectively communicate key trends (pipeline, similarity gaps, accuracy/ECE trade-offs, subspace overlap). Table formatting in the text is disrupted by the parser, but the content description and trends are clear. Figure 4's caption contains a garbled formula due to parsing, but the textual explanation of subspace overlap is sufficient.

### Limitations & Broader Impact
- Do the authors acknowledge the key limitations? Yes. They discuss computational overhead of activation collection, LoRA rank constraints, sensitivity to ICL demo order, and risks of extreme alignment. They also note ICL is not always superior.
- Are there fundamental limitations they missed?
  1. **Catastrophic Forgetting / Capability Erosion:** The paper explicitly aligns weights to mimic ICL behavior on a specific task. This likely creates strong task-specific specialization. The impact on zero-shot capabilities for other tasks or general language modeling perplexity is not measured. This is crucial for practical adoption.
  2. **Activation Storage Scalability:** For large models (e.g., 70B+) or large datasets, storing full activation tensors $A \in \mathbb{R}^{L \times G \times d}$ for every training sample is memory-intensive. The paper uses LoRA to save parameter memory but does not address the I/O and RAM costs of loading/aligning massive activation matrices, which may limit real-world applicability.
- Are there failure modes or negative societal impacts not discussed? Failure modes regarding poor ICL demonstrations propagating errors into weights are partially covered. Societal impact is minimal for this technical contribution, so omission is acceptable.

### Overall Assessment
This paper presents a clear, well-motivated investigation into the mechanistic differences between ICL and SFT, proposing IA2 as a practical activation-alignment priming step to improve SFT in low-data regimes. The empirical evaluation is extensive, statistically rigorous, and demonstrates consistent gains over SFT-only baselines across classification and generation tasks. The work aligns well with ICLR's interest in understanding model internals and improving training signals.

However, several concerns must be addressed to fully validate the contribution. First, the mechanism behind activation alignment via MSE is under-justified; aligning hidden states derived from fundamentally different contexts (with vs. without demonstrations) risks matching superficial statistics rather than functional circuits. Second, a critical ablation controlling for total training steps/compute is missing to ensure gains are due to the activation signal rather than a different optimization trajectory. Third, the reliance on 1B-4B models, particularly where 1B performance on reasoning tasks is near random, limits the strength of the claims; scaling to mid-range models (7B+) is needed to show IA2 enhances capable reasoning rather than just formatting. Finally, the practical costs of activation storage and potential impacts on model generalization beyond the target task require discussion.

If the authors can clarify the mechanical validity of the activation objective, provide compute-matched baselines, and discuss scalability/generalization constraints, the paper's core contribution stands as a solid, empirically grounded advancement in model adaptation. As is, it is a strong candidate for ICLR pending resolution of the mechanistic and control concerns.

# Neutral Reviewer
## Balanced Review

### Summary
This paper investigates the mechanistic differences between In-Context Learning (ICL) and Supervised Fine-Tuning (SFT), demonstrating that the two adaptation methods produce divergent internal activation patterns despite similar surface-level outputs. To bridge this gap, the authors propose IA2 (ICL Activation Alignment), a two-step priming technique that first aligns a model's intermediate self-attention activations with those generated during ICL, followed by standard SFT. Extensive experiments across 12 benchmarks, two model families, and single/multi-token tasks show that IA2 priming consistently improves low-data SFT accuracy and calibration, suggesting that injecting ICL's internal computation into weight updates yields more generalizable adaptation.

### Strengths
1. **Extensive and rigorous empirical validation:** The authors train and evaluate thousands of models across 12 datasets, varying shot counts ($N=2$ to $16$), model scales (1B–4B), and task types. The results consistently show IA2→SFT outperforming SFT-only in both accuracy and Expected Calibration Error (ECE) in low-data regimes (Tables 1, 2, Appendix C).
2. **Strong mechanistic analysis:** The paper goes beyond black-box metrics by quantifying activation similarity (Figures 2–3) and LoRA weight subspace overlap (§5.2). The observation that IA2 explores a distinct, high-performing weight subspace largely orthogonal to SFT-only training provides compelling evidence that the method captures a fundamentally different training signal.
3. **Practical utility with minimal inference overhead:** The activation collection step is offline and one-time. Once primed, the IA2→SFT model incurs identical inference latency and memory costs as standard SFT or LoRA, making it immediately deployable for parameter-efficient adaptation pipelines.
4. **Robustness across PEFT methods and training signals:** The approach seamlessly integrates with different parameter-efficient methods (LoRA, IA$^3$) and shows consistent gains even when trained on self-generated ICL response tokens rather than ground-truth labels (Table 4, §6), highlighting the richness of the activation alignment signal.

### Weaknesses
1. **Limited scalability analysis and resource reporting:** Experiments are confined to 1B–4B models. Modern open-weight LLMs (7B–8B+) exhibit qualitatively different ICL dynamics and scaling behaviors. The paper does not report the memory footprint or FLOP cost of storing and processing $L \times R \times d$ activation tensors for longer sequences, which could become a practical bottleneck for larger models or longer context windows.
2. **Narrow baseline comparisons for context distillation:** While the paper positions IA2 against standard SFT, ICL, and soft-label distillation (Table 4), it lacks direct empirical comparisons to recent context-to-weight distillation methods (e.g., Snell et al., 2022; Shen et al., 2025; Charakorn et al., 2025) that also aim to internalize demonstrations into adapters or weights. Without head-to-head evaluations under identical data/shot budgets, it is difficult to isolate whether activation matching specifically drives the gains or simply matches the broader trend of context distillation.
3. **Incomplete analysis of multi-token reasoning gaps:** Table 5 shows IA2→SFT still trails ICL on math reasoning tasks (GSM8K/HMathA). The authors hypothesize this stems from LoRA rank limitations, but provide no ablation (e.g., higher ranks, targeted layer training, or full-rank tests) to verify this claim. It remains unclear whether the gap is purely a capacity constraint or reflects a fundamental mismatch in how ICL handles long-form chain-of-thought reasoning versus weight-updated models.
4. **Subspace overlap metric lacks formal grounding:** The weight subspace overlap formula (§5.2) is presented without clear derivation or statistical justification for why a ~39% overlap correlates with functional improvement. The connection to activation similarity and downstream performance remains largely correlational rather than causal.

### Novelty & Significance
The concept of distilling ICL behavior into SFT weights is not entirely new, but focusing on intermediate self-attention activations rather than final logits or hidden states offers a fresh, mechanistically motivated perspective that aligns well with ICLR’s emphasis on understanding internal model dynamics. The paper is clearly written, logically structured, and provides thorough experimental appendices, hyperparameter sweeps, and code links, meeting ICLR’s reproducibility standards for empirical papers. The consistent gains in low-data accuracy and calibration, supported by subspace and activation analysis, make this a significant and practically useful contribution for parameter-efficient adaptation. While it falls slightly short of theoretical novelty and broader model scaling, its rigorous evaluation and actionable pipeline make it a strong candidate for acceptance, pending minor empirical expansions.

### Suggestions for Improvement
1. **Add direct comparisons to state-of-the-art context distillation baselines:** Evaluate IA2 against at least one recent method that compresses ICL demonstrations into parameters or adapters (e.g., prompt weight internalization or adapter generation) using identical datasets, shot counts, and PEFT ranks.
2. **Report compute/memory overhead and test scaling to ≥7B models:** Provide explicit metrics for activation storage size, FLOPs for the IA2 priming phase, and peak GPU memory. A single evaluation on a 7B–8B model would substantially strengthen claims of practical scalability.
3. **Ablate LoRA capacity and layer selection for reasoning tasks:** Conduct controlled experiments varying LoRA rank (e.g., 8, 16, 32) and applying IA2 only to specific middle layers (where activation misalignment is highest per Figure 2). This would clarify whether the ICL performance gap is purely a parameter bottleneck or a representational mismatch for chain-of-thought tasks.
4. **Formalize the subspace overlap analysis:** Provide a clearer mathematical derivation of the subspace metric, justify the choice of Frobenius norm formulation, and discuss how overlap quantitatively relates to functional similarity or generalization bounds. Adding a perturbation or linear probing experiment would strengthen the causal link between subspace alignment and performance gains.

# Spark Finder Review
## How to Improve This Paper

### Missing Experiments (top 3-5 only)
1. Add a standard activation distillation baseline (e.g., direct MSE or KL divergence distillation on hidden states without the specific IA2 targeting). Without it, claim that IA2 provides a unique "ICL alignment signal" is not convincing, as improvements could simply stem from conventional output-state matching.
2. Perform a training order ablation (SFT → IA2 vs. IA2 → SFT). Without testing the reverse sequence, the core claim that IA2 must act as a "priming" step to shift the model into a distinct weight subspace is not convincingly validated.
3. Validate findings on 7B–8B+ parameter models. ICLR expects scalability, and because ICL/SFT dynamics and LoRA optimization change significantly with model size, restricting experiments to 1B–4B models undermines the generalizability of the contribution.

### Deeper Analysis Needed (top 3-5 only)
1. Provide a mechanistic analysis of *why* activation alignment improves accuracy and calibration. Claiming IA2 "incentivizes ICL-like internal reasoning" is not convincing without causal probing or representational analysis showing how feature extraction or shortcut avoidance actually changes.
2. Quantify the computational and memory overhead of IA2's activation collection relative to standard SFT training. Claiming the method is "practically useful" requires a concrete FLOP/time-to-convergence comparison; if the collection cost dwarfs the marginal accuracy gains, the practical value collapses.
3. Disentangle LoRA subspace overlap from base model influence and optimization trajectories. The claim that SFT and IA2 find "orthogonal" functional solutions is not convincing without task-vector projection or linear probing to prove the learned subspaces represent genuinely different adaptation mechanisms rather than just different gradient paths.

### Visualizations & Case Studies
1. Visualize attention maps or context attribution scores (e.g., gradient-weight attention) for ICL vs. SFT vs. IA2→SFT. This would directly reveal whether IA2 truly replicates ICL's context utilization pattern or if performance gains stem from unrelated feature amplification.
2. Include reliability diagrams (calibration curves) alongside ECE numbers. Visualizing confidence vs. accuracy across bins would expose whether IA2 legitimately fixes overconfident errors or merely compresses the probability distribution, which is critical for the calibration claim.
3. Plot activation similarity (asim) and training loss over the full IA2 → SFT timeline. Showing how rapidly the SFT phase erodes the IA2-induced alignment would prove whether the "priming" effect is robust or if the two objectives fundamentally conflict during fine-tuning.

### Obvious Next Steps
1. Conduct a systematic OOD robustness analysis relative to ICL's baseline. The paper asserts IA2 improves SFT by adopting ICL's "generalizability," but without rigorously tracking the OOD performance gap between ICL and IA2→SFT across distribution shifts, this core promise remains unverified.
2. Integrate demonstration selection or ordering optimization into the activation collection phase. Since ICL is highly sensitive to demo composition, building IA2 on random demonstrations creates an uncontrolled upper bound; optimizing prompts before collection should have been included to prove the method's maximum capability.

# Final Consolidated Review
## Summary
This paper proposes IA2, a two-step parameter-efficient fine-tuning pipeline that first aligns LoRA weights to match the internal self-attention activations produced during in-context learning, then performs standard supervised fine-tuning. The authors argue that aligning intermediate functional states transfers ICL's calibration and generalization benefits to SFT without incurring inference overhead. Extensive experiments across 12 benchmarks and 1B–4B models demonstrate that IA2→SFT consistently outperforms SFT-only baselines in accuracy and expected calibration error in low-data regimes.

## Strengths
- **Exceptionally rigorous empirical validation:** The scale of experimentation (>13,000 models, consistent data splits, multiple few-shot regimes, 5-seed averaging with statistical significance testing) provides robust, reproducible evidence that IA2→SFT reliably outperforms SFT-only across classification and generation tasks.
- **Compelling mechanistic analysis:** Quantitative activation similarity and LoRA weight subspace overlap analyses convincingly demonstrate that IA2 steers optimization into a distinct, largely orthogonal parameter subspace that SFT alone cannot access, offering tangible evidence of a novel training signal.
- **High practical utility with zero inference penalty:** Activation collection is a one-time offline step. The resulting primed model incurs identical inference latency and memory costs as standard PEFT, making it immediately deployable for production fine-tuning pipelines.

## Weaknesses
- **Confounding of training signal vs. optimization trajectory:** The IA2→SFT pipeline involves two sequential convergence phases, effectively doubling optimization steps compared to SFT-only. Without a strict compute- or step-matched baseline (e.g., extended SFT training or curriculum learning), it remains unclear whether observed gains stem from the activation alignment signal or merely a longer, more stabilized optimization path. This confound fundamentally weakens the attribution of improvements to IA2's mechanistic objective.
- **Questionable scalability and weak reasoning baselines:** Experiments are restricted to 1B–4B models. On multi-token reasoning (e.g., GSM8K), the 1B baseline achieves ~14–37% accuracy, indicating severe capability ceilings. Gains at this scale may reflect improved output formatting or shortcut learning rather than genuine reasoning enhancement. For ICLR, demonstrating that IA2 unlocks or preserves capabilities in capable modern architectures (≥7B) is essential.
- **Correlational rather than causal mechanistic claims:** The paper posits that higher activation similarity drives improved calibration and generalization, but relies entirely on correlational metrics. Without causal interventions (e.g., activation patching, representational probing, or feature attribution maps), there is no direct evidence that IA2 modifies internal reasoning circuits or genuinely replicates ICL's functional behavior, versus merely matching superficial hidden-state statistics.
- **Missing critical baselines and architectural ablations:** The absence of direct empirical comparisons against modern context-to-weight distillation methods (e.g., prompt weight internalization, adaptive adapter generation) leaves it unclear whether activation matching offers unique advantages over output-driven distillation trends. Furthermore, the claim that IA2 must act as a *primer* is unvalidated without testing the reverse training order (SFT→IA2), leaving the necessity of the sequential pipeline speculative.

## Nice-to-Haves
- Quantify exact memory footprint and FLOP overhead for activation storage and preprocessing relative to standard SFT to fully contextualize the "practical utility" claim.
- Include reliability diagrams (calibration curves) alongside ECE tables to visually substantiate that IA2 legitimately resolves overconfident mispredictions rather than merely compressing output distributions.
- Ablate layer-selective alignment. Figure 2 shows middle layers exhibit the highest ICL/SFT divergence; targeting only these layers could improve signal-to-noise ratio and drastically reduce storage/compute costs.

## Novel Insights
The paper's most significant conceptual contribution is the empirical demonstration that SFT and ICL traverse fundamentally orthogonal optimization subspaces, and that bridging this gap requires intermediate state matching rather than output-level distillation. This shifts the paradigm of context injection from mimicking final token probabilities to aligning the model's internal processing dynamics, suggesting that low-data calibration and generalization improvements are emergent properties of forcing parameter updates to replicate the attentional routing patterns that in-context demonstrations naturally induce.

## Potentially Missed Related Work
- **Prompt Internalization / Continuous Prompt Distillation (e.g., Chen et al., 2025; Shen et al., 2025):** Recent works compress in-context demonstrations directly into continuous vectors or adapter weights via self-distillation gradients. Comparing IA2 against these methods would isolate whether activation-space matching yields unique functional benefits over prompt-weight distillation.
- **Causal Activation Steering & Circuit Analysis (e.g., Turner et al. / Stolfo et al., 2024-2025):** Literature demonstrating targeted activation manipulation for behavior control could provide the theoretical scaffolding needed to move IA2's similarity metrics from correlational to mechanistic, offering formal links between MSE alignment and altered information flow.

## Suggestions
1. **Implement a compute-matched control:** Train SFT-only for an equivalent number of total updates/epochs (or use early-stopping with validation loss matching) to definitively isolate the IA2 activation signal from prolonged optimization effects.
2. **Validate training order necessity:** Run a full SFT→IA2 ablation across a subset of datasets to empirically prove that IA2 functions as a necessary primer rather than simply a complementary regularizer.
3. **Scale to ≥7B models:** Extend evaluation to at least one modern mid-scale architecture. Demonstrating that IA2 improves or preserves performance when baseline reasoning accuracy is already competitive (≥70% on GSM8K) is critical for establishing real-world relevance.
4. **Add causal representational analysis:** Incorporate linear probing or activation ablation experiments to demonstrate that IA2 concretely alters task-specific feature extraction or reduces shortcut reliance, moving beyond cosine similarity as a proxy for functional equivalence.
