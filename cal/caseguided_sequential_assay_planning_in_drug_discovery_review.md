# Harsh Critic Review
## Section-by-Section Critical Review

### Title & Abstract
- **Does the title accurately reflect the contribution?** Yes. It clearly signals the domain (drug discovery), the task (sequential assay planning), and the mechanism (case-guided).
- **Does the abstract clearly state the problem, method, and key results?** The problem (simulator-free sequential planning under resource constraints), method (IBMDP: similarity-weighted implicit transition model + ensemble MCTS-DPW), and results (resource savings vs heuristics, superior alignment with optimal policy in synthetic settings) are all concisely stated.
- **Are any claims in the abstract unsupported by the paper?** The claim of "reduced resource consumption by up to 92%" is mathematically supported by Table 1 (Scenario 2: $5200 → $400), but as discussed below, this statistic rests on only 4 illustrative compounds rather than a broad benchmark. The abstract should ideally temper "up to 92%" with aggregate metrics or clarify it refers to representative cases.

### Introduction & Motivation
- **Is the problem well-motivated? Is the gap in prior work clearly identified?** Strongly motivated. The tension between costly, irreversible assays and the lack of mechanistic simulators is a genuine bottleneck in early-stage discovery. The gap—standard RL requires `(s,a,s')` tuples or simulators, which don't exist here—is accurately framed.
- **Are the contributions clearly stated and accurate?** Yes. The three contributions (evidence-adaptive implicit dynamics, similarity-weighted Bayesian belief, ensemble MCTS for stability) precisely map to Sections 3 and 4.
- **Does the introduction over-claim or under-sell?** The framing is balanced. However, stating that direct comparisons to related work are "fundamentally unfair" (Section 1 & Appendix C) slightly overstates incompatibility. While model-free RL or standard POMDP solvers are indeed mismatched, Bayesian Experimental Design (BED) and contextual bandits share the information-gathering core and warrant at least a conceptual baseline comparison or explicit adaptation discussion.

### Method / Approach
- **Is the method clearly described and reproducible?** Algorithm 1, the distance metric (Eq. 5), and the sampling mechanism are well-specified. The appendices provide necessary implementation details (hyperparameters, data generation). Given the supplementary code, reproducibility is achievable.
- **Are key assumptions stated and justified?** Yes. The authors explicitly note the reliance on representative historical data `D`, the choice of variance-normalized Euclidean distance, and the conditional independence approximation for likelihoods given a prototype (Appendix A). The limitation of renormalizing over `Ig` when `|Ig| ≪ N` is also correctly flagged.
- **Are there logical gaps in the derivation or reasoning?** 
  - The reward function is purely cost-based (`-ρ^T c`), while constraints (`H(s_T) ≤ ε`, `L(s) ≥ τ`) are enforced via heavy penalty (`-10^6`) during MCTS rollouts (Alg 1, line 9). This is a standard Lagrangian/heuristic approach, but it does not theoretically guarantee constraint satisfaction. A brief discussion of how penalty scaling affects feasibility would strengthen the constrained MDP formulation.
  - The distance metric (Eq. 5) sums over measured features `K_t`. As `|K_t|` grows during the horizon, distances naturally increase, potentially causing weight collapse (`w_i → 0` except for nearest neighbors). While progressive focusing is desirable, the absence of explicit normalization by `|K_t|` could accelerate variance underestimation late in the horizon. This is minor but worth noting.
- **Are there edge cases or failure modes not discussed?** The authors cover data sparsity and metric mismatch well. However, the impact of *distribution shift* (e.g., a candidate from a novel chemotype chemically distant from `D`) is only implicitly covered. If no historical record is sufficiently similar, the implicit transition degenerates to uniform sampling or arbitrary nearest-neighbor bias. Failure mode detection (e.g., a min-weight threshold triggering an "escape" or expert fallback) is missing but highly relevant for deployment.
- **For theoretical claims:** The consistency proof in Appendix D is rigorous for the linear/independent synthetic setting. The POMDP mapping (Appendix A) is conceptually sound and correctly frames the similarity weights as a tempered posterior over discrete prototypes.

### Experiments & Results
- **Do the experiments actually test the paper's claims?** The synthetic benchmark rigorously tests the planning quality against a computable optimum. The real-world study demonstrates practical utility against a rule-of-thumb heuristic.
- **Are baselines appropriate and fairly compared?** 
  - Synthetic: VI-Theo (optimal) and VI-Sim (deterministic counterpart) are excellent baselines that isolate the value of stochastic ensemble planning.
  - Real-world: Only a rule-based QSAR heuristic is used. Given the information-gathering nature of the task, omitting a myopic Bayesian Experimental Design (BED) or Expected Improvement (BO) baseline—even if adapted for batching and state-based stopping—weakens the empirical claim that IBMDP's multi-step lookahead is necessary. Dismissing these entirely in Appendix C overlooks that BED is highly relevant to sequential experimental planning, even without a simulator.
- **Are there missing ablations that would materially change conclusions?** 
  - **Ensemble vs. Single Run:** While alignment rates (Top-1 vs Top-2) are shown, a direct variance/consistency comparison of ensemble vs. single MCTS-DPW runs on the synthetic benchmark would quantitatively justify the `N_e=50` computational overhead.
  - **Batch Size `m`:** No analysis of how throughput constraints affect policy quality vs. cost.
- **Are error bars / statistical significance reported?** The synthetic results (Table 2/5) aggregate 100 trials, which is acceptable. However, the real-world results (Table 1, Figure 2) report outcomes for exactly **4 hand-selected compounds**. This is insufficient for ICLR. To substantiate the "up to 92% reduction" claim and demonstrate robustness beyond representative edge cases, the authors should evaluate on the full `N=220` dataset (e.g., via leave-one-out or K-fold cross-validation) and report aggregate mean/median cost, feasibility rates, and uncertainty coverage.
- **Do the results support the claims made, or are they cherry-picked?** The synthetic results strongly support the planning claims. The real-world results, while illustrative, risk appearing cherry-picked due to the 4-example scale and descriptive categorization (baseline confirmation, heuristic challenge, etc.). Aggregate statistics across the full historical pool are needed.
- **Are datasets and evaluation metrics appropriate?** The CNS dataset and synthetic generator are well-matched to the problem. Metrics (cost, `H(s)`, policy alignment) are appropriate.

### Writing & Clarity
- **Are there sections that are confusing or poorly explained?** The methodology is generally clear. The transition from the constrained objective (Eq. 3) to the penalty-based MCTS rollout could be slightly more explicit: the paper states a large penalty is applied, but does not detail how the planner handles trajectories that violate `L(s) ≥ τ` mid-path (are they pruned entirely or just penalized?). Clarifying this would improve reproducibility.
- **Are figures and tables clear and informative?** Table 1 and Figure 2 effectively communicate the trade-offs. Figure 4 clearly illustrates the `τ` hyperparameter's effect on cost/confidence. The tables and plots are well-integrated with the text and directly support the narrative.

### Limitations & Broader Impact
- **Do the authors acknowledge the key limitations?** Yes, Section 8 appropriately flags historical data coverage/bias, similarity metric assumptions, computational scaling, and hyperparameter sensitivity.
- **Are there fundamental limitations they missed?** 
  - **Novelty/Extrapolation Limits:** As noted above, performance likely degrades gracefully only within the chemical manifold of `D`. Compounds with scaffolds or mechanisms unseen in historical data will receive unreliable similarity weights. The limitations section should explicitly discuss this extrapolation boundary and potential mitigation (e.g., uncertainty-aware distance thresholds or active learning loops to expand `D`).
  - **Assay Correlation Handling:** The method preserves correlations *within* a sampled historical record. If a batch contains assays that rarely co-occur in `D`, the planner relies on single-record joint distributions, which may not capture population-level dependencies between those specific assays.
- **Are there failure modes or negative societal impacts not discussed?** The ethics statement is strong, highlighting reduced animal use and transparency regarding data bias. It correctly identifies that biased historical data could lead to suboptimal compound advancement. Adding a brief note on the risk of algorithmic lock-in (e.g., systematically avoiding novel chemical spaces due to sparse historical support) would further strengthen the broader impact discussion.

### Overall Assessment
This paper presents a principled and highly practical framework for sequential experimental planning in simulator-poor environments. The core methodological contribution—framing case-based similarity weighting as an implicit Bayesian belief update, combined with ensemble MCTS-DPW to navigate combinatorial action spaces under state-based constraints—is novel, theoretically grounded (POMDP equivalence, consistency proof), and addresses a genuine bottleneck in computational drug discovery. The synthetic benchmarks are well-designed and convincingly demonstrate the advantage of stochastic ensemble planning over deterministic alternatives. However, the real-world empirical validation is currently underpowered, relying on only four illustrative compounds rather than aggregate evaluation across the available `N=220` dataset. Additionally, while the authors justify the lack of direct RL comparisons, the absence of adapted Bayesian Experimental Design or contextual bandit baselines leaves a gap in demonstrating the necessity of multi-step lookahead for this specific problem class. With a more comprehensive evaluation scale (e.g., leave-one-out cross-validation), minor discussion of distribution-shift failure modes, and possibly a lightweight comparison to a bed/bandit heuristic, this work would strongly meet the ICLR acceptance bar. The contribution stands on solid theoretical and algorithmic grounds and offers a clear path to high-impact application.

# Neutral Reviewer
## Balanced Review

### Summary
The paper introduces the Implicit Bayesian Markov Decision Process (IBMDP), a model-based planning framework designed for sequential experimental design in domains like drug discovery where explicit simulators or $(s, a, s')$ transition tuples are unavailable. By constructing a similarity-weighted, nonparametric belief over historical compound records and coupling it with an ensemble Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS-DPW) planner, the method generates stable, resource-efficient assay sequences. Experiments on a real-world CNS drug discovery dataset and a synthetic environment with a ground-truth optimal policy demonstrate significant resource savings and higher alignment with optimal decisions compared to deterministic or heuristic alternatives.

### Strengths
1. **Practical Problem Formulation:** The paper directly tackles a highly realistic, underexplored regime in scientific discovery: multi-step planning with static historical data and no environment simulator. This aligns well with ICLR's growing focus on ML for science and real-world decision-making.
2. **Rigorous Theoretical Grounding:** Appendix A formally maps the similarity-weighted case retrieval to a POMDP belief update over latent historical prototypes, transforming an ostensibly heuristic mechanism into a principled tempered Bayesian posterior. The consistency proof for the variance estimator (Appendix D.6) further strengthens the method's mathematical validity under defined conditions.
3. **Effective Planning Architecture:** The use of ensemble MCTS with majority voting (MLASP) is a well-motivated design choice that directly addresses the high variance of implicit sampling models and combinatorial action spaces. The empirical results (Table 2, 47% Top-1 / 66% Top-2 alignment) convincingly show that stochastic exploration outperforms deterministic value iteration in this setting.
4. **Well-Designed Synthetic Benchmark:** Creating a synthetic environment with a computable theoretical optimum (VI-Theo) is a strong methodological contribution. It cleanly isolates planning quality from model misspecification, a standard often expected in top-tier ML venues.
5. **Clarity and Reproducibility:** The manuscript is exceptionally well-organized, featuring a comprehensive global notation reference (Appendix G), detailed algorithm pseudocode, explicit hyperparameter selection methodology, and a promised code/data release, fully meeting ICLR's transparency expectations.

### Weaknesses
1. **Weak Real-World Baselines:** The empirical validation in Section 5.1 relies primarily on a simple, hand-tuned rule-based heuristic. While the authors argue in Section 7 that standard RL/BO comparisons are "apples to oranges," ICLR typically expects at least one adapted, competitive baseline (e.g., constrained Bayesian Optimization with RF/GP surrogates, Thompson Sampling for assay selection, or a lightweight offline RL method). Without this, the 92% resource reduction claim lacks strong comparative context.
2. **Computational Bottlenecks at Scale:** Appendix B.2 notes that planning takes ~1 hour per compound with $|D|=220$, and worst-case complexity scales as $O(N_e \cdot n_{itr} \cdot |D| \cdot d)$. In real-world pharmaceutical pipelines, $N$ easily exceeds $10^5$. The paper lacks discussion or implementation of acceleration techniques (e.g., approximate nearest neighbor search, dynamic case filtering, or kernel approximations) to make the method viable for enterprise-scale deployment.
3. **Restrictive Synthetic Assumptions:** The theoretical consistency proof (Appendix D.6) and synthetic optimal policy rely heavily on linear feature-target relationships and conditional independence across assays. Real biochemical systems exhibit strong cross-assay correlations and non-linear interactions. The current setup does not empirically stress-test how the Gaussian kernel and implicit sampler degrade when these assumptions are violated.
4. **Hyperparameter Sensitivity Not Fully Quantified:** Appendix B.1 acknowledges sensitivity to $\lambda_w$, $\lambda_k$, $\epsilon$, and $\tau$, and notes selecting $\lambda_w=1.0$ over the theoretically derived $0.5$. However, no systematic sensitivity analysis or robustness curves are provided, making it difficult to assess how brittle the policy is to bandwidth or threshold misspecification in practice.

### Novelty & Significance
The novelty is moderate-to-high. While similarity-based retrieval, kernel methods, and MCTS are individually well-established, the paper makes a distinct contribution by tightly integrating them into a simulator-free, implicit belief-updated MDP framework. Reframing historical case sampling as a Bayesian posterior predictive distribution and using ensemble MCTS to stabilize planning under non-stationary implicit dynamics is a clever and principled synthesis. The significance for applied machine learning and computational chemistry is substantial, as it offers a practical pathway to optimize high-stakes experimental workflows without requiring expensive mechanistic models or online interaction. The work is well-positioned for ICLR's scope, provided the empirical baselines and scalability concerns are addressed.

### Suggestions for Improvement
1. **Incorporate Adapted Baselines:** Add at least one non-heuristic baseline to the real-world experiments, such as constrained Bayesian Optimization (using a surrogate model and an acquisition function tuned for batch selection) or a simple Thompson Sampling policy. This would contextualize IBMDP's performance against standard sequential design methods adapted to the offline setting.
2. **Address Scalability Explicitly:** Discuss and ideally implement a retrieval optimization for MCTS node expansions, such as FAISS/ANN search over historical embeddings or a hierarchical case-clustering strategy to reduce $O(|D|)$ distance evaluations. Provide a scaling analysis showing runtime/performance trade-offs as $N$ grows from hundreds to tens of thousands.
3. **Add Sensitivity and Ablation Analyses:** Include a figure or table systematically varying $\lambda_w$ and $\tau/\epsilon$ thresholds in the synthetic environment. This will demonstrate policy robustness, justify the chosen hyperparameters empirically, and show how the Pareto frontier shifts with belief constraints.
4. **Test Non-Linear/Correlated Regimes:** Augment the synthetic benchmark (or real-world validation) with a setting that introduces deliberate cross-assay correlations or non-linear target dependencies. Report how the Gaussian kernel and independent similarity assumption perform, and discuss potential extensions (e.g., learned Mahalanobis distances or neural similarity metrics) to capture complex biological structure.
5. **Clarify Constraint Handling in MCTS:** The paper uses a hard penalty ($-10^6$) for violating $L(s) < \tau$. Discuss whether this causes premature pruning of high-reward but initially risky paths in the search tree. Consider experimenting with or discussing soft barrier functions or Lagrangian relaxation within MCTS to improve exploration under tight feasibility constraints.

## How to Improve This Paper

### Missing Experiments (top 3-5 only)
1. Report aggregate statistics (mean, median, variance, confidence intervals) for resource consumption across the full $N=220$ compounds instead of cherry-picking four representative scenarios. Without dataset-wide validation, the claim of "up to 92% reduction" is anecdotal and statistically insufficient for ICLR.
2. Benchmark directly against established simulator-free methods like multi-step Bayesian Optimization (e.g., Knowledge Gradient) or Thompson Sampling adapted for sequential costs. Dismissing these as "fundamentally unfair" ignores standard review expectations; you must empirically prove why case-guided MCTS outperforms rigorous experimental design baselines.
3. Provide systematic ablations for ensemble size ($N_e$), MCTS iterations, and similarity bandwidth ($\lambda_w$). The paper's central claim that ensembling stabilizes stochastic planning is unverified if you do not demonstrate how performance scales or degrades across these critical hyperparameters.

### Deeper Analysis Needed (top 3-5 only)
1. Analyze the actual cost regret of suboptimal matches against the VI-Theo baseline, not just exact policy alignment percentages. A 47% match rate is uninformative if mismatches cost nearly the same; you must quantify the financial/resource penalty of deviations to prove superior planning quality.
2. Quantify constraint violation rates and termination behavior across synthetic trials. The method enforces feasibility via threshold penalties, but without reporting how often trajectories violate $L(s_t) \geq \tau$ or fail to reach $\epsilon$, the framework's reliability for high-stakes decision-making remains unsubstantiated.
3. Stress-test the implicit variance estimator on data with correlated or non-linear feature dependencies. The synthetic proof assumes diagonal covariance and independent assays, creating a biased testbed that overstates similarity-based consistency; real drug discovery requires demonstrating robustness to cross-assay correlations.

### Visualizations & Case Studies
1. Visualize exact state trajectories ($H(s_t)$ decay and $L(s_t)$ progression over steps $t$) for both optimal and failing planning runs. This reveals whether the ensemble genuinely corrects poor stochastic rollouts or merely averages out high-variance noise without improving underlying policy quality.
2. Plot the temporal evolution of similarity weight distributions over historical compounds at each decision step. Showing which past analogs dominate the belief and how sharply the weights concentrate after new assay evidence is required to empirically validate the "adaptive case-guided dynamics" claim.
3. Provide a full-dataset scatter or hexbin plot of total assay cost vs. terminal uncertainty, overlaid with baseline performance. A single curated Pareto curve cannot prove consistent dominance; density visualization is necessary to show whether IBMDP succeeds broadly or only in localized regions of the feature space.

### Obvious Next Steps
1. Optimize the inference bottleneck by implementing approximate nearest-neighbor search or learned embeddings to replace the $O(N \cdot d)$ distance computation. Reporting one-hour wall-clock runtime per compound on $N=220$ undermines any claim of deployability for industrial-scale screening campaigns.
2. Formalize, open-source, and explicitly detail the complete evaluation codebase, fixed random seeds, and baseline configurations. Vague reproducibility statements and hidden hyperparameter sweeps fall well below the ICLR reproducibility standard for model-based RL methods.
3. Integrate explicit modeling of assay throughput and diminishing returns directly into the node expansion and reward aggregation. Capping parallel assays at a hard limit ($m=3$) without modeling synergistic or redundant information gain oversimplifies real experimental logistics and limits the planner's strategic depth.

# Final Consolidated Review
## Summary
The paper proposes the Implicit Bayesian Markov Decision Process (IBMDP), a model-based planning framework for sequential experimental design in simulator-free, data-rich domains like drug discovery. By treating historical compound records as latent prototypes and using similarity-weighted sampling to construct an implicit generative transition model, IBMDP enables multi-step planning without explicit $(s, a, s')$ tuples or environment simulators. An ensemble MCTS-DPW planner with majority voting (MLASP) aggregates stochastic trajectories to produce stable, resource-efficient assay sequences, validated on a synthetic benchmark with a ground-truth optimal policy and a real-world CNS drug discovery dataset.

## Strengths
- **Principled theoretical framing of an implicit model:** The paper rigorously maps its similarity-weighted case retrieval to a formal POMDP belief update over discrete historical prototypes (Appendix A). This transforms what could be viewed as a heuristic retrieval mechanism into a mathematically grounded tempered Bayesian posterior, complete with a consistency proof for the variance estimator under linear/independent assumptions (Appendix D.6).
- **Effective architectural solution to combinatorial uncertainty:** By coupling the implicit transition model with ensemble MCTS-DPW and constructing the MLASP via majority voting, the method directly addresses the high variance inherent in nonparametric sampling and batch action selection. The synthetic results cleanly demonstrate that stochastic ensemble exploration (47% Top-1 alignment, 66% Top-2 coverage) outperforms a deterministic VI-Sim counterpart (36%), validating the design choice.
- **Well-isolated synthetic benchmark design:** Constructing a VI-Theo baseline with analytically computable optimal policies and exact uncertainty dynamics provides a rare and rigorous testbed for planning quality in a domain where true ground truth is typically inaccessible. This cleanly separates planner efficacy from model misspecification.
- **Clarity and reproducibility commitment:** The manuscript is highly structured, providing explicit algorithm pseudocode, a comprehensive notation reference, detailed hyperparameter justification, and a promised supplementary code release that meets transparency expectations.

## Weaknesses
- **Underpowered real-world empirical validation:** The central empirical claim of "up to 92% resource reduction" is substantiated using only four hand-selected compounds (Table 1, Figure 2). While categorized to cover different heuristic scenarios, evaluating on just 4 out of an available $N=220$ dataset is statistically insufficient for a top-tier venue. Without aggregate metrics (e.g., leave-one-out or $K$-fold cross-validation across the full historical pool), the robustness, variance, and generalizability of these savings cannot be verified, leaving open the risk of selective presentation.
- **Practical computational bottlenecks at scale:** Appendix B.2 acknowledges that generating a single policy requires approximately one hour on a modern laptop for $|D|=220$, with worst-case complexity scaling linearly as $O(N_e \cdot n_{itr} \cdot |D| \cdot d)$. In real-world pharmaceutical pipelines where historical databases often exceed $10^5$ compounds, this exhaustive distance computation during every tree expansion becomes prohibitive. The paper lacks any discussion or implementation of acceleration strategies (e.g., approximate nearest-neighbor search, dynamic case filtering, or indexing) to bridge the gap between a proof-of-concept and deployable tool.

## Nice-to-Haves
- **Quantitative cost regret analysis:** Reporting exact policy alignment percentages is informative, but supplementing this with financial/resource regret for mismatches against VI-Theo would better quantify the practical penalty of suboptimal recommendations.
- **Systematic hyperparameter sensitivity & constraint tracking:** Adding ablation curves for ensemble size ($N_e$) and similarity bandwidth ($\lambda_w$), alongside explicit reporting of constraint violation rates ($L(s) < \tau$) across synthetic trials, would empirically justify the chosen configurations and demonstrate reliability under varying confidence thresholds.
- **Lightweight adapted baseline:** While the paper correctly scopes out direct comparisons to standard online RL or simulator-dependent BED, implementing a single adapted offline baseline (e.g., constrained Bayesian Optimization or Thompson Sampling modified for batching and state-based stopping) would contextualize the marginal value of multi-step lookahead in this specific setting.
- **Explicit discussion of distribution shift failure modes:** The limitations section covers data coverage, but adding a concrete failure detection heuristic (e.g., a minimum similarity weight threshold that triggers a safe/expert fallback) would strengthen the deployment readiness for novel chemotypes.

## Novel Insights
The paper’s most significant conceptual contribution is reframing static historical datasets not as training distributions for explicit dynamics learning, but as a discrete belief space over latent "prototypes." By interpreting similarity-weighted sampling as a direct, nonparametric implementation of Bayesian belief updating within a POMDP, the method elegantly sidesteps the need for $(s, a, s')$ trajectories or mechanistic simulators. Crucially, because transitions are generated by sampling entire historical profiles rather than predicting marginal assay outcomes, the framework intrinsically preserves cross-assay correlations and biochemical interactions that would be prohibitively complex to model explicitly. The ensemble MCTS layer then leverages this implicit belief structure to navigate combinatorial batch actions, effectively treating historical analogs as stochastic world states.

## Suggestions
- Expand the real-world evaluation to the full $N=220$ dataset using cross-validation or bootstrapping, and report aggregate statistics (mean/median cost reduction, feasibility rates, and terminal uncertainty distributions) with confidence intervals to rigorously substantiate the resource savings claim.
- Implement and evaluate at least one computational acceleration technique for the similarity module (e.g., FAISS/ANN indexing or hierarchical case clustering) to demonstrate how runtime scales with $|D|$, and provide a clear analysis of the trade-off between retrieval speed and planning quality.
