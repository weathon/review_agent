# Harsh Critic Review
## Section-by-Section Critical Review

### Title & Abstract
- **Title:** Accurately reflects the core contribution. "Case-guided sequential assay planning" correctly signals the nonparametric, historical-data-driven approach in a drug discovery context.
- **Abstract:** Clearly states the problem (simulator-free, resource-constrained sequential planning), the method (IBMDP with similarity-weighted belief updating and ensemble MCTS), and key results (92% cost reduction in a real-world case, superior alignment with a theoretical optimum in synthetic settings).
- **Unsupported Claims:** The claim "reduced resource consumption by up to 92%" is technically supported by Table 1 (Scenario 1), but framing it as a headline result without noting that it represents a best-case among four hand-picked examples is slightly promotional. The abstract would benefit from reporting median/average improvements across a broader set of compounds to better represent expected utility.

### Introduction & Motivation
- **Motivation & Gap:** Well-motivated. The lack of $(s,a,s')$ tuples in experimental sciences and the myopic nature of heuristic playbooks create a clear, practically important gap. The framing of this as a constrained POMDP-style planning problem with adaptive uncertainty metrics is compelling.
- **Contributions:** Clearly stated and accurate to the proposed framework. The three pillars (adaptive dynamics, Bayesian belief mechanism, robust ensemble MCTS) map directly to Sections 3 and 4.
- **Over-claim/Under-sell:** The introduction does not over-claim; it explicitly notes the trade-off of formal convergence guarantees for practical applicability. If anything, it under-sells the computational cost, which is deferred to the Limitations and Appendix B without early context for practitioners reading the introduction.

### Method / Approach
- **Clarity & Reproducibility:** The method is well-described. Algorithm 1, combined with the detailed appendices (B, D), provides a reproducible pipeline. Equations 1-7 clearly define the state, reward, constraints, similarity kernel, and transition sampling.
- **Assumptions:** Key assumptions include the appropriateness of the variance-normalized Euclidean kernel and the representativeness of $D$. However, a critical methodological tension exists: Appendix A justifies the Bayesian weight update under a *conditional independence* assumption for assay outcomes given a latent prototype $Z$, yet Section 3 ("Similarity-Based State Transition") explicitly samples *full historical records*, thereby preserving empirical correlations between assays. This is a practically sensible choice, but the paper should reconcile this disconnect more rigorously. Ensembling mitigates variance, but it does not resolve the theoretical mismatch between the derived likelihood product and the joint sampling mechanism.
- **Logical Gaps:** Equation 3 formulates the objective with *hard* constraints ($H(s_T) \le \epsilon$, $L(s_t) \ge \tau$). However, Algorithm 1, Step 9, enforces the $\tau$ constraint via a large negative reward penalty ($-10^6$) during rollouts. This transforms it into a penalized/unconstrained MDP rather than a constrained MDP. The planner could theoretically violate $\tau$ if exploration leads it to paths with high reward despite the penalty. This implementation detail should be explicitly linked to the theoretical formulation, and sensitivity to the penalty magnitude should be discussed.
- **Theoretical Claims:** The POMDP mapping in Appendix A is conceptually sound and correctly frames the similarity weights as a tempered posterior. Appendix D correctly proves consistency for the *deterministic* estimator (VI-Sim) under the linear/independent synthetic setting, but does not provide convergence bounds for IBMDP itself. The authors are transparent about this trade-off (Appendix A.6), which is appropriate.

### Experiments & Results
- **Test Claims:** The experiments partially validate the claims. The synthetic benchmark directly tests planning quality against a ground-truth VI policy. The real-world case study demonstrates cost efficiency, but only on four illustrative scenarios. It does not adequately test generalization across a broader chemical space or varying budget regimes.
- **Baselines:** The comparison to a simple QSAR heuristic (Section 5.1) is too weak for ICLR standards. While Appendix C argues that adapting BO, standard RL, or POMDP solvers is "fundamentally unfair," this reasoning is insufficient for a top-tier venue. A rigorous evaluation requires at least one adapted computational baseline (e.g., Bayesian Optimization with a GP surrogate + expected improvement, Thompson Sampling for combinatorial bandits, or a learned transition model + MPC) to demonstrate that the gains come from the ensemble MCTS + implicit dynamics, not merely from outperforming naïve rules.
- **Missing Ablations:** Critical components lack systematic ablation. The impact of ensemble size $N_e$ (Fig 3 shows one histogram but no systematic curve), the sensitivity to the bandwidth $\lambda_w$ (chosen empirically as 1.0 vs theoretical 0.5), and the effect of the batch size limit $m$ are not quantified. Without these, it is difficult to isolate which component drives the reported improvements.
- **Error Bars / Significance:** Table 2 reports alignment percentages over 100 synthetic trials but provides no confidence intervals, variance, or statistical significance tests (e.g., binomial tests for 47% vs 36%). Given the stochastic nature of both the implicit model and MCTS, reporting $95\%$ CIs is essential.
- **Cherry-picking Risk:** Table 1 and Figure 2 show four representative compounds selected to cover specific heuristic scenarios. There is no aggregate performance metric (mean/median cost savings, uncertainty reduction distribution) across the full historical dataset or held-out test set. This raises concerns about cherry-picking favorable cases.
- **Datasets & Metrics:** The real-world CNS dataset ($N=220$) is small but appropriate for early-stage discovery benchmarks. The metrics (cost, terminal uncertainty $H$, goal likelihood $L$, policy alignment) are well-matched to the constrained planning objective.

### Writing & Clarity
- **Confusing Sections:** The constraint formulation in Eq 3 vs the reward-penalty implementation in Alg 1 Step 9 creates a minor but notable conceptual gap that may confuse readers expecting strict feasibility guarantees. The transition from the independent-likelihood derivation in Appendix A to the correlated-record sampling in Section 3 also requires clearer exposition to avoid appearing contradictory.
- **Figures & Tables:** Generally clear and informative. Figure 2 and Figure 4 effectively illustrate the Pareto trade-offs and threshold effects. Table 1 aligns well with the text, though mapping rows explicitly to Figure 2 panels via compound identifiers would improve navigability. Table 2 is minimalist; adding variance/CIs would significantly strengthen it.

### Limitations & Broader Impact
- **Acknowledged Limitations:** The authors honestly address historical data coverage/bias, similarity metric assumptions, computational scaling ($O(N_e \cdot n_{itr} \cdot |D| \cdot d)$), and hyperparameter sensitivity. The trade-off of theoretical guarantees for practical applicability is explicitly stated.
- **Missed Limitations:** The paper does not discuss covariate shift or chemical space drift: if new candidate compounds lie outside the manifold of historical data $D$, the distance kernel will struggle to assign meaningful weights, potentially degrading to uniform random sampling. Additionally, the soft constraint implementation risks systematic $\tau$ violations under high penalty mis-specification, which is a practical deployment concern not addressed.
- **Societal Impact:** The Ethics Statement appropriately highlights the dual benefit of reduced costs and minimized animal testing. The risk of bias propagating to certain chemical classes (potentially exacerbating historical inequities in molecular screening) is mentioned. Further discussion on how practitioners should validate model calibration before human/animal trials would strengthen this section.

### Overall Assessment
This paper tackles a highly relevant, practically constrained problem in drug discovery and proposes a principled, reproducible framework that bridges similarity-based Bayesian case reasoning with robust ensemble MCTS planning. The implicit POMDP formulation is conceptually sound, and the honest acknowledgment of theoretical trade-offs vs. practical applicability is commendable. However, the empirical evaluation falls short of ICLR's rigor standards. The real-world validation relies on only four hand-picked case studies without aggregate statistics or variance reporting, and the baseline comparison against a simplistic rule-based heuristic is insufficient to demonstrate methodological advancement over adapted computational alternatives (e.g., BO-TS, surrogate-based MPC). Additionally, the lack of confidence intervals on synthetic results, missing ablations on $N_e$ and $\lambda_w$, and the unresolved tension between the independence assumption in the Bayesian derivation and correlated record sampling in practice require attention. The core contribution stands and addresses a genuine gap in simulator-free planning, but acceptance hinges on significantly strengthening the empirical validation, reporting statistical uncertainty, and providing at least one meaningfully adapted computational baseline to contextualize the reported gains.

# Neutral Reviewer
## Balanced Review

### Summary
This paper introduces the Implicit Bayesian Markov Decision Process (IBMDP), a reinforcement learning framework for sequential experimental design in domains lacking environment simulators or explicit transition data. IBMDP constructs a nonparametric, similarity-weighted belief over historical outcomes to implicitly model transition dynamics, and employs an ensemble Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) planner to generate stable, resource-efficient assay sequences. The method is evaluated on a real-world CNS drug discovery task, where it substantially reduces resource consumption compared to rule-based heuristics, and on a synthetic benchmark with a computable optimal policy to validate planning quality.

### Strengths
1. **Clear Problem Formulation for Simulator-Free Settings:** The paper directly addresses a realistic and underexplored constraint in experimental sciences: planning must rely solely on static historical records without an explicit simulator or $(s, a, s')$ tuples. The formulation of assay batching, stopping decisions, and state-dependent constraints ($H(s) \leq \epsilon, L(s) \geq \tau$) accurately captures practical drug discovery workflows.
2. **Strong Theoretical Grounding:** Appendix A provides a rigorous mapping of the similarity-weighted case-based mechanism to a POMDP belief update. The derivation shows that recomputing kernel weights after new observations is mathematically equivalent to a principled Bayesian posterior update, justifying the "implicit model" approach beyond heuristic intuition.
3. **Rigorous Synthetic Benchmark Design:** The authors construct a synthetic environment with a tractable ground-truth policy (VI-Theo) and a deterministic similarity-based planner (VI-Sim). This controlled setup cleanly isolates the value of stochastic ensemble MCTS over deterministic planning, showing meaningful gains in policy-space coverage (Top-2 alignment at 66% vs. 36% for VI-Sim).
4. **Practical Real-World Validation:** The CNS case study demonstrates tangible utility. The claimed 92% reduction in resource consumption relative to standard panel-run heuristics, alongside explicit Pareto frontiers balancing cost and uncertainty (Figure 2/4), provides compelling evidence of the framework's applied potential.

### Weaknesses
1. **Lack of Competitive Baselines in Evaluation:** The paper explicitly argues in Appendix C that comparing against Bayesian Optimization, Bayesian Experimental Design, or standard POMDP solvers is "fundamentally unfair." While the data regimes differ, ICLR standards expect at least adapted sequential experimental design baselines (e.g., myopic or look-ahead Bayesian optimization with cost-aware acquisition functions) to contextualize IBMDP's performance. Relying only on simple heuristics and a custom VI-Sim weakens the empirical rigor.
2. **Limited Statistical Aggregation on Real-World Task:** Table 1 and the corresponding scenarios only report results for 4 hand-picked compounds. There is no aggregate statistical reporting (e.g., mean cost/uncertainty reduction, variance, or success rates across the full $N=220$ dataset or multiple candidates). This makes it difficult to assess robustness and generalizability beyond favorable cases.
3. **Terminology Conflating Belief Updates with Environment Dynamics:** The paper frequently refers to the transition dynamics as "non-stationary" (Section 1, Contributions). In RL/POMDP literature, non-stationarity implies the environment itself changes over time. Here, the underlying assay response function is static; only the agent's *belief* over transitions evolves as evidence accumulates. This phrasing may confuse reviewers regarding the theoretical setting and obscures that this is standard Bayesian belief propagation, not environment drift.
4. **Scalability and Computational Overhead Concerns:** The per-step complexity is $O(N_e \cdot n_{\text{itr}} \cdot |D| \cdot d)$. While acceptable for $|D|=220$, modern preclinical datasets often contain $10^5$-$10^6$ compounds. The reliance on full-distance computations at every MCTS node expansion will become a bottleneck, yet the paper offers only a brief mention of potential subsampling without empirical analysis or proposed approximations (e.g., approximate nearest neighbors).

### Novelty & Significance
The core novelty lies in synthesizing kernel-weighted case retrieval, implicit Bayesian belief modeling, and ensemble MCTS into a cohesive planner for simulator-free sequential experimental design. The approach is practically novel for cheminformatics/drug discovery, directly tackling the data-rich/simulator-poor regime that limits standard model-based RL. For the broader ML community, the framework offers a clean instantiation of how implicit generative models can drive multi-step decision-making without explicit likelihoods or simulators. However, from a core RL perspective, the novelty is incremental: case-based planning, kernel density estimation for transition modeling, and MCTS ensembles are established techniques repackaged with a clear POMDP interpretation. The significance is high for ML-driven science, but moderate for fundamental algorithmic RL theory.

### Suggestions for Improvement
1. **Include or Adapt a Stronger Baseline:** To meet ICLR's evaluation standards, add a comparison to at least one cost-aware sequential experimental design method (e.g., Bayesian Optimization with a look-ahead acquisition function like Knowledge Gradient, or an implicit-BED policy trained offline). If direct application is infeasible, clearly adapt the baseline to the constraint setting rather than dismissing it outright.
2. **Provide Aggregate Real-World Statistics:** Report mean, median, and variance of resource savings, steps to termination, and goal-likelihood attainment across a larger, randomly sampled subset of the historical dataset. This will demonstrate statistical robustness beyond illustrative examples.
3. **Clarify Theoretical Terminology and Guarantees:** Replace references to "non-stationary dynamics" with "adaptively evolving belief over transitions" or "belief-state dependent transitions." Additionally, expand the convergence discussion (Appendix A.6) by explicitly bounding the approximation error of the kernel estimator relative to dataset coverage and bandwidth, or cite relevant nonparametric consistency results for kNN/kernel regression under the stated assumptions.
4. **Address Scalability with Practical Approximations:** Discuss and ideally prototype an approximate similarity retrieval method (e.g., FAISS, LSH, or learned embeddings) to reduce the $O(|D|)$ weight computation per MCTS step. Provide a small ablation on how retrieval approximation affects planning quality and runtime, which would significantly strengthen the claims regarding enterprise-scale deployment.

# Spark Finder Review
## How to Improve This Paper

### Missing Experiments (top 3-5 only)
1. Run comprehensive comparisons against adapted cost-aware Bayesian Optimization or Thompson Sampling baselines on the real-world dataset. Claiming superiority over simple heuristics without competitive algorithmic baselines fails to meet ICLR's empirical validation standards.
2. Report aggregate performance statistics (mean, standard deviation, and significance tests) across a properly withheld test set, rather than only detailing four hand-selected compounds. The "up to 92% reduction" claim is statistically unsubstantiated and risks cherry-picking bias.
3. Perform an explicit ablation on historical dataset size ($N$) and similarity kernel choice to quantify performance degradation under data sparsity or metric misspecification. The nonparametric core claim cannot hold without empirical bounds on data dependence.

### Deeper Analysis Needed (top 3-5 only)
1. Quantify the computational planning overhead (~1 hour/compound) relative to actual experimental turnaround times and laboratory costs. If algorithmic compute/time exceeds lab wait times, the net resource savings claim collapses in practice.
2. Analyze how strong cross-assay correlations in the real data affect the similarity-weighted transition model, especially since the synthetic benchmark explicitly assumes feature independence. This analysis is required to verify whether sampling actually preserves dependencies or introduces bias.
3. Conduct a rigorous sensitivity analysis on $\lambda_w$, thresholds ($\tau, \epsilon$), and ensemble size ($N_e$) using systematic grid or randomized search to prove robustness. Without it, the framework's claimed stability remains an unverified tuning-dependent anecdote.

### Visualizations & Case Studies (top 3-5 only)
1. Plot complete multi-step decision trajectories showing cumulative cost, selected assays, and the stepwise evolution of $H(s)$ and $L(s)$ for successful vs. failed compounds. This reveals whether the adaptive planner genuinely concentrates on uncertainty reduction or gets trapped by myopic cost minimization.
2. Visualize the posterior weight redistribution over historical analogs at each planning step to empirically validate the claimed POMDP/Bayesian update equivalence. Without this, the theoretical mapping remains an algebraic assertion rather than a demonstrated mechanism.
3. Include a failure mode heatmap or case study highlighting compounds where the MLASP violates constraints or selects highly suboptimal assays, explicitly linking failures to regions of sparse historical coverage. This exposes the method's operational boundaries and prevents overgeneralized claims.

### Obvious Next Steps (what should have been in this paper)
1. Implement and benchmark an approximate nearest-neighbor (ANN) similarity search or stratified subsampling strategy to reduce the $O(|D|)$ node complexity, proving the method's viability for industry-scale historical databases.
2. Extend the formulation to explicitly handle missing or partially observed historical assays rather than filtering $D$ down to complete records, which misrepresents real-world drug discovery data availability.
3. Directly benchmark against a structurally adapted multi-step implicit Bayesian experimental design or RL planner, countering Appendix C's "unfair comparison" argument by demonstrating that the required modifications do not invalidate head-to-head evaluation.

# Final Consolidated Review
## Summary
This paper introduces the Implicit Bayesian Markov Decision Process (IBMDP), a reinforcement learning framework designed for sequential experimental design in simulator-free, data-rich domains like early-stage drug discovery. IBMDP constructs a nonparametric belief over transition dynamics by similarity-weighting historical compound records and employs an ensemble Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) planner to optimize assay sequencing under uncertainty and resource constraints. The method is evaluated on a real-world CNS drug discovery case study and a synthetic benchmark featuring a computable ground-truth optimal policy.

## Strengths
- **Rigorous Theoretical Framing of Implicit Dynamics:** The paper successfully maps a similarity-weighted case retrieval mechanism to a formal POMDP belief update (Appendix A). The derivation clearly demonstrates that recomputing weights after each observed assay corresponds to a principled, tempered Bayesian posterior update over latent historical prototypes, elevating the method beyond a simple nearest-neighbor heuristic.
- **Well-Designed Synthetic Benchmark for Planner Validation:** The construction of a controlled environment with an analytically tractable optimal policy (VI-Theo) cleanly isolates the value of stochastic ensemble planning. The finding that IBMDP achieves 47% Top-1 and 66% Top-2 alignment with the theoretical optimum—significantly outperforming a deterministic similarity-based VI baseline (36%)—provides strong evidence that ensemble MCTS effectively navigates the policy space in this regime.
- **Accurate Problem Formulation for Experimental Sciences:** The explicit modeling of assay batching, end-of-experiment stopping actions, and state-dependent uncertainty/goal-likelihood functionals faithfully captures the high-stakes, constrained nature of real-world assay planning where $(s, a, s')$ tuples are structurally unavailable.

## Weaknesses
- **Insufficient Empirical Validation & Statistical Rigor:** The real-world evaluation is restricted to four illustrative compounds without aggregate statistics, variance reporting, or significance testing. The headline claim of "up to 92% resource reduction" is derived from a single best-case scenario and lacks population-level substantiation. For ICLR, demonstrating robust performance across a statistically meaningful cohort is a fundamental requirement, not an optional addition.
- **Inadequate Baseline Comparisons:** The paper explicitly dismisses established sequential experimental design methods (e.g., cost-aware Bayesian Optimization, Thompson Sampling) as "fundamentally unfair" in Appendix C without attempting to adapt them. While the exact data regimes differ, ICLR standards require at least one competitive, computationally grounded baseline. Relying solely on a rule-based heuristic and a self-constructed deterministic VI variant prevents readers from isolating whether improvements originate from the ensemble planner or simply from outperforming naive domain rules.
- **Discrepancy Between Theoretical Constraints and Implementation:** Equation 3 formulates the objective with hard constraints on terminal uncertainty ($H(s_T) \le \epsilon$) and stepwise feasibility ($L(s_t) \ge \tau$). However, Algorithm 1 (Step 9) enforces these via a massive negative reward penalty ($-10^6$) during rollouts, which mathematically converts the problem into a soft-constrained/penalized MDP. This implementation risks constraint violation during exploration, and the paper provides no analysis of penalty sensitivity or guarantees that feasibility is actually maintained.
- **Terminological Imprecision Regarding "Non-Stationarity":** The contribution section characterizes the evolving similarity weights as "non-stationary dynamics." In RL/POMDP literature, non-stationarity refers to the environment's transition function changing over time. Here, the underlying biochemical system is static; only the agent's belief distribution evolves. This phrasing conflates belief-state conditioning with environment drift and obscures the standard Bayesian nature of the update mechanism.

## Nice-to-Haves
- Systematic ablations over ensemble size ($N_e$), similarity bandwidth ($\lambda_w$), and batch limit ($m$), accompanied by confidence intervals for the synthetic alignment metrics, would quantitatively establish planning stability and hyperparameter robustness.
- Approximate nearest-neighbor (ANN) retrieval or stratified subsampling to reduce the $O(|D|)$ per-node distance computation would meaningfully address scalability concerns for industry-scale preclinical datasets.

## Novel Insights
The paper's most compelling insight is the operationalization of Bayesian case-based reasoning within a multi-step RL planner, effectively bridging the gap between static historical databases and dynamic decision-making. By treating historical compounds as latent prototypes and using kernel-weighted sampling as an approximate posterior predictive, IBMDP circumvents the need for explicit mechanistic simulators while naturally preserving empirical cross-assay correlations. This reframes implicit model-based planning from a purely theoretical construct into a practical, constraint-aware tool that directly addresses the "data-rich, simulator-poor" bottleneck prevalent in experimental sciences.

## Potentially Missed Related Work
- **Cost-aware Bayesian Optimization / Knowledge Gradient methods (e.g., Frazier et al., "The Knowledge-Gradient Policy for Correlated Normal Beliefs", NeurIPS 2009; Lee et al., "Cost-aware multi-fidelity Gaussian process bandit optimization", AISTATS 2021).** While the authors argue against direct BO comparison, modern cost-acquisition frameworks offer highly relevant methodological baselines for sequential design under strict budget constraints.
- **Implicit Bayesian Experimental Design (e.g., Kleinegesse & Gutmann, "Bayesian experimental design for implicit models by mutual information neural estimation", ICML 2020).** These works specifically tackle intractable likelihoods via policy learning or info-theoretic surrogates and provide a direct algorithmic contrast to IBMDP's reward-driven, constraint-based formulation.

## Suggestions
1. Expand the real-world evaluation to report mean/median cost, steps-to-termination, and feasibility attainment rates across a randomly sampled held-out subset of the historical dataset. Include confidence intervals or binomial tests for the synthetic alignment results to meet ICLR's statistical rigor standards.
2. Implement and benchmark at least one adapted computational baseline (e.g., Thompson Sampling for combinatorial arms or a look-ahead BO variant with cost penalties) to rigorously contextualize IBMDP's algorithmic contributions relative to established sequential design methods.
3. Explicitly reconcile the theoretical hard-constraint objective with the soft-constraint MCTS implementation, including a brief analysis of how penalty magnitude impacts constraint violation rates. Additionally, update all references to "non-stationary dynamics" to "belief-adaptive transitions" or "evidence-conditioned dynamics" to align with standard POMDP terminology and prevent conceptual confusion.
