# Harsh Critic Review
## Section-by-Section Critical Review

**Title & Abstract:** The title accurately reflects the technical contribution. The abstract claims that UnCoVAEr provides (i) confounded concept identification, (ii) unbiased ATE via backdoor adjustment, and (iii) proxy recovery, validated on MorphoMNIST. These claims are well-aligned with the content, but the abstract implies practical applicability to real-world partially annotated datasets while the empirical validation is entirely synthetic. I recommend clarifying that the current validation is a controlled proof-of-concept rather than a demonstration of out-of-the-box real-world utility.

**Introduction & Motivation:** The motivation is strong and well-grounded in practical interpretability challenges (medical imaging, facial attributes). The transition from these high-stakes domains to MorphoMNIST creates a validity gap that needs explicit bridging. While synthetic benchmarks are standard for initial causal method development, the introduction heavily emphasizes clinical/auditing use cases without acknowledging the substantial leap in distributional complexity, annotation noise, and unmodeled causal pathways. A brief paragraph acknowledging this gap and outlining what properties a real dataset would need to satisfy the method's assumptions would strengthen the motivation and set appropriate expectations for ICLR readers.

**Related Work:** The survey adequately covers CEVAE variants, CBMs, and proxy-based causal inference. However, the connection to proximal causal inference literature is underdeveloped. Proximal inference requires two distinct proxies (typically a negative control for treatment and one for outcome) or strict completeness/rank conditions on the bridge functions (Miao et al., 2018; Tchetgen Tchetgen et al., 2020). UnCoVAEr uses a single observed proxy $X$, partitioned heuristically into $Z_C$ and $Z_S$. The paper does not discuss whether this structural assumption satisfies completeness, nor does it contrast its approach with formal proximal identification guarantees. Engaging more deeply with how partitioned VAEs approximate or violate proximal bridge function requirements would significantly strengthen the theoretical positioning.

**Preliminaries & Problem Setup:** 
- **Causal Graph & Assumptions:** Figure 2 assumes $X \leftarrow (C, Z_C, Z_S)$, $C \leftarrow Z_C$, and $Y \leftarrow (C, Z_C)$. The footnote correctly notes $Y \perp X \mid C, Z_C$, which is a strong assumption. In real vision datasets, $Y$ often depends on visual features beyond the annotated concepts $C$ and the specific confounder $Z_C$ (e.g., texture, background, sensor noise). The method effectively assumes all predictive signal for $Y$ outside $C$ is captured by $Z_C$, which may be violated in practice.
- **Identification Requirements:** Section 3 states that identification rests on "standard proxy-type and overlap assumptions" but does not formalize them. For ICLR, it's critical to explicitly state the required conditions (e.g., positivity, no unobserved colliders, and crucially, the completeness condition ensuring $Z_C$ is a sufficient proxy). The claim that "if the confounder leaves no observable imprint, identification is impossible" is trivially true but doesn't address when the *learned* proxy $Z_C$ is theoretically guaranteed to suffice.
- **Property (P3):** Modeling $Z_C$ as strictly binary limits the method to confounders that are inherently categorical. Many real-world visual confounders (lighting intensity, scanner gain, demographic continua) are continuous. The authors should clarify whether extending $Z_C$ to a continuous prior is trivial or breaks the identification/estimation pipeline.

**Method (UnCoVAEr):**
- **ATE Estimation Formula (Eq. 7):** The adjustment procedure is the most critical technical concern. The text estimates $E[Y | do(C_i=c)]$ by averaging over test images and sampling $Z_C$ from $q_{\phi_C}(Z_C | X, C, Y)$. This computes $\mathbb{E}_X[\mathbb{E}[Y \mid do(C_i), Z_C(X)]]$ rather than the standard backdoor integral $\mathbb{E}_{Z_C}[p(Y \mid do(C_i), Z_C)]$. Conditioning on $X$ in the adjustment step is theoretically problematic: $X$ is a descendant of $Z_C$ and $C$, and conditioning on descendants can introduce selection bias or violate the ignorability required for valid adjustment, especially if the encoder is misspecified or if $Z_C$ captures non-causal image variation. The authors must clarify why aggregating conditional posteriors approximates the marginal adjustment validly, or transition to properly marginalizing over the aggregate posterior / prior $p(Z_C)$ as in standard CEVAE.
- **Auxiliary Outcome Head:** The auxiliary predictor $q_{\xi_Y}(Y | X, C)$ is trained to minimize prediction error using raw pixels $X$. While this sharpens gradients, it creates a competing pathway for $Y$ prediction that bypasses $Z_C$ and $C$. If the auxiliary head learns to predict $Y$ from spurious image features, the mutual information regularizer between $Z_C$ and $Z_S$ may not fully prevent $Z_C$ from absorbing task-irrelevant visual noise. An ablation comparing ATEs computed via $p_{\theta_Y}(Y|C,Z_C)$ vs. $q_{\xi_Y}(Y|X,C)$ would clarify whether the causal decoder alone is sufficient for unbiased estimation.
- **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The performance depends on $\lambda_{C}, \lambda_{Y}, \lambda_{MI}$, Gumbel temperature schedule, and the dimensionality $K$ of $Z_C$. No ablation or sensitivity analysis is provided. Given ICLR's reproducibility standards, a brief analysis showing robustness to $\lambda_{MI}$ or $K$ is necessary to demonstrate that results aren't fragile to regularization strength.

**Experiments & Results:**
- **Dataset & Confounding Structure:** The semi-synthetic MorphoMNIST setup is clean and appropriate for isolating causal mechanisms. However, the confounding strength $\alpha$ directly manipulates a deterministic/stochastic label-generation rule. This creates a highly structured, linear-ish confounding pattern that favors VAE-based disentanglement. Real-world confounding is rarely this well-behaved.
- **Baseline Performance & Multiple Confounders:** Table 1 shows that in the multiple confounder setting (XOR mechanism), both Naive and CBM outperform UnCoVAEr (MAE ~0.01 vs ~0.07). The discussion acknowledges this but attributes it to naive methods "exploiting the intensity-Y relation." Since intensity does not cause $Y$ by construction, this "exploitation" implies the model is inadvertently picking up the correct statistical association due to the specific XOR structure of the synthetic data. This is an important finding: when confounders interact non-additively, simple associative models can sometimes achieve lower ATE error than structured causal models. The authors should analyze *why* CBM succeeds here (e.g., is it coincidental correlation alignment?) and clarify under what conditions their method's inductive bias becomes a liability.
- **Statistical Rigor:** Standard deviations are reported, but they are large in several settings (e.g., Naive ±0.18, Image-adjustment ±0.24). Without paired statistical tests or confidence intervals on the *difference* between methods, it's unclear whether the observed improvements are statistically significant across seeds. ICLR reviewers expect formal significance testing, especially when claims of "substantially less biased" are made.
- **Confounding Detection Heuristic:** The criterion flags concepts when bootstrap CIs for ATE and ATE_naive do not overlap. This is a practical heuristic but lacks theoretical guarantees regarding false positive/negative rates under finite samples or weak confounding. Fig 3 shows high false positive rates in single confounder settings and degraded detection in multiple confounders. The authors should discuss the statistical power of this test and whether alternative metrics (e.g., permutation tests or effect-size thresholds) would be more robust.

**Writing & Clarity:** The paper is generally well-structured and readable. However, the description of the ATE estimation procedure (Section 4, "ATE estimation" paragraph and Eq. 7) conflates conditional and marginal adjustment. Clarifying the exact estimand being targeted and the steps taken to approximate $\sum_{Z_C} p(Y|C, Z_C)p(Z_C)$ vs $\sum_{Z_C} p(Y|C, Z_C)p(Z_C|X)$ would eliminate reader confusion. Additionally, the footnote in Section 4 correctly identifies that $p(Y|C,Z_C)$ suffices, which makes the auxiliary head's role purely auxiliary; this should be explicitly stated to avoid ambiguity about which network drives the causal estimates.

**Limitations & Future Work:** The limitations section appropriately notes the reliance on visual traces and struggles with non-linear confounders. However, it misses three critical limitations: (1) the binary restriction on $Z_C$ limits applicability to continuous confounders, (2) the theoretical gap regarding completeness/bridge functions in proxy learning, and (3) the marginalization concern in Eq. 7. Expanding the limitations to address these theoretical and methodological boundaries would significantly strengthen the paper's scholarly rigor.

### Overall Assessment
UnCoVAEr addresses an important and timely problem in causal interpretability: estimating concept effects when the annotation set is incomplete and latent visual confounders exist. The partitioned latent space formulation is a sensible inductive bias, and the controlled MorphoMNIST experiments demonstrate that structuring $Z_C$ and $Z_S$ can improve ATE estimation in single and shared confounder regimes. However, the paper has notable gaps that fall short of ICLR's bar for theoretical soundness and empirical rigor. The ATE estimation procedure conditions on $X$ rather than properly marginalizing over the confounder distribution, raising concerns about adjustment validity in the presence of imperfect encoders or descendants of confounders. The theoretical connection to proximal identification (completeness conditions, bridge functions) is absent, and the binary constraint on $Z_C$ limits scope. Empirically, while UnCoVAEr wins in simpler confounding settings, it is outperformed by a standard CBM in the multiple confounder case, suggesting fragility under non-linear interactions; this should be analyzed more deeply rather than dismissed. Finally, the lack of statistical significance testing for the reported improvements and limited ablation on key hyperparameters ($\lambda_{MI}$, $Z_C$ dimensionality) weaken the experimental claims. With clearer theoretical justification for the adjustment integral, statistical rigor, and a more candid analysis of failure modes, this work could be a solid ICLR contribution. As it stands, it reads as a promising proof-of-concept that requires stronger causal formalization and more robust evaluation before claiming practical utility for partially annotated datasets.

# Neutral Reviewer
## Balanced Review

### Summary
The paper introduces UnCoVAEr, a structured variational autoencoder that partitions image representations into a discrete confounder-related latent and a continuous residual component to estimate unbiased causal effects of observed concepts under visual latent confounding. By learning a proxy for unobserved confounders and applying backdoor adjustment, the method enables reliable concept-level causal inference and provides a bootstrap-based criterion for detecting confounded concepts. Empirical results on semi-synthetic MorphoMNIST variants show reduced ATE estimation bias and improved out-of-distribution robustness compared to existing concept-based and latent-variable baselines.

### Strengths
1. **Clear Problem Motivating & Well-Defined Causal Setup:** The paper convincingly argues that incomplete concept annotations violate ignorability assumptions and bias concept-effect estimators (Section 1). The causal graph (Figure 2) and formal backdoor adjustment formulation (Eqs. 2-3) are precise and directly map to the proposed architecture.
2. **Principled Architectural Inductive Bias:** Partitioning the latent space into $Z_C$ (discrete confounder proxy) and $Z_S$ (continuous residual) with a CLUB-based mutual information penalty (Section 4) aligns well with proximal causal inference and mitigates the known failure modes of monolithic VAEs like CEVAE. The use of separate auxiliary heads for $C$ and $Y$ further stabilizes the latent structure.
3. **Rigorous Experimental Design & Ablations:** The three semi-synthetic regimes (single, common, multiple confounders) systematically isolate different confounding pathways. Table 1 and Figure 3 show consistent improvements over CaCE, CBM, Res-CBM, and image-adjustment methods, with thorough ablations confirming the necessity of the $Z_C/Z_S$ split and the reconstruction term.
4. **High Reproducibility Transparency:** Appendix A meets ICLR's strong reproducibility expectations: full code, configuration files, dataset generation scripts, explicit hyperparameters, compute resources, and 5-seed averaging with logged randomness are all provided.

### Weaknesses
1. **Limited Dataset Scope & Real-World Validation:** All experiments are confined to MorphoMNIST, a low-dimensional, semi-synthetic benchmark with clean, known causal ground truth. The introduction cites medical imaging and facial attribute datasets, but the empirical section lacks validation on any real-world, partially annotated image benchmark, limiting claims of practical utility.
2. **Lack of Identifiability Guarantees:** While the paper references proximal identification literature, it does not provide formal analysis of when the learned $Z_C$ satisfies adjustment sufficiency (P1) given the variational approximation and MI penalty. Given established critiques of CEVAE's consistency under misspecification (Rissanen & Marttinen, 2021), the absence of theoretical conditions or consistency bounds for partitioned VAEs is a notable gap.
3. **Sensitivity to Hyperparameters & Optimization Regime:** The method relies on careful balancing of $\lambda_C, \lambda_Y, \lambda_{MI}$ and Gumbel-Softmax temperature schedules, yet only a single configuration is reported. VAEs with discrete relaxations and MI regularization are known to be sensitive to these choices, and the paper does not analyze how variations affect posterior collapse, proxy quality, or ATE bias.
4. **Instability in Complex Confounding & Detection False Positives:** Section 6 acknowledges that the per-concept $Z_{C_i}$ variant becomes unstable under multiple confounders, and the detection criterion yields false positives in the single confounder case when naive estimates approximate the true ATE. The paper lacks a diagnostic or fallback strategy for practitioners when the MI regularizer fails or CIs behave unpredictably.

### Novelty & Significance
**Novelty:** The work occupies a well-motivated intersection of concept-based explanation and proximal causal inference. The specific contribution—a partitioned VAE with discrete confounder proxies, continuous residuals, and an explicit MI independence penalty for debiasing concept effects—is a solid architectural and methodological advancement over CEVAE and causal CBMs, which either miss the latent confounding structure or assume observed completeness.
**Significance:** High. Trustworthy concept-level explanations require causal validity, and latent visual confounding is a pervasive threat to auditability in high-stakes domains. UnCoVAEr provides a practical, implementable framework for detecting and correcting such biases, aligning closely with ICLR's emphasis on causal representation learning, interpretability, and robustness. The empirical gains are clear, though broader validation would strengthen real-world relevance.

### Suggestions for Improvement
1. **Broaden Evaluation to Real/Complex Benchmarks:** Include at least one real-world, partially annotated dataset (e.g., CUB-200 with sparse attributes, CelebA for fairness, or a public medical imaging cohort) to demonstrate that the proxy learning and adjustment scale beyond MorphoMNIST's controlled setting.
2. **Formalize Identification & Variational Bound Analysis:** Provide a theorem or structured discussion detailing sufficient conditions under which the $(Z_C, Z_S)$ partition recovers a valid adjustment proxy, explicitly linking the ELBO, MI penalty, and proximal completeness/overlap assumptions. Discuss how the variational gap impacts consistency.
3. **Hyperparameter & Temperature Sensitivity Study:** Report results across a range of $\lambda_{MI}$ and Gumbel temperature schedules. Provide practical guidance (e.g., diagnostic checks for MI convergence or posterior entropy thresholds) to help practitioners stabilize training.
4. **Refine Confounding Detection Metric:** Instead of a binary CI non-overlap test, calibrate detection using a standardized effect-size threshold or report precision/recall/F1 against known confounded/unconfounded concepts across seeds. This will clarify false positive rates and improve practical utility.
5. **Complexity & Scalability Analysis:** Discuss how marginalization over $Z_C$ and the number of auxiliary heads scale with the number of concepts $M$, latent dimensions $K$, and dataset size. Include training/inference time and memory profiling to assess feasibility for larger models.

## How to Improve This Paper

### Missing Experiments (top 3-5 only)
1. Evaluate high-dimensional, realistic image datasets (e.g., CelebA, CUB, or CheXpert with simulated concept incompleteness) because relying solely on 32x32 MorphoMNIST undermines the claim of being a "practical tool for partially annotated image datasets."
2. Include modern deep proximal causal inference baselines (e.g., Deep Proxy Causal Learning, neural maximum moment restriction) because comparing only to CEVAE and CBMs leaves the superiority claim over state-of-the-art proxy methods incomplete.
3. Quantify the confounder-detection accuracy using precision, recall, and F1 against ground-truth confounded labels across all seeds; reporting only qualitative detection rates does not prove the method reliably identifies confounded concepts.
4. Conduct a systematic sensitivity sweep over $Z_C$ dimensionality ($K$) and the MI regularization weight ($\lambda_{\text{MI}}$), as causal VAEs are notoriously unstable; without it, the reported ATE improvements may be artifacts of over-tuning rather than algorithmic robustness.

### Deeper Analysis Needed (top 3-5 only)
1. Quantify mutual information between learned $Z_C$ and the true ground-truth confounders vs. $Z_S$, because without proving $Z_C$ selectively captures the causal factors rather than noise, the backdoor adjustment rests on an unverified architectural assumption.
2. Empirically verify d-separation by testing whether $(C_i \perp Y \mid Z_C, C_{-i})$ actually holds in the learned representation; if conditional independence fails, the backdoor formula is mathematically invalid and the causal claims collapse.
3. Evaluate robustness to concept annotation noise and continuous concept misspecification, since real-world annotations are never perfectly binary oracle labels; without this, the method's deployability in realistic settings remains unproven.

### Visualizations & Case Studies
1. Show counterfactual image generations by traversing $Z_C$ while holding $Z_S$ constant to demonstrate whether $Z_C$ aligns with true morphological confounders (e.g., thickness), because abstract metrics cannot verify causal disentanglement without visual proxy validation.
2. Plot the full observational $p(Y|C)$ distribution, UnCoVAEr-adjusted $p(Y|do(C))$, and Oracle ground-truth side-by-side per concept, as visualizing bias reduction directly validates the MAE table beyond averaged scalars.
3. Provide concrete failure-case visualizations for the XOR confounding regime to show whether $Z_C$ entangles with irrelevant features or collapses to $Z_S$, exposing the exact breakdown point of the partitioned latent assumption.

### Obvious Next Steps
1. Formalize identifiability conditions for the $Z_C/Z_S$ partition under VAE variational inference, or empirically demonstrate recoverability across initialization seeds, because unconstrained deep latent models cannot guarantee true confounder recovery without strict causal inductive biases.
2. Develop and validate a scalable training scheme for $Z_C$ when $>5$ concepts exist, as the reported per-concept proxy variant becomes unstable; without a path to scaling, the method is limited to toy concept sets.
3. Isolate and test the contribution of the auxiliary outcome head $q_{\xi_Y}$ to the ATE estimates by freezing or ablating it during inference, to rule out label leakage or shortcut learning that artificially reduces adjustment error.

# Final Consolidated Review
## Summary
The paper introduces UnCoVAEr, a partitioned variational autoencoder that isolates discrete confounder-related latents ($Z_C$) from continuous residual latents ($Z_S$) to estimate unbiased causal effects of human-interpretable concepts under visual latent confounding. By learning a confounder proxy from partially annotated images and applying backdoor adjustment, the method simultaneously corrects Average Treatment Effect (ATE) estimates and flags confounded concepts via a bootstrap heuristic. Evaluated across three controlled MorphoMNIST confounding regimes, UnCoVAEr consistently reduces ATE bias and demonstrates improved out-of-distribution robustness compared to CEVAE, CBM, and image-adjustment baselines.

## Strengths
- **Principled architectural inductive bias:** The explicit partitioning of the latent space into confounder-specific and residual components, regularized via CLUB-based mutual information minimization, directly targets the known failure modes of monolithic latent-variable models (e.g., CEVAE) and provides a clean structural prior for proxy learning.
- **Rigorous experimental design & thorough ablations:** The three confounding variants (single, common, multiple) systematically isolate distinct causal pathways. Table 1 and Figure 3, combined with ablations that validate the necessity of the $Z_C/Z_S$ split and the reconstruction term, clearly demonstrate where and why the method succeeds.
- **Strong reproducibility & OOD robustness:** The codebase includes full implementation details, configurations, dataset scripts, compute logs, and 5-seed reproducibility. Moreover, the consistent performance gap when confounding strength shifts (ID $\alpha=0.9$ $\to$ OOD $\alpha=0.6$) indicates the learned $Z_C$ captures stable causal structure rather than overfitting spurious correlations.

## Weaknesses
- **Missing empirical diagnostics for proxy validity:** While the method assumes $Z_C$ satisfies adjustment sufficiency (blocks backdoor paths), the paper does not quantify how tightly $Z_C$ aligns with true ground-truth confounders (e.g., mutual information or correlation metrics), nor does it empirically verify the critical d-separation property $(C_i \perp Y \mid Z_C, C_{-i})$. Without these diagnostics, the reduction in ATE error could theoretically stem from favorable dataset structure rather than valid causal isolation, weakening confidence in the adjustment procedure's generalizability.
- **Fragility under interacting/non-linear confounders & detection instability:** In the multiple-confounder (XOR) regime, UnCoVAEr is notably outperformed by naive/CBM baselines, and the per-concept proxy variant becomes unstable. Additionally, the bootstrap confounding-detection heuristic yields false positives in weak-confounding settings. This demonstrates that the partitioned VAE inductive bias struggles when confounders interact non-additively, limiting reliability in complex real-world scenarios.
- **Theoretical gap in identifiability & continuous confounder restriction:** The method constrains $Z_C$ to be strictly binary (P3), which inherently limits applicability to continuous or graded visual confounders (e.g., lighting intensity, demographic continua). More critically, while proximal causal identification requires specific completeness/rank conditions, the paper does not formalize or empirically verify when the variational approximation guarantees $Z_C$ acts as a sufficient proxy. Given established critiques of CEVAE consistency under misspecification, the absence of identifiability bounds or bridge-function analysis leaves the theoretical grounding underdeveloped for ICLR.

## Nice-to-Haves
- Provide a hyperparameter sensitivity sweep over $\lambda_{MI}$, $K$ ($Z_C$ dimensionality), and Gumbel-Softmax temperature schedules to demonstrate training robustness and guide practitioners.
- Report formal statistical significance testing (e.g., paired bootstrap tests on MAE differences across seeds) to certify that performance gains over baselines are not driven by high variance.
- Discuss computational and architectural scaling with larger concept sets ($M$) and high-resolution images, including memory/time profiling for marginalization over $Z_C$.
- Clarify in the limitations how the strong assumption $Y \perp X \mid C, Z_C$ may be violated in natural images with rich unannotated predictive features, and how practitioners might diagnose such violations.

## Novel Insights
The paper effectively illustrates that structuring the latent space into confounder-specific and residual components via mutual information regularization provides a robust inductive bias for isolating causal signals from visual proxies. The empirical finding that naive associative models can outperform structured causal estimators under specific non-linear (XOR) confounding regimes highlights a critical tension between statistical exploitability and causal validity, underscoring the need for explicit diagnostic checks when deploying causal interpretability tools in the wild.

## Suggestions
- Add empirical diagnostics that explicitly measure the alignment of $Z_C$ with ground-truth confounders (e.g., MI/correlation) and test the assumed conditional independence d-separation property on held-out data to verify adjustment validity.
- Formalize or empirically map the relationship between the learned proxy and proximal causal identification conditions (completeness/overlap) to clarify the theoretical guarantees of the partitioned ELBO.
- Expand the analysis of the XOR failure mode: identify the precise structural conditions that cause the proxy to break down, and propose a practical fallback or diagnostic threshold for end-users.
- Include the recommended hyperparameter sensitivity analysis to prove that the method's gains are algorithmic rather than artifacts of careful tuning.
