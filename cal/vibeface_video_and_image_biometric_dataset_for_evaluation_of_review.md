# Harsh Critic Review
## Section-by-Section Critical Review

### Title & Abstract
- **Does the title accurately reflect the contribution?** Yes, though it is somewhat generic. It does not highlight the dataset's core differentiators: demographic balancing, controlled eKYC-style video capture, or the specific focus on fairness and robustness in compliance-driven settings.
- **Does the abstract clearly state the problem, method, and key results?** The problem (lack of realistic eKYC video data and demographic imbalance in existing datasets) and method (50 subjects, multimodal capture across devices/lighting) are clear. However, the "key results" are framed as establishing a "new benchmark for evaluating robustness and fairness" without stating any quantitative findings or demonstrating the dataset's actual utility in revealing novel model behaviors.
- **Are any claims in the abstract unsupported by the paper?** The claim that VIBEFACE "establishes a new benchmark" is premature. A true benchmark requires standardized evaluation protocols, statistical validation, and often accompanying code/reproducibility infrastructure. The paper only provides basic accuracy rates at a single threshold, which falls short of ICLR's expectations for a rigorous dataset contribution.

### Introduction & Motivation
- **Is the problem well-motivated? Is the gap in prior work clearly identified?** The motivation is clear and practically relevant: eKYC workflows demand dynamic video verification under unconstrained conditions, and existing datasets largely consist of static images or lack demographic/ethical compliance. The gap in eKYC-style video benchmarks is well-articulated.
- **Are the contributions clearly stated and accurate?** Contributions are explicitly listed. However, they overstate the dataset's immediate impact by implying it solves fairness benchmarking. With only 50 subjects, the dataset cannot reliably measure demographic bias at a statistically meaningful level, a point the introduction glosses over.
- **Does the introduction over-claim or under-sell?** It slightly over-claims regarding the dataset's ability to support "unbiased and practically deployable facial verification systems." Real-world deployment involves network constraints, compression artifacts, active spoofing attacks, and highly variable user behavior, none of which are captured in this studio-controlled collection. The introduction would benefit from explicitly scoping the dataset's intended use to controlled protocol evaluation rather than full deployment readiness.

### Method / Approach
- **Is the method clearly described and reproducible?** The acquisition protocol, device types, lighting conditions, scenario definitions (Sec 3.2), and session structure (Sec 3.3) are well-documented. Ethical compliance and licensing (Sec 3.4, 3.5) are thoroughly addressed. This section is generally strong for reproducibility.
- **Are key assumptions stated and justified?** The paper assumes that a controlled studio environment with deliberate lighting/pose variations can adequately simulate real-world eKYC conditions. This is a significant assumption that is not critically justified. Studio lighting lacks the dynamic range, mixed illuminants, and motion blur of actual mobile capture, and operator-supervised sessions reduce the natural behavioral variance seen in unsupervised eKYC.
- **Are there logical gaps in the derivation or reasoning?** The design intentionally excludes spoofing/presentation attacks, framing the dataset as a bona fide resource. However, eKYC security relies intrinsically on liveness detection and PAD. Omitting attack types creates a logical gap between the stated application domain (eKYC) and the dataset's actual coverage (only bona fide data).
- **Are there edge cases or failure modes not discussed?** The protocol handles occlusion via eyeglasses and weak light, but fails to account for real-world edge cases critical to eKYC: heavy makeup variations, facial masks, extreme motion blur, video compression artifacts from network upload, and heterogeneous camera ISP pipelines (despite using 3 phones). The metadata collection relies on self-reported race and gender, which introduces potential noise but is standard; however, no discussion of how demographic self-identification might affect fairness analysis is provided.

### Experiments & Results
- **Do the experiments actually test the paper's claims?** The experiments test face detection and verification across conditions and demographics, which aligns partially with robustness claims. However, they do not rigorously test fairness or eKYC generalization. There is no cross-device or cross-session generalization analysis, nor any analysis of how video temporal dynamics affect verification compared to single-frame evaluation.
- **Are baselines appropriate and fairly compared?** The chosen baselines (MTCNN, RetinaFace, MediaPipe for detection; ArcFace, MagFace for verification) are standard but notably outdated for an ICLR submission. Modern robust/fair models (e.g., FaceViT, AdaFace, or recent WebFace-pretrained architectures) and stronger detectors (e.g., SCRFD, YOLOv8-Face) are absent, making it difficult to gauge current model performance or dataset difficulty.
- **Are there missing ablations that would materially change conclusions?** Yes. Critical missing analyses include: (1) video-score aggregation methodology (frame-level to subject-level), (2) cross-session generalization (e.g., enroll in Session A, verify in Session D/E), (3) impact of resolution/compression differences across devices, and (4) statistical significance testing for demographic disparities.
- **Are error bars / statistical significance reported?** No. All tables report point estimates (proportions/percentages) without confidence intervals, bootstrap estimates, or hypothesis testing. Given the cohort size of 50, confidence intervals would be extremely wide, making demographic fairness claims statistically fragile.
- **Do the results support the claims made, or are they cherry-picked?** The results are descriptive but rely on a non-standard evaluation protocol. Reporting verification success at a **fixed threshold of 0.5** (Sec 4.2) is a critical methodological flaw for face recognition. ICLR and biometrics standards require ROC curves, DET analysis, TAR@FPR@10^-3, or EER. A fixed threshold conflates model calibration with model performance and makes cross-model comparison unreliable. The results also lack a clear protocol for how video frames are sampled, scored, and aggregated.
- **Are datasets and evaluation metrics appropriate?** The dataset structure is interesting but undersized for modern representation learning benchmarking. The metric choice (fixed-threshold accuracy) is inappropriate for biometric verification, undermining the validity of Tables 3 and 4.

### Writing & Clarity
- **Are there sections that are confusing or poorly explained?** The aggregation of frame-level results in Tables 3 and 4 is unclear. It is not specified whether the reported values are macro-averages across subjects, subject-level micro-averages, or session-weighted means. The text mentions grouping image scenarios into OAV and FV, but the table rows show both scenario-specific (12-16) and grouped (OAV/FV) rows without explaining the relationship, causing confusion in result interpretation.
- **Are figures and tables clear and informative?** Figure 1's demographic pyramid and racial distribution are clear. Tables 1 and 2 effectively summarize dataset properties. Tables 3 and 4 are dense but lack column headers for gender and age group alignment, making it slightly difficult to parse the demographic breakdown at a glance. The figures and tables adequately illustrate the protocol but would benefit from explicit captions clarifying how multi-device data was pooled.

### Limitations & Broader Impact
- **Do the authors acknowledge the key limitations?** No. There is no dedicated Limitations section. The Conclusion briefly mentions potential for PAD research but does not acknowledge the fundamental constraints of the work.
- **Are there fundamental limitations they missed?** Several critical limitations are omitted: (1) 50 subjects severely limit statistical power and intra-class variability, making robustness/fairness claims speculative; (2) studio-controlled acquisition limits ecological validity for real eKYC deployment; (3) lack of presentation attacks spoofs limits utility for security-critical eKYC pipelines; (4) fixed-threshold evaluation prevents proper calibration of verification trade-offs.
- **Are there failure modes or negative societal impacts not discussed?** The paper discusses GDPR and consent thoroughly but does not address the risk that small, artificially balanced datasets can create a false sense of fairness. Models benchmarked on VIBEFACE may perform well in this controlled, balanced setting but degrade catastrophically in deployment where demographics are skewed and environmental conditions are uncontrolled. This deployment gap and its potential for algorithmic harm are not discussed.

### Overall Assessment
VIBEFACE addresses a practically relevant gap in the lack of eKYC-style video datasets with explicit demographic balancing and ethical compliance, and its detailed acquisition protocol offers a solid foundation for the community. However, in its current form, it falls short of ICLR's rigorous standards for dataset papers. The cohort size of 50 subjects severely limits statistical power for meaningful fairness or robustness claims. The evaluation methodology contains a fundamental flaw: using a fixed similarity threshold (0.5) instead of standard operating curves (ROC/DET, TAR@FPR, or EER) invalidates the verification comparisons and conflates model architecture with calibration artifacts. The exclusion of liveness/PAD data weakens the connection to the stated eKYC application, and the controlled studio environment, despite varied lighting, lacks the chaotic conditions of real mobile authentication. A dedicated Limitations section, inclusion of modern baselines, proper statistical reporting (confidence intervals), cross-session/device ablation, and standard biometric evaluation metrics are necessary before the contribution can be considered robust enough for ICLR.

# Neutral Reviewer
## Balanced Review

### Summary
The paper introduces VIBEFACE, a multimodal facial biometric dataset comprising 2,250 images and 1,550 videos from 50 demographically balanced subjects. The dataset is explicitly designed to simulate electronic Know Your Client (eKYC) workflows, capturing diverse poses, lighting conditions, and behavioral actions (e.g., blinking, head rotation, face touching) under mobile device constraints. The authors provide baseline evaluations of modern face detection and verification models to demonstrate the dataset's utility in exposing performance degradation across challenging conditions and demographic subgroups.

### Strengths
1. **Well-Designed Real-World Protocol:** The eKYC scenario design (Scenarios 12–18) captures natural user interactions and liveness cues (blinking, expressions, partial occlusion, directed gaze), offering a practical testbed for video-based verification beyond static image benchmarks.
2. **Rigorous Demographic & Environmental Balancing:** The dataset explicitly balances participants across gender (25/25), race (~12–13 per group), age (18–69), and Fitzpatrick skin tones, while systematically varying lighting and eyeglass occlusion across five controlled sessions (Section 3.3, Table 2).
3. **Strong Ethical & Legal Foundations:** Full compliance with GDPR and the EU AI Act, explicit informed consent, anonymization, controlled-access licensing, and participant withdrawal rights directly address the privacy and consent controversies that have led to the withdrawal of many legacy web-scraped datasets (Section 3.4).
4. **Transparent Baseline Benchmarking:** The structured evaluation of state-of-the-art detectors (RetinaFace, MediaPipe, MTCNN) and verifiers (ArcFace, MagFace) clearly identifies failure modes in weak lighting, off-angle views, and glasses occlusion, with per-dimension breakdowns in Tables 3 and 4.

### Weaknesses
1. **Limited Scale for Modern ML Evaluation:** At only 50 identities, the dataset lacks the statistical power required to train or fine-tune contemporary deep architectures, train robust fairness-aware models, or reliably quantify subgroup performance variance, which falls short of typical ICLR dataset scale expectations.
2. **Shallow Verification Protocol:** Experiments rely on a single fixed threshold (0.5) and report raw accuracy percentages rather than standard biometric metrics (e.g., ROC/AUC, EER, FMR/FNMR curves). No cross-scenario or cross-session train/test splits are defined, making it unclear how models would generalize in practice.
3. **Underutilization of Video Modality:** Videos are evaluated by extracting frames at a uniform 6 FPS, discarding temporal dynamics that are central to eKYC tasks. No temporal or sequence-modeling architectures are benchmarked, limiting insights into video-based verification or presentation attack detection.
4. **Lack of Statistical Rigor in Fairness Analysis:** Demographic performance differences are reported as point estimates without confidence intervals, variance measures, or statistical significance tests. Claims regarding bias or model fairness therefore lack quantitative validation.

### Novelty & Significance
**Novelty:** Moderate. The explicit eKYC workflow simulation, combined with controlled multi-condition acquisition and ethical sourcing, differentiates VIBEFACE from larger but legally ambiguous datasets. However, dataset collection itself is incremental, and the core novelty lies in scenario design rather than methodological innovation.  
**Clarity:** High. The paper is well-structured, with precise descriptions of sessions, scenarios, demographic metadata, and experimental setups. Tables and protocol descriptions are easy to follow and align closely with ICLR's expectation for transparent dataset documentation.  
**Reproducibility:** Moderate. Capture devices, resolution standards, format conversions, and baseline model choices are clearly stated. However, the absence of official train/val/test splits, open evaluation code, and detailed hyperparameter/threshold justification limits immediate reproducibility by independent researchers.  
**Significance:** Moderate for core machine learning, but High for applied biometrics and regulated AI. The dataset's small scale restricts broad algorithmic impact, but it provides a valuable, legally compliant benchmark for fairness auditing, domain-gap analysis, and liveness/eKYC protocol testing. Its significance would increase if positioned explicitly as an evaluation/auditing resource rather than a training dataset.

### Suggestions for Improvement
1. **Adopt Standard Biometric Evaluation Metrics:** Supplement fixed-threshold accuracy with ROC curves, EER, FMR/FNMR equal-error rates, and calibration analysis to meet ICLR's empirical rigor standards and enable direct comparison with literature.
2. **Define Official Data Splits & Open Evaluation Code:** Provide explicit train/validation/test partitions (e.g., leave-one-subject-out or cross-session splits) and release evaluation scripts to prevent data leakage and ensure reproducible benchmarking.
3. **Benchmark Temporal/Video-Centric Models:** Instead of frame-wise subsampling, evaluate sequence-aware architectures (e.g., 3D CNNs, temporal pooling, or vision transformers with positional/temporal attention) to fully leverage the eKYC video modality and assess dynamic behavior modeling.
4. **Strengthen Statistical Reporting:** Include 95% confidence intervals, variance estimates, or non-parametric tests for demographic subgroup comparisons. This will substantiate fairness claims and allow reviewers to distinguish meaningful bias from sampling noise.
5. **Clarify Intended ML Positioning:** Explicitly frame VIBEFACE as a protocol-compliance and fairness-auditing benchmark rather than a training corpus. Discuss use cases such as domain adaptation, bias mitigation evaluation, or synthetic data validation to align the dataset's scope with realistic ICLR community applications.

# Spark Finder Review
## How to Improve This Paper

### Missing Experiments (top 3-5 only)
1. Impostor/Genuine Pair Evaluation with ROC/DET Curves: The verification task only reports same-subject accuracy using an arbitrary fixed threshold; add a protocol that samples genuine and impostor pairs and reports FAR, FRR, and EER, because real biometric verification claims cannot be validated without open-set evaluation.
2. Cross-Subject Splits & Leakage Prevention: The current benchmark appears to evaluate on the same 50 subjects without held-out sets; implement K-fold or leave-one-subject-out splits for both detection and verification to prove generalization to unseen identities, as intra-subject matching artificially inflates results.
3. SOTA Model Fine-Tuning & Domain Transfer: Testing only pre-trained ArcFace and MagFace off-the-shelf is insufficient; add experiments that fine-tune modern recognition architectures (e.g., AdaFace, ViT-based backbones) on VIBEFACE and evaluate cross-dataset transfer to standard benchmarks, because a dataset's utility hinges on its ability to drive measurable algorithmic gains.

### Deeper Analysis Needed (top 3-5 only)
1. Statistical Significance & Confidence Intervals: Demographic and session-wise accuracy drops are reported as single point estimates without variance; compute bootstrapped confidence intervals or apply paired statistical tests to prove that observed disparities are not sampling artifacts of a 50-subject cohort.
2. Threshold Calibration & Score Distribution Analysis: Relying on a hardcoded 0.5 similarity threshold invalidates fair cross-model comparison; provide threshold sweeps, score distribution plots, and minimum classification error analysis, because ArcFace and MagFace produce fundamentally different embedding scales.
3. Temporal Consistency in eKYC Scenarios: Verification and detection are evaluated at fixed 6 FPS, ignoring video dynamics; analyze frame-to-frame score stability and action-specific failure patterns to confirm the dataset actually captures eKYC temporal challenges rather than acting as a collection of independent frames.

### Visualizations & Case Studies (top 3-5 only)
1. Embedding Space Clustering (t-SNE/UMAP): Visualize identity embeddings across all five sessions per subject to reveal whether environmental variations (glasses, weak light) cause intra-class dispersion to exceed inter-class separation, directly evidencing benchmark difficulty.
2. Failure Case Gallery Across Conditions: Provide side-by-side visual examples of detection drops and verification false rejections for each challenging session, because without qualitative evidence, it is impossible to distinguish whether failures stem from pose, illumination, occlusion, or model architecture limits.

### Obvious Next Steps
1. Adopt Standard Biometric Evaluation Frameworks: Align the benchmark with ISO/IEC 19795 or NIST FRVT protocols (gallery/probe definitions, impostor generation, DET/EIR reporting), otherwise the dataset will be unusable for actual compliance or production benchmarking.
2. Scale Justification or Cohort Expansion: 50 subjects is orders of magnitude below modern face recognition standards; provide a learning-curve ablation demonstrating predictable performance scaling with subject count, or explicitly limit claims to a "proof-of-concept" dataset.
3. Operationalize PAD/Deepfake Claims or Remove Them: The conclusion positions VIBEFACE for presentation attack and injection attack research but provides zero attack samples; add replay/print/mask baselines to substantiate the claim, or excise the PAD promise to maintain technical credibility.

# Final Consolidated Review
## Summary
VIBEFACE introduces a multimodal facial biometric dataset specifically designed to evaluate verification algorithms under electronic Know Your Client (eKYC) workflows. It features 50 demographically balanced subjects captured across five systematic sessions varying lighting, eyeglass occlusion, and consumer devices, yielding 2,250 images and 1,550 videos. The dataset uniquely pairs static imagery with scripted interactive video protocols (e.g., head rotation, blinking, partial occlusion) and provides baseline evaluations that expose model failure modes across environmental and demographic conditions.

## Strengths
- **Targeted eKYC Scenario Design:** Unlike traditional datasets focused on static frontal imagery, VIBEFACE explicitly models dynamic verification behaviors (Scenarios 12–18) such as directed gaze, expression shifts, and hand-mediated occlusion. This directly addresses a critical gap in compliance-driven biometric evaluation where liveness and user interaction dynamics are essential.
- **Rigorous Ethical and Demographic Balancing:** The dataset enforces explicit 50/50 gender parity, proportional representation across four racial categories, age stratification (18–69), and Fitzpatrick skin tone coverage. Coupled with GDPR/AI Act compliance, explicit informed consent, and controlled-access licensing, it provides a legally sound, reproducible alternative to ethically problematic web-scraped corpora.
- **Transparent, Condition-Isolating Baselines:** The controlled session design cleanly separates environmental variables (flash, weak natural light, glasses). The resulting per-condition breakdowns in Tables 3 and 4 clearly demonstrate how standard detectors and verification models degrade under specific real-world constraints, offering immediate audit value for practitioners.

## Weaknesses
- **Fixed-Threshold Verification Metric Undermines Cross-Model Comparison:** Section 4.2 evaluates ArcFace and MagFace using a hardcoded similarity threshold of 0.5, reporting results as simple accuracy proportions. Different face recognition architectures produce fundamentally different cosine similarity distributions and calibration curves. A fixed threshold conflates model calibration with discriminative capability, making it impossible to determine whether observed performance gaps reflect true architectural weaknesses or merely threshold misalignment. This obscures security-critical trade-offs (false acceptance vs. false rejection) that are essential for biometric benchmarking.
- **Underutilization of the Video Modality:** Despite positioning the dataset around eKYC video workflows, the evaluation treats videos as independent frame collections sampled at a fixed 6 FPS (Sec 4.1). This discards the temporal coherence, motion dynamics, and sequential action patterns that are central to eKYC verification and liveness detection. Without sequence-aware evaluation or at least temporal consistency analysis, the dataset's primary differentiator from static imaging collections is not fully leveraged or validated.

## Nice-to-Haves
- **Statistical Reporting for Demographic Breakdowns:** Given the cohort size, reporting demographic performance disparities as point estimates without confidence intervals or variance metrics makes it difficult to distinguish meaningful bias from sampling noise. Adding 95% CIs or non-parametric tests would solidify any fairness-oriented claims.
- **Explicit Cross-Subject/Generalization Protocol:** While the benchmark focuses on evaluating off-the-shelf models on the full cohort, defining a formal evaluation protocol (e.g., leave-one-session-out or cross-subject splits) and clarifying whether Tables 3/4 report macro- or micro-averages would prevent accidental data leakage and improve reproducibility for subsequent researchers.
- **Temporal/Spatial Failure Analysis:** Visualizing embedding stability across frames within a single video sequence, or providing qualitative failure galleries per session, would help the community diagnose whether degradation stems from illumination, pose, occlusion, or model architecture limits.

## Novel Insights
VIBEFACE effectively reframes facial biometric dataset design away from internet-scale accumulation and toward procedural realism and ethical auditability. By treating the collection as a controlled stress-test rather than a training corpus, the authors demonstrate that carefully structured environmental and interaction protocols can systematically expose operational vulnerabilities in production-ready models. This positions the dataset as a compliance and fairness auditing tool, highlighting that robust biometric deployment requires not just larger data, but better-structured evaluation scenarios that mirror actual user workflows.

## Suggestions
- Replace the fixed-threshold accuracy metric with standard biometric evaluation protocols: report ROC/DET curves, Equal Error Rates (EER), or True Acceptance Rate @ fixed False Rejection/Acceptance Rates. This is a minimal change that will immediately strengthen the validity of Tables 3 and 4 enable fair cross-model comparison.
- Clarify the frame-level aggregation methodology in Tables 3 and 4 (e.g., explicitly state whether values represent micro-averages across all frames or macro-averages per subject) to ensure precise interpretation of demographic and session-wise performance.
- When framing claims about fairness or real-world eKYC readiness, explicitly calibrate the language to the 50-subject scale and controlled studio environment to prevent overextrapolation to uncontrolled mobile deployment scenarios.
