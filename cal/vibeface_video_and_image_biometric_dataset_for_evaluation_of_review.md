# Harsh Critic Review
## Section-by-Section Critical Review

### Title & Abstract
- **Does the title accurately reflect the contribution?** The title is descriptive but slightly awkward ("Evaluation of Faces" rather than "Evaluation of Facial Recognition Systems" or "Face Verification"). It accurately signals a dataset focused on video and image modalities.
- **Does the abstract clearly state the problem, method, and key results?** Yes. It clearly identifies the gap (lack of eKYC-style datasets with demographic balance), describes VIBEFACE (2,250 images, 1,550 videos, 50 subjects, mobile-captured, ethically sourced), and summarizes the intended use.
- **Are any claims in the abstract unsupported by the paper?** The claim that the dataset "establishes a new benchmark for evaluating the robustness and fairness of biometric verification systems" is currently overstated. The paper lacks standardized biometric evaluation protocols (e.g., ROC/DET curves, impostor trials, statistical significance testing across demographics) and the scale (N=50) is exceptionally small for modern representation learning or fairness benchmarking. These limitations are not hinted at in the abstract but are critical to evaluating whether a true "benchmark" has been established.

### Introduction & Motivation
- **Is the problem well-motivated? Is the gap in prior work clearly identified?** The motivation is strong. The rise of eKYC, its reliance on unconstrained mobile video, and the historical lack of ethically sourced, demographically balanced datasets with explicit verification workflow simulation are well-identified gaps. Prior work references (VGGFace2, SOTERIA, etc.) adequately contextualize the landscape.
- **Are the contributions clearly stated and accurate?** Contributions are clearly listed (multimodal eKYC simulation, demographic balance across age/race/gender, ethical compliance, baseline results). However, the introduction frames the dataset as ready for "fair and generalizable verification systems" without acknowledging the controlled-studio acquisition paradigm (detailed in Section 3) or the limited subject pool. This creates a subtle mismatch between the ambitious claims and the actual data scope.
- **Does the introduction over-claim or under-sell?** It slightly over-claims regarding real-world ecological validity and benchmark readiness, while under-selling the specific, controlled nature of the data. A more precise scoping (e.g., "controlled eKYC simulation dataset" rather than implicit real-world unconstrained capture) would align better with the methodology.

### Method / Approach
- **Is the method clearly described and reproducible?** The acquisition protocol is well-documented: 5 participants demographics, 5 lighting/occlusion sessions, 10 photo scenarios, 9 video scenarios, 3 specific smartphone models, format conversions, and consistent operator supervision. Another researcher could replicate the collection pipeline.
- **Are key assumptions stated and justified?** A critical contradiction exists between the stated motivation ("unconstrained conditions - at home, in variable lighting, and across heterogeneous mobile devices" in Section 1) and the actual methodology ("controlled studio environment, each session in a separate room specifically arranged to ensure consistent experimental conditions" in Section 3). While simulating eKYC workflows via subject instructions is valid, the assumption that studio-captured data with controlled lighting adequately proxies real-world eKYC variability needs explicit justification or careful scoping. Additionally, the flash session (Session B) exclusively uses the rear camera, meaning there is no front-facing flash simulation despite this being common in document/eKYC workflows.
- **Are there logical gaps in the derivation or reasoning?** The dataset design intentionally mixes acquisition devices and lighting/glasses conditions, which is sound. However, using the reference image from Session B (flash, rear camera) as the sole enrollment template for verification (Section 4.2) creates a significant cross-modal mismatch with all query samples (front camera, varying lighting). While this mimics real-world ID-to-live matching, the methodological choice dramatically inflates difficulty and should be explicitly discussed as an experimental design constraint rather than a standard setup.
- **Edge cases/failure modes:** The dataset includes hand occlusion (scenario 17) and expressive dynamics, but these are excluded from the benchmark (Section 4.1). Excluding them is reasonable for detection, but their absence from the verification evaluation leaves a gap in how the claimed eKYC dynamics are actually stress-tested.

### Experiments & Results
- **Do the experiments actually test the paper's claims?** They partially test robustness and fairness by reporting performance across lighting, glasses, demographics, and scenarios. However, the evaluation protocol lacks the rigor required for a biometrics verification benchmark.
- **Are baselines appropriate and fairly compared?** The baselines (MTCNN, RetinaFace, MediaPipe for detection; ArcFace, MagFace for verification) are historically important but outdated for a 2026 submission. Modern face verification relies on ViT-based architectures, magnitude-aware losses (e.g., Sub-Center ArcFace, AdaFace), and self-supervised pretraining. Evaluating only two models from 2019/2021 weakens the claim of providing a contemporary benchmark.
- **Are there missing ablations that would materially change conclusions?** Crucially, **impostor trials are entirely missing**. Section 4.2 states verification success is the "percentage of frames in which the face was correctly authenticated" using a fixed threshold of 0.5. This implies only genuine (1:1 same-identity) matches were tested. Without impostor pairs, False Acceptance Rate (FAR) cannot be computed, rendering False Match/Non-Match analysis, EER, or TAR@FAR impossible. This is a fundamental flaw for any verification benchmark.
- **Error bars / statistical significance:** None are reported. Performance differences across age/race groups (e.g., Caucasians performing worse in Table 4, or MTCNN dropping for African descent) are reported as point estimates without variance, confidence intervals, or statistical tests. Given the demographic focus, this omission severely undermines the fairness claims.
- **Do the results support the claims / cherry-picked?** Results are not cherry-picked, but the metric choice inflates perceived performance. Frame-level accuracy over short video sequences ignores temporal correlation; a single subject's video will yield highly dependent frame-wise results, artificially squeezing variance and overstating robustness. Standard biometric evaluation uses trial-level metrics across independent pairs.
- **Datasets and evaluation metrics appropriate?** The dataset is appropriately scoped for controlled simulation, but the metrics are inappropriate. A fixed cosine similarity threshold of 0.5 is arbitrary unless embedding scaling is explicitly justified. For ICLR standards, benchmark papers must provide standardized, reproducible evaluation protocols with proper trade-off curves (ROC/DET) and statistically grounded demographic fairness analysis.

### Writing & Clarity
- **Are there sections that are confusing or poorly explained?** Section 4.2's verification setup is confusing due to the lack of impostor description and the arbitrary threshold. Readers cannot determine whether this is closed-set, open-set, or 1:1 verification, nor how threshold 0.5 corresponds to security operating points.
- **Are figures and tables clear and informative?** Tables 3 and 4 are dense but well-structured. The demographic pyramid and scenario examples (Figures 1-3) effectively communicate dataset diversity and acquisition setup. Table 1's comparison is conceptually clear despite parser formatting artifacts. No clarity issues impede understanding of the core contribution.

### Limitations & Broader Impact
- **Do the authors acknowledge the key limitations?** The paper briefly mentions ethical compliance, controlled access, and the exclusion of certain scenarios from detection testing. It does not explicitly acknowledge the limited scale (N=50), the studio vs. real-world ecological gap, the lack of impostor matching in verification, or the arbitrary fixed threshold.
- **Are there fundamental limitations they missed?** Three critical ones: (1) Scale is too small for training or evaluating modern representation learning models; it is strictly a diagnostic evaluation set. (2) The verification protocol only measures genuine acceptance, missing half of the security equation (false acceptances). (3) The conclusion suggests applicability to Presentation Attack Detection (PAD) and deepfake detection, but the dataset contains zero attack or synthetic samples, making this claim unsupported and potentially misleading.
- **Are there failure modes or negative societal impacts not discussed?** The controlled-access licensing and GDPR/AI Act compliance are well-handled. However, the paper does not discuss the risk of using a 50-subject dataset to certify fairness in deployed biometric systems, which could lead to false confidence in real-world equity. This deployment risk should be noted.

### Overall Assessment
This paper introduces VIBEFACE, a carefully constructed, ethically sourced dataset simulating eKYC workflows with strong demographic balancing. The acquisition protocol is systematic, and the intent to support fairness and robustness research in facial biometrics is clear. However, as submitted to ICLR, the paper currently falls short of the bar for benchmark contributions. The evaluation protocol lacks fundamental biometric standards: there are no impostor trials, no ROC/DET analysis, no statistical significance testing across demographics, and an arbitrary fixed verification threshold. Combined with an outdated baseline selection, frame-level correlated metrics, and a studio-controlled collection paradigm that contradicts the "unconstrained" claims in the introduction, the results cannot robustly support the assertion that VIBEFACE establishes a new verification or fairness benchmark. Additionally, the suggestion that the dataset can be used for PAD/deepfake research is unsupported due to the absence of attack samples. The dataset itself has merit as a targeted, small-scale diagnostic resource, but the paper requires substantial revision: scoping claims to match the dataset's scale and controlled nature, incorporating standard 1:1 verification protocols with impostor pairs, adding statistical rigor to demographic analysis, and updating baselines to modern architectures.

# Neutral Reviewer
## Balanced Review

### Summary
The paper introduces VIBEFACE, a multimodal facial dataset specifically designed to evaluate face verification algorithms under realistic electronic Know Your Client (eKYC) conditions while addressing demographic fairness. It contains 2,250 still images and 1,550 videos captured from 50 demographically balanced subjects across five controlled lighting/device sessions, simulating typical eKYC workflows through dynamic head movements, blinking, and occlusion. The authors provide baseline evaluations for face detection and verification across standard models, demonstrating how environmental conditions and demographic attributes impact model performance.

### Strengths
1. **Well-Defined Experimental Protocol**: The dataset features a rigorous, reproducible acquisition design with 10+ explicit scenarios (Section 3.2) and 5 distinct sessions varying lighting, eyeglass usage, and capture devices. This structured approach allows for fine-grained analysis of specific failure modes.
2. **Strong Ethical & Compliance Framework**: Explicit adherence to GDPR and the AI Act, combined with informed consent, anonymization, and controlled-access licensing, directly addresses the community's growing concerns regarding legally and ethically sourced biometric data.
3. **Comprehensive Disaggregated Baselining**: Tables 3 and 4 provide detection and verification rates broken down by session, demographic group, and scenario. This granularity offers immediate diagnostic value for researchers studying demographic bias and environmental robustness.

### Weaknesses
1. **Limited Statistical Scale**: With only 50 unique identities, the dataset lacks the statistical power required to robustly validate claims of demographic fairness or algorithmic generalization. Modern ML benchmarks typically require hundreds to thousands of subjects to yield reliable parity estimates, especially when subdivided across age, race, and gender.
2. **Shallow Evaluation Methodology**: The verification benchmark relies on an arbitrary fixed similarity threshold (0.5) rather than standard biometric metrics. Key measures such as Equal Error Rate (EER), Receiver Operating Characteristic (ROC) curves, True Acceptance Rate at low False Acceptance Rates (e.g., TAR@FAR=10⁻⁴), and statistical hypothesis testing for demographic disparities are absent.
3. **Narrow eKYC Simulation**: While the video scenarios mimic user interactions, they lack actual presentation attack/spoofing samples, liveness ground truth, and document-to-face cross-modal pairs. This limits the dataset's direct applicability to core eKYC validation tasks like Presentation Attack Detection (PAD) or identity document verification.
4. **Baseline Model Coverage**: Only three detectors and two verifiers (ArcFace, MagFace) are evaluated, omitting recent state-of-the-art architectures (e.g., InsightFace, vision-transformer-based face models, or modern quality-aware networks), which weakens the benchmark's relevance to current ML research.

### Novelty & Significance
**Novelty**: The explicit focus on eKYC-style dynamic capture sequences combined with strict demographic balancing and full regulatory compliance is a meaningful contribution. It distinguishes VIBEFACE from large-scale web-scraped datasets and older mobile biometric databases.
**Clarity**: The paper is exceptionally well-organized, with clear definitions of scenarios, sessions, metadata, and evaluation protocols. The narrative logically progresses from motivation to data collection to benchmarking.
**Reproducibility**: Collection protocols and metadata schemas are thoroughly documented, but reproducibility is hampered by the controlled-access license, the absence of standardized train/val/test splits or cross-validation protocols, and the lack of accompanying evaluation code.
**Significance**: The dataset holds moderate significance as a targeted diagnostic tool for stress-testing model robustness to lighting, pose, and demographic shifts under ethically sourced conditions. However, due to its small cohort size and basic evaluation suite, it is unlikely to serve as a primary leaderboard benchmark for mainstream deep face recognition research without expanded scale and standardized metrics.

### Suggestions for Improvement
1. **Adopt Standard Biometric Evaluation Metrics**: Replace the fixed 0.5 threshold with threshold-independent measures (EER, ROC/AUC, DET curves, TAR@FAR). Apply bootstrap confidence intervals or permutation tests to determine whether observed demographic/session performance gaps are statistically significant given the sample size.
2. **Expand Scale or Reframe Scope**: If subject expansion is infeasible, explicitly position VIBEFACE as a high-fidelity *diagnostic/stress-test* benchmark rather than a broad training dataset. Clearly state its appropriate use cases (e.g., controlled ablation studies, bias auditing pipelines) to set accurate community expectations.
3. **Deepen the eKYC Fidelity**: Incorporate bona fide vs. spoof attack labels, liveness challenge ground truth, or paired document-photo sessions. This would align the dataset with actual eKYC compliance pipelines and unlock PAD research as claimed in the conclusion.
4. **Improve Accessibility & Reproducibility**: Release a standardized evaluation script, define explicit data splits or a leave-k-subjects-out cross-validation protocol, and provide tiered licensing that facilitates academic benchmarking while maintaining privacy controls. Consider hosting a hidden test partition for blind community challenges.

# Spark Finder Review
## How to Improve This Paper

### Missing Experiments (top 3-5 only)
1. Standard biometric verification metrics (ROC/DET curves, EER, and FMR@FNMR) evaluated across varied operating points. Using a single arbitrary threshold of 0.5 prevents proper assessment of security trade-offs and fails to meet ICLR/biometric evaluation standards, making the robustness claims unverifiable.
2. Controlled ablation studies isolating specific acquisition variables (e.g., glasses vs. no glasses under identical lighting, or specific lighting conditions across the same device). Because sessions combine multiple confounding factors (lighting, occlusion, pose, device), current results cannot determine which eKYC-specific factor actually drives performance degradation.
3. Presentation Attack Detection (PAD) / Liveness benchmarking using a baseline motion or texture-based method. The paper heavily markets eKYC video utility and claims suitability for PAD research, yet omits even a zero-shot evaluation of standard liveness models on the dynamic behavioral videos, leaving the core use-case unsubstantiated.

### Deeper Analysis Needed (top 3-5 only)
1. Statistical significance testing (e.g., bootstrap confidence intervals or hypothesis tests) for the reported demographic performance disparities. Raw percentage differences on a 50-subject dataset may stem from sampling noise rather than systematic bias, undermining the paper's central fairness contribution.
2. Intra-class vs. inter-class embedding distance analysis across sessions and devices. Without quantifying whether device/lighting variations cause identity embeddings to drift closer to impostor distributions than to genuine matches, the practical deployability of models on this dataset remains theoretically unclear.
3. Critical discussion on the impact of the 50-identity scale on generalization claims. Modern face verification models are trained and evaluated on thousands-to-millions of IDs; the authors must rigorously justify why a 50-subject pool provides reliable benchmarking or explicitly frame the dataset as strictly protocol-bound rather than statistically representative.

### Visualizations & Case Studies (top 3-5 only)
1. DET and ROC curves stratified by racial, age, and gender groups. Aggregate accuracy hides operating-point-specific bias, and these curves would directly expose whether fairness degrades at low false-acceptance regimes critical for high-security eKYC applications.
2. Temporal similarity score trajectories for individual video sequences across the forced eKYC actions (head rotation, blinking, mouth opening). Showing how verification scores fluctuate or collapse during specific mandated motions reveals whether the eKYC protocol actually strengthens verification with temporal aggregation or merely adds noise.
3. Histograms of genuine vs. impostor match scores under challenging conditions (e.g., weak light + glasses). Overlapping distributions would immediately expose score inseparability issues that raw success-rate tables mask, clarifying whether verification failures are due to poor model architecture or fundamentally ambiguous data conditions.

### Obvious Next Steps (top 3-5 only)
1. Define and release a standardized, reproducible evaluation protocol with explicit train/validation/test identity splits. Without frozen partitions and clear cross-identity pairing rules, future researchers cannot legitimately compare against these baselines, violating ICLR dataset reproducibility expectations.
2. Compute and report the in-corpus False Match Rate (FMR) baseline across challenging sessions. Verification accuracy is meaningless for security systems without knowing the impostor false acceptance rate; establishing this baseline is a prerequisite for any dataset claiming practical eKYC utility.
3. Include a fine-tuning experiment showing whether updating ArcFace/MagFace on a held-in subset of VIBEFACE improves performance on the held-out conditions. Dataset papers must demonstrate that collecting new data yields measurable downstream gains; otherwise, the resource provides little value over existing benchmarks.

# Final Consolidated Review
## Summary
This paper introduces VIBEFACE, a demographically balanced, ethically sourced dataset of facial images and short videos designed to simulate electronic Know Your Client (eKYC) onboarding workflows. The authors detail a multi-session, controlled acquisition protocol spanning varied lighting, device models, and explicit behavioral actions, and report baseline face detection and verification results broken down by demographic and environmental conditions.

## Strengths
- **Ethically rigorous and legally compliant documentation:** The dataset directly addresses ethical and privacy failures of historical web-scraped facial corpora through explicit GDPR/AI Act compliance, informed consent procedures, anonymized identifiers, and a controlled-access research license. This sets a responsible, reproducible standard for biometric data collection.
- **Purpose-built eKYC workflow simulation:** Unlike generic recognition datasets, VIBEFACE explicitly structures dynamic video scenarios around real-world mobile verification gestures (mandated blinks, head rotations, hand occlusions). This targeted design provides a clear operational context for stress-testing model robustness under simulated onboarding constraints.

## Weaknesses
- **Fundamentally flawed verification protocol:** Section 4.2 reports only genuine (same-identity) match accuracy at an arbitrary fixed threshold (0.5) with zero impostor trials. Without False Match/Non-Match pairs, it is impossible to compute FAR, EER, or ROC/DET curves. This omission invalidates the core claim that VIBEFACE serves as a verification or security benchmark, as no dataset paper can evaluate biometric robustness without quantifying the trade-off between genuine acceptance and impostor false accepts.
- **Statistical scale severely undermines fairness claims:** With only 50 subjects, demographic strata (race, age, gender) contain as few as 6–8 individuals per subgroup. Reporting raw accuracy disparities across these micro-samples without bootstrap confidence intervals, variance estimates, or hypothesis testing makes it impossible to differentiate true algorithmic bias from sampling noise. The dataset lacks the statistical power to support assertions of fairness or demographic parity.
- **Motivation contradicts acquisition ecology:** The introduction emphasizes evaluating systems under "unconstrained conditions - at home, in variable lighting," yet Section 3 explicitly states data was captured in a "controlled studio environment" with operator supervision, standardized backgrounds, and fixed pose framing. This studio-controlled paradigm fails to capture the unstructured motion blur, chaotic lighting, and extreme pose variations of actual remote eKYC, limiting ecological validity and overstating real-world applicability.
- **Outdated baselines and unsupported downstream claims:** The verification models (ArcFace 2019, MagFace 2021) and detectors are outdated relative to current state-of-the-art, weakening the benchmark's contemporary relevance. Additionally, the conclusion asserts applicability to Presentation Attack Detection (PAD) and deepfake analysis despite the dataset containing exclusively bona-fide, non-spoof samples, which is misleading.

## Nice-to-Haves
- Isolating confounding acquisition variables (e.g., comparing glasses vs. no-glasses under identical lighting within the same session) to disentangle specific degradation factors.
- Conducting ablations on modern architectures (e.g., ViT-based recognizers or quality-aware nets) and including fine-tuning experiments to demonstrate measurable downstream representation improvement.
- Publishing a standardized evaluation script with explicit train/validation/test identity splits to prevent data leakage in future community comparisons.

## Potentially Missed Related Work
- *AdaFace (Kim et al., CVPR 2022) / Sub-Center ArcFace* — relevant as modern magnitude-aware and robust baselines that would better contextualize the reported verification degradation under challenging sessions.
- *Modern Mobile PAD/Liveness Benchmarks (e.g., SiW-B, CelebA-Spoof)* — relevant for positioning VIBEFACE's authentic behavioral sequences against established spoof-aware protocols, clarifying its specific niche in the biometrics landscape.

## Suggestions
1. **Overhaul the verification evaluation:** Incorporate explicit impostor trials, adopt threshold-independent metrics (ROC/DET, EER, TAR@FAR), and apply statistical hypothesis testing (e.g., permutation tests or bootstrapped CIs) to all demographic comparisons. Without this, the fairness and robustness claims are empirically unsupported.
2. **Reframe scope and claims:** Explicitly position VIBEFACE as a *controlled diagnostic/stress-test suite* rather than a general-purpose statistical benchmark. Remove the PAD/deepfake applicability claim unless attack samples are synthesized or added, and acknowledge the studio-controlled acquisition paradigm upfront to align expectations with ecological reality.
3. **Standardize reproducibility infrastructure:** Release frozen cross-identity protocol definitions, train/test splits, and accompanying evaluation code. ICLR dataset contributions require locked evaluation pipelines to prevent protocol drift and ensure future methods can be fairly compared against your baselines.
