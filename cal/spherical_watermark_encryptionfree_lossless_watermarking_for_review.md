# Harsh Critic Review
## Section-by-Section Critical Review

### Title & Abstract
- **Does the title accurately reflect the contribution?** Yes. It correctly highlights the core mechanism (spherical mapping), the claimed properties (encryption-free, lossless), and the target architecture (diffusion models).
- **Does the abstract clearly state the problem, method, and key results?** The abstract efficiently outlines the provenance problem, criticizes prior key-management/cryptographic overhead, and describes the 3-step mapping (binary embedding → spherical projection + orthogonal rotation → chi-square scaling). It clearly states empirical results on fidelity, traceability, and efficiency.
- **Are any claims in the abstract unsupported by the paper?** Two terms warrant clarification: (1) *"Encryption-free"* is used to mean "no per-image stream cipher or nonce," but the method still relies on a fixed secret signature (`K = {T, C}`) shared offline, making it a fixed-key symmetric scheme rather than truly keyless. (2) *"Lossless"* in standard watermarking literature typically implies exact invertibility or zero bit-error without error correction. The method explicitly uses repetition codes (`N=31`) and majority voting for extraction, meaning it relies on redundancy to tolerate inversion noise. The abstract should clarify that "lossless" here refers to *distributional preservation/undetectability* rather than exact, error-free bit recovery without redundancy.

### Introduction & Motivation
- **Is the problem well-motivated? Is the gap in prior work clearly identified?** Yes. The need for scalable, undetectable provenance in diffusion models is well-established. The critique of Gaussian Shading (per-image key overhead) and PRC Watermark (heavy belief-propagation decoding, latency, error floors) accurately pinpoints practical deployment bottlenecks.
- **Are the contributions clearly stated and accurate?** The three contributions are clearly enumerated and align with the paper's content. The shift from heavy cryptographic constructs to geometric/algebraic mapping is a meaningful conceptual step.
- **Does the introduction over-claim or under-sell?** The introduction slightly over-claims by framing the method as universally "lossless" without acknowledging the inherent trade-off between repetition count (`N`), extraction robustness, and payload capacity. It also frames the method as "encryption-free" which, while technically distinguishing it from stream-cipher baselines, may mislead crypto-aware readers into thinking no secret material is required, whereas `T` and `C` are secret keys. A brief clarification here would strengthen the framing.

### Method / Approach
- **Is the method clearly described and reproducible?** Yes. Equations (9)-(13), Algorithm 1, and the signature construction are explicit. The pipeline is modular and reproducible given the fixed seed/keys.
- **Are key assumptions stated and justified?** The method assumes that DDIM/ODE inversion can accurately recover the initial latent `z_T` from the generated image (`z_0`), and that the VAE encoding/decoding process introduces negligible distributional shift relative to `N(0, I)`. While standard for latent-space watermarking, this assumption is the method's primary vulnerability and is only briefly defended via empirical ablations rather than bounded theoretically.
- **Are there logical gaps in the derivation or reasoning?** The theoretical guarantee relies on the input vector being a spherical 3-design (Thm 3.2) and asymptotic convergence via Stein's method (Lemma 3.3). While mathematically sound, the bound on the Wasserstein distance in Eq. (44) is `O(lx^{-1/2})`, which assumes asymptotic dimensionality. For practical `lx = 16384`, this is negligible, but the finite-sample deviation from exact Gaussianity (especially in higher moments) is hand-waved by stating it is "statistically indistinguishable." A tighter finite-sample concentration bound or explicit KL/total-variation bound would strengthen the theoretical claim.
- **Are there edge cases or failure modes not discussed?** The method assumes `N` is odd to avoid majority-vote ties. More critically, it does not discuss what happens when the diffusion model uses non-standard schedulers, high guidance scales, or classifier-free guidance during inversion, all of which are known to increase inversion error and break exact latent recovery. Appendix F.5 shows robustness, but the theoretical limit imposed by inversion non-invertibility lacks discussion.

### Experiments & Results
- **Do the experiments actually test the paper's claims?** Yes. Undetectability is tested via FID and trained MLP/ResNet classifiers. Traceability is tested via ACC and TPR@1%FPR under clean, post-processing, and adversarial (WEvade) settings. Computational efficiency is directly measured.
- **Are baselines appropriate and fairly compared?** Baselines span traditional, latent-based, and state-of-the-art lossless methods. The comparison to PRC and Gaussian Shading is fair. Tables 2, 12-14 consistently show PRC's accuracy drop under capacity/load, while Spherical Watermark maintains high TPR.
- **Are there missing ablations that would materially change conclusions?** The paper ablates modules, `s`, `N`, and solvers. However, it lacks an ablation on **VAE reconstruction/inversion fidelity**. Since extraction depends on inverting the generated image back to `z_T`, the accuracy is fundamentally bounded by VAE error + ODE solver drift. Quantifying extraction success as a function of VAE reconstruction error (or comparing to a method that doesn't require inversion) would clarify the practical ceiling.
- **Are error bars / statistical significance reported?** Mean ± std over 5 runs is provided. While acceptable given diffusion model compute costs, 5 runs is on the lower end for ICLR, particularly for the 4-order-of-magnitude speedup claim in Figure 4. Reporting variance for timing and confirming that the gap remains statistically significant across varying GPU loads would be rigorous.
- **Do the results support the claims made, or are they cherry-picked?** Results appear consistent across datasets and models (SD v1.5, v2.1, v3, FLUX). The adversarial robustness (Table 2, 12-14) convincingly supports the claim that lossless/distribution-preserving schemes resist detector-aware attacks better than lossy schemes. No cherry-picking is evident.
- **Are datasets and evaluation metrics appropriate?** COCO/SDP prompts and SD v1.5/v2.1 are standard. FID, ACC, TPR@1%FPR, and runtime are appropriate. The use of TPR@1%FPR correctly accounts for false alarm constraints in real provenance systems.

### Writing & Clarity
- **Are there sections that are confusing or poorly explained?** The definition of "lossless" in Section 3.1 (Eq. 2-4) conflates computational indistinguishability of the noise prior with exact watermark traceability. The paper uses "lossless" to mean `z_w` is indistinguishable from `z ~ N(0,I)`, which is better termed "undetectable" or "distribution-preserving." This semantic shift from standard watermarking terminology could confuse reviewers/readers.
- **Are figures and tables clear and informative?** Figures 2, 4, 5, and 6 are clear and well-labeled. Table 2 efficiently summarizes the main trade-offs. The only minor clarity issue is Figure 2's y-axis scaling and Figure 5's dense subplots, but these are readable. The appendices are well-structured and directly answer anticipated questions (e.g., Appendix D's AWGN channel analysis, Appendix E's detector-driven optimization proof).

### Limitations & Broader Impact
- **Do the authors acknowledge the key limitations?** Yes. Section 5 notes that higher-order moments may deviate from the true Gaussian prior and that strong inversion-breaking attacks can compromise recovery. They correctly state the scope is provenance, not robust forgery/editing resilience.
- **Are there fundamental limitations they missed?** (1) **Inversion Bottleneck:** The reliance on accurate ODE/VAE inversion is the Achilles' heel of all latent-space watermarking. The paper treats it as practically solved but doesn't discuss how modern diffusion models with rectified flows or non-Gaussian priors (e.g., SDXL's latent space, FLUX's DiT) might violate the `z ~ N(0,I)` assumption, requiring adaptation. (2) **Key Management Trade-off:** While "per-image keys" are eliminated, the fixed signature `K` becomes a single point of failure. If `K` leaks, all watermarks are trivially removable. This is standard for symmetric watermarking, but worth explicit acknowledgment as a security limitation.
- **Are there failure modes or negative societal impacts not discussed?** Beyond provenance, the method could be misused for persistent tracking or fingerprinting without user consent. The Ethics Statement is boilerplate. A brief discussion on how developers should manage signature rotation or allow user opting-out would align better with ICLR's responsible AI expectations.

### Overall Assessment
This is a strong, technically sound submission that makes a meaningful practical contribution to diffusion model watermarking. By replacing heavy cryptographic encoding and per-image key management with a geometric spherical mapping and fixed orthogonal rotation, the authors deliver a method that is empirically indistinguishable from the base prior, highly robust to common and adversarial perturbations, and computationally trivial to extract. The theoretical framing via spherical t-designs and Stein's method is novel to the watermarking literature and provides useful asymptotic guarantees. The primary concerns are semantic and scoping: the term "lossless" is used to describe distributional indistinguishability rather than error-free extraction, and the method's fundamental dependence on accurate VAE/ODE inversion—which is known to degrade with guidance scale and model architecture—is treated empirically rather than bounded theoretically. Clarifying the "encryption-free" terminology (it remains a fixed-secret symmetric scheme), tightening the finite-sample distributional bounds, and explicitly discussing the inversion fidelity ceiling would elevate the paper significantly. Despite these points, the empirical rigor, clear ablations, and meaningful improvement over PRC/Gaussian Shading in both robustness and latency make this a solid candidate for ICLR acceptance.

# Neutral Reviewer
## Balanced Review

### Summary
This paper introduces Spherical Watermark, an encryption-free and lossless watermarking framework for diffusion models that embeds high-entropy binary traces directly into the initial Gaussian noise prior. By mapping watermark bits onto a unit sphere, applying a fixed orthogonal rotation, and scaling with a chi-square distribution, the method provably matches the target prior up to third-order moments. Extensive experiments across multiple generative architectures demonstrate that the approach achieves near-perfect undetectability, high extraction robustness against diverse attacks, and significantly lower computational overhead compared to cryptographic lossless baselines.

### Strengths
1. **Mathematically Grounded and Conceptually Clean Design:** The pipeline (repetition/padding → sphere projection → orthogonal rotation → χ² scaling) elegantly bridges discrete binary watermarks and continuous Gaussian priors. Theoretical analysis in Theorem 3.1, Theorem 3.2, and Lemmas 3.3–3.4 rigorously establishes 2/3-wise independence, spherical 3-design properties, and asymptotic convergence to a standard normal distribution.
2. **Strong Empirical Performance and Computational Efficiency:** Results consistently show that FID matches unwatermarked baselines (Table 1), classifier detectability hovers near chance (~50% in Figure 2), and extraction ACC/TPR remains >95% under post-processing and adversarial attacks (Tables 2, 12–14). Crucially, extraction latency is ~4 orders of magnitude faster than PRC Watermark (Figure 4), directly addressing a major practical bottleneck in prior work.
3. **Comprehensive and Rigorous Evaluation:** The empirical validation aligns well with ICLR standards, spanning multiple backbones (SD v1.5/v2.1, v3, FLUX.1-DEV, Glow, Guided Diffusion), extensive ablations (modules, `s` and `N`, solvers, timesteps, latent noise injection), and diverse threat scenarios (WEvade adversarial attacks, editing, regeneration). The inclusion of high-quality visualizations and statistical reporting (mean ± 1σ over 5 runs) enhances transparency and reproducibility.

### Weaknesses
1. **Limited Statistical Guarantees Beyond Third Order:** The theoretical framework only guarantees moment matching up to degree 3 (spherical 3-design). While empirically robust against standard MLP/ResNet detectors, the scheme lacks formal analysis against higher-order statistical tests or advanced likelihood-ratio discriminators, which could compromise undetectability in high-security provenance settings. The authors acknowledge this in Section 5 but frame the "indistinguishability" claim strongly in the abstract and introduction.
2. **Inversion Assumptions Limit Practical CFG Applicability:** Watermark extraction (Eq. 12) assumes an empty-condition ODE inversion with a guidance scale of 1.0. In real-world deployments, users generate images with high classifier-free guidance scales (e.g., 7.5) and specific prompts. The paper does not sufficiently characterize how inversion inaccuracies under standard CFG sampling affect extraction fidelity, relying instead on a simplified inversion setup that may not reflect production pipelines.
3. **Static Secret Architecture and Multi-User Scalability:** The framework eliminates per-image keys by relying on a fixed, model-level secret signature `K = [T, C]`. While this reduces storage overhead, it introduces a single-point-of-failure risk. The paper does not discuss key rotation, revocation, multi-tenant isolation, or resistance to collusion attacks where aggregating multiple watermarked images could estimate `K`. For large-scale API services, this limits operational security.

### Novelty & Significance
The work offers a meaningful conceptual advancement in lossless watermarking by replacing heavy cryptographic error-correction (PRC) and per-image cipher management (Gaussian Shading) with a geometric/probabilistic mapping rooted in spherical design theory. While the polar decomposition and chi-squared scaling are mathematically classical, their specific adaptation to latent diffusion watermarking is novel and well-executed. Its significance lies in demonstrating that provable distribution-preservation and practical efficiency need not be mutually exclusive, making lossless watermarking more viable for high-throughput generative AI. However, its immediate impact is somewhat tempered by the static key model and reliance on highly accurate latent inversion, which may require additional engineering for complex, prompt-conditioned deployment scenarios.

### Suggestions for Improvement
1. **Formalize the Key Management Threat Model:** Explicitly discuss the security implications of a fixed shared secret `K`. Propose or analyze practical key-rotation schemes, hierarchical key derivation for multi-user APIs, and resistance to statistical key-estimation or collusion attacks to strengthen the security narrative.
2. **Evaluate Extraction Under Standard Classifier-Free Guidance:** Extend the inversion experiments to images generated with typical guidance scales (e.g., 7.5) and diverse text prompts. Discuss or integrate prompt-aware inversion techniques (e.g., null-text embedding or classifier-free inversion) and quantify their impact on watermark extraction accuracy.
3. **Characterize Operational Robustness Limits and Error Floors:** Systematically map the trade-offs between capacity (`lm`), redundancy (`N`), sparsity (`s`), and distortion severity. Explicitly identify the noise/attack thresholds where extraction fails, and compare these empirical error floors to the information-theoretic or coding-theoretic bounds of PRC to clarify the practical regime of each method.
4. **Strengthen Statistical Indistinguishability Evaluation:** Supplement classifier-based detectability tests with formal goodness-of-fit tests (e.g., Henze-Zirkler, energy distance, or higher-order moment analysis) on both latent and pixel-space outputs. This will empirically bound the limits of the 3-design guarantee and preemptively address potential higher-order distributional attacks.

# Spark Finder Review
## How to Improve This Paper

### Missing Experiments (top 3-5 only)
1. Add evaluation on large-scale unwatermarked natural image datasets (e.g., ImageNet) to measure end-to-end False Positive Rates. Without this, the "traceability" claim lacks practical validity for real-world provenance where genuine images must not trigger false alarms.
2. Benchmark against modern watermark removal techniques, specifically anti-watermark model fine-tuning (e.g., LoRA erasure) and recent inversion-breaking attacks. Relying only on basic post-processing and a 2023 adversarial baseline significantly underestimates current adversarial capabilities.
3. Systematically map the capacity-robustness trade-off for the proposed method up to high capacities (e.g., >2048 bits) to match the failure point analysis provided for PRC. Failing to test the proposed method's own breaking point leaves the scalability and "superior robustness" claims empirically unverified.

### Deeper Analysis Needed (top 3-5 only)
1. Quantify the detectability of higher-order statistical moments (degree 4+) to validate the "computational indistinguishability" claim. Theoretical 3-design matching is insufficient; modern ML detectors can easily exploit higher-order joint distributions or cumulants in high dimensions to break the lossless guarantee.
2. Decompose the Bit Error Rate (BER) into distinct error components from VAE reconstruction, ODE inversion approximation, and external perturbations. Conflating inversion artifacts with attack-induced degradation obscures the true watermark strength and prevents accurate attribution of robustness gains.
3. Derive analytical bounds for error propagation within the binary mixing matrix $\mathbf{T}$ and the majority-vote decoder as sparsity $s$ and repetition $N$ vary. Treating these as empirical knobs without a theoretical link to decoding reliability makes parameter selection heuristic and undermines the exact extraction guarantee.

### Visualizations & Case Studies
1. Provide Q-Q plots or empirical marginal/conditional moment distributions comparing watermarked latents directly against true Gaussian priors. Aggregate metrics like FID obscure geometric deviations; direct visualization is required to confirm that the spherical projection practically achieves the claimed distributional match.
2. Present detailed failure case studies with corrupted latent reconstructions and decoded bit patterns under the strongest tested attacks. Exposing exactly where and how the inversion or majority vote collapses reveals whether failures are catastrophic (total loss) or correctable, which is critical for assessing operational risk.
3. Visualize the detector response curves and decision margins for the proposed method versus lossy baselines across varying corruption levels. This would expose whether the claimed losslessness stems from a genuinely flat statistical landscape around the prior or simply high classification noise, validating the undetectability argument visually.

### Obvious Next Steps
1. Formalize the security threat model for the "encryption-free" design against known-structure adversaries attempting to deduce the fixed orthogonal matrix $\mathbf{C}$ or invert the mixing matrix via known/ciphertext-plaintext analysis. Assuming security without analyzing key recovery under realistic threat models fundamentally undermines the provenance claim.
2. Evaluate the pipeline under severe platform-induced degradation where images undergo aggressive compression, cropping, or color quantization *before* developer extraction. The current setup underestimates the cumulative inversion error introduced by real-world social media pipelines that can easily break the invertibility assumption.
3. Conduct a rigorous statistical power analysis to confirm that the specific latent dimension ($l_x=16384$) is finite-sample sufficient to reject normality under standard multivariate goodness-of-fit tests. Relying on asymptotic convergence for a fixed dimension risks leaving subtle but exploitable distributional shifts undetected in practice.

# Final Consolidated Review
## Summary
This paper proposes Spherical Watermark, an encryption-free and lossless watermarking framework for diffusion models that maps binary watermark payloads to the model’s initial Gaussian noise prior. By combining a fixed binary embedding matrix with spherical projection, orthogonal rotation, and chi-square radius scaling, the method provably matches the target prior up to third-order moments. Extensive experiments across multiple diffusion and flow-based architectures demonstrate that the approach achieves exact distributional preservation, high extraction robustness under various attacks, and extraction speeds orders of magnitude faster than cryptographic baselines.

## Strengths
- **Conceptually elegant and mathematically rigorous mapping:** The pipeline replaces heavy cryptographic error-correction and per-image key management with a clean geometric transformation. The theoretical analysis (spherical 3-design guarantees via Theorem 3.2 and Stein’s method convergence in Lemma 3.3) cleanly bridges discrete binary payloads and continuous Gaussian priors, with proofs that are self-contained and directly aligned with the practical design.
- **Demonstrated efficiency and robustness without fidelity trade-offs:** The method consistently matches the Fréchet Inception Distance of unwatermarked baselines (Table 1) while maintaining >95% ACC and TPR under post-processing and adversarial attacks. Crucially, it achieves extraction latency roughly four orders of magnitude lower than PRC Watermark (Figure 4), directly resolving a major practical bottleneck in prior lossless schemes.
- **Thorough empirical validation and ablation:** The evaluation spans diverse backbones (SD v1.5/2.1, SD v3, FLUX.1-DEV, Glow, Guided Diffusion), multiple ODE solvers, and realistic threat models (WEvade, editing, regeneration). Sensitivity analyses on hyperparameters (sparsity $s$, repetition $N$), latent noise injection (Figure 17), and solver configurations confirm that the framework’s performance is stable, reproducible, and well-understood.

## Weaknesses
- **Extraction reliability under standard high-CFG generation is uncharacterized:** The inversion procedure (Eq. 12) and empirical setup rely on empty-condition, low-guidance ODE inversion to recover the initial latent. While the paper shows robustness to moderate solver drift and added latent noise (Figure 17, Table 4), practical text-to-image pipelines typically use high classifier-free guidance scales (e.g., 7.5) that amplify inversion approximation errors. The method’s theoretical lossless guarantee assumes accurate latent recovery, and the degradation curve under production-standard CFG sampling remains unquantified.
- **Fixed signature architecture introduces systemic security risks:** Eliminating per-image keys is a clear efficiency win, but relying on a single, model-level secret signature $K=\{\mathbf{T}, \mathbf{C}\}$ creates a single point of failure. If $K$ is compromised or statistically estimated from aggregated outputs, all watermarked images become trivially removable or forgeable. The paper focuses on the mapping mechanics but does not address key rotation, multi-tenant isolation, or resistance to collusion/key-estimation attacks, which limits operational security for large-scale API deployments.

## Nice-to-Haves
- Provide Q-Q plots or empirical conditional moment distributions to visually verify the practical Gaussian alignment of the watermarked latents, complementing the aggregate FID and classifier-based undetectability tests.
- Derive analytical bounds linking sparsity $s$, repetition count $N$, and the maximum tolerable inversion error, which would transform the current empirical parameter tuning into a principled design guideline.
- Discuss practical key management strategies (e.g., periodic signature rotation, hierarchical key derivation for API tenants) and formally analyze the threat model for fixed-structure adversaries attempting to recover $\mathbf{C}$ or $\mathbf{T}$.

## Novel Insights
The work reframes latent-space watermarking from a cryptographic coding problem into a geometric moment-matching problem. By demonstrating that structured orthogonal mixing and polar decomposition alone can bridge discrete payloads and diffusion priors without sacrificing statistical indistinguishability, the paper reveals that distribution-preserving embedding does not inherently require heavy cryptographic overhead. This decoupling of capacity from computational complexity suggests a broader design principle: provable undetectability in generative models can be achieved through algebraic structure and controlled randomness rather than iterative encryption or error-correcting codes, opening a cleaner path for scalable provenance tracking.

## Suggestions
- Extend the inversion and extraction experiments to include generations produced with standard high CFG scales (e.g., 7.5) and diverse text prompts. Quantify how the resulting inversion drift affects ACC/TPR, and clarify whether prompt-aware or null-text inversion techniques are needed for robust real-world deployment.
- Add a dedicated subsection or appendix discussing the security implications of the fixed signature $K$, outlining realistic threat models (key leakage, statistical estimation from large output batches) and proposing lightweight mitigation strategies compatible with the encryption-free design.
