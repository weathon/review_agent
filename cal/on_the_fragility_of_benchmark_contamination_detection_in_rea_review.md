# Harsh Critic Review
## Section-by-Section Critical Review

### Title & Abstract
- **Title accuracy:** The title accurately captures the core contribution: it identifies and analyzes vulnerabilities in existing contamination detection methods specifically within the context of Large Reasoning Models (LRMs).
- **Abstract clarity:** The abstract clearly outlines the problem (benchmark contamination in LRMs), the two-stage methodology (pre-LRM SFT+RL concealment vs. post-LRM CoT SFT evasion), the theoretical mechanism (PPO-style clipping/IS), and the implications (need for new protocols). 
- **Support for claims:** The abstract claims that evading detection is "alarmingly easy" and that RL inherently exhibits "concealment capability." These claims are well-supported by the empirical results in Figures 2 & 3 and Table 3, and the ablation isolating the clipping term. The claim about post-LRM CoT contamination performing "near random guesses" is directly supported by Table 5. No overclaiming detected.

### Introduction & Motivation
- **Motivation & Gap:** The problem is highly relevant to the current ICLR/ML landscape, where LRM leaderboards drive rapid development but evaluation integrity is paramount. The authors clearly identify a critical gap: existing contamination detectors rely on memorization assumptions and fixed reference distributions, which break down when LRMs (1) generalize reasoning patterns via long CoT, and (2) undergo RL fine-tuning that alters log-probability dynamics.
- **Contributions:** Clearly and accurately stated: (1) Systematic empirical evaluation across two contamination stages, (2) Theoretical analysis linking PPO-style clipping to detection degradation, and (3) Empirical validation of why post-LRM contamination evades detection.
- **Tone:** The introduction appropriately frames the urgency without over-selling. It accurately positions the work relative to prior concealment studies (e.g., data augmentation vs. algorithmic training dynamics).

### Method / Approach
- **Clarity & Reproducibility:** The methodology is clearly described. Contamination pipelines (SFT with distilled CoT, RL with GRPO/RAFT/RAFT++, and post-LRM SFT) are detailed in Section 3 and Appendix D. Hyperparameters, dataset splits, and evaluation setups (AUROC on 8 rollouts) are provided. Code is promised. Reproducibility appears strong.
- **Assumptions & Justification:** 
  - The theoretical analysis (Theorem 3.1) assumes a **tabular setting** and **small natural gradient steps**. While standard for analytical tractability, the extension to high-dimensional neural function approximation with multi-step, finite-learning-rate updates is non-trivial. The paper acknowledges this simplification but does not discuss how function approximation or optimization noise might alter the covariance decomposition. The authors should explicitly acknowledge this as a theoretical limitation and justify why the tabular insight is expected to hold in practice (which the empirical results do support, but the connection should be stated).
  - The analysis focuses only on **correct trajectories** (`r=1`), stating that assessing erroneous outputs is "not especially meaningful" for contamination detection. This is reasonable for verifiable math/code tasks, but it limits the theory's applicability to domains with dense or noisy rewards.
- **Logical Gaps:** The derivation logically connects PPO-style clipping to the contraction of the member/non-member NLL gap. The intuition that clipping dampens off-policy trajectories (which are more frequent/varied for non-members) is sound and aligns with the empirical results.
- **RL Setup Nuance:** The experiments remove the KL-divergence term from GRPO ("follow DAPO"). Standard PPO/GRPO implementations typically include a KL penalty to prevent policy collapse toward high-reward modes. The interaction between the KL term and the clipping mechanism could materially affect entropy dynamics and log-prob convergence. The method section should clarify how the absence of KL regularization influences the observed concealment and whether results would hold for KL-regularized variants.

### Experiments & Results
- **Testing Claims:** The experiments directly test the claims. Stage I demonstrates AUROC degradation after RL across 10 detectors and 6 benchmarks. Stage II shows post-LRM SFT evasion. The ablation removing clipping (Table 3) cleanly isolates the theoretical mechanism.
- **Baselines & Fairness:** The selection of 10 detectors across 5 categories is comprehensive and appropriate. Comparison against RAFT (no clipping) vs RAFT++/GRPO (clipping) is a strong, controlled experimental design.
- **Missing Ablations / Confounders:** 
  - **Statistical Robustness:** RL training is notoriously seed-sensitive. The paper reports AUROC averages over 8 evaluation rollouts but does not report variance across multiple training seeds for the RL fine-tuning phase. For ICLR, demonstrating that the AUROC drop is robust across training seeds (not an artifact of a single optimization trajectory) is important.
  - **Stage II Evaluation Confounder:** Section 4 attributes near-random AUROC to LRM "generalization," arguing that models assign higher confidence to non-members that share distributional similarity. However, detectors (e.g., Loss, Min-K%) are computed on **8 stochastically generated responses** per question, not on fixed ground-truth reference CoTs. LRMs produce long, highly diverse chains. High generation variance inherently widens log-probability distributions, reducing separability (AUROC) regardless of true generalization. The overlap in Fig. 4 and Fig. 13/14 shows both distributions shifting right, but it does not disentangle whether this is due to (a) true distributional generalization or (b) increased evaluation noise from stochastic long-text generation. The authors should address this confounder, e.g., by computing log-probs on fixed reference CoTs (if available) or by controlling for response length/variance in the detection metric.
- **Results Support Claims:** The results consistently support the claims. The monotonic decline in Fig. 2 and the cross-benchmark consistency in Table 2/5 rule out cherry-picking.
- **Datasets & Metrics:** Benchmarks (AIME, GPQA, Olympiad, AMC, Minerva) are standard and appropriate for LRMs. AUROC is the correct metric for detection separability.

### Writing & Clarity
- **Clarity:** The paper is well-organized and logically flows from empirical observation (Sec 3.1) to theoretical explanation (Sec 3.2) to practical validation (Sec 3.2.1) and extension to advanced models (Sec 4). 
- **Figures/Tables:** Figures 3, 4, 7, 8, 9 effectively visualize the log-prob distribution convergence. Tables 1, 2, 3, 5 are clear and directly support the narrative. Appendix tables provide necessary ablation depth without cluttering the main text.
- **Potential Confusion:** The transition from the theoretical covariance decomposition to the empirical RAFT++ ablation could be slightly smoother. Specifically, Table 3's caption and layout contain minor parser artifacts, but the scientific content remains understandable. The explanation of why `Cov(ℓk, ρt mt)` is more negative for non-members in RAFT++ is concise; a brief intuitive sentence linking higher loss variance in non-members to more frequent clipping would aid readability.

### Limitations & Broader Impact
- **Acknowledged Limitations:** The authors correctly acknowledge that they do not propose a new detection algorithm and that current memorization-based assumptions are breaking down for LRMs.
- **Missed Fundamental Limitations:**
  - **Non-Clipping RL Methods:** The paper claims "a broad class of RL methods" may exhibit similar concealment, but the empirical and theoretical evidence is specific to PPO-style algorithms. Modern reasoning models also utilize DPO, ORPO, ReMax, and other preference optimization methods that do not use importance sampling/clipping. It is unclear if these methods conceal contamination or if the phenomenon is unique to on-policy clipping. This scope should be clarified.
  - **Evaluation Noise Artifact:** As noted in Experiments, the failure of detectors in Stage II may be partially confounded by high variance in evaluating probabilities on stochastic generations. This should be explicitly listed as a limitation.
- **Broader Impact:** The impact on leaderboard fairness and evaluation integrity is well-articulated. The recommendation for releasing intermediate checkpoints and developing generalization-aware detectors is constructive. No major negative societal impacts are overlooked, though the paper could briefly note that if developers exploit this concealment, it may accelerate an "arms race" in opaque benchmark optimization.

### Overall Assessment
This is a strong, timely, and well-executed paper that addresses a critical vulnerability in the evaluation of Large Reasoning Models. The empirical design is rigorous, the isolation of the clipping mechanism via RAFT/RAFT++ ablation is elegant, and the theoretical analysis provides a solid foundation for understanding why RL fine-tuning collapses log-probability separability. The findings have clear implications for the ML community, particularly regarding leaderboard integrity and the design of next-generation contamination detectors. The contribution stands firmly, though it requires clarification on two fronts before final acceptance: (1) addressing the potential evaluation confounder in Stage II, where high generation variance in long CoTs could artificially degrade AUROC independent of true generalization, and (2) acknowledging the scope of the RL analysis (tabular assumption, KL-regularization interaction, and applicability to non-clipping preference optimization). With these clarifications and the inclusion of statistical robustness across training seeds, this paper will be a valuable addition to the literature.

# Neutral Reviewer
## Balanced Review

### Summary
This paper systematically investigates the effectiveness of existing benchmark contamination detection methods in Large Reasoning Models (LRMs), revealing two critical failure modes. First, it demonstrates that subsequent PPO/GRPO-style RL training conceals evidence of prior SFT contamination, primarily because the objective's importance sampling and clipping mechanism contracts the log-probability gap between seen (member) and unseen (non-member) data. Second, it shows that applying CoT SFT contamination to already advanced LRMs yields massive performance inflation while evading detection, as the models generalize to distributionally similar unseen samples rather than merely memorizing, breaking the foundational assumptions of current detectors.

### Strengths
1. **Strong Empirical Rigor & Well-Designed Ablations:** The authors thoroughly evaluate 10 representative detection methods across multiple base and advanced LRM checkpoints. The controlled comparison between RAFT (no clipping) and RAFT++/GRPO (with clipping) cleanly isolates the clipping term as the primary driver of concealment, directly validating the core hypothesis (Table 3 & Appendix E.5).
2. **Novel Theoretical Grounding:** Unlike many empirical-only contamination studies, the paper provides a formal analysis of NLL drift under PPO-style objectives (Theorem 3.1). The derivation successfully links the clipping/importance sampling term to a reduction in the covariance gap between members and non-members, offering mechanistic insight into why RL obscures contamination signals.
3. **Clear Identification of LRM-Specific Vulnerabilities:** The two-stage framework (pre-LRM evolution vs. post-LRM fine-tuning) accurately reflects modern training pipelines. The finding that advanced LRMs shift log-probabilities for *both* members and non-members after CoT SFT (Figure 4) provides compelling evidence that generalization, not just memorization, invalidates current detection paradigms.
4. **High Reproducibility:** The paper provides a public codebase, exhaustive hyperparameters, dataset split details, compute specifications, and comprehensive step-by-step AUROC tracking (Appendices D & E). This level of documentation meets or exceeds ICLR's reproducibility standards.

### Weaknesses
1. **Theoretical Simplifications Limit Practical Scope:** The proof assumes a tabular MDP, single-step natural gradient updates, binary rewards, and omits the KL divergence term often used in stable RL training. While necessary for tractability, the paper does not discuss how multi-step optimization, group-normalized advantages (GRPO's standard deviation denominator), or explicit KL penalties might alter the concealment dynamics in real-world training.
2. **Stage II Generalization Claim Lacks Quantitative Rigor:** The assertion that advanced LRMs "generalize rather than memorize" is primarily supported by visual log-prob distribution overlap (Fig. 4). The paper lacks statistical tests for distribution convergence or quantitative metrics (e.g., OOD benchmark drop, causal tracing of reasoning steps) to rigorously distinguish true reasoning generalization from superficial pattern matching or distributional shift.
3. **Solution Space Remains Underdeveloped:** The conclusion appropriately highlights the need for new detection methods and checkpoint auditing but offers only high-level recommendations. The absence of a lightweight diagnostic baseline or a concrete proposal for a contamination-robust evaluation protocol reduces the actionable impact for practitioners and future researchers.
4. **Non-Mathematical Domains Are Peripheral:** The primary analysis focuses on math/reasoning benchmarks. While LiveCodeBench and MMLU-Pro are briefly explored in Appendix E.11, they lack the same depth (no theoretical framing, limited detector evaluation, minimal discussion). Domain-specific concealment dynamics deserve more prominent integration to claim broad relevance.

### Novelty & Significance
**Novelty:** High. This is the first work to examine contamination detection through the specific lens of LRM training dynamics (CoT SFT + RLVR). The discovery that algorithmic components of RL (clipping/importance sampling) inherently suppress detection signals, combined with the demonstration that CoT fine-tuning induces distribution-wide confidence shifts, represents a clear conceptual advance over prior memorization-focused contamination research.
**Clarity:** High. The manuscript is well-structured, with logical progression from empirical findings to theoretical grounding. Tables and figures effectively communicate key trends. Parsing artifacts should be disregarded per instructions.
**Reproducibility:** High. Detailed experimental protocols, open code, and extensive appendices provide a complete blueprint for replication and extension.
**Significance:** High. The findings directly challenge the reliability of current LRM leaderboards and dataset curation practices. By exposing both algorithmic concealment and the failure of memorization-based detectors on advanced models, the paper provides critical groundwork for developing trustworthy evaluation protocols in the post-reasoning era.

### Suggestions for Improvement
1. **Bridge Theory and Practical RLVR Dynamics:** Explicitly discuss the impact of the omitted KL term and GRPO's advantage normalization on the concealment mechanism. A brief ablation or theoretical remark on how these practical components might accelerate, delay, or modify the NLL gap contraction would strengthen the connection to real-world training.
2. **Quantify Stage II Generalization:** Move beyond visual log-prob overlap. Introduce statistical tests (e.g., Wasserstein distance or KL divergence between member/non-member distributions) and evaluate out-of-distribution reasoning benchmarks to definitively prove that performance gains stem from generalization rather than implicit overfitting to prompt templates or domain syntax.
3. **Propose a Concrete Diagnostic or Protocol:** Instead of solely outlining problems, introduce a lightweight baseline or evaluation guideline tailored to LRMs (e.g., a "clipping-gradient monitor" during training, a standardized multi-checkpoint auditing pipeline, or a prompt-structure perturbation metric). This would significantly increase the paper's practical utility for the ICLR community.
4. **Elevate Non-Math Domain Analysis:** Integrate the coding and general QA findings into the main discussion. Analyze whether concealment strength correlates with task structure (e.g., deterministic code execution vs. open-math reasoning) to clarify if the RL concealment mechanism is domain-agnostic or task-dependent.
5. **Clarify the Weakness of Direct RL Contamination:** The paper notes that direct RL contamination yields minimal performance inflation compared to SFT. Briefly explain this phenomenon (e.g., reward sparsity, short training horizon, or verifiable reward limitations) to set accurate expectations for developers and prevent misinterpretation of RL's role in contamination.

# Spark Finder Review
## How to Improve This Paper

### Missing Experiments (top 3-5 only)
1. Add a distributionally disjoint RL training control (e.g., train on coding data while evaluating on math benchmarks) because without it, you cannot rule out that the AUROC drop is caused by general domain smoothing rather than the PPO-style clipping objective.
2. Evaluate at least one non-rejection-sampling RL algorithm (e.g., DPO, ORPO, or ReMax) because claiming a "broad class of RL methods" inherently conceals contamination requires empirical evidence outside the GRPO/RAFT family.
3. Report confidence intervals or multiple random seeds for Stage II AUROC results because values fluctuating between 45–65% without variance metrics make the "near random guess" conclusion statistically unconvincing to ICLR reviewers.

### Deeper Analysis Needed (top 3-5 only)
1. Track the per-training-step NLL trajectory for members versus non-members during GRPO, because isolated before/after log-prob distributions cannot causally validate your theoretical claim that clipping specifically reduces non-member variance.
2. Compare Stage II contamination on LRMs against similarly scaled instruction-tuned models under identical setups to isolate whether detection failure stems from CoT reasoning generalization or merely from higher model capacity/parameterization.
3. Quantify the memorization-versus-generalization tradeoff by evaluating contaminated LRMs on structurally perturbed but semantically identical benchmark variants, as pass@1 inflation alone cannot distinguish between actual skill acquisition and template regurgitation.

### Visualizations & Case Studies
1. Provide side-by-side chain-of-thought traces for carefully matched member/non-member question pairs before and after Stage II contamination to visually demonstrate whether models internalize reusable reasoning schemas or simply memorize step sequences.
2. Plot the empirical correlation between the clipping mask activation rate (or importance sampling ratio distribution) and the AUROC decay curve across training steps to directly ground your theoretical mechanism in observable training dynamics.

### Obvious Next Steps
1. Propose or adapt a single detector baseline (e.g., reasoning-pattern consistency or cross-model entropy divergence) to prove that detection is not fundamentally hopeless, as a purely negative paper lacks actionable scientific value for this venue.
2. Run a contamination dosage sweep (e.g., 5%, 10%, 20% of benchmark data) in Stage II to identify the precise threshold where detectors break, which is essential for quantifying real-world leaderboard manipulation risks.

# Final Consolidated Review
## Summary
This paper systematically investigates why existing benchmark contamination detectors fail for Large Reasoning Models (LRMs). It identifies two distinct failure modes aligned with modern training pipelines: (1) in the pre-LRM stage, subsequent PPO/GRPO-style RL training conceals prior SFT contamination evidence by contracting the log-probability gap between seen and unseen data, driven specifically by importance sampling and clipping terms; (2) in the post-LRM stage, CoT fine-tuning on advanced models yields massive performance inflation while evading detection, as LRMs generalize confidence to distributionally similar unseen samples rather than merely memorizing, breaking core detector assumptions.

## Strengths
- **Rigorous empirical isolation of the concealment mechanism:** The controlled comparison between RAFT (no clipping) and RAFT++/GRPO (with clipping) cleanly isolates the PPO-style clipping/importance sampling term as the primary driver of detection degradation. This directly validates the theoretical claims and demonstrates strong experimental design.
- **Timely, realistic problem framing grounded in LRM training dynamics:** The two-stage analysis accurately mirrors how modern reasoning models are developed. The finding that RL inherently suppresses contamination signals while preserving performance gains, coupled with the demonstration that CoT SFT shifts confidence distributions for both members and non-members, provides critical insights for leaderboard integrity.
- **Novel theoretical analysis with clear empirical grounding:** The derivation of NLL drift under PPO-style objectives (Theorem 3.1) successfully links algorithmic components to the contraction of the member/non-member separability. The theory is thoroughly backed by ablation studies, step-wise AUROC tracking, and log-probability distribution visualization across multiple model scales and benchmarks.

## Weaknesses
- **Overgeneralization to a "broad class of RL methods":** The paper claims the concealment effect generalizes broadly, but both the theoretical decomposition and empirical validation are strictly contingent on PPO-style importance sampling and clipping. Modern reasoning model training increasingly relies on offline preference optimization (e.g., DPO, ORPO) or non-clipping online algorithms that lack this mechanism. The core claim should be explicitly scoped to clipping-based RL algorithms, acknowledging that other optimization paradigms may not inherently exhibit this specific concealment behavior.
- **Unresolved confounder between generalization and generation variance in Stage II:** The conclusion that post-LRM evasion stems from model generalization is compelling, but detection scores are computed over 8 stochastically generated long-CoT responses. High output variance in extended reasoning traces inherently widens log-probability distributions, which can artificially depress AUROC independent of true distributional generalization. The paper does not fully disentangle whether the near-random detector performance is driven by genuine confidence shifts to similar unseen questions or by evaluation noise from stochastic long-text generation.
- **Theoretical simplifications limit direct mapping to practical RLVR training:** The proof relies on a tabular setting, single-step natural gradients, binary rewards, and omits the KL divergence penalty and GRPO's standard deviation normalization. While necessary for tractability and empirically supported, the paper lacks discussion on how these practical components (particularly KL penalties that constrain policy entropy and advantage normalization) might interact with, accelerate, or mitigate the NLL gap contraction observed in real-world multi-step optimization.

## Nice-to-Haves
- Provide multiple training seeds or confidence intervals for the RL fine-tuning experiments to quantify variance and strengthen the statistical robustness of the AUROC degradation trends.
- Extend the empirical scope by testing one non-clipping preference optimization algorithm (e.g., DPO or ORPO) to empirically bound the concealment claim and clarify its algorithmic boundaries.
- For Stage II, complement the visual log-prob distributions with quantitative divergence metrics (e.g., Wasserstein distance or KL divergence) between member and non-member distributions, or control for response length/variance to better isolate the generalization signal.
- Briefly discuss why direct RL contamination yields minimal performance inflation compared to SFT contamination (e.g., due to reward sparsity, verifier limitations, or shorter effective exposure), as this contextualizes the practical threat landscape.

## Novel Insights
The paper reveals a counterintuitive algorithmic vulnerability: the very components of RL designed to stabilize training (importance sampling and clipping) inadvertently neutralize the statistical signals that contamination detectors depend on. Furthermore, it demonstrates that advanced LRMs do not simply "memorize" benchmark data during CoT fine-tuning; instead, they internalize reasoning patterns and project increased confidence onto distributionally similar, yet unseen, questions. This fundamentally breaks the memorization-vs-unseen dichotomy, showing that contamination in the reasoning era is not merely a data leakage issue but a byproduct of how LRMs acquire and generalize reasoning capabilities, demanding a complete rethink of evaluation trustworthiness.

## Suggestions
- Explicitly revise the claims regarding "a broad class of RL methods" to specify that the concealment mechanism is tied to on-policy clipping/importance sampling objectives. Add a brief discussion noting that offline preference optimization methods (DPO/ORPO) operate under different mathematical frameworks and may not inherently conceal contamination.
- In the Section 4 (Stage II) analysis, add a control experiment or explicit discussion that accounts for evaluation variance. For example, compute detection scores using greedy decoding or fixed reference CoTs (where available) to verify that the AUROC drop persists when generation stochasticity is minimized, thereby strengthening the generalization claim.
- In the theoretical analysis discussion, add a short section addressing how practical GRPO components (KL regularization, advantage normalization across groups, multi-step updates) might modulate the NLL gap contraction rate, framing the tabular analysis as a foundational mechanism that interacts with, rather than ignores, real-world training dynamics.
