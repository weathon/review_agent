# Harsh Critic Review
## Section-by-Section Critical Review

### Title & Abstract
- **Title Accuracy**: The title accurately reflects the core contribution: an unsupervised, instance-specific learning framework for dynamic CO. It correctly signals the absence of external training data.
- **Abstract Clarity**: The abstract clearly defines the problem (temporal graph snapshots), the method (DyCO-GNN leveraging structural similarity via shrink-and-perturb), and the evaluated tasks (MaxCut, MIS, TSP). The key results are stated concisely.
- **Unsupported Claims**: The claim of achieving solutions "up to 3–60x faster" is presented without context. While the paper compares against static PI-GNN's *converged* runtime, the 60x upper bound appears stretched relative to the experimental tables (which typically show ~2–8x speedups to reach equivalent or better ApR). The abstract should temper this phrasing or clarify that it refers to specific snapshot/time-budget regimes to avoid overpromising.

### Introduction & Motivation
- **Motivation & Gap**: The motivation is strong. The shift from static to dynamic CO is practically relevant, and the identification that instance-specific methods (PI-GNN family) solve each snapshot from scratch despite high structural overlap is a valid and useful gap.
- **Contributions**: Clearly stated and grounded in the subsequent sections. The three contributions (method, empirical validation, speed/quality trade-off) align directly with the paper's content.
- **Over-claim/Under-sell**: The statement "To the best of our knowledge, our work is the first to apply machine learning to DCO problems" is likely overstated or too narrowly scoped. Significant prior work applies RL and meta-learning to dynamic routing, scheduling, and evolving graphs (e.g., DRL for dynamic TSP/VRP, online neural solvers). The authors later clarify they focus on *unsupervised, instance-specific, offline-training-free* methods, but the abstract/intro framing should explicitly qualify "first" within this specific paradigm to maintain academic precision. Otherwise, the introduction is well-calibrated and does not under-sell.

### Method / Approach
- **Clarity & Reproducibility**: The method is clearly described via Algorithms 1 and 2. The shrink-and-perturb (SP) mechanism is straightforward, and the pseudocode leaves little ambiguity for reproduction.
- **Assumptions & Justification**: The core assumption is that structural overlap across snapshots makes warm-starting beneficial, but that naive warm-start fails due to overconfidence/local optima saturation. This is justified empirically (Fig 1) and by citing optimization/initialization literature. The adaptation of SP from supervised learning (Ash & Adams, 2020) to unsupervised instance optimization is conceptually sound, though the mechanism by which parameter shrinking "decreases confidence" in a non-linear GNN is slightly hand-waved without analyzing activation saturation effects.
- **Logical Gaps / Edge Cases**: A critical technical detail is missing: Section 3.2 states the method considers scenarios where "the node set $V_p^t$ and edge set $E_p^t$ change over time." However, GNN architectures are typically static in parameter dimensions. If $|V^t|$ changes, how are embeddings and adjacency matrices aligned with $\theta^{t-1}$? The experiments (Sec 5.1) actually keep node sets fixed and only modify edges, which sidesteps this. The method section should explicitly clarify whether it supports dynamic node insertion/deletion, and if so, how parameter dimensions are handled (e.g., zero-padding, projection matrices). Otherwise, the scope should be restricted to evolving edge sets/weights.
- **Theoretical Claims**: Theorem 1 provides analytical support for perturbations improving MaxCut quality, but it applies to the Goemans-Williamson SDP relaxation, not to the GNN's continuous relaxation. The connection is analogical rather than rigorous proof for DyCO-GNN. This is acceptable as supplementary intuition, but the authors should avoid implying it theoretically guarantees SP's efficacy in neural settings.

### Experiments & Results
- **Testing Claims**: The experiments directly test the paper's claims by benchmarking static, naive warm-start, and SP variants across multiple budgets and datasets. The design aligns well with the stated objectives.
- **Baselines**: Static and warm-started PI-GNN are appropriate internal baselines. The exclusion of pre-trained generalizable methods is justified by the paper's design philosophy, though it limits claims about absolute dominance over state-of-the-art ML-CO. Non-neural baselines are fairly included in Appendix D.3.
- **Missing Ablations**: The study covers SP applied to different layers and parameter sensitivity. An ablation systematically varying the *magnitude of structural change* (beyond a single sensitivity plot) and the *number of snapshots* would strengthen claims about robustness under different temporal dynamics, though not strictly necessary for acceptance.
- **Statistical Reporting**: A notable omission: While Section 5.2 states experiments were repeated 5 (MaxCut/MIS) or 10 (TSP) times, Tables 1–3 report only single-point means without standard deviations, confidence intervals, or significance tests. ICLR standards require variance reporting to distinguish signal from optimization stochasticity, especially given the non-convex training dynamics.
- **Result Support & Cherry-Picking**: Results generally support the claim that DyCO-GNN closes the medium-to-low budget gap. However, the TSP evaluation (Table 3) uses the **best-performing checkpoint** and includes "total decoding time over all checkpoints available." In a true online/streaming setting, the solver cannot know which checkpoint is best without hindsight or additional validation passes. This introduces an optimistic bias. Reporting the final checkpoint or implementing a practical early-stopping/heuristic selection criterion is necessary for fair comparison.
- **Datasets & Metrics**: ApR averaged over snapshots is appropriate. Dataset selection (Infectious, UC Social, DBLP, TSPLIB) is standard but synthetically dynamicized: edge additions/removals are applied linearly, and TSP uses a single moving node. While pragmatic, these constructions may not capture complex real-world dynamics (bursty changes, feature evolution, arbitrary node insertions). The authors should acknowledge this simplification.

### Writing & Clarity
- **Confusing Sections**: Section 4.1 attributes warm-start failure to "gradients become small due to overconfidence." While intuitive for saturation-prone losses, PI-GNN's continuous relaxation and rounding mechanics are architecture-dependent. A brief clarification of how gradient vanishing manifests in their specific loss/relaxation setup would prevent ambiguity.
- **Figures & Tables**: Tables 1–3 are well-structured and easy to parse. Figures 2–4 (snapshot-level ApRs) conceptually support the robustness claim, though the OCR extraction in this review makes them hard to verify. The main text adequately describes trends, so clarity is maintained.
- **No formatting/style nitpicks applied**: The logical flow from problem → limitation of baseline → proposed method → theory → experiment → discussion is clean and professional.

### Limitations & Broader Impact
- **Acknowledged Limitations**: The authors correctly identify that applying SP to different layers yields varying performance and note the lack of explicit decoupling between the embedding and GNN layers. They propose adaptive SP as future work.
- **Missed Limitations**:
  1. **Dynamic Node Support**: As noted, the paper does not discuss how the method handles topological changes where $|V^t|$ varies, despite mentioning it as a setting.
  2. **Online Stopping Criterion**: The method runs for a fixed `epochws` per snapshot. In practice, convergence times vary. An adaptive stopping strategy is necessary for real deployment.
  3. **Checkpoint Bias**: The TSP best-checkpoint methodology is an evaluation limitation that should be explicitly noted.
- **Broader Impact / Failure Modes**: The paper lacks a formal broader impact statement. While not strictly mandatory, ICLR encourages it. No negative societal impacts are apparent. Failure modes under drastic, non-smooth graph shifts (distribution shift across snapshots) are not discussed, though sensitivity to $\Delta$ edges is explored.

### Overall Assessment
This paper identifies a meaningful and underexplored gap in instance-specific neural combinatorial optimization: adapting learned heuristics across temporally evolving graph snapshots without offline training. The core idea—adapting shrink-and-perturb initialization to warm-start unsupervised GNN solvers—is novel, intuitively motivated, and empirically validated across three canonical CO problems. The results convincingly show that DyCO-GNN mitigates the local optima trapping of naive warm-starting and consistently finds higher-quality solutions under moderate time budgets. However, several methodological and reporting issues must be addressed before full ICLR standards are met: (1) the mechanism for handling varying node counts across snapshots is undefined; (2) TSP evaluation relies on hindsight-based best-checkpoint selection, which inflates performance in online settings; (3) statistical variance across repeated runs is absent from the main tables; and (4) the "first ML for DCO" and "3–60x speedup" claims require careful qualification to avoid overstatement. The theoretical support, while mathematically valid for SDP relaxations, remains analogical for the GNN setting. If the authors resolve the checkpointing bias, add statistical reporting, clarify topological adaptation limits, and temper promotional claims, this will be a strong, practical contribution to learning for optimization. The contribution stands, but requires moderate revisions to meet ICLR's rigor bar.

# Neutral Reviewer
## Balanced Review

### Summary
The paper introduces DyCO-GNN, an unsupervised learning framework for dynamic combinatorial optimization that operates without external training data, relying solely on runtime self-adaptation to evolving graph snapshots. Building on instance-specific optimization (PI-GNN), the method replaces naive parameter warm-starting with a "shrink and perturb" (SP) initialization strategy to mitigate gradient vanishing and escape local optima across snapshots. Extensive experiments on dynamic MaxCut, MIS, and TSP demonstrate that DyCO-GNN consistently achieves higher approximation ratios than static and warm-started baselines, often reaching superior solutions in a fraction of the runtime.

### Strengths
1. **Addresses a meaningful gap in learning-for-CO research:** The paper successfully bridges instance-specific unsupervised optimization with dynamic graph settings, a combination that has been largely overlooked in favor of static or fully trained generalizable models. (Sec 1 & 2)
2. **Well-motivated and effective core mechanism:** The identification of naive warm-start failure due to overconfident predictions and vanishing gradients (Sec 4.1, Fig 1) is insightful. The adaptation of SP as a principled "soft reset" (Sec 4.2) is simple, requires only two hyperparameters, and empirically prevents performance degradation across relaxed time budgets (Tables 1–3).
3. **Thorough and transparent experimental design:** The evaluation covers three distinct CO problems, multiple real-world and benchmark datasets, and a comprehensive sweep of time budgets. The inclusion of layer-wise SP ablations (embedding vs. GNN vs. full) and sensitivity analyses (Appendix D.4) demonstrates robustness to architectural choices and hyperparameters.
4. **Strong reproducibility:** The paper provides complete implementation details: model architecture (embedding dimensions, hidden sizes, GNN operators), optimizer settings, learning rates, epoch configurations, penalty terms, decoding strategies, hardware specs, and default SP values (Sec 5.2, Appendix C). This is sufficient for independent replication.

### Weaknesses
1. **Limited algorithmic novelty:** DyCO-GNN's primary contribution is an initialization heuristic applied to an existing framework. The SP technique (`θ ← λ_shrink·θ_prev + λ_perturb·ϵ`) originates from supervised warm-start literature (Ash & Adams, 2020). While the adaptation to unsupervised instance optimization is novel in application, the method lacks architectural or optimization-theoretic innovation specific to GNNs or combinatorial landscapes.
2. **Theoretical analysis is loosely coupled to the empirical method:** Theorem 1 analyzes perturbation effects within the Goemans-Williamson SDP relaxation for MaxCut. While mathematically correct, the SDP+rounding paradigm differs fundamentally from the paper's actual pipeline (GNN continuous relaxation + gradient descent + deterministic rounding). The theorem does not directly analyze gradient dynamics, loss landscapes, or the non-convexity inherent in the GNN objective, limiting its direct relevance to DyCO-GNN. (Sec 4.3, Appendix E)
3. **Narrow dynamic setting and restricted baseline scope:** The dynamic graphs are constructed via simple linear edge additions/deletions or a single moving node (Sec 5.1), which does not reflect complex real-world volatility like structural rewiring, weight oscillations, or varying topologies. Additionally, the explicit exclusion of trained generalizable models and classical dynamic heuristics from the main comparison (Sec 5) makes it difficult to contextualize the practical competitiveness of DyCO-GNN against established dynamic optimization pipelines. (Sec 5, Appendix D.3)
4. **Lack of computational complexity breakdown:** While wall-clock times are reported, the paper does not separate the overhead of SP, GNN forward passes, gradient computation, and rounding steps. This makes it unclear whether the reported 3–60x speedups stem from genuine algorithmic efficiency or simply from fewer epochs to a comparable loss threshold, limiting interpretability for systems-focused readers.

### Novelty & Significance
**Novelty:** Moderate. The extension of PI-GNN to dynamic settings via a principled initialization strategy is a solid, incremental contribution. The method is algorithmically simple and does not propose new architectural components or optimization frameworks, but it successfully identifies and solves a concrete failure mode in dynamic instance-specific learning.
**Clarity:** High. The paper is exceptionally well-structured, with clear definitions of the QUBO formulations, dynamic graph assumptions, and algorithmic procedures. Tables and pseudocode are readable, and the narrative logically progresses from problem identification to solution, theory, and validation.
**Reproducibility:** High. All necessary hyperparameters, dataset preprocessing steps, model configurations, and evaluation procedures are explicitly documented. Code is not provided, but the details are sufficient for reproduction by experienced researchers.
**Significance:** Moderate to High within its niche. The approach is practically valuable for resource-constrained, rapidly evolving environments where offline training datasets are unavailable or distribution shifts are severe. However, the per-instance optimization paradigm inherently limits scalability compared to trained generalizable models, and the synthetic dynamic generation restricts immediate deployment to complex real-world workflows.

### Suggestions for Improvement
1. **Bridge the theoretical and empirical gaps:** Either adapt the perturbation analysis to the non-convex GNN training objective (e.g., analyzing loss landscape curvature or gradient diversity post-SP) or explicitly reframe Theorem 1 as conceptual motivation rather than direct analytical support. Discuss explicitly why perturbation benefits might transfer between SDP relaxations and GNN-based continuous relaxations.
2. **Contextualize against traditional dynamic solvers:** Include direct comparisons to established dynamic heuristics (e.g., adaptive local search, simulated annealing with warm starts, or reactive metaheuristics) in the main text. This will better validate the claim of "superior performance" outside the PI-GNN family and strengthen practical relevance.
3. **Clarify terminology around "training data":** The title emphasizes learning "without training data," but PI-GNN treats the target instance as both training and test data during runtime optimization. Consider reframing as "runtime self-adaptation" or "inference-time optimization" to avoid confusion with offline pretraining or meta-learning paradigms.
4. **Provide a runtime/compute breakdown:** Report the wall-clock time contribution of SP initialization, forward/backward passes, and decoding per snapshot. Analyze whether speedups come from faster convergence, fewer iterations, or reduced computational overhead, and discuss the trade-off between solution quality and strict latency constraints.
5. **Discuss scalability and large-graph applicability:** Address how DyCO-GNN handles graphs with >50k nodes, where per-instance gradient descent becomes memory/time bottlenecked. Suggest or ablate strategies like early stopping criteria, mini-batch neighborhood training, or hierarchical GNNs to demonstrate awareness of real-world computational constraints.

# Spark Finder Review
## How to Improve This Paper

### Missing Experiments (top 3-5 only)
1. Compare against classical dynamic/incremental baselines (e.g., OR-Tools/Gurobi warm-start, dynamic local search, LKH-3 for TSP). The claims of "practical effectiveness" and "3–60x speedup" are unconvincing when only static neural baselines are evaluated, as traditional solvers are specifically optimized for incremental graph updates.
2. Report standard deviations and perform statistical significance tests across the repeated runs. Marginal ApR differences (often <0.001) cannot be presented as meaningful superiority without quantifying variance to rule out random optimization noise or seed-dependent stochasticity.
3. Evaluate on long-horizon trajectories (>20 snapshots) with irregular, non-linear structural changes. Limiting evaluation to 5–10 snapshots with artificial linear edge additions fails to stress-test whether DyCO-GNN suffers from compounding optimization drift or parameter instability over realistic timeframes.

### Deeper Analysis Needed (top 3-5 only)
1. Replace or fundamentally reframe Theorem 1, which analyzes Goemans–Williamson SDP rounding, a process completely disconnected from gradient descent on GNN parameters. This mismatch severely weakens the paper's theoretical credibility; the analysis must instead target the non-convex loss landscape or gradient behavior of instance-specific neural QUBO optimization.
2. Provide an ablation strictly isolating the "shrink" and "perturb" components to quantify their individual contributions. Without this, it is impossible to determine if performance gains stem from the proposed principled initialization or trivial artifacts like implicit learning-rate annealing or effective random restarts.
3. Analyze QUBO penalty sensitivity during dynamic transitions, especially for TSP feasibility and MIS edge deletions. If solution quality heavily relies on manual penalty scaling rather than the learned GNN representations, the claim of robust, instance-specific optimization is significantly undermined.

### Visualizations & Case Studies
1. Plot per-snapshot gradient norms and optimization trajectories for static versus DyCO-GNN runs. This is essential to empirically validate the central hypothesis that naive warm-starting causes gradient stagnation and that SP successfully restores informative gradient diversity.
2. Show solution-difference heatmaps (e.g., node flips or TSP path deviations) between consecutive snapshots relative to ground-truth changes. This reveals whether DyCO-GNN makes localized, meaningful updates aligned with the dynamic input changes or frequently collapses into entirely unrelated solutions due to perturbation noise.
3. Visualize embedding or weight magnitude distributions before and after applying the "shrink" operation. This would directly prove that the initialization step actually reduces parameter confidence (as claimed in Section 4.1) rather than acting as a mathematically vacuous scaling factor.

### Obvious Next Steps
1. Implement an adaptive mechanism for $\lambda_{\text{shrink}}$ and $\lambda_{\text{perturb}}$ instead of using fixed values. The sensitivity analysis (Appendix D.4) shows severe performance degradation with minor parameter shifts, making manual tuning impractical for real-world deployment where dynamic patterns are unknown.
2. Integrate standard local search operators (e.g., 2-opt for TSP, Kernighan–Lin for MaxCut) as a post-processing refinement step. Neural relaxations rarely output discrete valid solutions immediately; hybridizing with classical local search is a standard expectation in CO to prove the method can compete with state-of-the-art heuristics in quality, not just relative speedup.

# Final Consolidated Review
## Summary
This paper introduces DyCO-GNN, an unsupervised, instance-specific learning framework for Dynamic Combinatorial Optimization (DCO) that requires no offline training data. Building on the PI-GNN paradigm, the method applies a "shrink-and-perturb" (SP) initialization strategy between temporally evolving graph snapshots to prevent gradient stagnation and local optima trapping inherent in naive parameter warm-starting. The approach is empirically validated on dynamic MaxCut, MIS, and TSP, claiming superior solution quality under strict to moderate time budgets compared to running the solver from scratch or using naive warm-starts.

## Strengths
- **Well-motivated failure mode analysis & elegant intervention:** The paper correctly identifies that naive warm-starting instance-specific GNNs leads to overconfident predictions and vanishing gradients across snapshots (Fig. 1). Adapting the shrink-and-perturb mechanism as a principled "soft reset" is simple, requires only two hyperparameters, and empirically restores gradient diversity without breaking the offline-training-free constraint.
- **Comprehensive and reproducible empirical sweep:** The evaluation systematically tests three canonical CO problems across four real-world/benchmark datasets, sweeping multiple time budgets and SP application layers. Detailed implementation constants (architectures, optimization schedules, penalty coefficients, hardware) are provided, enabling straightforward replication.
- **Clear scoping within the instance-specific learning niche:** The authors explicitly justify excluding heavily pre-trained generalizable models, focusing instead on the underexplored regime of rapid, distribution-shift-resistant adaptation where offline datasets are unavailable or prohibitively expensive to generate.

## Weaknesses
- **Hindsight bias invalidates key TSP claims:** For dynamic TSP, the methodology selects the *best-performing checkpoint* across the optimization horizon and reports cumulative decoding time (Table 3). In a true dynamic/streaming setting, the solver must commit to a decision within the allocated time budget per snapshot without future knowledge. This retrospective checkpoint selection introduces severe optimistic bias and undermines the claimed speedups and solution quality.
- **Incremental algorithmic novelty:** DyCO-GNN's core mechanism is a direct application of the shrink-and-perturb initialization trick from supervised warm-start literature (Ash & Adams, 2020) to the PI-GNN framework. While empirically effective, the paper does not adapt the heuristic to the non-convex QUBO relaxation landscape, nor does it propose architecture-specific modifications or theoretical insights into why SP outperforms alternative resets (e.g., random restarts, learning-rate cycling) for GNN-based CO.
- **Narrow dynamic graph construction and missing classical baselines:** The dynamic scenarios are synthetically generated via linear edge accumulation or a single moving TSP node, failing to capture realistic volatility like structural rewiring, weight oscillations, or adversarial changes. Furthermore, excluding classical dynamic/incremental solvers (e.g., warm-started OR-Tools, adaptive local search, LKH-3 for TSP) and DRL methods for dynamic routing limits the practical claims of "superior performance." The reported gains are primarily relative to weak PI-GNN variants rather than established dynamic optimization pipelines.
- **Missing statistical reporting for stochastic optimization:** Experiments are repeated 5–10 times due to non-convex training dynamics, yet Tables 1–3 report only single-point means. Without standard deviations or significance testing, marginal ApR improvements (often <0.001–0.005) cannot be reliably distinguished from optimization noise or seed-dependent variance.

## Nice-to-Haves
- Decouple the "shrink" and "perturb" components in ablation studies to isolate their individual contributions versus implicit learning-rate scaling or accidental random restarts.
- Visualize per-snapshot gradient norms and embedding magnitude distributions to empirically verify the "overconfidence" hypothesis and confirm that SP meaningfully resets gradient diversity.
- Bridge the theoretical gap by analyzing perturbation effects directly on the GNN's continuous relaxation loss landscape, rather than relying on an SDP-based theorem (Theorem 1) that operates under fundamentally different convexity and rounding assumptions.
- Implement adaptive or heuristic-based $\lambda_{shrink}$ and $\lambda_{perturb}$ scheduling, as fixed values degrade performance when temporal graph volatility varies across trajectories (Appendix D.4).

## Novel Insights
The paper successfully reframes dynamic combinatorial optimization not as a problem of building generalizable policies, but as a sequence of highly correlated instance-specific optimizations where temporal structural overlap can be weaponized via controlled parameter destabilization. By treating warm-start failure as a consequence of optimization-induced "overconfidence" rather than distribution shift, the authors demonstrate that a simple linear perturbation of model parameters acts as a computationally cheap regularizer that prevents premature convergence to snapshot-local minima. This insight shifts the DCO paradigm from learning transferable features to maintaining optimization plasticity across time-evolving instances.

## Potentially Missed Related Work
- **Dynamic/Incremental Heuristics & Metaheuristics** (e.g., reactive simulated annealing, kernel-based incremental local search) — highly relevant for establishing competitive baselines under the exact same "no-training" constraint.
- **Deep Reinforcement Learning for Dynamic Routing/CO** (e.g., Zhang et al., TNNLS 2021; Kool et al., ICLR 2019 extensions to online VRP) — relevant to contextualize the "first ML for DCO" claim and benchmark against learned policy adaptation.
- **Online/Streaming Optimization for Graph Problems** (e.g., dynamic spectral clustering, online LP/Gurobi warm-start strategies) — relevant for contrasting gradient-based GNN adaptation against classical incremental solver warm-starts.

## Suggestions
1. **Fix the evaluation protocol for dynamic settings:** Replace the "best checkpoint" TSP evaluation with either a strict forward-online protocol (commit to the final checkpoint within `epochws` per snapshot) or a practical online early-stopping criterion based on loss plateaus or validation heuristics. Report wall-clock time strictly within the allowed budget.
2. **Include classical dynamic baselines:** Add warm-started traditional solvers (OR-Tools, LKH-3, or NetworkX adaptive heuristics) to the main tables. Even if DyCO-GNN matches rather than dominates them, this contextualizes the method's practical viability against non-learned pipelines.
3. **Report variance and statistical significance:** Add standard deviations to all main result tables. Apply bootstrap or paired t-tests where mean differences are marginal to confirm that improvements over naïve warm-starts are robust across random seeds.
4. **Clarify scope vs. implementation:** Section 3.2 mentions varying node sets $V_p^t$, but experiments keep $|V|$ fixed and the current GNN architecture cannot handle dynamic node insertion/deletion without parameter remapping or zero-padding. Either explicitly restrict claims to evolving edges/weights or implement and evaluate a scalable node-alignment strategy.
