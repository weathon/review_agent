# Harsh Critic Review
## Section-by-Section Critical Review

### Title & Abstract
- **Title Accuracy:** The title accurately reflects the scope: a learning-based approach for DCO that avoids offline dataset training.
- **Abstract Clarity:** The problem, method (instance-specific GNN with Shrink & Perturb warm-starts), and scope (MaxCut, MIS, TSP) are clearly stated.
- **Claim Support:** The claim "achieving high-quality solutions up to 3–60x faster" is ambiguously phrased. The text indicates DyCO-GNN reaches the *static PI-GNN's final approximation ratio* faster, but the abstract lacks this crucial context. Furthermore, labeling instance-specific gradient optimization as "without training data" may confuse readers; it is better described as "unsupervised, test-time optimization" to distinguish it from inductive, zero-shot learning. The speedup claim should be quantified against a specific baseline and target quality threshold in the abstract.

### Introduction & Motivation
- **Motivation & Gap:** The transition from static PI-GNN to dynamic settings is well-motivated, and the identification of the local-optima trapping issue in naive warm-starts provides a concrete research gap.
- **Contribution Accuracy:** The claim "To the best of our knowledge, our work is the first to apply machine learning to DCO problems" is **strongly overclaimed and likely inaccurate**. There is existing literature on reinforcement learning and online learning for dynamic routing, vehicle scheduling, and dynamic TSP (e.g., RL-based dynamic TSP approaches cited in your reference list like Zhang et al., 2021, and broader online CO literature). The introduction should explicitly scope the "first" claim to the *instance-specific, unsupervised GNN* paradigm or acknowledge prior ML-based dynamic/online optimization work to maintain academic rigor.
- **Positioning:** The contributions are otherwise clearly stated. The focus on the PI-GNN family is a reasonable scope decision, provided the introduction carefully delineates the boundary between this work and generalizable/RL-based dynamic methods.

### Method / Approach
- **Clarity & Reproducibility:** The method is clearly described. Algorithm 1 and Algorithm 2 provide a reproducible framework, and the integration of Shrink & Perturb (SP) is simple to implement.
- **Assumptions & Justification:** The method assumes high structural overlap between consecutive snapshots. While justified for the tested datasets, the performance degradation under larger structural changes (Figure 5) indicates a hard assumption that limits robustness to rapid topology shifts.
- **Logical Gaps & Failure Modes:** The explanation in Section 4.1 for warm-start failure ("gradients become small due to... overconfidence") is presented as a mechanism but lacks empirical verification. It would strengthen the method if the authors visualized gradient norms or loss landscapes to confirm this hypothesis versus standard saturation effects in sigmoid/softmax outputs.
- **Theoretical Claims:** **There is a significant disconnect between Theorem 1 and DyCO-GNN.** Theorem 1 proves that perturbation helps an SDP solution for MaxCut under the Goemans-Williamson framework. However, PI-GNN/DyCO-GNN does not solve an SDP; it performs gradient descent on a non-convex, parameterized GNN with a QUBO relaxation. The mathematical properties of the PSD cone and metric projections on the SDP manifold do not directly translate to the highly non-convex loss surfaces of deep GNNs. The theory section reads as an analogy rather than a direct justification for DyCO-GNN's efficacy. The authors should either provide a theoretical bridge (e.g., analyzing perturbation in non-convex parameter space) or reframe the section as "Motivation from Convex Relaxation Analysis."

### Experiments & Results
- **Testing Claims:** The experiments do test the core claim that DyCO-GNN outperforms static/warm-start PI-GNN under time budgets. However, the **dynamic horizon is extremely limited** (only 10 snapshots for MaxCut/MIS and 5 for TSP). Real-world DCO typically involves dozens to hundreds of temporal steps. Evaluating only a handful of steps makes it difficult to assess long-horizon drift, cumulative error, or true adaptation capabilities.
- **Baselines:** The comparison is fair within the PI-GNN family. However, excluding state-of-the-art online/dynamic heuristics or learning-based routing baselines from the main text limits the perceived practical utility. Appendix D.3 adds non-neural baselines, but the main text should discuss these results to contextualize whether DyCO-GNN offers value beyond traditional greedy/metaheuristics.
- **Missing Analysis & Statistical Rigor:** **Error bars or standard deviations are completely missing from all tables.** Non-convex optimization is highly sensitive to random initialization. With only 5 or 10 runs, the variance could easily span the performance gaps reported. ICLR standards require reporting mean ± std across seeds to verify statistical significance.
- **Cherry-Picking / Result Quality:** The results for MaxCut and MIS are consistent. However, **TSP performance is concerning.** Table 3 reports ApR values well above 1.0 (e.g., ~1.31, ~1.76, >2.0 in some cases). This indicates solutions are 30% to 100% worse than optimal, which contradicts the abstract's claim of "high-quality solutions" for TSP. The method appears to struggle significantly with the penalty-based constraints of TSP, a critical limitation that should be highlighted rather than smoothed over.
- **Metadata/Clarity Issues:** Table 3 caption states "Values closer to 1 are better (↓)," which is contradictory. If ApR = $\ell(x)/\ell(x^*) \ge 1$, the value approaches 1 from above, so the arrow should be omitted or clarified.

### Writing & Clarity
- **Confusing Sections:** Section 4.3 (Theoretical Support) is the most confusing part due to the lack of connective tissue between the SDP proof and the GNN methodology, as noted above. The reader is left questioning how a theorem about SDP projections justifies perturbing GNN weights.
- **Figures & Tables:** The figures (2, 3, 4) effectively visualize snapshot-level performance, though the parser artifacts in the text make them hard to reference precisely. The tables are informative but would greatly benefit from variance shading. The discussion of "emb" vs "GNN" vs "full" SP application in Tables 1-3 is clear, though the lack of a decisive winner across tasks leads to a somewhat ambiguous recommendation for practitioners.

### Limitations & Broader Impact
- **Acknowledged Limitations:** The authors correctly identify that the optimal layer for SP application varies and suggest adaptive mechanisms as future work. They also conduct sensitivity analysis on $\lambda_{shrink}$ and $\lambda_{perturb}$.
- **Missed Fundamental Limitations:** 
    1. **Scalability of Dynamics:** The evaluation on very short graph sequences (≤10 steps) is a major limitation for a paper claiming contributions to "Dynamic" CO. The method's behavior over 50+ snapshots, where parameter drift or catastrophic interference could occur, is unknown.
    2. **Hyperparameter Sensitivity vs. Robustness Claim:** Section 4.2 claims SP achieves results "without sensitive hyperparameter tuning," yet Tables 11 and 12 show that performance, particularly on MIS, fluctuates significantly with $\lambda_{shrink}$ (e.g., ApR dropping from 0.92 to 0.79 when $\lambda$ changes from 0.4 to 0.8). The claim of robustness should be tempered.
- **Societal Impact:** Standard CO impacts (efficiency, resource optimization) are implied. No specific negative societal risks are discussed, which is acceptable but a brief note on the risks of deploying optimization models in critical infrastructure (given the high error rates seen in TSP) would be prudent.

### Overall Assessment
The paper presents a clean, effective adaptation of "shrink and perturb" to instance-specific GNN optimization, demonstrating empirical benefits over naive warm-starts for dynamic MaxCut and MIS on short horizons. The core idea is simple and reproducible. However, the contribution faces significant hurdles for ICLR acceptance in its current form. The claim of being the "first ML for DCO" is inaccurate and must be scoped to avoid conflict with extensive RL and online learning literature. More critically, there is a theoretical justification gap: the SDP-based theorem does not rigorously support the non-convex GNN optimization dynamics, and the empirical results for TSP show substantial approximation errors (>30%) that contradict broader quality claims. Finally, the absence of variance reporting across initialization seeds and the evaluation on extremely short temporal horizons (≤10 snapshots) weaken the statistical and practical rigor of the dynamic claims. Addressing the novelty framing, adding error bars, clarifying the theory-method link, and tempering quality claims regarding TSP are necessary to substantiate the paper's contributions.

# Neutral Reviewer
## Balanced Review

### Summary
DyCO-GNN introduces an unsupervised, instance-specific learning framework for dynamic combinatorial optimization (DCO) that eliminates the need for external training datasets by directly optimizing a QUBO relaxation over each graph snapshot. To overcome the local optima and gradient vanishing issues of naive warm-starting, the method applies a "shrink and perturb" initialization strategy adapted from supervised learning warm-start literature. Empirical evaluations across dynamic MaxCut, MIS, and TSP demonstrate that this approach consistently matches or exceeds the solution quality of fully converged static baselines while achieving 3–60x speedups under varying computational budgets.

### Strengths
1. **Clear identification of a genuine gap and a targeted solution:** The paper correctly identifies that naive warm-starting in instance-specific GNN optimization leads to premature confidence and suboptimal convergence (Figure 1). The proposed shrink-and-perturb (SP) mechanism is a simple, mathematically grounded fix that effectively restores gradient diversity without architectural changes (Section 4.2).
2. **Thorough empirical evaluation and ablation design:** Experiments span three distinct CO problems, multiple real-world dynamic networks (Infectious, UC Social, DBLP), and TSPLIB benchmarks. The authors systematically test different SP application scopes (embedding, GNN, full) and provide detailed wall-clock time vs. epoch analyses (Tables 1–3, Figures 2–4), offering practical guidance for resource-constrained deployment.
3. **Strong reproducibility and transparent engineering:** The paper provides explicit hyperparameters, network dimensions, optimizer settings, penalty terms, and post-processing/decoding strategies (Section 5.2, Appendix C). Sensitivity analyses for SP parameters and layer scopes (Appendix D.4) further demonstrate robustness and facilitate independent verification.
4. **Theoretical grounding for perturbation benefits:** Theorem 1 formally proves that adding Gaussian noise to an SDP relaxation (inspired by Goemans-Williamson) strictly increases the probability of discovering the optimal cut under mild assumptions (Section 4.3, Appendix E), providing mathematical intuition that aligns with the empirical behavior of DyCO-GNN.

### Weaknesses
1. **Restricted baseline scope limits comparative claims:** The paper explicitly excludes distribution-generalizing ML methods and classical dynamic/streaming heuristics (Section 5), stating this is to isolate PI-GNN innovations. However, this significantly narrows the benchmark, making it unclear how DyCO-GNN stacks up against established incremental algorithms or modern metaheuristics that are highly competitive in dynamic settings (Appendix D.3 only briefly compares to simple greedy and a NetworkX MIS heuristic).
2. **Theoretical analysis is partially disconnected from the proposed method:** Theorem 1 and Corollary 1 apply to SDP relaxations and assume a locally continuous SDP solver, whereas DyCO-GNN optimizes a highly non-convex GNN-based loss via gradient descent. While the theorem provides valuable intuition, it does not constitute a rigorous convergence or generalization bound for the actual GNN optimization landscape used in DyCO-GNN.
3. **Limited scale and synthetic snapshot construction:** TSP experiments use very small instances (≤71 nodes), and dynamic graph snapshots are generated via linear edge addition/deletion rather than realistic, event-driven dynamics. Real-world DCO often involves larger scales and irregular temporal patterns, which may expose scalability bottlenecks in the current GNN decoding and gradient computation pipeline.
4. **Fixed hyperparameters may not be universally optimal:** While the paper argues for robustness, Appendix D.4 shows that $\lambda_{shrink}$ drastically affects performance across time budgets (e.g., higher $\lambda_{shrink}$ helps early epochs but degrades performance as epochs increase). Relying on a single static $\lambda_{shrink}=0.4, \lambda_{perturb}=0.1$ setting without adaptive tuning may limit performance on graphs with varying degrees of temporal volatility.

### Novelty & Significance
**Novelty:** Moderate to High. The core algorithmic contribution is an elegant adaptation of the SP warm-start technique to instance-specific GNN optimization for dynamic graphs. It does not propose a new architecture or learning paradigm, but rather a principled initialization strategy with clear theoretical motivation. **Clarity:** High. The paper is well-structured, the methodology is clearly delineated, and the figures/tables effectively communicate the speed-quality trade-offs. **Reproducibility:** High. Detailed implementation settings, dataset preprocessing pipelines, hyperparameter grids, and sensitivity analyses are provided. Code release would further strengthen this. **Significance:** High for practical, edge-deployed, or rapidly changing optimization systems where training data is unavailable and inference latency is critical. However, the narrow baseline selection and small-scale dynamic benchmarks constrain its immediate impact on the broader ICLR CO community, which increasingly prioritizes scalability, distributional generalization, and rigorous comparisons to state-of-the-art solvers/heuristics.

### Suggestions for Improvement
1. **Include at least one strong dynamic baseline:** Add comparisons to a modern incremental heuristic or streaming metaheuristic (e.g., dynamic local search, evolutionary algorithms for DCO, or a recent dynamic graph neural approach) to contextualize DyCO-GNN's performance beyond the PI-GNN family and validate its practical advantage in realistic settings.
2. **Bridge the theoretical and empirical gaps:** Either extend the theoretical analysis to non-convex GNN loss landscapes (e.g., using landscape perturbation theory for gradient-based CO) or explicitly frame Theorem 1 in the main text as an intuitive motivation for SP rather than a direct proof for DyCO-GNN. Discuss how the GNN's differentiable relaxation interacts with the SP perturbation in practice.
3. **Scale experiments and diversify temporal dynamics:** Evaluate on larger-scale benchmarks (e.g., TSPLIB instances with 100–500+ nodes, dynamic graphs with >50 snapshots) and test non-synthetic evolution patterns (e.g., bursty edge arrivals, node mobility traces). Report scalability metrics (peak GPU memory, wall-clock vs. node/edge count) to assess deployment feasibility.
4. **Develop or prototype an adaptive SP mechanism:** Incorporate the paper's own suggestion (Section 6) to make $\lambda_{shrink}$ and $\lambda_{perturb}$ adaptive based on a simple heuristic of snapshot change magnitude (e.g., edge Jaccard distance or node position delta). Demonstrate that dynamic tuning yields more stable performance across varying volatility levels.
5. **Clarify TSP decoding interactions:** TSP results vary significantly depending on greedy vs. beam search decoding (Appendix C). Provide an explicit analysis or table showing how decoding strategy interacts with warm-start and SP, as suboptimal decoding can mask or exaggerate the true benefits of the learned representations.

# Final Consolidated Review
## Summary
DyCO-GNN proposes an unsupervised, instance-specific graph neural network framework for dynamic combinatorial optimization (DCO) that eliminates the need for offline training data. To address the premature convergence to poor local optima observed when naively warm-starting optimization across sequential graph snapshots, the method applies a "shrink and perturb" (SP) initialization strategy adapted from supervised learning. Empirical evaluations on dynamic MaxCut, MIS, and TSP demonstrate that this approach consistently recovers or exceeds the solution quality of fully converged static baselines while requiring significantly fewer optimization steps, positioning it as a practical tool for resource-constrained, rapidly evolving environments.

## Strengths
- **Identifies and resolves a concrete optimization pathology:** The paper clearly demonstrates that naive warm-starting of instance-specific GNNs leads to gradient saturation and trapping in suboptimal local minima as the model becomes "overconfident" in prior snapshots. The SP mechanism is a simple, parameter-efficient intervention that effectively restores gradient diversity without architectural modifications or additional training.
- **Systematic and transparent experimental design:** The evaluation spans three distinct CO problems, multiple real-world dynamic networks, and varying computational budgets. The authors provide explicit hyperparameters, penalty formulations, decoding strategies (e.g., beam search vs. greedy for TSP), and sensitivity analyses across SP parameters and layer scopes. This level of engineering transparency offers clear, actionable guidance for practitioners.
- **Consistent and substantial speed-quality trade-offs:** DyCO-GNN reliably matches or surpasses the final approximation ratios of static baselines using only a fraction of the optimization steps (often 1.67%–33% of the total runtime). This directly addresses the core motivation: enabling high-quality decisions under strict, real-time latency constraints where solving from scratch is infeasible.

## Weaknesses
- **Narrow baseline scope limits practical validation:** The evaluation explicitly excludes distribution-generalizing machine learning methods and established classical dynamic/streaming heuristics from the main text. While this isolates innovations within the PI-GNN family, it leaves DyCO-GNN's competitive standing against state-of-the-art incremental solvers or dynamic metaheuristics unverified. The brief non-neural comparisons in Appendix D.3 are insufficient to contextualize whether the method offers meaningful advantages over existing CO pipelines in realistic deployments.
- **Theoretical disconnect with the proposed method:** Theorem 1 proves that perturbation improves the success probability of the Goemans-Williamson SDP relaxation for MaxCut. However, DyCO-GNN optimizes a highly non-convex, parameterized GNN with a QUBO relaxation via gradient descent. The geometric properties of PSD cone projections and metric continuity do not transfer to the complex loss landscapes of deep neural networks. Consequently, the theorem functions as intuitive motivation rather than a rigorous justification for DyCO-GNN's dynamics, and its presentation risks overstating the mathematical grounding of the approach.
- **Limited temporal horizon and highly regular snapshot construction:** The evaluation uses very few steps (≤10 for MaxCut/MIS, 5 for TSP) and synthetic, linear trajectory generation (steady edge additions/deletions, single-node linear movement). This severely limits insight into long-horizon behavior, such as cumulative parameter drift, catastrophic interference, or robustness to bursty, irregular topology shifts characteristic of real-world dynamic systems.
- **TSP performance contradicts broad quality claims:** The method struggles significantly with dynamic TSP, yielding approximation ratios frequently >1.3 and occasionally approaching 1.7–2.0. While penalty-based QUBO encodings are known to be challenging for gradient-based solvers, these results directly conflict with the abstract's claim of "high-quality solutions" across all tested problems. The paper does not adequately reconcile this gap or explicitly scope TSP as a failure mode of the current formulation.

## Nice-to-Haves
- **Empirical verification of the optimization hypothesis:** Plotting gradient norms, Hessian conditioning, or low-dimensional projections of the parameter space across snapshots would directly validate the claim that shrinking restores usable gradient diversity and disrupts premature confidence.
- **Statistical variance reporting:** While mean trends are pronounced, reporting standard deviations or confidence intervals across random initialization seeds would better quantify result stability for non-convex optimization.
- **Ablation against standard restart strategies:** Comparing SP against common non-convex optimization tricks (e.g., learning rate warm-restarts, momentum resetting, or cyclic scheduling) would help isolate whether SP provides unique benefits or acts as a proxy for known gradient recovery techniques.

## Novel Insights
The paper's core conceptual contribution lies in successfully repurposing a technique designed to fix generalization gaps in supervised fine-tuning ("shrink and perturb") for an entirely different regime: unsupervised, instance-specific non-convex optimization over sequential graphs. In doing so, it exposes a hidden failure mode in dynamic PI-GNN pipelines: the optimization process implicitly accumulates parameter "overconfidence," causing gradients to vanish and trapping the solver in snapshot-local basins. The key insight is that deliberately degrading parameter magnitude and injecting controlled Gaussian noise between steps functions as a principled soft-reset. This gently destabilizes the optimization trajectory just enough to escape local minima while preserving structural priors learned from previous snapshots, effectively decoupling fast convergence from solution quality degradation. This reframes warm-starting in dynamic CO not as a simple parameter copy operation, but as a carefully balanced exploration-exploitation transition.

## Suggestions
- Reframe Section 4.3 to explicitly position Theorem 1 as motivational intuition derived from convex relaxation analysis, clarifying that it does not constitute a formal bound or convergence guarantee for the non-convex GNN training dynamics used in DyCO-GNN.
- Scope the novelty claim in the introduction precisely to "unsupervised, instance-specific GNN optimization for DCO," acknowledging existing reinforcement learning and online learning approaches for dynamic routing and vehicle scheduling to avoid inaccurate "first to apply ML" statements.
- Include at least one strong classical dynamic baseline (e.g., a specialized dynamic local search, streaming incremental heuristic, or dynamic MIP warm-start) in the main experimental comparison to properly contextualize DyCO-GNN's practical utility beyond its own family.
- Revise the abstract and conclusion to accurately reflect TSP performance limitations, explicitly discussing how penalty-heavy QUBO formulations interact with gradient-based GNN optimization, and consider whether adaptive decoding or constraint-specific architectural tweaks are necessary for routing problems.
- If space permits, restructure Table 1–3 results or add a complementary plot showing the exact wall-clock time required for each method to cross fixed ApR thresholds (e.g., 0.95, 0.98), providing direct, unambiguous validation of the "3–60x faster" speedup claim.
