# Harsh Critic Review
## Section-by-Section Critical Review

### Title & Abstract
- **Title Accuracy:** The title accurately reflects the core contribution: leveraging non-curated data to guide world models for efficient RL.
- **Abstract Clarity:** The abstract clearly delineates the problem (underutilization of reward-free, mixed-quality, multi-embodiment data), the method (experience rehearsal and execution guidance alongside world model pre-training), and key results (significant gains across 72 tasks).
- **Claims Check:** The claim of "nearly twice the aggregate score" is substantiated by the IQM and mean results in the appendix tables, though the magnitude should be contextualized against the specific baselines. The abstract states naive fine-tuning fails, which is directly addressed in the empirical ablation (Fig 6), making the claim supported. However, the abstract implies a broad solution, while the method is specifically tailored for actor-critic model-based RL (Dreamer-style), a nuance not captured in the abstract.

### Introduction & Motivation
- **Motivation & Gap:** The motivation is strong and aligns well with current trends in scaling RL data. The paper convincingly argues that requiring curated, reward-labeled, or task-specific offline data is a bottleneck, and identifying distributional shift as the root cause for naive fine-tuning failure is a valuable diagnostic contribution.
- **Contributions:** Contributions C1-C4 are precise and accurately reflect the paper's content. The identification of the fine-tuning gap (C2) and the proposed reuse mechanisms (C3) are clearly stated.
- **Claim Calibration:** The introduction suggests prior world model pre-training methods show "only marginal improvements," which risks under-selling concurrent work that uses advanced architectures or different pre-training objectives. However, the authors later provide an aligned comparison with iVideoGPT (Sec A.2), which mitigates this concern. The introduction is otherwise well-scoped and avoids over-claiming by specifying the "in-domain" assumption early on.

### Method / Approach
- **Clarity & Reproducibility:** The method is well-described, and Algorithm 1 provides a clear blueprint of the pipeline. The modifications to the RSSM (zero-padding actions, scaling parameters) are practical choices. Hyperparameters are listed in Appendix J, and implementation details are provided, supporting reproducibility.
- **Assumptions & Justification:** The paper assumes **in-domain** offline data (reward-free but from related embodiments/tasks). This is justified given the retrieval mechanism's reliance on representation similarity, but it limits applicability to truly "in-the-wild" datasets with significant embodiment mismatches or domain gaps. The use of zero-padding for multi-embodiment action spaces is pragmatic but lacks analysis on whether the world model learns to effectively ignore padding, which could introduce bias in dynamics prediction for lower-dimensional agents.
- **Logical Gaps & Edge Cases:** The retrieval mechanism (Eq. 5) relies solely on the distance between the initial observation of the online rollout and offline trajectories. For long-horizon or sparse-reward tasks, initial state similarity may not guarantee trajectory relevance to the task goal. While Fig. 14 shows robustness to irrelevant data, the mechanism for preventing retrieval from sampling actively misleading or contradictory behaviors is not discussed.
- **Theoretical Claims:** The theoretical analysis in Appendix B is a notable weak point. Proposition 1 is a straightforward property of mixture distributions and does not constitute a novel proof. Proposition 2 is a direct application of Kakade & Langford (2002) to a mixed policy; it does not account for the specific dynamics of world model rollouts, representation shift during fine-tuning, or the stochastic schedule used in execution guidance. The theory does not materially advance the understanding of *why* NCRL works beyond standard RL literature.

### Experiments & Results
- **Testing Claims:** The experiments comprehensively test the claims. Evaluating on 72 tasks across locomotion and manipulation with a strict 150k sample budget provides strong evidence for sample efficiency. The ablation study (Fig. 6, Fig. 8) isolates the effects of pre-training, retrieval, and guidance, confirming that the combination is necessary for robust performance.
- **Baselines:** The baseline selection is appropriate, covering visual RL (R3M), offline RL (ExPLORe, UDS-RLPD, JSRL-BC), and model-based RL (DreamerV3, DrQ-v2, iVideoGPT). Notably, baselines like ExPLORe and JSRL-BC are given curated task-relevant subsets, which actually strengthens NCRL's claim: if baselines with cleaner data still lose to NCRL using noisy, multi-task data, the proposed method is superior. The aligned iVideoGPT experiment is a crucial fairness check.
- **Ablations & Robustness:** The ablations are solid. Sensitivity to the guidance schedule (Fig. 12) and robustness to injected irrelevant data (Fig. 14) address potential hyperparameter brittleness. A minor missing ablation is the retrieval capacity $k$ or embedding dimension used for retrieval, which could impact performance on tasks with denser state-space coverage.
- **Statistical Rigor:** Results report means with 95% confidence intervals and use 5 seeds for NCRL vs 3 for baselines. While standard in RL, aggregate claims across 72 tasks would benefit from statistical significance testing (e.g., t-tests or bootstrap r-estimates) rather than visual inspection of curves.
- **Dataset Realism:** The Meta-World dataset is constructed by injecting Gaussian noise into TDMPC2 trajectories. While this simulates "mixed quality," it does not fully capture the distributional characteristics of real non-curated data (e.g., teleoperation variance, failed recoveries, or multi-step distribution shifts). The DMControl data comes from curiosity-driven exploration, which may lack goal-directed structure. The results are impressive, but the gap to truly uncurated, human-in-the-wild data remains.

### Writing & Clarity
- **Clarity Issues:** The paper is generally well-structured and readable. The explanation of distribution shift in Sec 3.3 is clear and well-supported by the visualization in Fig. 2.
- **Figures/Tables:** Figures are informative. Fig. 2 effectively illustrates the distributional shift and retrieval effect. Fig. 3 and Fig. 4 clearly show performance gains. Some axis labels in the appendix figures are small, but this is minor.
- **Theory Section:** As noted, Appendix B reads more like a literature review of known bounds than a theoretical analysis tailored to NCRL. This may confuse readers expecting formal guarantees on the retrieval mechanism or world model fine-tuning stability.
- **Parser Artifacts:** Ignored as instructed. Content is coherent.

### Limitations & Broader Impact
- **Acknowledged Limitations:** The authors correctly identify key limitations: reliance on RSSM (scaling constraints), generalization to novel embodiments, restriction to in-domain data, and simulation-only evaluation.
- **Missed Limitations:** 
  1. **Compute & Memory Overhead:** The paper states retrieval takes "seconds" using FAISS but does not discuss the memory footprint of storing embeddings for 10M+ state-action pairs or the latency impact during the RL training loop. In large-scale online RL, retrieval overhead and GPU memory fragmentation from dual buffers (online + retrieved) could be significant bottlenecks.
  2. **Reward Utilization:** The method learns rewards solely from online interactions. Even without explicit labels, non-curated data often contains implicit reward signals (e.g., via contrastive or preference modeling). Ignoring this limits the data's utility during the critical low-sample regime.
- **Broader Impact & Failure Modes:** The ethics statement is standard. A more nuanced discussion of failure modes would address scenarios where the offline data distribution is actively adversarial or contradictory to the target task dynamics. The current robustness checks only go up to 100% irrelevant data, which doesn't fully test the boundary conditions of retrieval-based rehearsal.

### Overall Assessment
This is a strong empirical contribution to model-based and offline-to-online reinforcement learning. The paper identifies a tangible bottleneck in world model fine-tuning (distributional shift and narrow initial state coverage) and proposes two practical, well-motivated mechanisms (experience rehearsal and execution guidance) that yield consistent, substantial gains across a large suite of 72 tasks. The experimental design is rigorous, with thoughtful baseline comparisons and comprehensive ablations that validate the necessity of each component. The primary weaknesses lie in the theoretical framing, which relies on restating existing bounds without addressing method-specific nuances, and the synthetic construction of the non-curated dataset, which may underrepresent the complexity of real-world offline data. Additionally, the computational overhead and memory implications of large-scale retrieval during online training are under-discussed. Despite these concerns, the empirical evidence is compelling, the methodology is sound, and the work addresses a realistic and increasingly important problem setting. The contribution stands as a solid advance for sample-efficient RL leveraging pre-existing, uncurated datasets.

# Neutral Reviewer
## Balanced Review

### Summary
This paper proposes NCRL, a pipeline for improving the sample efficiency of online reinforcement learning by leveraging reward-free, mixed-quality, and multi-embodiment offline data. The authors identify distributional shift between offline pre-training data and online fine-tuning data as the primary cause of failure in naive world model fine-tuning, and address it via two techniques: retrieval-based experience rehearsal and execution guidance through a behavior-cloned prior policy. Evaluated across 72 visuomotor tasks, NCRL demonstrates substantial sample efficiency gains over training-from-scratch and existing offline-to-online baselines.

### Strengths
1. **Well-Motivated and Practical Problem Formulation:** The focus on non-curated, reward-free, mixed-quality, multi-embodiment data reflects real-world data collection challenges more accurately than prior work relying on expert or reward-labeled datasets. The problem setup is clearly defined in Sec. 3.1 and aligns with current trends in scalable robotics and RL.
2. **Clear Diagnostic Insight:** The identification of distributional shift as the bottleneck for naive world model fine-tuning is well-supported by both qualitative (t-SNE visualization in Fig. 2) and quantitative (Wasserstein distance reduction in Fig. 2 right) analysis. This diagnostic directly motivates the proposed interventions.
3. **Extensive and Rigorous Empirical Evaluation:** The evaluation spans 72 diverse tasks across DMControl and Meta-World under a strict 150k sample budget. Comparisons against strong baselines (DrQ-v2, DreamerV3, ExPLORe, JSRL-BC, iVideoGPT) are comprehensive, and the method consistently achieves near double the aggregate score of scratch baselines (Sec. 4.1, Tables 3-5, Fig. 3).
4. **Effective, Computationally Light Components:** Experience rehearsal (latent-space trajectory retrieval) and execution guidance (policy interleaving) are simple yet highly effective. The retrieval precision (Table 2), robustness to irrelevant data injection (Fig. 14), and hyperparameter insensitivity (Fig. 12) provide strong empirical justification for the design choices.
5. **Strong Reproducibility:** The paper provides detailed implementation specifics, full algorithmic pseudocode (Alg. 1), hyperparameter tables (Table 6, Sec. J), compute resource breakdown, and promises open code/datasets, meeting ICLR's high bar for reproducibility.

### Weaknesses
1. **Simplistic Retrieval Mechanism:** The retrieval strategy relies solely on Euclidean distance between initial observation latents (Eq. 5). While effective for many tasks (Table 2), it may struggle with long-horizon or context-dependent tasks where initial frames are insufficient to capture task relevance. The paper acknowledges this (Sec. A.3) but does not explore temporally-aware or multi-view retrieval alternatives.
2. **Overstated Theoretical Contribution:** The theoretical justification for execution guidance (Sec. B.2) directly applies the performance difference bound from Kakade & Langford (2002). While mathematically sound, it repackages existing theory to justify a heuristic rather than providing novel theoretical insights into distribution shift or world model fine-tuning dynamics.
3. **Ambiguity in Domain-Match vs. Method Architecture:** The comparison with methods like iVideoGPT relies partly on domain alignment (NCRL uses in-domain pre-training data, while baselines use cross-embodiment data). Although an aligned ablation is provided (Sec. A.2), the inherent advantage of domain-specific data over cross-domain data is well-known, which slightly complicates attributing all gains purely to the proposed fine-tuning techniques.
4. **Compute Overhead Not Quantified:** While sample efficiency is thoroughly analyzed, the wall-clock time and compute overhead introduced by retrieval (Faiss indexing, distance computation) and alternating policy execution during data collection are not reported. For a method claiming "efficiency," clarifying computational trade-offs is necessary.

### Novelty & Significance
The paper offers moderate-to-high novelty in the ICLR context. While the individual components (data replay, BC prior mixing, world model pre-training) draw from established RL literature, their specific integration to solve the distributional shift problem in non-curated, multi-embodiment fine-tuning is novel and practically impactful. The empirical scale (72 tasks, rigorous baselines, ablations) and clear problem framing elevate its significance. It directly addresses a critical bottleneck in offline-to-online RL and provides a robust, reproducible pipeline that advances how the community can leverage untapped offline datasets. It comfortably meets ICLR's bar for empirical rigor and practical relevance, though the theoretical contributions are incremental.

### Suggestions for Improvement
1. **Enhance Retrieval Robustness:** Explore or at least discuss trajectory-level similarity metrics (e.g., contrastive learning over full trajectories, or incorporating action sequences) to mitigate failures in long-horizon tasks where initial frames are ambiguous.
2. **Quantify Computational Trade-offs:** Report the latency of the retrieval process, indexing overhead, and wall-clock time compared to baselines to confirm that improved sample efficiency does not come at an unacceptable computational cost.
3. **Clarify Data Splits and Generalization Bounds:** Explicitly detail the degree of semantic/environmental overlap between the non-curated pre-training data and the downstream fine-tuning tasks. This will help readers better disentangle the benefits of domain alignment from the proposed fine-tuning techniques.
4. **Refine Theoretical Claims:** Reframe Proposition 2 to clearly position the Kakade & Langford bound as a justification for policy mixing rather than a novel theoretical contribution. Focusing the theory section on novel bounds regarding distribution shift mitigation in model-based fine-tuning would be more impactful.
5. **Provide Adaptive Scheduling:** Investigate a lightweight, adaptive mechanism for the execution guidance schedule (e.g., triggered by value function uncertainty, prediction error, or return thresholds) to reduce reliance on manual linear annealing hyperparameter tuning across different task difficulties.

# Spark Finder Review
## How to Improve This Paper

### Missing Experiments (top 3-5 only)
1. **Add a retrieval baseline (e.g., random offline replay or uniform subsampling).** The experience rehearsal contribution relies entirely on the assumption that neural distance retrieval yields superior trajectories; without comparing against random selection, you cannot prove that intelligent filtering is the active ingredient.
2. **Test execution guidance with predominantly failed offline data.** Since your data is mixed-quality, the BC prior $\pi_{bc}$ may be suboptimal; you must show how NCRL degrades as the quality of retrieved data worsens to prove guidance doesn't introduce negative transfer.
3. **Evaluate with genuinely out-of-domain offline data.** Your "non-curated" datasets are synthetically generated from the exact same benchmark domains using exploratory agents; testing on external unlabeled video or trajectories from unrelated embodiments is required to validate the claim that abundant in-the-wild data is useful.

### Deeper Analysis Needed (top 3-5 only)
1. **Quantify world model prediction error and value bias during fine-tuning.** Attributing failure to "distributional shift" is qualitative; providing metrics like latent dynamics error, reward model calibration error, or value function variance with vs. without rehearsal is needed to trust the stabilization mechanism.
2. **Replace the cited policy improvement bound with model-specific analysis.** Proposition 2 is a standard monotonic improvement bound (Kakade & Langford 2002) that ignores model-based errors and reward-free priors; a derivation bounding the impact of retrieval-induced shift on model bias or rollout value would substantiate the method.
3. **Perform component-wise credit assignment.** It is unclear whether experience rehearsal primarily anchors the world model dynamics or aids the reward function learning, and whether execution guidance improves initial data collection quality or prevents model collapse; disentangling these effects would clarify the distinct utility of each module.

### Visualizations & Case Studies
1. **Compare long-horizon world model rollouts with and without rehearsal.** Visualizing how far imagined trajectories stay on track against ground truth for the same initial state would directly prove whether rehearsal prevents model divergence or collapse.
2. **Visualize retrieval precision failure modes.** Since Table 2 shows the retrieval can grab conflicting tasks (e.g., "Door Close" for "Door Open"), a case study showing the agent's recovery from these semantically inverted priors would expose whether the method is robust or fragile.
3. **Plot execution guidance switching dynamics and reward trajectory.** Show a timeline of episodes indicating exactly when $\pi_{bc}$ switches to $\pi_{\phi}$ and the immediate reward/value signal before/after the handoff to prove mid-episode switching actively rescues poor states rather than adding noise.

### Obvious Next Steps
1. **Report total compute efficiency, not just sample efficiency.** Pre-training requires ~48 GPU hours, which you must combine with online steps to plot performance vs. total compute; without this, the claim of "efficient RL" is unsupported against training-from-scratch baselines.
2. **Include a frozen world model ablation.** The paper claims naive fine-tuning fails, but a control where the pre-trained model is frozen during RL (using only offline replay for data) is essential to determine if fine-tuning is truly necessary or if the proposed methods work with a static model.
3. **Demonstrate cross-embodiment or multi-task fine-tuning.** While the world model is trained on multi-embodiment data, all evaluations are single-task; showing simultaneous fine-tuning on multiple tasks or cross-embodiment transfer would validate the core advantage of the multi-embodiment setup.

# Final Consolidated Review
## Summary
The paper proposes NCRL, a pipeline to improve the sample efficiency of online reinforcement learning by leveraging reward-free, mixed-quality, and multi-embodiment offline data. Identifying distributional shift between offline pre-training and online fine-tuning as the bottleneck for naive world model adaptation, the authors introduce retrieval-based experience rehearsal and execution guidance via a behavior-cloned prior. Evaluated across 72 visuomotor tasks under strict sample budgets, NCRL demonstrates substantial sample efficiency gains over training-from-scratch and established offline-to-online baselines.

## Strengths
- **Clear Diagnostic & Direct Problem Framing:** The paper effectively isolates distributional shift as the primary reason naive world model fine-tuning fails on hard-exploration tasks. This insight is convincingly supported by t-SNE visualizations and quantitative Wasserstein distance analysis, cleanly motivating the proposed rehearsal and guidance mechanisms without architectural overcomplication.
- **Rigorous & Scaled Empirical Validation:** The evaluation across 72 tasks (DMControl and Meta-World) under a strict 150k sample budget is thorough. NCRL consistently outperforms model-free, model-based, and offline RL baselines, and the aligned comparison with iVideoGPT controls for experimental confounds, providing strong evidence for sample efficiency.
- **Practical & Robust Design Choices:** Experience rehearsal and execution guidance are conceptually lightweight yet highly effective. The method shows strong empirical robustness to injected irrelevant data and hyperparameter schedule variations, and the detailed algorithmic pseudocode, hyperparameter tables, and open-source commitment meet high reproducibility standards.

## Weaknesses
- **Retrieval Mechanism Lacks Necessary Ablations & Depth Analysis:** Experience retrieval relies exclusively on Euclidean distance between initial observation latents (Eq. 5) but is never benchmarked against a random or uniform offline replay baseline. Without this control, it is impossible to isolate whether the gains stem from intelligent neural-distance retrieval or merely from re-injecting any offline data during fine-tuning. Additionally, initial-state similarity is a weak proxy for task relevance in long-horizon or sparse-reward settings, a limitation the paper acknowledges but does not quantify.
- **Overstated "Non-Curated" Framing vs. Synthetic Data:** The paper positions the setting around leveraging abundant, in-the-wild, non-curated data, yet the experimental datasets are synthetically generated from the exact downstream benchmarks (curiosity-driven RL for DMControl, noise-corrupted TDMPC for Meta-World). This constitutes a standard mixed-quality offline setting with strong domain alignment. The impressive results likely benefit heavily from this inherent overlap, and the method's ability to generalize to truly out-of-domain or human-in-the-wild video remains untested and unsubstantiated.
- **Missing Total Compute & Overhead Accounting:** The paper heavily emphasizes sample efficiency but omits a transparent accounting of wall-clock time and memory footprint. With ~48 GPU hours for pretraining, continuous Faiss indexing, and dual replay buffer management, the total computational cost relative to training-from-scratch or lighter baselines is unclear. Without this, claims of "efficient RL" are only partially valid, as sample efficiency does not necessarily translate to compute efficiency.
- **Negligible Theoretical Contribution & Mechanistic Opacity:** The theoretical appendix simply repackages standard results (mixture distribution properties and Kakade & Langford's 2002 performance difference bound) without deriving novel guarantees specific to world model dynamics, representation drift, or the proposed retrieval schedule. It serves as post-hoc justification rather than advancing mechanistic understanding. Furthermore, the paper does not track world model prediction error or value bias during fine-tuning, leaving open whether rehearsal truly stabilizes latent dynamics or merely provides better initialization states.

## Nice-to-Haves
- Quantify world model prediction error and reward model calibration during online fine-tuning with vs. without rehearsal to empirically trace the stabilization mechanism beyond distribution distance metrics.
- Provide an ablation comparing frozen vs. fully fine-tuned world models combined with the proposed pipeline to clarify how much performance stems from latent dynamics adaptation versus data/guidance strategies.
- Explore adaptive, signal-driven scheduling for execution guidance (e.g., triggered by model prediction uncertainty or critic variance) to reduce manual linear annealing tuning across tasks of varying difficulty.

## Novel Insights
NCRL’s primary conceptual contribution lies in shifting the bottleneck for offline-to-online transfer from architectural pre-training capacity to distributional alignment during fine-tuning. By demonstrating that a simple, task-agnostic world model can be effectively adapted through targeted state-space reanchoring (experience rehearsal) and exploratory steering (execution guidance), the work reframes how the community should approach non-curated data utilization. Instead of designing increasingly complex self-supervised objectives or scaling models, the paper pragmatically shows that bridging the offline-online state coverage gap during the critical low-data regime yields disproportionate gains in sample efficiency and task success.

## Potentially Missed Related Work
- **Trajectory- or Temporal-Contrastive Retrieval for Experience Replay (e.g., temporal contrastive learning in RL, or sequence-level similarity metrics).** Relevant because the current method relies on static initial observation latents, which may miss crucial temporal dynamics or goal-aligned state transitions necessary for complex, multi-step tasks.
- **Compute-Aware RL Benchmarks and Wall-Clock Efficiency Studies.** Relevant for contextualizing whether sample efficiency gains hold under realistic compute budgets, especially when pre-training and continuous retrieval overhead are factored into total training time.

## Suggestions
- Explicitly benchmark the neural-distance retrieval against a random or uniform subsampling baseline of the same offline budget to prove that the retrieval mechanism itself is an active ingredient in the performance gains.
- Report total wall-clock training time, indexing latency, and GPU memory footprint for the full pipeline, including pre-training, to substantiate claims of holistic efficiency over compute-heavy baselines.
- Reframe the theoretical section to clearly position existing bounds as heuristic justifications for policy mixing rather than novel theoretical contributions, and replace the space with empirical tracking of latent dynamics error, reward calibration, and value function variance during the fine-tuning phase.
