# Harsh Critic Review
## Section-by-Section Critical Review

### Title & Abstract
- **Does the title accurately reflect the contribution?** The title emphasizes "Guard LLM Unlearning in Context via Negative Detection and Reasoning." This is reasonably accurate, as the paper presents an inference-time routing framework rather than parameter modification. However, "unlearning" traditionally implies altering model parameters or representations; the method is functionally a highly optimized inference-time guardrail/filter. The title could more precisely signal that the contribution is a *system-level behavioral intervention* rather than model-centric unlearning.
- **Does the abstract clearly state the problem, method, and key results?** Yes. It correctly identifies the need for retain-data-free, black-box compatible, and continual unlearning. It summarizes DRAGON's two-stage pipeline (detection + CoT intervention), claims of zero base-model modification, and introduces new evaluation metrics. Results are summarized as strong unlearning capability, scalability, and applicability.
- **Are any claims in the abstract unsupported by the paper?** The claim of a "training-free" approach is slightly overstated in the abstract. While the *base LLM* is never fine-tuned, the framework explicitly requires training a RoBERTa-based scoring model (§4.1, §C.3) and SFT-fine-tuning an 8B guard model (§4.2). The abstract should clarify that "training-free" applies specifically to the target/base LLM.

### Introduction & Motivation
- **Is the problem well-motivated? Is the gap in prior work clearly identified?** Yes. The introduction effectively highlights the computational cost, retain-data dependency, and catastrophic forgetting risks of gradient-based unlearning, while pointing out the underexploration of training-free methods in realistic, data-limited settings. The motivation for continual unlearning (§1, §2) is clear and practically relevant.
- **Are the contributions clearly stated and accurate?** The four contributions are clearly enumerated. However, Contribution 1 ("novel systematic unlearning framework") leans heavily on system engineering and prompt routing. ICLR typically expects a clearer articulation of the *machine learning novelty* (e.g., the detection fusion mechanism, the CoT distillation objective, or the theoretical/empirical justification for in-context behavioral steering) rather than system integration.
- **Does the introduction over-claim or under-sell?** The introduction slightly over-claims the "flexibility" and "scalability" without immediately acknowledging that detection accuracy and guard model generalization are hard ceilings for the method's effectiveness. If the detector's recall drops, the framework fails to unlearn entirely (since the base model is untouched). This fundamental dependency should be framed earlier to set realistic expectations.

### Method / Approach
- **Is the method clearly described and reproducible?** The pipeline is well-structured: Unlearn Store creation → Dual-layer detection (embedding similarity + trained scorer) → Policy/CoT retrieval → Guard model SFT → In-context injection. Implementation details in §C and Appendix F are sufficiently detailed for reproduction. However, Equation (1) and (2) contain parser artifacts that obscure the exact mathematical formulation, though the textual descriptions salvage clarity.
- **Are key assumptions stated and justified?** The method assumes: (1) high-quality paraphrased forget prompts are available to populate the Unlearn Store; (2) the base LLM has strong instruction-following capabilities; (3) a small amount of synthetic/paraphrased data is sufficient to train the detector and guard model. These are justified for practical deployment but limit applicability in scenarios where even synthetic paraphrasing is restricted or computationally prohibitive.
- **Are there logical gaps in the derivation or reasoning?** The core logical gap lies in conflating *detection accuracy* with *unlearning success*. Since the base model is never updated, any prompt that evades the detector (§4.1) will be answered using the original, unmodified weights. The paper treats this as "soft unlearning" but does not formally bound the unlearning guarantee based on detector recall/precision. A probabilistic or worst-case bound linking detector performance to actual unlearning efficacy would strengthen the theoretical grounding.
- **Are there edge cases or failure modes not discussed?** Yes. (1) **System prompt injection:** If a user modifies system instructions, the prepended CoT guardrails can be bypassed. (2) **Over-intervention:** High semantic similarity triggers on benign prompts containing shared vocabulary (e.g., "biology" in general academic contexts) may cause unnecessary refusals, degrading utility. While FPR is reported, precision-impact on nuanced benign queries is not deeply analyzed. (3) **Domain shift in continuous setting:** As the Unlearn Store grows, cosine similarity retrieval may suffer from noise without explicit scaling mechanisms tested.

### Experiments & Results
- **Do the experiments actually test the paper's claims?** The experiments thoroughly evaluate forget quality (DS, KFR, ProbAcc), utility preservation (MU, MMLU), and continual stability (DDS, DUS) across TOFU, WMDP, and MUSE. The ablations (§6.2, §6.3, Appendix D) isolate the CoT component, detection alternatives, and paraphrase counts. However, they do not test the framework under true *black-box* conditions with closed-source models at scale beyond small API samples, limiting claims about production scalability.
- **Are baselines appropriate and fairly compared?** Baselines are strong and include state-of-the-art methods (NPO-RT, RMU, ICUL+). The comparison is generally fair. The authors transparently note that ICUL+ operates under an idealized setting, and Filter-Prompting uses a highly accurate classifier. This honesty is commendable, though it subtly shifts the focus: DRAGON's strong performance relies heavily on building a superior detector+CoT pipeline rather than a fundamentally new unlearning objective.
- **Are there missing ablations that would materially change conclusions?** 
  1. **Unlearn Store scale:** Table 16/17 tests up to 800 paraphrases. Real-world deployments require thousands/millions. How does latency, false positive rate, and detection recall scale with 10k/100k entries? This is critical for the scalability claim.
  2. **Guard model data size:** The impact of reducing the CoT training dataset below 800 examples is not shown. Understanding the data efficiency of the guard SFT would clarify the "one-time cost" claim.
- **Are error bars / statistical significance reported?** No. All tables (1, 2, 3, etc.) report single-run determinate scores. ICLR standards strongly expect standard deviations, confidence intervals, or at least multiple seeds for fine-tuning-based components (detector and guard model) to rule out variance-driven results.
- **Do the results support the claims made, or are they cherry-picked?** Results consistently support the claims across model sizes and tasks. Table 1 and 2 show clear improvements. However, the novel metrics (RQ, DDS, DUS) are evaluated without external validation (e.g., human correlation studies), making it difficult to assert that a higher DDS/RQ strictly correlates with safer/more useful models in practice.
- **Are datasets and evaluation metrics appropriate?** Datasets are standard. The novelty of RQ, DDS, and DUS is interesting but heuristic. RQ's arbitrary weighting (1, 1, 0.2 for template similarity, refusal rate, gibberish score) lacks justification or sensitivity analysis (§D.8). DDS's `β=0.5` penalty for upward deviation is also unvalidated. Without ablation on these hyperparameters or human alignment studies, the metrics risk being ad-hoc.

### Writing & Clarity
- **Are there sections that are confusing or poorly explained?** The formulation of DDS and DUS in §3.2 suffers from heavy notation clutter and formatting artifacts, making the exact computation slightly opaque. Additionally, the transition between "Unlearning Prompt Detection" and "In Context Intervention" could better explain the routing logic: specifically, how the base model handles the prepended CoT vs. original prompt when both contain task-relevant keywords.
- **Are figures and tables clear and informative?** Tables are dense but informative. Table 14 (MUSE) uses color coding in text, which is lost in plaintext but conceptually clear. Figure 2a/2b effectively visualizes scaling trends. However, the absence of error bars on all plots/tables is a significant clarity and rigor gap for ICLR review.

### Limitations & Broader Impact
- **Do the authors acknowledge the key limitations?** Yes. Latency overhead for intervened prompts (§D.7), potential misuse for censorship (Ethics Statement), and reliance on detection accuracy are acknowledged. The "Theory Gap" discussion (§E) explicitly notes the lack of theoretical guarantees.
- **Are there fundamental limitations they missed?** The most critical limitation is the **lack of weight-level erasure**. Because the base model remains fully memorized, any scenario where the DRAGON guardrail can be circumvented (e.g., via prompt injection, distillation attacks on the base model before DRAGON deployment, or direct weight extraction by an attacker) renders the "unlearning" completely ineffective. This should be prominently featured as a security boundary limitation. Additionally, the detector requires periodic retraining as paraphrase distributions and attack strategies drift, which is only touched on briefly.
- **Are there failure modes or negative societal impacts not discussed?** The paper discusses misuse for selective fact erasure. It does not discuss the risk of **over-censorship**: if the Unlearn Store contains broad concepts (e.g., "cybersecurity"), the similarity-based detector might flag legitimate security research or educational queries, leading to systemic refusal of benign knowledge. A discussion on precision-recall trade-offs for sensitive domains would strengthen the impact analysis.

### Overall Assessment
DRAGON presents a well-engineered, inference-time unlearning framework that effectively addresses practical constraints like black-box deployment, retain-data unavailability, and continual unlearning requests. The empirical results are strong, demonstrating superior forget-utility trade-offs over strong fine-tuning baselines across multiple benchmarks, and the introduction of RQ, DDS, and DUS highlights a valuable focus on evaluation beyond static accuracy. However, the work's positioning requires careful calibration for ICLR: it is fundamentally a sophisticated routing and guardrail system rather than a novel parameter-level unlearning algorithm. The primary concerns are (1) the arbitrary weighting and lack of human validation for the proposed metrics, (2) the absence of error bars or statistical significance testing across multiple seeds, (3) untested scalability of the detection module to large Unlearn Stores, and (4) the lack of a formal discussion bounding unlearning guarantees by detector recall. The contribution stands and is practically valuable, but to meet the ICLR bar, the authors must rigorously validate their metrics, report statistical confidence, explicitly frame the method as behavioral unlearning with clear security boundaries, and provide ablations on detection scaling. Addressing these points will significantly strengthen the paper's scientific rigor and impact.

# Neutral Reviewer
## Balanced Review

### Summary
DRAGON proposes a training-free, in-context unlearning framework designed for black-box LLMs operating without access to retain data. The system routes incoming prompts through a dual-layer detection module; matches trigger a lightweight, fine-tuned guard model to generate chain-of-thought (CoT) instructions that steer the base model toward safe refusals or alternative responses. Alongside the framework, the authors introduce three evaluation metrics: Refusal Quality (RQ) to penalize incoherent refusals, and Dynamic Deviation Score (DDS) / Dynamic Utility Score (DUS) to measure stability across continual unlearning steps. Extensive experiments across privacy, safety, and copyright benchmarks demonstrate strong unlearning efficacy, utility preservation, and robustness to adversarial perturbations.

### Strengths
1. **Addresses Critical Real-World Constraints:** By decoupling unlearning from gradient-based fine-tuning and eliminating the need for retain data, DRAGON directly targets deployment barriers for proprietary/black-box LLMs. The modular detection → policy retrieval → CoT generation pipeline is highly scalable and compatible with API-only models.
2. **Rigorous and Comprehensive Empirical Validation:** The paper evaluates across 9+ LLMs (including open-weight and closed-source), three distinct unlearning tasks (WMDP, TOFU, MUSE), and a continual unlearning setting. DRAGON consistently achieves top or near-top performance in forgetting metrics while preserving general utility (Tables 1, 2, 3, 13).
3. **Meaningful Metric Innovations:** RQ, DDS, and DUS fill recognized gaps in unlearning evaluation. RQ effectively penalizes degenerate/gibberish outputs common in naive guardrails, while DDS/DUS capture cumulative performance drift across sequential unlearning requests, aligning closely with practical deployment concerns (Sec 3.2, App C.2).
4. **Thorough Robustness and Ablation Studies:** The paper systematically validates each component: ablating the CoT guard vs. static templates, comparing detection against RoBERTa/GPT-4o classifiers, testing threshold sensitivity, and evaluating resistance to typos, language mixing, and AIM jailbreaks (Sec 6, App D.6). Results consistently demonstrate stability.

### Weaknesses
1. **Theoretical Mechanism is Underexplored:** The authors acknowledge a "theory gap" (App E) and rely on empirical evidence to justify why CoT instructions effectively suppress memorized knowledge. Without formal analysis of how in-context reasoning reshapes token probability distributions or interferes with recall pathways, the method remains heuristic rather than principled.
2. **Unaddressed Latency and Context Overhead:** While framed as "low cost," in-context intervention substantially increases prompt length and inference time for flagged queries. Table 31 shows TOFU inference latency jumps from ~43ms (retain) to ~665ms (forget) due to injected context and CoT reasoning. The paper dismisses this as acceptable for safety but lacks analysis of throughput implications for high-traffic production systems.
3. **Arbitrary Metric Hyperparameters:** RQ assigns fixed weights (1.0, 1.0, 0.2) to template similarity, refusal classification, and gibberish detection without ablation or statistical justification. Similarly, DDS uses β = 0.5 to balance average trade-off vs. stability, but no sensitivity analysis is provided. These choices could disproportionately influence comparative rankings.
4. **Synthetic Data Dependency Contradicts Privacy-First Premise:** The CoT dataset relies on GPT-4o for reasoning instruction synthesis. While justified for harmful knowledge, this pipeline introduces external API calls and synthetic data generation that may conflict with strict data-locality or regulatory requirements for highly sensitive privacy unlearning tasks.

### Novelty & Significance
The work scores well on ICLR's bar for practical impact and methodological clarity. While training-free unlearning and in-context steering are not entirely new, the specific integration of a robust detection router with a CoT-generating guard model for continual, retain-free unlearning represents a meaningful architectural contribution. The proposed metrics are highly relevant to evaluating real-world deployment stability. The paper is written clearly, with well-documented experimental setups, detailed hyperparameters, and accessible code, ensuring strong reproducibility. Its significance lies in offering a deployable, model-agnostic alternative to expensive fine-tuning pipelines, directly addressing regulatory compliance and dynamic request scenarios.

### Suggestions for Improvement
1. **Justify Metric Design Choices:** Provide a sensitivity analysis for the RQ weighting scheme and DDS β parameter. Demonstrate that rankings remain stable under reasonable perturbations (e.g., β ∈ [0.3, 0.7] or weight variations) to strengthen confidence in the evaluation framework.
2. **Quantify and Mitigate Latency Overhead:** Explicitly report token overhead and latency across guard models. Discuss practical optimizations such as context compression, virtual instruction tokens, or caching of CoT patterns for high-frequency forget queries to improve production viability.
3. **Strengthen Theoretical Grounding:** Add a brief mechanistic analysis (e.g., via attention head ablation, early-token entropy, or activation steering metrics) explaining how CoT instructions modulate the base model's decoding trajectory away from memorized modes. This would elevate the work from empirical engineering to theoretically informed methodology.
4. **Clarify Privacy-Preserving Data Pipelines:** Outline a fully local alternative to GPT-4o for CoT dataset generation (e.g., self-play with smaller open models, rule-based CoT templates, or privacy-preserving synthetic data pipelines) to ensure the framework strictly aligns with its core premise of secure, data-limited deployment.

# Spark Finder Review
## How to Improve This Paper

### Missing Experiments (top 3-5 only)
1. Evaluate detection robustness against advanced prompt-obfuscation/jailbreak attacks (e.g., GCG, PAIR, base64/ASCII encoding) rather than only typos and language mixes; without this, the claim that the detector reliably guards deployed LLMs against adversarial forget retrieval is unproven.
2. Compare DRAGON against a simple retrieval-matching + static refusal pipeline (bypassing the fine-tuned CoT guard model) to isolate whether the additional training cost and complexity of the guard model are strictly necessary for strong unlearning performance.
3. Benchmark detection accuracy and retrieval latency as the Unlearn Store scales from hundreds to tens of thousands of entries; the paper claims scalability to continual unlearning but provides no empirical evidence of performance degradation or collision risks under realistic store growth.

### Deeper Analysis Needed (top 3-5 only)
1. Analyze how the heavy weighting of template cosine-similarity in Refusal Quality (RQ) artificially inflates scores for rigid, robotic refusals while penalizing nuanced conversational safety responses, which could misrepresent real-world usability.
2. Provide justification or sensitivity analysis for the β=0.5 hyperparameter in the Dynamic Deviation Score (DDS); without it, the metric's balance between average trade-off and trajectory stability appears arbitrary and undermines claims of reliable continual unlearning monitoring.
3. Quantitatively verify that the CoT guard model does not induce refusal-style mode collapse or degrade response diversity on edge cases, as over-regularization during in-context steering would contradict the claimed preservation of general language utility.

### Visualizations & Case Studies
1. Provide qualitative failure cases showing both false negatives (forget prompts that evade detection and leak memorized data) and false positives (benign queries that trigger unnecessary CoT intervention and degrade response quality), which will expose the true practical limits of the framework.
2. Visualize early-generation token logit shifts or attention distributions when the CoT instruction is prepended, to empirically verify the mechanistic claim that stepwise reasoning actively steers the base model away from memorized probability modes.
3. Plot Precision-Recall or ROC curves for the detection module across a continuous range of thresholds, rather than disjoint tables, to transparently reveal the operating trade-off between safety coverage and over-refusal rates.

### Obvious Next Steps
1. Run a continual unlearning simulation with cross-domain distribution shift (e.g., sequentially unlearning hazardous knowledge, then privacy records, then copyrighted text) instead of only incremental TOFU splits, to demonstrate real-world stability beyond homogeneous dataset partitions.
2. Quantify the exact token-cost, context-window consumption, and multi-turn latency overhead of dynamically prepending CoT + policy instructions, proving the method's viability for production chat interfaces rather than single-turn benchmarks.
3. Investigate how DRAGON's generated refusal policies interact or conflict with the base model's native RLHF safety filters, as dual-intervention routing frequently produces contradictory or unstable refusals in deployed aligned models.

# Final Consolidated Review
## Summary
DRAGON introduces an inference-time routing framework for LLM unlearning that operates without modifying base model weights or requiring retain data. The system employs a dual-layer detection module to identify forget-worthy prompts, which are then intercepted by a fine-tuned guard model generating chain-of-thought (CoT) instructions to steer the base LLM toward safe refusals or alternative responses. The authors also propose three evaluation metrics (Refusal Quality, Dynamic Deviation Score, Dynamic Utility Score) to capture refusal coherence and stability across continual unlearning steps. Experiments across privacy, safety, and copyright benchmarks demonstrate strong forget-utility trade-offs and robustness to basic adversarial perturbations across multiple model scales.

## Strengths
- **Directly targets practical deployment constraints:** By decoupling unlearning from gradient-based fine-tuning and eliminating retain-data requirements, the framework offers a model-agnostic, API-compatible solution for continual unlearning. The modular detection → policy retrieval → CoT generation pipeline is architecturally sound for real-world, dynamic request environments.
- **Comprehensive and consistent empirical validation:** The method is evaluated across 9+ LLMs (including open-weight and closed-source) and three distinct benchmarks (WMDP, TOFU, MUSE). DRAGON consistently achieves top-tier performance in forgetting metrics while maintaining general utility, with extensive ablations isolating the contributions of the detector, CoT guard, and paraphrase scaling (e.g., Tables 1–4, Appendix D).
- **Targeted metric innovations for deployment stability:** RQ, DDS, and DUS address recognized blind spots in static unlearning evaluation. RQ effectively penalizes degenerate/gibberish outputs common in naive routing, while DDS/DUS capture cumulative drift across sequential unlearning rounds, aligning the evaluation closer to real-world operational stability.

## Weaknesses
- **Novel metrics lack sensitivity analysis and hyperparameter justification:** The proposed metrics rely on fixed, unvalidated design choices: RQ arbitrarily weights template similarity, refusal classification, and gibberish detection at 1.0, 1.0, and 0.2 respectively, while DDS uses a fixed $\beta=0.5$ to penalize upward trajectory deviations. Without sensitivity analysis or ablation, it remains unclear whether DRAGON's ranking advantages over baselines are intrinsic or driven by these hyperparameters. This matters because unvalidated metric design can artificially inflate performance disparities and weaken the paper's comparative claims.
- **Untested detection scalability and operational precision limits:** The paper evaluates the Unlearn Store up to 800 paraphrases but claims scalability to continual/massive unlearning regimes. Detection latency, false-positive rates, and vector-search collision risks under 10k–100k+ entries are not empirically bounded. Additionally, while aggregate FPR is reported, the precision-recall trade-off on nuanced benign queries (e.g., academic cybersecurity or medical research containing overlapping terminology) lacks systematic analysis, making the practical over-refusal risk unclear.
- **Security boundary and "training-free" framing require explicit calibration:** The method is a behavioral guardrail, not a weight-erasure technique. Unlearning efficacy is strictly bounded by detector recall; if a prompt evades routing or bypasses the prepended CoT (e.g., via system prompt injection or advanced obfuscation), the base model's memorization remains fully intact. While the paper acknowledges this in the "Theory Gap" discussion, it should explicitly frame DRAGON as a *soft, routing-dependent* intervention rather than claiming equivalence to parametric unlearning guarantees. Furthermore, the abstract's "training-free" claim is technically scoped only to the base LLM; training a RoBERTa scorer and SFT-fine-tuning an 8B guard model per category introduces non-trivial auxiliary overhead that should be transparently scoped.

## Nice-to-Haves
- Report standard deviations or multi-seed runs for fine-tuning components (detector and guard model) to rule out variance-driven results, though consistency across 9 base models mitigates this concern.
- Provide a sensitivity analysis for latency and context-window overhead in multi-turn, high-throughput production simulations, including potential mitigations like context compression or CoT caching.
- Evaluate detector robustness against advanced obfuscation techniques (e.g., base64 encoding, adversarial jailbreak prompts) beyond typos and language mixing to further stress-test the routing boundary.
- Explore mechanistic analysis (e.g., early-token entropy shifts or attention redistribution) to empirically verify how prepended CoT instructions steer the base model away from memorized decoding trajectories.

## Novel Insights
DRAGON reframes LLM unlearning from a parametric weight-editing problem to a dynamic inference-time routing challenge. By treating unlearning as a behavioral intervention mediated through a detection module and reasoning-augmented CoT steering, the work highlights that continual, retain-data-free unlearning can be achieved through systematic prompt interception rather than destructive gradient updates. Coupled with metrics that evaluate refusal quality and trajectory stability, the paper shifts the evaluation paradigm from static accuracy snapshots to operational reliability under sequential requests, offering a practical blueprint for deploying compliant, black-box LLMs without catastrophic utility degradation.

## Suggestions
1. **Conduct hyperparameter sensitivity analysis for RQ and DDS:** Test RQ weight variations (e.g., $\{0.5, 1.0, 2.0\}$ across components) and DDS $\beta \in [0.3, 0.8]$. Demonstrate that DRAGON's performance advantage over baselines remains stable under reasonable perturbations to strengthen confidence in the evaluation framework.
2. **Benchmark detection at scale:** Report detection latency, recall, and false-positive rates as the Unlearn Store expands to 5k, 10k, and 50k synthetic entries. Include a brief analysis of vector-search optimization strategies (e.g., HNSW indexing, representative centroid selection) to empirically ground the scalability claims.
3. **Explicitly scope the security boundary and auxiliary training overhead:** Clarify in the abstract and introduction that DRAGON is a routing-based behavioral safeguard with unlearning guarantees strictly bounded by detector recall/precision. Clearly delineate which components are trained (detector, guard model) vs. untouched (base LLM), and provide a fully local, zero-API pipeline specification for high-privacy deployments to align with the paper's compliance-focused premise.
