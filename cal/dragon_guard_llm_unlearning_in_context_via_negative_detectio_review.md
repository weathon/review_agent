# Harsh Critic Review
## Section-by-Section Critical Review

### Title & Abstract
- **Title accurately reflects contribution?** Yes. "Guard LLM Unlearning in Context via Negative Detection and Reasoning" correctly captures the routing-based, prompt-level intervention approach.
- **Abstract clearly states problem, method, key results?** Yes. It clearly identifies the retain-data dependency and computational cost of fine-tuning methods, proposes DRAGON's detection + CoT routing pipeline, and highlights strong empirical results across unlearning tasks and model scales.
- **Unsupported claims in abstract?** The claim that DRAGON demonstrates "equivalent capability in more practical, data-limited scenarios" compared to training-based methods is somewhat strong. While results on TOFU/WMDP are competitive, the paper primarily evaluates on synthetic/fictitious datasets where distributional coverage of the synthetic "unlearn store" is tightly controlled. Equivalence in truly opaque, real-world data-limited deployments is not rigorously demonstrated, as real-world queries may exhibit higher lexical/semantic variance than the paraphrased store covers.

### Introduction & Motivation
- **Problem well-motivated & gap identified?** Yes. The tension between forgetting quality, model utility, retain-data unavailability, and sequential unlearning requests is clearly articulated. The gap for training-free, routing-based unlearning that operates without retain data is well-positioned.
- **Contributions clearly stated and accurate?** Yes. Four contributions are listed and align with the paper's content: systematic framework, detection mechanism, novel metrics, and extensive experiments.
- **Over-claim or under-sell?** Minor over-claim in stating the approach incurs "no additional cost when scaling to larger models." While it avoids weight updates, it does incur measurable inference latency and context window overhead (Table 31 shows ~665ms for TOFU-forget10 on Llama2-7B). The framing should explicitly distinguish *training cost* from *inference overhead* to avoid confusion.

### Method / Approach
- **Clearly described and reproducible?** Mostly. The pipeline (detection → retrieval → CoT generation → intervention) is logical, and prompt templates/dataset curation are in the appendix. However, the core detection scoring mechanism lacks normalization/weighting details.
- **Key assumptions stated and justified?** Assumptions are present but lightly justified: (1) that a small set of synthetic/paraphrased prompts (e.g., 4× original + BERTScore filtering) adequately spans the query distribution for a given concept/author; (2) that the base model's instruction-following fidelity is strong enough to reliably adhere to the injected CoT prefix. The paper assumes the former scales adequately and the latter holds across model families, but does not empirically bound these assumptions.
- **Logical gaps in derivation/reasoning?** Yes, in Equations 4 and 5. The confidence score `f(x, D_u)` sums disparate, unnormalized signals: exact match (binary 0/1), cosine similarity ([0,1]), BERTScore/ROUGE (unbounded or differently scaled), and binary classifier probabilities. Summing these without explicit weighting or calibration (e.g., `w1*EM + w2*sim + w3*I`) makes the threshold `τ` opaque and dataset-specific. The sensitivity study (Appendix D.5, Tables 23-24) shows threshold robustness but does not address how the raw scores are internally balanced. This is a non-trivial methodological gap.
- **Edge cases/failure modes?** The impact of false negatives (harmful query bypasses detector → full leak) and false positives (benign query routed → unnecessary refusal/utility hit) is not quantified in terms of real-world deployment risk. Additionally, the paper does not discuss how the system handles highly adversarial prompt injection designed to override or ignore the prepended CoT instruction, beyond basic language-mix/typo perturbations.

### Experiments & Results
- **Experiments test claims?** Yes. WMDP (concept unlearning), TOFU (privacy/attribute unlearning), and MUSE (copyright) test the core claims. Continual unlearning (Sec 6.1) tests stability claims.
- **Baselines appropriate & fairly compared?** Broad coverage of training-based (GA, KL, NPO-RT, RMU, ALKN, GRU) and training-free (Prompting, Filter-Prompting, ICUL+) methods. However, ICUL+ is evaluated in an "idealized" setting with full retain/forget data access, making it an asymmetric comparator for the data-limited setting. This is acknowledged but should be interpreted cautiously.
- **Missing ablations?** Yes. (1) An ablation on the *combination weights/normalization* of the detection module components (Eq 4/5) is entirely missing and would clarify whether one signal dominates. (2) No ablation on the *guard model's decoding parameters* (temperature, max reasoning steps), which directly affect CoT quality and intervention reliability.
- **Error bars / statistical significance?** None reported. LLM evaluations exhibit non-trivial run-to-run variance, especially for open-ended generation metrics like ROUGE and RQ. Presenting results as single deterministic values without seeding/reporting variance falls short of current ICLR empirical standards.
- **Results support claims or cherry-picked?** Results consistently favor DRAGON, but the custom Refusal Quality (RQ) metric yields disproportionately high scores for DRAGON (>0.6–1.3) compared to baselines (~0.34–0.44) in Table 1. While RQ is mathematically unbounded up to ~2.2, the large gap suggests the metric heavily rewards template-aligned refusals and may not fully capture contextual coherence. The paper would benefit from a correlation analysis between RQ and human preference ratings to confirm it doesn't over-reward formulaic refusals.
- **Datasets & metrics appropriate?** Datasets are standard. The introduced metrics (RQ, DDS, DUS) address real gaps (continual stability, refusal coherence), but DDS's penalty for upward trajectory deviations (Eq 1) and the arbitrary `β=0.5` lack rigorous justification. DUS (Eq 2) is a simple stability measure but useful. Both should be treated as diagnostic heuristics rather than definitive performance proofs.

### Writing & Clarity
- **Confusing/poorly explained sections?** The exact prompt assembly pipeline is fragmented between Section 4.2 and Appendix F.5. Readers must cross-reference extensively to understand how the policy, CoT instruction, and original query are concatenated. Consolidating the prompt template structure into the main text would improve clarity.
- **Figures & tables clear?** Tables 1–4 are well-structured and directly support claims. Figure 2 effectively shows scaling trends. Minor parser artifacts exist in appendix tables, but the underlying data is interpretable and not a substantive weakness.

### Limitations & Broader Impact
- **Key limitations acknowledged?** Yes. Latency overhead for routed prompts, scaling challenges for million-rule unlearn stores, and risks of selective erasure/misinformation are noted.
- **Missed fundamental limitations?** The method's effectiveness is fundamentally bounded by the base model's instruction-following capacity. Models with weaker zero-shot CoT compliance or different alignment paradigms may ignore the injected prefix, leading to hard failures. This dependency is not explicitly discussed. Additionally, the paper assumes the guard model can be trained once and generalized across base models (Sec D.7), but cross-architecture generalization of reasoning prompts is not guaranteed; adapter-level tuning per model family might be necessary in practice.
- **Failure modes / negative societal impacts?** The broader impact section appropriately flags misuse risks. However, it misses discussing "unlearning theater" scenarios where deployment appears compliant due to prompt routing, but the underlying weights still memorize sensitive data, potentially creating a false sense of security for auditors/regulators relying on black-box API testing.

### Overall Assessment
DRAGON presents a timely, practically motivated training-free unlearning framework that effectively routes queries through a detection module and a CoT-based guard model to bypass weight updates. The empirical results across TOFU, WMDP, and continual settings are strong, and the introduction of stability-focused metrics (DDS, DUS) and refusal quality assessment addresses genuine evaluation gaps in the unlearning literature. However, to meet ICLR's standards for methodological rigor and reproducibility, the paper requires several critical revisions: (1) formalizing the confidence score calculation in Eqs. 4–5 with explicit normalization and ablation over component weights; (2) reporting experimental variance (error bars across seeds/runs), especially for heuristic metrics; (3) clarifying the prompt construction pipeline in the main text; and (4) explicitly discussing the system's dependency on base-model instruction fidelity and the potential for "unlearning theater" in compliance auditing. Addressing these points would significantly strengthen the paper's empirical validity and deployment transparency, making it a solid candidate for acceptance.

# Neutral Reviewer
## Balanced Review

### Summary
DRAGON proposes a training-free, in-context unlearning framework for LLMs that intercepts forget-targeted queries via a dual-layer detection module and routes them through a lightweight, fine-tuned guard model to generate Chain-of-Thought refusal instructions. These instructions are prepended to the input prompt to guide the base LLM's output without modifying its weights or requiring retain data. The method is designed for black-box and continual unlearning scenarios, and introduces novel evaluation metrics (Refusal Quality, Dynamic Deviation Score, and Dynamic Utility Score) to better capture refusal coherence and long-term utility stability.

### Strengths
1. **Practical deployment focus addressing real-world constraints:** The framework explicitly avoids base model fine-tuning and retain data, making it compatible with closed-source APIs and dynamic, continual unlearning requests. The system updates only a local "Unlearn Store" and a lightweight scoring model, which aligns well with real-world data retention limitations (Sec 1, 4.1, 6.1).
2. **Strong and consistent empirical performance:** Extensive experiments across hazardous knowledge (WMDP), privacy records (TOFU), and copyright unlearning (MUSE) show DRAGON consistently achieves the best forgetting-utility trade-offs across multiple model families and scales (7B to 47B+), often matching or exceeding fine-tuning baselines while preserving general capabilities (Tables 1, 2, 3, 13, 14).
3. **Thoughtful evaluation metrics addressing current gaps:** The proposed Refusal Quality (RQ) metric jointly penalizes nonsensical outputs while rewarding coherent refusals. Furthermore, DDS and DUS explicitly track stability and cumulative utility degradation in continual settings, addressing limitations of static, single-step evaluations (Sec 3.2, D.8).
4. **Thorough robustness and ablation analysis:** The paper rigorously evaluates detection accuracy under adversarial conditions (AIM jailbreaks, typos, language mixing), out-of-distribution prompts, and sensitivity to thresholds and guard model choices, demonstrating the framework's resilience (Sec 6.3, D.6, Tables 5, 6, 27-29).

### Weaknesses
1. **Partial reliance on external API models introduces privacy/cost trade-offs:** The CoT instruction dataset is curated using GPT-4o (or similar proprietary reasoning models). While the authors note this is acceptable for harmful knowledge, it poses genuine risks for highly sensitive private-data unlearning scenarios, undermining the "training-free/privacy-preserving" claim for that use case (Sec 4.2).
2. **Not fully "training-free" in practice:** The method requires fine-tuning a separate guard model (~8B parameters) and training a RoBERTa-based scoring classifier, plus maintaining vector databases. The operational overhead, storage costs, and inference latency for forget queries (~600-1000ms per query according to Table 31) are under-discussed relative to the "lightweight" framing (Sec 4, D.7).
3. **Novel metrics lack formal justification and broader validation:** DDS and DUS use heuristic weightings (e.g., β=0.5 without ablation) and are primarily validated on the TOFU dataset. The paper lacks quantitative correlation studies with human judgment or established continual learning stability baselines, making it unclear how universally applicable they are (Sec 3.2, D.8).
4. **Some baseline comparisons operate under idealized assumptions:** ICUL+ and Filter-Prompting are run in highly favorable settings (e.g., perfect classifier accuracy, no false positives in routing). While acknowledged by the authors, this slightly inflates DRAGON's relative advantage and makes isolated component contribution harder to disentangle (Sec 5.2, C.1.2, D.1).

### Novelty & Significance
The novelty is incremental but practically significant. DRAGON effectively integrates established concepts (prompt-based filtering, CoT safety prompting, in-context steering) into a cohesive, modular pipeline specifically optimized for deployment constraints like black-box access and continual deletions. Clarity is generally high; the methodology, metrics, and experimental setup are well-structured, though minor formatting artifacts in equations (likely parser-related) slightly obscure the DDS/DUS formulations. Reproducibility is strong: code is provided, hyperparameters and baseline implementations are detailed in the appendix, and dataset splits are standardized. Significance is notable for ICLR, as it addresses a clear gap in scalable, retain-data-free unlearning and proposes metrics that better reflect real-world deployment stability. The work will likely interest researchers and practitioners focused on trustworthy AI, model deployment pipelines, and continual alignment.

### Suggestions for Improvement
1. **Clarify and optimize computational/latency overhead:** Provide a systematic analysis of end-to-end latency and throughput costs, and explore practical mitigations such as caching frequent guard outputs, using distilled smaller guard models (1B-3B), or compressing CoT interventions to reduce context window overhead in production.
2. **Strengthen metric validation and justification:** Ablate the β weighting in DDS, provide quantitative agreement metrics between DDS/DUS and human judgments or alternative stability baselines, and explicitly discuss failure modes where these metrics might diverge from practical utility.
3. **Provide fully local CoT data generation recipes:** To address privacy concerns, include a reproducible pipeline for generating high-quality CoT instructions using open-weight reasoning models, along with a discussion of the performance trade-offs when avoiding proprietary APIs.
4. **Improve baseline fairness and component isolation:** Run Filter-Prompting and ICUL+ with matched detection overhead (e.g., same classifier false positive/negative rates) and include an ablation isolating the exact contribution of the scoring model vs. embedding similarity in the detection stage to clarify which component drives the reported robustness.

# Spark Finder Review
## How to Improve This Paper

### Missing Experiments (top 3-5 only)
1. Compare DRAGON against training-free baselines strictly without access to retain data. Mixing data-requiring and data-free methods in the main results obscures whether performance gains come from the proposed architecture or from unfair baseline handicaps.
2. Evaluate scalability on production black-box APIs (e.g., GPT-4, Claude) under strict context window and rate limits. The paper claims API compatibility, but the combined token overhead of detection, policy retrieval, and CoT generation drastically inflates latency and cost, which must be quantified in realistic deployment conditions.
3. Test continual unlearning across significantly more steps (e.g., 10–20+ sequential forget requests). Validating utility stability with only three data fractions (1%, 5%, 10%) is insufficient; performance collapse or detector drift over prolonged deployment directly contradicts the continual unlearning claims.

### Deeper Analysis Needed (top 3-5 only)
1. Distinguish between true knowledge suppression and behavioral censorship by probing internal retention on indirect, multi-hop, or conceptually linked queries. Without this, DRAGON may simply act as a prompt-level filter rather than achieving meaningful unlearning, directly undermining the core contribution.
2. Quantify the real-world impact of false positives and false negatives on downstream utility and safety. High detection accuracy is insufficient; ICLR requires analysis of how misclassifications translate to actual utility degradation or harmful leakage in end-to-end pipeline evaluations.
3. Correlate the proposed metrics (Refusal Quality, DDS, DUS) with human judgments or established unlearning benchmarks. Introducing novel evaluation metrics without validating their alignment with human-interpretable unlearning quality or stability risks appearing mathematically constructed rather than empirically grounded.

### Visualizations & Case Studies
1. Provide explicit failure cases where adversarial rephrasing or semantic drift bypasses the detector, showing both the base model's unguarded output and DRAGON's intervention. This directly tests whether the detection module generalizes beyond surface-level matching or merely memorizes paraphrase patterns.
2. Plot refusal success rate against CoT instruction length and query complexity across diverse task types. This reveals whether the reasoning guard actually scales to harder queries or degrades, exposing the practical limits of the proposed intervention mechanism.
3. Visualize utility trajectories across extended continual unlearning horizons alongside fine-tuning baselines. A stability metric is only convincing when paired with longitudinal plots that clearly show whether general capabilities silently degrade during repeated unlearning rounds.

### Obvious Next Steps
1. Conduct mechanistic interpretability analysis (e.g., activation mapping or logit lensing) to demonstrate how prepended CoTs alter knowledge retrieval pathways. The paper lacks evidence that reasoning instructions actually suppress memorized activations rather than simply overriding final tokens via instruction following.
2. Perform a systematic ablation isolating the contribution of each detector component (scoring model vs. similarity threshold) under domain shift. Understanding which component drives detection failures is critical for deployment where prompt distributions constantly evolve.
3. Open-source the complete Unlearn Store generation pipeline and guard model training data. Relying on proprietary API (GPT-4o) for CoT synthesis without full transparency hinders reproducibility and obscures whether synthetic bias or memorization is driving the guard model's performance.

# Final Consolidated Review
## Summary
DRAGON proposes a training-free, routing-based framework for LLM unlearning that intercepts forget-targeted queries via a dual-layer detector and routes them through a fine-tuned guard model to generate Chain-of-Thought refusal instructions. By prepending these instructions to the base model prompt, the method achieves behavioral suppression without modifying model weights or requiring retain data, alongside new diagnostic metrics for refusal quality and continual stability.

## Strengths
- **Strong empirical performance across diverse models and tasks:** DRAGON consistently achieves superior unlearning-utility trade-offs on TOFU, WMDP, and MUSE across model scales from 1.5B to 47B+ parameters, outperforming both fine-tuning and prompt-based baselines while preserving general language capabilities (Tables 1, 2, 13, 14).
- **Practical deployment alignment with real-world constraints:** The architecture explicitly decouples unlearning from base model retraining and retain-data dependencies. Sequential deletion requests are handled by updating a local Unlearn Store and lightweight scoring model, making it directly applicable to black-box APIs and dynamic compliance environments (§4.1, §6.1).
- **Comprehensive robustness analysis:** The detection pipeline is rigorously tested against jailbreak attacks (AIM), typographical/noisy perturbations, language mixing, and out-of-distribution prompts, demonstrating resilience to common evasion tactics that typically break prompt-filtering methods (Tables 6, 25, 27-29).

## Weaknesses
- **Methodological opacity in the detection scoring mechanism:** The confidence scores (Eqs. 4–5) heuristically sum disparate, uncalibrated signals—binary exact match, cosine similarity, BERTScore, ROUGE-L, and classifier indicator probabilities—without explicit weighting, normalization, or calibration. This makes the routing threshold τ inherently dataset-dependent and obscures which component drives true/false decisions, limiting reproducibility and theoretical grounding.
- **Novel evaluation metrics lack rigorous validation:** While RQ, DDS, and DUS target genuine evaluation gaps, they rely on arbitrary hyperparameters (e.g., β=0.5 in DDS) and uncalibrated component weights. The paper provides only qualitative assertions of alignment with human judgment and lacks quantitative correlation studies or ablations over weighting schemes, creating a risk that the metrics reward template adherence rather than genuine refusal coherence or stability (§3.2, Appendix D.8).
- **Overstated "unlearning" framing vs. behavioral suppression:** DRAGON achieves output-level censorship via prompt routing rather than parameter-level knowledge erasure or activation modification. It cannot mitigate white-box extraction, gradient-inversion attacks, or training data reconstruction. Presenting in-context intervention as functionally equivalent to algorithmic unlearning without explicit mechanistic caveats misrepresents the security guarantees and compliance utility of the system (Appendix E acknowledges this distinction but does not adequately frame it in the main text).
- **Unaddressed inference overhead and privacy paradox:** Routing queries through an 8B guard model introduces ~600–1000ms latency and substantial token overhead for forget-targeted prompts (Table 31). Moreover, synthesizing high-quality CoT instructions relies on proprietary reasoning models (GPT-4o), which directly contradicts the privacy-preserving premise when applied to the exact sensitive-data unlearning scenarios the paper claims to address (§4.2).

## Nice-to-Haves
- Extend continual unlearning experiments beyond three sequential steps (1%, 5%, 10%) to 10+ rounds to verify long-term detector drift and cumulative utility degradation claims.
- Report experimental variance across multiple random seeds and provide an ablation isolating the individual contribution of each detector component (scoring model vs. similarity search) under domain shift.
- Clarify prompt assembly by consolidating the policy retrieval, CoT injection, and query concatenation steps into a single, explicit template in the main text rather than scattering them across sections and appendix subsections.

## Novel Insights
DRAGON effectively reconceptualizes LLM unlearning from a weight-update optimization problem to a dynamic routing and instruction-steering pipeline. By treating the base model as a frozen executor and leveraging a distilled guard model to generate context-aware Chain-of-Thought constraints, the framework bypasses the catastrophic forgetting and retain-data bottlenecks that plague gradient-based approaches. This architectural shift suggests that for deployment-constrained environments, calibrated behavioral suppression via in-context reasoning may offer superior practical stability than theoretically pure but computationally expensive parameter erasure, provided the routing detector maintains high precision and the base model exhibits reliable instruction compliance.

## Potentially Missed Related Work
- Cheng et al. (2024), *Remaining-data-free machine unlearning by suppressing sample contribution* — Provides a relevant gradient-free baseline for comparison in the data-limited regime.
- Deliberative Alignment (Guan et al., 2024) and recent systematic guardrail frameworks — Directly overlap with the CoT-based safety steering component; discussing architectural differences (e.g., offline distillation vs. online reasoning steering) would better delineate DRAGON's novelty.
- Mechanistic unlearning works focusing on activation-level erasure (e.g., BELT, MEMIT variants) — Would provide useful contrast for clarifying the boundary between DRAGON's routing approach and true parameter-level knowledge removal.

## Suggestions
- Formalize the detection confidence score by introducing explicit component weights or probability calibration (e.g., temperature scaling or Platt scaling), accompanied by an ablation quantifying false positive/negative contributions from each signal.
- Conduct a human evaluation study or correlate DDS/DUS/RQ with established continuous learning stability baselines to validate that the proposed metrics align with expert assessments of refusal quality and utility preservation.
- Explicitly frame DRAGON as a behavioral suppression and deployment-safe routing system rather than a parameter-erasure method in the abstract and introduction, and add a dedicated discussion on failure modes against white-box or activation-based extraction attacks.
- Replace proprietary API dependencies for CoT synthesis with open-weight reasoning distillation pipelines in the primary methodology, and report end-to-end latency, cost, and throughput under realistic API rate limits to substantiate deployment claims.
