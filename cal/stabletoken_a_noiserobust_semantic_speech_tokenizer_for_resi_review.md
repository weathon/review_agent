# Harsh Critic Review
## Section-by-Section Critical Review

**Title & Abstract**
The title accurately reflects the core contribution, and the abstract clearly articulates the problem (tokenizer fragility), the root causes (single-path architecture, distant supervision), and the proposed solution (multi-branch voting + consensus training). The claim of "slashing UED by over 60%" is numerically supported by the results. However, the abstract states that the training signal is "distant" because ASR loss is "indifferent to intermediate token instability," but it does not clarify how the proposed consensus loss actually bridges this gap during training. Does the model optimize for discrete token stability, or continuous representation stability? This distinction is important for evaluating the strength of the abstract's claims.

**Introduction & Motivation**
The motivation is well-grounded and timely. Identifying the disconnect between linguistic fidelity and acoustic robustness in modern VQ-based semantic tokenizers addresses a genuine bottleneck in SpeechLLM deployment. The articulation of the two core flaws (brittle architecture, distant supervision) is logical and well-supported by Figure 1. The introduction successfully positions StableToken against naive ensembles and token-level consistency objectives, justifying the need for a co-designed architecture and training paradigm. The contributions are clearly enumerated. No major concerns.

**Method / Approach**
The methodological design is generally sound, but several technical clarifications and assumptions require addressing for full reproducibility and theoretical rigor:
* **Training vs. Inference Signal Flow (Sec 2.2 & 2.4):** The paper describes binarization via `sign()` with STE, and averaging binary vectors to produce $\mathbf{s}_{final}$ during training. However, it is unclear how the downstream ASR decoder consumes these representations. Does the cross-entropy loss ($\mathcal{L}_{ASR}$) operate on the continuous $\mathbf{s}_{final}$, a continuous approximation of the quantized vectors, or the hard discrete tokens? If the ASR head receives continuous signals, the "discrete stability" is only enforced implicitly via the quantization boundary, which weakens the claim that the training signal directly supervises token stability.
* **Consensus Loss Mechanics (Eq 4):** $\mathcal{L}_{consensus}$ minimizes the $L_2$ distance of each branch's pre-quantization vector to the global mean. While effective for variance reduction, this is mathematically equivalent to enforcing representation alignment. The paper notes this "prevents [the mean] from being corrupted by noisy inputs," but relies on the assumption that clean samples strictly dominate the gradient direction. What happens when the perturbation magnitude is high enough that the perturbed branches pull the mean toward a semantically incorrect region? The risk of representation collapse or gradient conflict with $\mathcal{L}_{ASR}$ should be discussed.
* **Independence Assumption for Bit-wise Voting (Sec 2.2):** The voting mechanism's efficacy relies on the assumption that bit-flip errors across branches are largely independent. However, all branches process outputs from the *same* shared Whisper backbone ($\mathbf{h}$ or $\mathbf{h}'$) before applying distinct linear projections $W_i$. If acoustic noise induces a systematic shift in the shared representations, the projections could all cross the quantization boundary in the same bits simultaneously, causing correlated failures. The paper should explicitly analyze or acknowledge this limitation.
* **Training Efficiency:** Appendix B.6 thoroughly benchmarks *inference* latency and memory, but omits *training* compute overhead. Forward/backward passes through $n$ branches will increase training time and GPU memory footprint non-negligibly compared to a single-path quantizer. Reporting training FLOPs or wall-clock multipliers is necessary for a complete efficiency profile.

**Experiments & Results**
The experimental design is extensive, but several concerns regarding baseline fairness, downstream setup, and statistical rigor need to be addressed:
* **Fairness of Baselines (Table 1 & Sec 3):** StableToken is initialized from `whisper-large-v3` and trained on ~150k hours of data. Most baselines use their official checkpoints, which vary widely in backbone architecture, training data scale, and data mixture. The massive UED reduction (e.g., 10.17% vs 26.17% for $S_3$ Tokenizer) could be partially driven by the stronger Whisper backbone or the sheer scale of StableToken's training data, rather than the voting architecture alone. A controlled ablation where all models share the same backbone and data scale would substantially strengthen the internal validity.
* **Downstream Training Conditions (Sec 3 & 4.2):** The downstream SpeechLLMs are fine-tuned using Qwen2.5-3B. It is critical to clarify whether the fine-tuning data includes noisy audio or only clean audio. If models are fine-tuned exclusively on clean tokens and evaluated on noisy audio, the observed robustness gap primarily reflects evaluation-time stability. If fine-tuned on noisy data, the results reflect both training and evaluation robustness. The experimental setup should explicitly state the noise conditions during downstream fine-tuning.
* **Statistical Significance:** Figures 3 and Tables 1-3 report single-point metrics without standard deviations, confidence intervals, or multi-seed averages. Fine-tuning 3B+ models on speech tasks is notoriously seed-sensitive, and UED calculations can vary with test set sampling. Reporting results across multiple seeds or providing variance estimates is standard practice at ICLR and necessary to confirm that the performance gaps are statistically meaningful rather than stochastic.
* **Sequential vs Factorial Ablation (Table 4):** The sequential ablation confirms each component matters, but a factorial ablation (e.g., single-branch + consensus loss) would more cleanly isolate the contribution of the voting mechanism from the consensus training objective. The current design conflates the architectural ensembling benefit with the gradient-coupling benefit.

**Writing & Clarity**
The paper is well-structured and clearly written. The logical flow from problem identification to architectural solution, training strategy, and empirical validation is coherent. Mathematical notation is consistent, and figures/tables are well-integrated. No clarity issues impede understanding.

**Limitations & Broader Impact**
The paper acknowledges generalizability to OOD noise but misses several important limitations:
* **Correlated Noise Failure Modes:** As noted in Methods, the shared encoder creates a bottleneck where highly correlated acoustic distortions could defeat the majority vote. Discussing this boundary condition would strengthen the paper's honesty.
* **Training Cost & Accessibility:** Omitting training overhead discussion limits reproducibility for labs with constrained compute. Providing training time/memory comparisons would help the community gauge adoption feasibility.
* **Vocabulary Size Confounder:** The paper notes StableToken uses a larger vocabulary (8192) than baselines (e.g., 4096 or 16384) and claims this makes stability "inherently more challenging." However, larger vocabularies also increase bits-per-token, which changes the probability space for UED. A discussion or small analysis controlling for codebook size would clarify whether gains are purely architectural.

### Overall Assessment
StableToken presents a well-motivated and technically sound approach to a recognized vulnerability in semantic speech tokenizers. The co-design of a bit-wise voting quantizer and a consensus training objective is elegant and yields impressive empirical gains in token stability and downstream SpeechLLM robustness. The paper is clearly written and covers a comprehensive set of tasks. However, to meet ICLR's rigorous standards for empirical fairness and methodological clarity, the authors should address three key points: (1) clarify the training-time signal flow to the ASR loss and justify how discrete stability is directly optimized, (2) strengthen baseline comparisons by controlling for backbone strength and data scale (or provide ablations that isolate the architectural contribution from data/backbone differences), and (3) report statistical variance across multiple seeds for downstream results. Additionally, discussing correlated failure modes and training compute overhead will improve transparency. With these revisions, the paper represents a solid, impactful contribution to robust speech representation learning and is likely to be well-received by the ICLR community.

# Neutral Reviewer
## Balanced Review

### Summary
This paper introduces StableToken, a noise-robust semantic speech tokenizer designed to address the instability of existing VQ-based tokenizers under acoustic perturbations. It proposes a co-designed architecture featuring a multi-branch Voting-LFQ quantizer that aggregates binary codes via bit-wise majority voting, paired with a Noise-Aware Consensus Training strategy that enforces alignment between clean and perturbed branch representations. Comprehensive evaluations demonstrate state-of-the-art token stability (reducing Unit Edit Distance by over 60%) while preserving reconstruction fidelity, directly yielding significant robustness improvements in downstream SpeechLLMs across ASR, SER, and TTS tasks.

### Strengths
1. **Clear Problem Identification & Motivation:** The paper convincingly establishes that semantic tokenizers suffer from architectural fragility (single-path quantization) and distant supervision (ASR-only loss), leading to token instability under noise that harms SpeechLLMs (Section 1, Figure 1). This framing directly targets a known deployment bottleneck.
2. **Well-Designed Architecture and Training Paradigm:** The Voting-LFQ module (Section 2.2) introduces a lightweight, parallel quantization structure that enables granular bit-level error correction, avoiding the coarse failure modes of token-level ensembles. The consensus loss (Section 2.3, Eq. 4) provides a smooth, explicit supervisory signal at the pre-quantization level, effectively circumventing gradient issues with discrete codes.
3. **Comprehensive and Multi-Level Empirical Validation:** The evaluation spans tokenizer-level metrics (UED, WER, MOS in Tables 1-2), downstream SpeechLLM tasks under varying SNRs and OOD noise (Figure 3, Table 3), ablation studies (Tables 4-5, 20), and rigorous efficiency benchmarks (Tables 12-14). The consistent widening of the performance gap under severe noise strongly supports the core hypothesis.
4. **Strong Documentation for Reproducibility:** Despite the scale of the 150k-hour training corpus, the paper provides extensive appendices detailing datasets (Table 7), hyperparameters (Tables 8, 11, 23), perturbation strategies (Table 9, B.3), prompt templates (F.3), and explicit computational overhead analysis. The promised public code release further supports reproducibility.

### Weaknesses
1. **Reliance on Large Proprietary/In-house Data Obscures Source of Gains:** The tokenizer is trained on ~150k hours of mixed public and in-house data (Section 3, Appendix B.1). While large-scale training is common in industry, it makes it difficult to disentangle whether robustness gains stem primarily from the proposed Voting-LFQ/consensus design or simply from massive data scale and diverse augmentations.
2. **Limited Direct Downstream Comparison to Specialized Robust Tokenizers:** While NAST and R-SPIN are discussed in related work and compared at the tokenizer level for UED (Table 1), they are absent from the downstream SpeechLLM evaluations (Section 4.2). This leaves open whether StableToken's architectural advantages translate consistently at the LLM integration level compared to methods explicitly trained for robustness.
3. **Lack of Formal Analysis of Voting Mechanism Limits:** The bit-wise voting mechanism is intuitively explained (Sections 2.2, 4.3.3), but the paper lacks a formal or probabilistic analysis of its error-correction bounds. For instance, the conditions under which the majority of branches can fail yet still recover the token are qualitatively described but not mathematically characterized or bounded against codebook size/dimensionality.
4. **Mixed/Competitive Results in Generative Tasks:** In Table 3, StableToken achieves superior TTS WER but shows only competitive (or slightly lower) MOS scores on SEED-TTS_EN compared to GLM-4-Voice (4.12 vs. 4.19). The paper acknowledges this but does not deeply analyze whether the focus on phonetic/semantic stability inadvertently suppresses fine-grained prosodic or timbral features that human evaluators reward in MOS.

### Novelty & Significance
**Novelty:** Moderate to High. While ensemble and voting concepts exist in broader ML, adapting them to semantic speech tokenization via a differentiable, bit-wise majority vote integrated with a clean/noisy consensus training objective is a novel and well-executed contribution. The co-design effectively tackles both architectural fragility and gradient scarcity in discrete spaces.
**Clarity:** High. The paper is logically structured, with a clear progression from problem identification to method, empirical validation, and analysis. Mathematical formulations are precise, and the experimental design is transparent despite minor parser-induced formatting artifacts.
**Reproducibility:** Good, with a caveat. The method description, hyperparameters, and evaluation protocols are thoroughly documented, meeting ICLR's reproducibility standards. However, full reproduction at comparable scale is contingent on the promised code release and is partially limited by the inclusion of proprietary in-house data.
**Significance:** High for the SpeechLLM and audio foundation model community. Token instability under real-world noise is a major barrier to reliable deployment. Demonstrating that stabilizing the tokenization layer directly and substantially improves downstream robustness across understanding, classification, and generation tasks provides a highly actionable pathway for future model design.

### Suggestions for Improvement
1. **Include Direct Downstream Comparisons to Robust Baselines:** Extend Table 3 and Figure 3 to include NAST and/or R-SPIN fine-tuned into the same Qwen2.5-3B SpeechLLM setup. This would confirm that StableToken's advantages hold beyond tokenizer-level UED metrics.
2. **Ablate Dataset Scale or Provide a Public-Data Checkpoint:** To isolate the contribution of the proposed architecture/training from data scale, include a controlled ablation training StableToken on a fully public subset (e.g., LibriLight + WenetSpeech ~15k hours) and report comparative UED/downstream results. Alternatively, release a checkpoint trained exclusively on open data to aid academic reproducibility.
3. **Add Formal or Empirical Error Bound Analysis for Bit-Wise Voting:** Include a brief theoretical derivation or empirical sweep showing token recovery probability as a function of branch error rate, bit-flip distribution, and codebook dimensionality (`d`). This would strengthen the methodological rigor and clarify failure modes.
4. **Deepen Discussion of TTS Trade-offs:** Explicitly analyze the slight MOS gap on SEED-TTS_EN. Quantify whether this correlates with reduced acoustic/prosodic variance in the tokens (e.g., using pitch contour or duration variance metrics) and discuss whether a multi-objective downstream loss could recover expressiveness without sacrificing robustness.
5. **Justify Consensus Loss Design Choices with Additional Baselines:** Section 4.3 and Appendix B.4 justify L2 loss over cosine similarity. Adding a brief ablation comparing L1, L2, and a contrastive alignment loss for the consensus objective would provide stronger empirical grounding for the design choice.

# Spark Finder Review
## How to Improve This Paper

### Missing Experiments (top 3-5 only)
1. **Single-Path Noise Consistency Baseline:** Train a standard single-path LFQ with identical noise augmentation and an MSE alignment loss between clean and perturbed forward passes to isolate the contribution of the multi-branch voting architecture. Without this, the claim that multi-branch voting is structurally necessary, rather than just stronger consistency training, remains unproven.
2. **Sequence Length Controlled Downstream Evaluation:** Downstream SpeechLLM results compare mismatched frame rates (e.g., GLM-4 at 12.5Hz vs. StableToken at 25Hz), conflating tokenization stability with LLM context-length effects. Evaluate all tokenizers at a strictly matched framerate or explicitly ablate sequence length to prove robustness gains stem from the tokenizer alone.
3. **Multi-Seed Statistical Significance Testing:** Report mean ± standard deviation for UED, WER, and SER metrics across ≥3 training/evaluation seeds. ICLR requires statistical validation to confirm that reported >60% relative improvements and SOTA claims fall outside normal evaluation variance.

### Deeper Analysis Needed (top 3-5 only)
1. **Codebook Utilization Under Noise:** Analyze whether the voting mechanism forces representation collapse into a narrow, "safe" subset of the vocabulary, which would degrade fine-grained phonetic or prosodic expressivity. The claim of preserved semantic richness is unsupported without showing that stable tokens maintain proportional codebook entropy and semantic coverage.
2. **Loss Gradient Conflict Analysis:** Quantify the cosine similarity or gradient interference between the consensus loss and the ASR cross-entropy loss during training. Demonstrating that enforcing pre-quantization invariance does not destabilize or slow semantic learning is essential to trust the multi-objective design.
3. **Bit-Level Semantic Attribution:** Map which specific bit positions in the LFQ correspond to phonetic features versus acoustic artifacts, and show how voting stabilizes linguistic bits while suppressing acoustic noise. The assertion that bit-wise voting outperforms token-level voting lacks empirical evidence linking granularity to semantic preservation.

### Visualizations & Case Studies
1. **Per-Bit Flip Distribution Heatmaps:** Visualize error rates across all 13 bit dimensions for each branch under varying noise types and SNRs. This directly tests the core hypothesis that bit-level errors remain sparse and correctable by majority vote.
2. **Latent Space Invariance Projections:** Provide t-SNE/UMAP plots of token embeddings before and after voting for paired clean/noisy segments. Visual alignment confirms representational stability and exposes where the consensus mechanism fails to correct corruption.
3. **Expanded Failure Case Catalog:** Show explicit utterances and noise conditions where voting incorrectly overrides the clean signal or fails to recover tokens, along with the resulting downstream transcription errors. Exposing failure boundaries is necessary to establish realistic performance limits.

### Obvious Next Steps
1. **Streaming Chunk Boundary Continuity Test:** Quantify token discontinuity rates at 30-second segmentation boundaries under noise, as independent per-chunk voting may introduce alignment artifacts that degrade long-form SpeechLLM coherence. This should be included given the stated 30s window constraint.
2. **Full System Latency Profiling:** Measure end-to-end inference latency for the complete tokenizer-to-SpeechLLM pipeline, not just the standalone tokenizer module. ICLR reviewers expect system-level overhead validation to confirm the "negligible inference cost" claim holds in production-scale pipelines.
3. **≥7B LLM Downstream Validation:** Fine-tune the tokenizer on a 7B or larger backbone to prove that token stability benefits scale to modern LLM capacities rather than plateauing on smaller models. Stability claims are only compelling if they generalize beyond the 3B evaluation budget.

# Final Consolidated Review
## Summary
This paper introduces StableToken, a semantic speech tokenizer designed to address the fragility of modern VQ-based tokenizers under acoustic perturbations. It proposes a co-designed architecture and training strategy: a multi-branch Voting-LFQ module that aggregates binary codes via bit-wise majority voting, paired with a noise-aware consensus training objective that aligns pre-quantization representations across clean and perturbed branches. The method achieves a >60% reduction in Unit Edit Distance (UED) under diverse noise conditions, maintains high reconstruction fidelity, and yields consistent robustness gains across downstream SpeechLLM tasks (ASR, SER, TTS).

## Strengths
- **Targeted Problem Identification & Elegant Co-Design:** The paper convincingly frames token instability as a bottleneck for SpeechLLMs and correctly diagnoses its dual roots: brittle single-path quantization and distant ASR-only supervision. The proposed solution co-designs a fine-grained bit-wise voting mechanism with a continuous pre-quantization consensus loss, effectively stabilizing discrete outputs without breaking end-to-end differentiability.
- **Comprehensive & Multi-Modal Empirical Validation:** Experiments rigorously evaluate stability at the tokenizer level (UED), reconstruction quality (WER, MOS), and downstream SpeechLLM performance under varied SNRs and OOD noise. The consistent widening of the robustness margin as noise increases strongly validates the core hypothesis.
- **Transparency & Efficiency Profiling:** The paper provides detailed appendices covering datasets, hyperparameters, perturbation pipelines, and prompt templates. The thorough analysis of inference latency, memory footprint, and boundary stability demonstrates that the multi-branch design introduces negligible overhead during deployment, addressing a common concern for ensemble-style architectures.

## Weaknesses
- **Confounding Factors in Baseline Comparisons (Backbone, Data Scale, & Frame Rate):** StableToken is trained on ~150k hours of mixed public/in-house data and built on Whisper-large-v3, while baselines use official checkpoints with differing architectures, data scales, and frame rates (e.g., 25Hz vs. 12.5Hz for GLM-4). The downstream LLM evaluation inherently conflates token stability with sequence-length effects on the 3B parameter backbone. While component ablations (Table 4) isolate architectural contributions, the lack of strictly controlled baselines (same backbone, data scale, and frame rate) makes it difficult to definitively attribute the massive >60% UED reduction solely to the voting mechanism.
- **Missing Downstream Comparisons to Explicitly Robust Baselines:** NAST and R-SPIN are included in tokenizer-level UED comparisons but omitted from SpeechLLM fine-tuning. Without evaluating how downstream models perform with these robust tokenizers under identical LLM fine-tuning conditions, it remains unclear whether StableToken's tokenizer-level advantages consistently propagate to the end-to-end system level compared to specialized alternatives.
- **Shared Encoding Bottleneck & Correlated Failure Risk:** The voting mechanism's theoretical efficacy relies on statistical independence across branches. However, all branches process representations derived from the *same* shared encoder hidden state before applying distinct linear projections. Acoustic noise that induces systematic shifts in the shared latent space could cause correlated boundary crossings across branches, undermining the majority vote. The paper does not analyze or discuss this failure mode despite its architectural relevance.
- **Lack of a Strong Single-Path Consistency Baseline:** Table 4 demonstrates that removing multi-branch voting degrades performance, but it does not compare against a single-path LFQ trained with the exact same noise-aware consistency objective. Without this, it is ambiguous whether the gains stem primarily from the bit-wise voting architecture or from the stronger multi-view training strategy itself.

## Nice-to-Haves
- Reporting mean ± standard deviation across multiple training/evaluation seeds for UED and downstream metrics would strengthen statistical confidence in the reported gains.
- Adding a brief theoretical or empirical analysis of the bit-wise voting mechanism's error-correction bounds as a function of codebook dimensionality and per-branch flip rates would provide clearer failure condition thresholds.
- Quantifying gradient interference (e.g., via cosine similarity or gradient projection conflict) between the consensus loss and the ASR cross-entropy objective during training would further validate the dual-objective design.
- Visualizing per-bit flip distributions across branches under varying SNRs would directly illustrate the sparsity assumption critical to the voting mechanism's success.

## Novel Insights
StableToken reframes speech tokenizer robustness by shifting error correction from the coarse token level to the granular bit level. By treating each bit position in the LFQ code as an independent voter fed by parallel linear projections of a shared latent state, the architecture can recover correct tokens even when a majority of branches output erroneous token indices, provided bit-level errors remain sparse. Coupling this with a pre-quantization consensus loss creates a self-stabilizing loop: clean branches anchor the averaged representation space, forcing perturbed branches to learn noise-invariant mappings without relying on discrete token matching. This elegantly sidesteps the gradient scarcity of discrete consistency objectives while structurally enforcing representational invariance.

## Suggestions
- **Controlled Downstream Evaluation:** Re-run the downstream SpeechLLM experiments using baselines matched to the same frame rate (25Hz) and, if feasible, re-fine-tune NAST or R-SPIN within the identical Qwen2.5-3B pipeline to confirm cross-method stability advantages at the system level.
- **Single-Path Consistency Baseline:** Train a standard single-path LFQ tokenizer with identical noise augmentation and the proposed consensus/alignment loss. This will definitively isolate the architectural value of bit-wise voting from the training curriculum.
- **Inter-Branch Correlation Analysis:** Compute and report the empirical correlation of pre-quantization vectors or bit-flip patterns across the 5 branches under varying noise types. If correlations are high under severe noise, acknowledge this as a known boundary condition and discuss potential mitigations (e.g., branch-specific feature dropout or lightweight auxiliary adapters).
- **Clarify Frame Rate & Sequence Length Impact:** Explicitly discuss how the 2x difference in token sequence length between 12.5Hz and 25Hz tokenizers might influence LLM attention span and generation quality in downstream tasks, and consider normalizing sequence length during fine-tuning to prove stability is the sole driver of gains.
