# Harsh Critic Review
## Section-by-Section Critical Review

### Introduction & Motivation
The motivation is well-articulated and addresses a genuine bottleneck in class-incremental semantic segmentation (CISS): the tension between preserving old knowledge and acquiring new classes under a static parameter budget. The identification of "parameter competition" and "underutilization of acquired knowledge" provides a clear rationale for the proposed distribution-based strategy. However, the introduction positions DKD primarily against traditional Knowledge Distillation (KD) methods that freeze the old model. For ICLR, it would be important to situate the work more precisely against recent **parameter-efficient fine-tuning (PEFT)** and **prompt/adapter-based continual learning** approaches (e.g., learning residual adapters or prompts for new classes while freezing the backbone). Since DKD claims to "release parameters," directly contrasting your pruning-and-aligning strategy with PEFT methods, which also modulate parameter usage for new classes without inference overhead, would significantly strengthen the novelty claim and positioning.

### Method
This section contains the core contributions but presents several mathematical and conceptual inconsistencies that require clarification and likely correction before the work can be fully evaluated:

*   **Equation (4) and Laplacian Formulation:** The definition of the position map $P_t(h,w)$ involves computing second-order spatial gradients of a norm term: $\frac{\partial^2}{\partial w^2} ||y_c^* - f_{t-1}||^2$. This formulation is mathematically ambiguous. $y_c^*$ appears to be a label or soft label map, while $f_{t-1}$ is a feature representation from the old model. These entities typically reside in different dimensional spaces (class space vs. feature space), and taking spatial derivatives of their norm mismatch is ill-posed. If the intent is to compute the Laplacian of a feature map or a specific activation, the notation and operationalization must be rigorously defined. How is this gradient computed in practice? Is it done via finite differences, convolution operators, or backpropagation through spatial coordinates? The current description hinders reproducibility.
*   **Equation (5) and Confidence Map:** The confidence map is defined as $C_t(h,w) = \frac{\langle y_c^*, f_t(h,w) \rangle}{||f_t(h,w)||_2}$. This dot product is dimensionally inconsistent unless $y_c^*$ is explicitly projected into the feature space or $f_t$ is projected into the class space. Without defining these projections, the operation is undefined. Furthermore, using a dot product between a potentially one-hot/soft label and a feature vector as a "projection strength" metric lacks theoretical grounding. The authors should clarify the spaces involved and justify why this specific similarity measure reflects the "necessity of spatial reuse."
*   **Equations (8), (9), and (10) for $L_{Max}$:** There is a critical error in the definition of the conditional entropy proxy. Equation (8) defines marginal entropy $H(Y_t) = -\sum y_{t,c} \log y_{t,c}$. Equation (9) defines $H(Y_t|Y_{t-1}) = -\frac{1}{B} \sum_b \sum_c y_{t,c}^{(b)} \log y_{t,c}^{(b)}$, which is mathematically **identical** to Equation (8). If these two terms are identical, their difference in Equation (10) becomes zero, rendering $L_{Max}$ vacuous. If Equation (9) is intended to represent a cross-entropy or distillation term (e.g., $-\sum y_{t-1} \log y_t$), the notation is incorrect and mislabels it as "conditional entropy." The Appendix derivation (Eq. 38) introduces a perturbation $\eta$, but the core loss definition in the main text must be corrected to accurately reflect the intended information-theoretic quantity.
*   **Pruning Target in $L_{Min}$:** The strategy applies a binary mask based on L2 norms to the old model weights and then distills the new model to match this *pruned* version. While the intuition is to reduce fitting to "low-sensitivity" parameters, L2 norm is a known poor proxy for semantic importance in Transformers, and zeroing out weights can disrupt pre-trained representations. The paper claims this "releases capacity," but in standard dense implementations, zeroed weights still occupy computation unless structural sparsity is enforced. The authors should clarify whether this masking is purely a mathematical regularizer for target generation or if it induces actual computational/memory savings.

### Experiments & Results
The empirical evaluation is extensive and generally strong. Testing across five VOC settings and four ADE20K settings with a ViT-B/16 backbone meets ICLR standards for thoroughness. The inclusion of disjoint settings (Appendix C.1) and repeated runs for variance (Appendix C.7) adds credibility.

*   **Ablation and Hyperparameters:** The ablation in Table 3 confirms the utility of each component. However, Table 4 reveals that $\gamma$ requires tuning based on the incremental setting ($\gamma=1$ for few steps, $\gamma=0.4$ for many steps). While the paper handles this, it effectively introduces a **setting-dependent hyperparameter** that limits the "plug-and-play" claim. A sensitivity analysis or an adaptive strategy for $\gamma$ would strengthen the robustness argument.
*   **Computational Overhead:** The paper notes a ~7-second increase per epoch but does not discuss the **memory overhead** of computing second-order spatial gradients (Eq. 4) and dual masks/distillation targets. For high-resolution semantic segmentation, these operations can be memory-intensive. Reporting peak GPU memory usage would provide a more complete efficiency profile.
*   **Baselines:** The comparison is fair, but given the "parameter release" theme, it would be highly beneficial to include a comparison against a simple parameter-isolation baseline (e.g., freezing the backbone and only training the classifier + a small adapter layer) to prove that DKD's complexity is justified beyond just allocating fewer parameters to new classes.

### Writing & Clarity / Theoretical Soundness
The writing is generally clear, but the methodological notation suffers from the inconsistencies highlighted above (Eq. 4, 5, 9). These are not minor stylistic issues; they impede the reproduction and verification of the theoretical claims. The appendices attempt to provide gradient analyses, but because the base definitions contain dimensional mismatches and identity errors, the derived gradients inherit these flaws. The authors must revise the mathematical formulation to ensure internal consistency. Additionally, the claim in the Abstract and Introduction of approaching the "joint learning upper bound" should be calibrated. While the results are strong, the gap to joint training in settings like 2-2 or 10-1 remains measurable (e.g., Table 1 shows ~77.5% vs 85.0% Joint). Claiming "close to" is acceptable, but the authors should ensure the text does not overstate the convergence to the upper bound.

### Limitations & Broader Impact
*   **Missing Limitations Section:** ICLR explicitly requires a discussion of limitations. The current manuscript lacks a dedicated Limitations section. Given the method's reliance on thresholding ($\tau$), sensitivity to $\gamma$, and the non-standard gradient computations, these should be explicitly acknowledged as potential failure modes or practical constraints.
*   **Failure Modes:** The paper does not discuss scenarios where parameter pruning might fail. For example, in domains where features are highly entangled (e.g., texture vs. object shape), L2-based pruning might remove semantically vital low-magnitude weights. Discussing this would demonstrate deeper domain understanding.
*   **Broader Impact:** The ethics statement is standard and sufficient. However, acknowledging the potential for **bias amplification** in incremental learning (e.g., if new classes are visually similar to underrepresented old classes, and pruning removes discriminative features) would add value.

### Overall Assessment
The paper addresses a significant problem in CISS and proposes an intuitively appealing minimization-maximization strategy to balance stability and plasticity. The empirical results are comprehensive, showing strong performance across diverse benchmarks and settings, with clear advantages over standard KD methods. However, the contribution is currently undermined by critical mathematical inconsistencies in the method section, particularly the ill-posed spatial gradient formulation in $L_{Esti}$ (Eq. 4), the dimensional mismatch in the confidence map (Eq. 5), and the erroneous definition of conditional entropy in $L_{Max}$ (Eq. 9/10), which renders the loss definition potentially vacuous or mislabeled. Furthermore, the absence of a Limitations section and the lack of analysis regarding memory overhead and setting-dependent hyperparameter tuning reduce the paper's readiness for ICLR. With rigorous correction of the mathematical formulations, a discussion of limitations, and clearer differentiation from PEFT-based approaches, this work has the potential to be a strong contribution to the field.

# Neutral Reviewer
## Balanced Review

### Summary
The paper proposes Distribution-based Knowledge Distillation (DKD), a minimization–maximization framework designed to address parameter competition and knowledge underutilization in class-incremental semantic segmentation (CISS). DKD dynamically prunes low-sensitivity weights in the frozen old model to free capacity (minimization) and employs Laplacian-based projection with entropy-driven constraints to align and maximize shared knowledge distributions between old and new classes (maximization). Extensive experiments across nine incremental settings on Pascal VOC and ADE20K demonstrate that DKD achieves state-of-the-art performance, closely approaching the joint-training upper bound while effectively mitigating catastrophic forgetting and class confusion.

### Strengths
1. **Comprehensive Empirical Validation:** The method is rigorously evaluated across diverse and challenging CISS settings (10-1, 2-2, 15-1, 19-1, 15-5 on VOC; 100-5, 100-10, 50-50, 100-50 on ADE20K). It consistently outperforms strong baselines (e.g., MBS, CoinSeg, IDEC) and demonstrates architecture-agnostic compatibility (ViT-B/16 and ResNet101), as shown in Tables 1, 2, and Appendix C.3.
2. **Clear Motivation & Systematic Ablations:** The paper effectively identifies that standard KD in static architectures causes parameter crowding and wastes valuable old representations. The component-wise ablations (Table 3/Table 12, Figure 6) convincingly isolate the impact of $L_{Min}$, $L_{Esti}$, and $L_{Max}$, proving their combined necessity for balancing stability and plasticity.
3. **Theoretical & Qualitative Rigor:** Appendix A provides gradient-level derivations for each loss term, mathematically linking $L_{Max}$ to mutual information maximization and $L_{Min}$ to soft target alignment. Additionally, t-SNE visualizations and class-similarity matrices (Figure 4b, Figure 6b-c) offer strong qualitative evidence of reduced inter-class entanglement and compact feature clustering.

### Weaknesses
1. **Unsubstantiated Computational Efficiency Claims:** The Laplacian-based projection estimation ($L_{Esti}$) requires computing second-order spatial gradients of feature maps to construct the position map $P_t$ (Eq. 4). Second-order gradient computations are typically memory and compute-intensive, yet the paper only reports a marginal ~7s/epoch training overhead compared to CKD. A detailed breakdown of FLOPs, memory overhead, or the specific approximation technique used (e.g., finite differences vs. Hessian-vector products) is missing, which hinders reproducibility and weakens the efficiency claim.
2. **Incremental Conceptual Novelty:** While the integration is empirically effective, the core components are established in continual learning: magnitude-based weight pruning/regularization ($L_{Min}$), gradient/curvature-aware feature alignment ($L_{Esti}$), and entropy/Mutual Information maximization for distillation ($L_{Max}$). The paper lacks a sharp comparative discussion against existing pruning-based or MI-based CISS methods, making DKD read more as a well-engineered synthesis than a fundamentally new algorithmic paradigm.
3. **Ambiguity in Mathematical Formulation:** Several key equations (particularly Eqs. 4–6 and the conditional entropy proxy for $L_{Max}$ in Eq. 10) suffer from dense, sometimes ambiguous notation. The exact computational graph for how $P_t$ and $C_t$ are integrated into the backward pass, and how the entropy terms are batch-normalized across old/new classes, is difficult to follow without explicit pseudocode, which is a barrier to exact reproduction.

### Novelty & Significance
**Novelty:** Moderate. The novelty lies primarily in the specific formulation of the minimization–maximization distribution strategy and its targeted application to the static-capacity bottleneck in CISS. Rather than introducing a wholly new learning paradigm, the work synthesizes weight importance estimation, curvature-aware projection, and information-theoretic distillation into a cohesive, rehearsal-free constraint mechanism.
**Significance:** High for the CISS and applied vision communities. Achieving near-joint-training performance across both VOC and ADE20K without storing or generating exemplars is a notable milestone. The method's plug-and-play nature, demonstrated through successful integration into existing frameworks (CoinSeg) and backbones, provides a reliable, practical solution for deployment scenarios with strict memory and inference-time constraints.

### Suggestions for Improvement
1. **Provide Computational & Complexity Analysis:** Quantify the exact training overhead (FLOPs, peak VRAM, wall-clock time per iteration) of the Laplacian projection step. Explicitly state how second-order derivatives are approximated in practice to justify the claimed efficiency and ensure scalability to larger models/datasets.
2. **Sharpen Distinctions from Prior Work:** Add a dedicated subsection in Related Work or the Introduction explicitly contrasting DKD with existing regularization-based methods that also use weight importance (e.g., EWC, MAS, LwF variants) and entropy-based continual learning. Clarify the unique algorithmic or theoretical insights of your minimization–maximization framework.
3. **Improve Reproducibility with Pseudocode:** Include a concise algorithm block summarizing the training loop, explicitly detailing the forward pass construction of $P_t$, $C_t$, and the backward propagation flow for the combined DKD objective. This will resolve ambiguities in the current equation-heavy presentation.
4. **Discuss Limitations & Failure Cases:** Briefly acknowledge scenarios where DKD may struggle (e.g., extreme fine-grained class overlap, very aggressive incremental chunk sizes, or highly non-stationary domains). Providing error visualizations or quantitative bounds for these edge cases would strengthen the paper's academic rigor.

# Spark Finder Review
## How to Improve This Paper

### Missing Experiments (top 3-5 only)
1. **Ablate pruning against standard KD baselines:** Train with exactly the same losses but freeze all weights (or use standard L2 regularization) instead of zeroing them, to isolate the pruning contribution. Without it, the claim that weight zeroing actively "releases capacity" is confounded by the distillation objectives themselves.
2. **Include contemporary ViT-native CISS baselines:** Compare against modern parameter-efficient (adapter/prompt-tuning) or foundation-model-based CISS methods, rather than relying primarily on ResNet-era distillation baselines. Omitting current SOTA paradigms severely limits the credibility of the "state-of-the-art" claim for ICLR.
3. **Profile exact training overhead for Laplacian computation:** Report training GFLOPs and memory consumption specifically for the pixel-wise second-order derivative in Eq. 4 at various resolutions. The claim of negligible overhead is undermined by the known computational cost of dense second-order differentiation on $512\times512$ feature maps.

### Deeper Analysis Needed (top 3-5 only)
1. **Validate the "capacity release" mechanism in dense architectures:** Analyze gradient covariance or effective rank changes when low-magnitude weights are masked to zero. Zeroing parameters in a fixed transformer backbone does not structurally remove them from the graph; without showing how this actually alters optimization paths, the "parameter competition" theory lacks empirical grounding.
2. **Disentangle $L_{Max}$ from generic confidence regularization:** Directly measure cross-step feature preservation (e.g., via old-class logit drift or feature CKA) rather than relying on final mIoU. This proves whether the entropy formulation genuinely enables "knowledge reuse" or merely acts as a batch-level class-balancing and smoothing heuristic.
3. **Assess numerical stability of the spatial Laplacian:** Quantify the variance and smoothness of $P_t(h,w)$ given that ViT decoders rely on bilinear upsampling/interpolation. Second-order finite differences are notoriously unstable on interpolated logits, making the "low-curvature coexistence" assumption mathematically fragile without smoothing analysis.

### Visualizations & Case Studies
1. **Spatial update heatmaps with vs. without pruning:** Render the magnitude of weight/gradients updated per step overlayed on feature maps. If parameter release genuinely frees capacity, update magnitudes should visibly concentrate more on new-class-relevant regions when DKD is active compared to the control.
2. **Direct rendering of position/confidence maps ($P_t, C_t$):** Overlay the computed Laplacian and confidence maps onto input images and ground truth masks across early and late steps. Visualizing these maps is mandatory to verify they actually identify meaningful class boundaries or coexistence zones, rather than generating noisy, uncorrelated heatmaps.
3. **Pairwise class feature similarity evolution plots:** Instead of global t-SNE, plot pairwise class distance metrics (e.g., linear CKA) across all steps. This directly reveals whether DKD maintains inter-class separation over time or if "reduced confusion" is just a snapshot artifact that fails in later steps.

### Obvious Next Steps
1. **Replace the global static threshold ($\tau=0.1$) with layer-adaptive sensitivity analysis:** Implement and benchmark a threshold tied to layer-specific statistics (e.g., Fisher Information or activation norms), as applying a uniform L2 cutoff across all ViT layers and datasets is highly prone to over-pruning critical representation layers.
2. **Formalize a forgetting bound rather than local gradient derivations:** Derive a theoretical upper bound linking the pruning mask and $L_{Max}$ components to the actual drift rate of old-class logits. ICLR expects theoretical analysis that bounds catastrophic forgetting, not just step-by-step chain-rule exercises in the appendix.
3. **Benchmark second-order Laplacian estimation against first-order proxies:** Evaluate if simpler, cheaper alternatives (e.g., attention map overlap or first-order gradient magnitude) yield comparable performance. If second-order differentiation adds massive compute for marginal gains, the method's complexity is unjustified.

# Final Consolidated Review
## Summary
The paper proposes Distribution-based Knowledge Distillation (DKD), a minimization–maximization framework for class-incremental semantic segmentation (CISS). To address the bottleneck of parameter competition and knowledge underutilization in static architectures, DKD dynamically masks low-sensitivity parameters in the previous model to relax distillation targets ($L_{Min}$), estimates spatial class-coexistence regions via Laplacian projection ($L_{Esti}$), and aligns old–new feature distributions using entropy-based mutual information maximization ($L_{Max}$). Evaluated across nine incremental settings on Pascal VOC and ADE20K, the method achieves state-of-the-art performance that closely approaches joint-training upper bounds, demonstrates strong resistance to catastrophic forgetting, and integrates seamlessly into both ViT and ResNet backbones.

## Strengths
- **Comprehensive & Robust Empirical Validation:** The method is rigorously benchmarked across 5 VOC and 4 ADE20K incremental settings, covering both few-step (19-1) and high-step (10-1, 2-2) regimes. It consistently outperforms strong baselines (e.g., MBS, CoinSeg, IDEC) and approaches joint-training upper bounds. Repeated runs confirm low variance (std $\approx$ 0.06–0.25), demonstrating reliability in long-horizon incremental scenarios.
- **Well-Motivated Framework with Clear Component Contributions:** The paper accurately diagnoses the "parameter crowding" limitation of traditional static KD methods that rigidly freeze old representations. The three-loss decomposition directly targets this issue, and ablation studies (Table 3, Appendix C.6) convincingly isolate each component's role in balancing stability and plasticity, with $L_{Min}$ showing particularly strong gains in new-class learning under high-step settings.
- **Architecture Agnosticism & Practical Utility:** DKD operates as a constraint mechanism rather than an architectural modification, enabling successful integration into both ResNet101 and ViT-B/16 pipelines. The successful drop-in application to existing frameworks (e.g., boosting CoinSeg's new-class mIoU by 20.2% in the 19-1 setting) highlights its plug-and-play applicability.

## Weaknesses
- **Ambiguity in Mathematical Formulation Hinders Reproducibility:** Key equations contain notational gaps that obscure the exact computational graph. Specifically, Eq. (4) computes a spatial Laplacian over $|y_c^* - f_t(h,w) - f_{t-1}(h,w)|^2$, but the dimensional mapping between the soft-label/pseudo-label space $y_c^*$ and the feature space $f$ is undefined. Similarly, Eq. (5) takes a dot product between these entities without specifying how $y_c^*$ is projected into the feature embedding space. While the spatial-smoothing intuition is clear, the lack of explicit tensor dimensions and forward/backward flow details makes exact replication difficult.
- **Unsubstantiated Computational Efficiency Claims:** The paper reports a marginal $\sim$7s/epoch training increase but omits peak GPU memory usage and FLOPs. Computing dense, pixel-wise second-order spatial derivatives on $512\times512$ feature maps (Eq. 4) is inherently memory-intensive. Without quantifying the VRAM footprint or clarifying whether finite differences, convolutional Laplacian kernels, or autodiff Hessian-vector products are used, the claim that $L_{Esti}$ imposes negligible overhead remains partially unsupported.
- **Lack of Mechanistic Evidence for "Parameter Release":** The $L_{Min}$ component frames L2-norm weight masking as actively "releasing capacity" for new classes. In standard dense implementations without compiled sparsity, zeroed weights still occupy compute and memory; the masking functionally acts as a target-shifting regularizer rather than true structural capacity reduction. The paper demonstrates improved final mIoU but lacks intermediate diagnostics (e.g., gradient norm shifts, effective feature rank via SVD/CKA, or optimization trajectory analysis) to empirically prove that the masking actually alters the optimization landscape to reduce parameter competition, as claimed.

## Nice-to-Haves
- Include a dedicated Limitations section, as required by ICLR, discussing sensitivity to the $\gamma$ hyperparameter (which shifts from 1.0 to 0.4 depending on step count), the threshold $\tau$ selection process, and potential failure modes in domains with highly entangled features.
- Provide a concise algorithm/pseudocode block summarizing the training loop, explicitly detailing how $P_t$ and $C_t$ are constructed and integrated into the backward pass.
- Expand the related work or discussion to explicitly contrast DKD's pruning-and-aligning strategy with contemporary parameter-efficient fine-tuning (PEFT) or adapter-based continual learning methods, which target similar static-capacity constraints through different mechanisms.
- Investigate whether cheaper, first-order spatial proxies (e.g., attention map overlap or first-order gradient magnitude) can approximate $L_{Esti}$'s benefits, as second-order differentiation may add complexity with marginal gains in certain regimes.

## Novel Insights
DKD reframes knowledge distillation for continual segmentation not as a rigid feature-matching constraint, but as a dynamic distribution allocation problem. By mathematically bounding the old model's output distribution through targeted parameter masking ($L_{Min}$) and explicitly maximizing the mutual information between batch-level marginal distributions and sample-level predictions ($L_{Max}$), the method decouples plasticity from stability without architectural expansion. The Laplacian projection step ($L_{Esti}$) further introduces spatial curvature as a prior for identifying class-coexistence zones, shifting the focus from pixel-wise confidence masking to geometric distribution alignment. This cohesive minimization–maximization formulation provides a principled, regularization-based pathway to navigate static-capacity bottlenecks in exemplar-free continual learning.

## Suggestions
1. **Clarify Dimensional Mappings & Add Pseudocode:** Explicitly define the projection or embedding space for $y_c^*$ and $f$ in Eqs. (4)–(5). Add a concise algorithm block summarizing the step-wise training loop, including forward construction of $P_t/C_t$ and their integration into gradient computation.
2. **Quantify Memory & Compute Overhead:** Report peak VRAM usage and FLOPs/mac for the proposed losses compared to standard KD (e.g., MKD/CKD). Specify the numerical implementation of the spatial Laplacian (e.g., $3\times3$ Sobel/Laplacian kernels vs. autodiff) to validate efficiency claims at scale.
3. **Empirically Validate "Capacity Release" Mechanism:** Introduce diagnostic metrics to prove that parameter competition is actively mitigated. For example, compare gradient covariance matrices or feature effective rank before/after pruning, or demonstrate that the masked old model genuinely alters optimization trajectories for new classes independent of standard KD targets.
