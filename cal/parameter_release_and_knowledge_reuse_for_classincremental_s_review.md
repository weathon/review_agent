# Harsh Critic Review
## Section-by-Section Critical Review

### Title & Abstract
- Does the title accurately reflect the contribution? Yes. The title clearly signals the focus on parameter management ("release") and leveraging prior knowledge ("reuse") within class-incremental semantic segmentation.
- Does the abstract clearly state the problem, method, and key results? Yes. It identifies the limitations of standard KD (parameter competition, underutilization), proposes DKD with a minimization-maximization strategy, and reports strong empirical results on VOC/ADE20K approaching joint learning.
- Are any claims in the abstract unsupported by the paper? The claim "average performance approaches that of joint learning (upper bound)" is supported by Tables 1 and 2, though the gap remains non-trivial in some high-step settings (e.g., 10-1 New performance). This is acceptable but the phrasing slightly oversells the absolute performance; "closing the gap to joint learning" might be more precise.

### Introduction & Motivation
- Is the problem well-motivated? Is the gap in prior work clearly identified? The motivation is solid. CISS with static architectures suffers from fixed capacity, and the critique that standard KD preserves old distributions too rigidly (causing "crowding") is a logical and relevant gap. Fig 1 (conceptually) supports this well.
- Are the contributions clearly stated and accurate? The contributions are clearly enumerated. However, Contribution 3 claims "Theoretical analysis... validate our approach." This is overstated. The appendix provides gradient derivations and inequality bounds showing directionality of updates, not formal proofs of convergence, generalization bounds, or optimality guarantees. I recommend softening this to "Gradient and information-theoretic analysis support the proposed constraints."
- Does the introduction over-claim or under-sell? The intro is balanced but frames the method as solving a novel problem of "parameter competition." While the packaging is compelling, pruning low-sensitivity parameters for capacity is a known strategy in model compression and incremental learning. The intro should more sharply distinguish DKD's specific implementation from prior structural pruning approaches in continual learning to avoid inflating novelty claims.

### Method / Approach
- Is the method clearly described and reproducible? Mostly, but there are critical ambiguities in the mathematical formulation that impede reproduction:
  - **Eq. 4 and Spatial Derivatives:** The definition of $P_t(h,w)$ involves $\frac{\partial^2}{\partial h^2}$ and $\frac{\partial^2}{\partial w^2}$ applied to the feature difference. In discrete deep learning frameworks, spatial coordinates are indices, not continuous variables for autodiff. The paper does not specify if this is implemented via Laplacian convolution kernels, numerical finite differences, or spatial attention. This must be explicitly stated in the text or algorithm box.
  - **Eq. 5 Dimension Mismatch:** The confidence map is defined as $C_t(h,w) = \frac{\langle y_c^*(h,w), f_t(h,w) \rangle}{\|f_t(h,w)\|_2}$. The text describes $y_c^*(h,w)$ as a label or output probability (scalar or vector of size $|C|$), while $f_t(h,w)$ is a feature vector in $\mathbb{R}^D$. The dot product is dimensionally inconsistent unless $y_c^*$ is projected to the feature space or represents classifier weights. If $y_c^*$ is a pseudo-label map, this equation likely intends cosine similarity with a class prototype or the old model's feature. This needs precise correction.
- Are key assumptions stated and justified? The method assumes that parameters with $L2$ norm below $\tau=0.1$ contribute negligibly to old knowledge. This is empirically validated in Appendix C.5 and Table 11, which is good practice. The assumption that minimizing conditional entropy and maximizing marginal entropy effectively aligns old/new distributions is standard in entropy regularization but relies on the batch being representative of the true marginal, which can be noisy in small incremental batches.
- Are there logical gaps in the derivation or reasoning? The gradient analysis in Appendix A derives update directions but assumes fixed confidence weights $\hat{w}$ during the gradient step for $J(f)$, while in practice $\hat{w}$ depends on $f$ itself. The chain rule dependency is omitted, which affects the exact descent direction claim.
- Are there edge cases or failure modes not discussed? If $f_t(h,w) \to 0$, Eq. 5 is undefined. A smoothing term or norm floor is typically needed. Also, the method performs pruning "once per step." If a long stream of tasks occurs, cumulative pruning of the old model might degrade the teacher signal significantly beyond 10-20 steps; the failure mode of excessive parameter release is only partially explored in the $\tau$ ablation.

### Experiments & Results
- Do the experiments actually test the paper's claims? Yes. The authors evaluate multiple incremental protocols (10-1, 2-2, 15-1, etc.) and demonstrate reduced class confusion via t-SNE and similarity matrices, directly testing the "mitigates crowding/confusion" claim.
- Are baselines appropriate and fairly compared? Baselines are comprehensive, covering CNN and Transformer backbones, and the authors reproduce several ViT baselines ($^\dagger$) to ensure fairness. The comparison to Joint Training provides the necessary upper bound context.
- Are there missing ablations that would materially change conclusions? 
  - **Combination Strategy:** While Table 3/12 ablates components, there is no ablation on whether $L_{Min}$ is strictly necessary if $L_{Esti}$ already handles spatial reuse. The synergy argument is made, but an ablation comparing DKD vs. $L_{Esti} + L_{Max}$ (without pruning) would strengthen the necessity of the "parameter release" component.
  - **Pruning Schedule:** The method prunes once per step. An ablation comparing one-time vs. iterative pruning during epochs would clarify if the "dynamic" claim holds or if it functions as a static mask per step.
- Are error bars / statistical significance reported? Standard deviations are reported in Table 5 and Appendix C.7 for 3 runs, showing low variance (~0.1). However, the main results in Tables 1 and 2 report single numbers. For ICLR, it is strongly recommended to report mean $\pm$ std for key settings or explicitly state that variation is negligible based on the reproducibility checks.
- Do the results support the claims made, or are they cherry-picked? Results appear consistent across settings and datasets. The gains in "New" classes are substantial, and "Old" classes are well-preserved. No obvious cherry-picking is detected.
- Are datasets and evaluation metrics appropriate? Pascal VOC and ADE20K are standard. mIoU is the correct metric. The inclusion of the disjoint setting in Appendix C.1 is a commendable addition for robustness.

### Writing & Clarity
- Are there sections that are confusing or poorly explained? As noted, Section 3.2 contains notational inconsistencies (Eq. 5 dot product) and ambiguous operations (Eq. 4 spatial calculus). The definition of $y_c^*$ shifts between "updated label" and "old knowledge representation," which confuses the reader regarding what is actually being aligned. Clarifying these definitions is essential.
- Are figures and tables clear and informative? The qualitative analysis (Fig 4, 6) effectively illustrates forgetting reduction and confusion mitigation. The t-SNE plots support the claim of better intra-class compactness. Table 3 and Table 4 are informative. Note: The main text should reference Appendix tables more smoothly to guide the reader to the ablations.

### Limitations & Broader Impact
- Do the authors acknowledge the key limitations? The authors acknowledge a slight training overhead (Table 5 context) and the computational cost is discussed. The ethics statement covers data privacy and surveillance risks adequately.
- Are there fundamental limitations they missed?
  - **Scalability to Long Streams:** The method is tested up to 11 steps. In real applications, streams may be longer. The cumulative effect of reusing a progressively pruned model may degrade distillation quality.
  - **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** While $\gamma$ is analyzed, the threshold $\tau$ likely varies across network architectures and initialization schemes. A fixed $\tau=0.1$ may not generalize to deeper/shallower networks without tuning.
- Are there failure modes or negative societal impacts not discussed? Failure modes in low-data regimes or when new classes are semantic subsets of old classes (fine-grained confusion) are not discussed. The broader impact section is adequate for this domain.

### Overall Assessment
This paper addresses a relevant problem in Class-Incremental Semantic Segmentation by proposing a method to alleviate parameter competition through selective pruning and enhanced knowledge reuse via distribution alignment. The empirical evaluation is extensive, covering multiple datasets, diverse incremental settings, and strong baselines, demonstrating convincing performance gains, particularly in learning new classes while retaining old ones. However, for ICLR standards, the paper requires revisions to strengthen methodological rigor and clarity. Specifically, the mathematical formulation of the Laplacian projection (Eq. 4) and Confidence map (Eq. 5) contains implementation ambiguities and dimensional inconsistencies that must be resolved to ensure reproducibility. The theoretical claims in the appendix should be recalibrated as gradient analyses rather than formal proofs. Additionally, reporting uncertainty measures in main tables and discussing the long-term stability of the cumulative pruning strategy would bolster the paper's robustness. If the authors can clarify the derivations and address the notation issues, the contribution is solid and empirically well-supported, positioning it as a valuable addition to the CISS literature.

# Neutral Reviewer
## Balanced Review

### Summary
This paper tackles catastrophic forgetting in class-incremental semantic segmentation (CISS) by proposing Distribution-based Knowledge Distillation (DKD), a minimization–maximization framework that replaces rigid frozen-model distillation. DKD dynamically softens the old model's output by pruning low-sensitivity parameters, employs a Laplacian-based projection estimation to locate spatially reusable features, and maximizes shared knowledge between old and new distributions via an entropy-driven loss. Comprehensive experiments on Pascal VOC 2012 and ADE20K across nine incremental configurations show that DKD achieves strong stability-plasticity trade-offs, consistently approaching joint-training performance while reducing class confusion.

### Strengths
1. **Well-motivated problem framing and method design:** The paper clearly identifies two overlooked limitations of standard KD in CISS: parameter competition from rigid old-model imitation and underutilization of previously learned representations. The minimization–maximization formulation (Eqs. 3, 6, 10) directly targets these issues with a coherent loss design.
2. **Rigorous empirical validation:** The method is evaluated across highly diverse incremental splits (e.g., 10-1, 2-2 on VOC; 100-5, 100-50 on ADE20K), demonstrating robustness. Tables 1 and 2 show consistent gains in Old, New, and All mIoU, often closing >90% of the gap to the joint-training upper bound. Ablation studies (Tables 3, 12) and multi-run error analysis (Table 5/13, std ≈ 0.06–0.25) confirm component contributions and result stability.
3. **Strong reproducibility and transparency:** The paper provides detailed hyperparameters, optimizer settings, learning rate schedules, dataset splits, and training infrastructure. A commitment to public code, combined with standard evaluation protocols (mIoU, old/new split, background handling), aligns well with ICLR's reproducibility expectations.

### Weaknesses
1. **Misleading terminology around "parameter release":** The method prunes low-magnitude weights in the *frozen* old model to generate a softer distillation target, but does not actually free architectural or parameter capacity in the *new* model. The fixed parameter-fitting space remains unchanged (Section 3.2a). This semantic gap may overstate the mechanism's capacity-allocation benefits and needs precise clarification.
2. **Limited computational analysis for second-order gradients:** Equation 4 computes spatial second-order derivatives to construct the position map. While theoretically motivated, second-order gradient computation introduces non-trivial FLOP and memory overhead, and can be numerically sensitive on high-resolution feature maps. The paper only reports a modest +7s/epoch wall-clock increase vs. CKD, but lacks FLOP/memory profiling and stability analysis under varying batch sizes or resolutions.
3. **Theoretical derivations remain descriptive rather than justificatory:** Appendix A provides gradient computations and basic bounds (e.g., reverse triangle inequality for $L_{lap}$, gradient direction for $L_{pro}$, MI proxy for $L_{Max}$). However, these derivations primarily show how the losses behave locally during gradient descent rather than establishing why the combined formulation guarantees better convergence, generalization, or forgetting resistance compared to simpler entropy or smoothing alternatives.
4. **Baseline comparisons mix backbone architectures:** Tables 1 and 2 interleave ResNet101 and ViT baselines. While ViT-vs-ViT comparisons exist, the aggregated presentation obscures whether gains stem from DKD or architectural differences with older ResNet methods, making strict SOTA positioning slightly ambiguous.

### Novelty & Significance
- **Novelty:** Moderate-High. Individual components (magnitude pruning, Laplacian smoothness, entropy regularization) are established, but their integration into a unified minimization–maximization distillation strategy for CISS is novel. Reframing KD from strict output matching to dynamic distribution alignment and reuse is a meaningful conceptual shift.
- **Clarity:** Good. The problem, pipeline, and experimental setup are well-structured. Some mathematical notation (e.g., overlapping symbols for old/new distributions, dense subscripting in Eq. 4/5) could be streamlined for readability. The distinction between modifying the distillation target vs. altering the new model's capacity needs sharper articulation.
- **Reproducibility:** High. Clear implementation details, hyperparameter sweeps for $\tau$ and $\gamma$, standard dataset splits, code availability, and repeated-run error analysis satisfy ICLR's reproducibility bar.
- **Significance:** High. Continual learning without replay is a critical open problem for deployable vision systems. DKD's near-joint-training performance across long-horizon incremental settings, backbone compatibility, and inference-cost neutrality make it highly relevant to the continual learning and representation learning communities.

### Suggestions for Improvement
1. **Clarify the "parameter release" mechanism:** Explicitly state that pruning operates on the old model to soften distillation targets rather than expanding the new model's parameter space. Consider renaming it to "adaptive target softening" or "pruning-guided distillation relaxation" to avoid architectural capacity claims.
2. **Add computational and stability profiling for $L_{Esti}$:** Report FLOPs, memory overhead, and numerical stability metrics (e.g., gradient norm variance) for the Laplacian projection compared to standard CKD/MKD. Discuss potential approximations or spatial sampling strategies to reduce second-order gradient costs on higher resolutions.
3. **Deepen the theoretical connection to optimization dynamics:** Extend Appendix A with a brief analysis of how $L_{Max}$ influences early vs. late training phases (e.g., via effective learning rate scaling or gradient alignment metrics). Alternatively, provide empirical gradient signal-to-noise or mutual information tracking curves to substantiate the information-theoretic claims.
4. **Standardize backbone comparisons:** Separate Table 1/2 into ResNet and ViT subtables or clearly flag backbone groups in the main text. Directly compare against the most recent ViT-based SOTAs (e.g., MBS, CoinSeg, INC) under identical hyperparameter settings to isolate DKD's contribution.
5. **Provide guidance on hyperparameter automation:** While $\tau=0.1$ and $\gamma \in \{0.4, 1.0\}$ work well, add a brief discussion or heuristic (e.g., gradient-magnitude scaling, validation-based search ranges) for adaptively selecting $\tau$ and $\gamma$ across drastically different class-incremental schedules, enhancing practical deployment readiness.

# Final Consolidated Review
## Summary
This paper addresses catastrophic forgetting in class-incremental semantic segmentation (CISS) by proposing Distribution-based Knowledge Distillation (DKD). The method reformulates standard knowledge distillation into a minimization–maximization framework: it minimizes old-knowledge distribution by pruning low-sensitivity weights in the frozen teacher model, and maximizes shared knowledge between old and new distributions using a Laplacian-based projection estimation and an entropy-driven objective. Extensive experiments across nine incremental settings on Pascal VOC and ADE20K demonstrate that DKD achieves a strong stability-plasticity trade-off, consistently narrowing the gap to joint-training upper bounds without increasing inference latency.

## Strengths
- **Rigorous empirical validation across diverse incremental regimes:** The method is evaluated on nine distinct step configurations (including long-horizon 10-1 and 100-5 protocols) across two standard benchmarks. Results show consistent gains in old, new, and combined class mIoU, with multi-run error analysis confirming low variance (~0.1 std), establishing robust performance claims.
- **Coherent methodological framing addressing an underexplored constraint:** By identifying that rigid frozen-model imitation in static architectures causes distribution "crowding," the paper shifts focus from exact representation matching to dynamic target softening and spatial knowledge reuse. The component-wise ablations (Tables 3/12) clearly demonstrate that each loss term contributes to mitigating specific failure modes.
- **Strong reproducibility standards:** The paper provides explicit dataset splits, optimizer schedules, hyperparameter sweeps ($\tau$, $\gamma$), and training infrastructure details. The commitment to open-source code and adherence to standard CISS evaluation protocols meet venue expectations for transparency.

## Weaknesses
- **Conceptual misalignment between terminology and actual mechanism:** The core premise of "parameter release" implies freeing architectural or fitting capacity within the *student* model to alleviate parameter competition. However, Section 3.2(a) and the pruning operations are exclusively applied to the *frozen old model* to generate a softer distillation target. The student's parameter space remains strictly fixed. This semantic gap misrepresents the method's capacity-allocation benefits and inflates novelty claims relative to established teacher softening or target relaxation techniques in continual learning.
- **Methodological ambiguities hinder reproducibility and efficiency validation:** Equation 5 defines a dot product between $y_c^*(h,w)$ (described as a label/probability map of size $|C|$) and $f_t(h,w)$ (a $D$-dimensional feature vector), which is dimensionally inconsistent without an explicit projection layer or classifier-weight mapping. Additionally, the discrete computation of spatial second-order derivatives in $P_t$ (Eq. 4) lacks implementation specifics (e.g., finite differences vs. Laplacian kernels vs. spatial attention). These omissions, combined with the absence of FLOPs, memory footprint, and sparsity-rate quantification post-pruning, leave the computational trade-offs and actual capacity gains empirically unverified.
- **Theoretical claims exceed analytical depth:** The appendix presents gradient derivations and basic information-theoretic bounds (reverse triangle inequality, MI proxy for $L_{Max}$), but labels them as "theoretical analysis" validating the approach. These derivations merely describe update directions under local assumptions (e.g., treating confidence weights $\hat{w}$ as fixed during the gradient step) rather than establishing convergence guarantees, generalization bounds, or formal proofs of superiority over standard entropy regularization. The lack of empirical tracking of mutual information or gradient alignment metrics further weakens the theoretical grounding of $L_{Max}$.

## Nice-to-Haves
- Visualize the learned position ($P_t$) and confidence ($C_t$) maps overlaid on sample images to verify they capture semantically meaningful coexistence regions rather than low-frequency artifacts.
- Provide a stress-test evaluation over longer incremental horizons (>15 steps) to assess whether cumulative pruning of the teacher model degrades the distillation signal over time.
- Include a cleaner head-to-head comparison table isolating only ViT-based baselines under identical training budgets to conclusively separate DKD's algorithmic contribution from backbone capacity differences.

## Novel Insights
The paper's most compelling contribution is reframing the stability-plasticity dilemma in static CISS models as a *target rigidity* problem rather than strictly an *capacity saturation* problem. By demonstrating that deliberately degrading low-sensitivity components of the frozen teacher model can paradoxically improve new-class plasticity and reduce inter-class confusion, DKD challenges the conventional wisdom that preserving exact old distributions is paramount. This insight—turning parameter pruning from a compression tool into a distribution-smoothing mechanism for continual distillation—opens a pragmatic pathway for knowledge reuse without architectural expansion or rehearsal buffers.

## Potentially Missed Related Work
- Parameter-efficient continual learning methods that employ dynamic teacher masking or prompt pruning (e.g., Continual LoRA/MoE-CL variants) as they share conceptual overlap with adaptive target softening.
- Information bottleneck approaches in continual representation learning that explicitly optimize mutual information bounds with differentiable proxies, which could provide stronger theoretical framing for $L_{Max}$.
- Recent works on gradient-based feature attribution for distillation targets in segmentation, which may offer alternative implementations for the proposed confidence/position maps.

## Suggestions
1. **Reframe terminology and claims:** Replace "parameter release" with "adaptive distillation softening" or "teacher-guided relaxation" throughout the manuscript to accurately reflect that the student's parameter space remains fixed and only the distillation target is pruned.
2. **Resolve mathematical ambiguities:** Explicitly clarify how $y_c^*$ is projected or aligned to $f_t$ in Eq. 5 to ensure dimensional consistency. Clearly state the discrete implementation strategy for spatial second-order derivatives in Eq. 4 to guarantee reproducibility.
3. **Add efficiency and sparsity quantification:** Report exact FLOPs, peak VRAM, and the percentage of pruned teacher parameters per step. Demonstrate empirically how the pruning rate correlates with the stability-plasticity trade-off rather than relying solely on a fixed $\tau$ grid search.
4. **Align theoretical claims with empirical reality:** Revise the appendix to explicitly label the derivations as gradient dynamics analysis. Supplement the mutual information claims with empirical tracking of $L_{Max}$ components or gradient similarity curves across training epochs to substantiate the alignment hypothesis.
