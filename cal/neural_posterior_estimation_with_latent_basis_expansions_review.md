# Harsh Critic Review
## Section-by-Section Critical Review

**Abstract & Introduction**
The abstract clearly positions LBF-NPE as a middle ground between simple exponential families and black-box flows/MDNs for Neural Posterior Estimation. The motivation is well-aligned with current ICLR interests in likelihood-free and amortized inference. However, there is a subtle tension in the framing that should be addressed early: the Introduction heavily leans on the theoretical appeal of convexity and global convergence guarantees (citing McNamara et al., 2024a), yet the most expressive variant used in the main experiments (Section 6.2, 6.3) employs *adaptive* basis functions with stereographic projection, which explicitly breaks the convexity structure (Section 4.4). The abstract should more precisely distinguish between the theoretical guarantees of the fixed-basis variant and the empirical performance of the adaptive variant. Additionally, the claim of "order-of-magnitude improvements in KL divergence" is striking but depends heavily on the baseline architectures chosen; the abstract should hint at the dimensional regime (low-dimensional latent spaces) where these gains are realized to set accurate expectations.

**Background (Section 2)**
The background is accurate and appropriately scoped. The distinction between ELBO-based VI (reverse KL, nuisance variable bottleneck) and NPE (forward KL, automatic marginalization) is well-motivated and correctly justifies why NPE enables the proposed low-dimensional focus. One minor suggestion: the connection between forward KL minimization and moment matching/sufficient statistics (which exponential families naturally facilitate) could be briefly mentioned to strengthen the theoretical motivation for choosing basis expansions specifically for NPE, rather than just stating it "frees us from closed-form normalization."

**Method: LBF-NPE (Section 3 & 4)**
*Section 3.1 & 3.2:* The parameterization $q(z; \eta) \propto h(z) \exp(\eta^\top s_\psi(z))$ is mathematically sound and correctly identifies the resulting family as an exponential family with neural sufficient statistics. The derivation of the gradient for the log-normalizer via self-normalized importance sampling (SNIS, Equations 7-10) follows standard wake-sleep/reweighted wake-sleep literature. 
**Crucial concern:** The gradient estimator is biased and its variance depends entirely on the proposal distribution $r(\tilde{z})$. Algorithm 1 takes $r$ as an input but never specifies how it is chosen or tuned in practice. Is it a fixed uniform distribution over the grid? The prior $p(z)$? A learned approximation? In regions where the true posterior has thin, high-density ridges (e.g., the spiral or bands in Section 6.2), a poor proposal will cause catastrophic SNIS variance or systematic bias. An ablation or clear justification for the choice of $r$ is needed for reproducibility and to assure reviewers that optimization trajectories are stable.

*Section 4.1 & 4.2 (Convexity & Affine Gradients):* Proposition 1 correctly establishes marginal convexity in $f$ or $s$ when the other is fixed. This is a strong theoretical point. However, as noted in the Intro, the adaptive experiments do not operate in this regime. The authors should explicitly state that the convexity guarantees strictly apply to the fixed-basis variant (Section 4.3) or under alternating optimization *if* sub-problems reach global optima (which is not guaranteed in practice).

*Section 4.4 (Stereographic Projection & Degeneracy):* The reparameterization to the unit hypersphere mitigates scaling degeneracy but leaves rotational degeneracy intact, as the loss only depends on the inner product $\hat{f}_\phi(x)^\top \hat{s}_\psi(z)$. The text acknowledges this but does not explain why it doesn't harm optimization. Rotational symmetry in the parameter space often creates flat manifolds or saddle points that can slow down gradient descent or cause erratic learning dynamics. The claim that stereographic projection "stabilizes training" is empirical; a brief analysis (e.g., gradient norm tracking, or comparison to simple $L_2$ normalization) in the appendix would strengthen this design choice. Additionally, the scaling hyperparameter $w$ in Equation 12 is introduced without discussing its sensitivity or how it's set.

**Experiments (Section 6 & Appendices)**
*Section 6.1 (Toy Sinusoidal):* Clean experiment demonstrating convergence stability vs. MDN local minima. Using fixed B-splines here aligns well with the convexity claims. Fair parameter matching (14 parameters for both methods). No major concerns.

*Section 6.2 (2D Cases):* Table 1 reports FKL, RKL, and NLL across Bands, Ring, and Spiral. LBF-NPE consistently outperforms NSF, RealNVP, and MDN. 
**Concern 1 (KL Estimation Precision):** The KL divergences are computed via numerical integration on a $100 \times 100$ grid (Appendix D.2). For highly curved or thin distributions like the spiral, grid discretization can significantly underestimate tail probabilities or smear sharp ridges, potentially distorting KL calculations. A sensitivity analysis to grid resolution (e.g., $100\times100$ vs $200\times200$) or an alternative KL estimation method (e.g., Monte Carlo with importance sampling) would increase confidence in the reported magnitudes, especially the "order-of-magnitude" claims.
**Concern 2 (Alternating Optimization):** Appendix D.2 states that $f_\phi$ and $s_\psi$ are updated alternately for 1000 steps each. While this leverages marginal convexity, alternating optimization in non-convex joint spaces can cycle or converge to suboptimal Nash equilibria rather than joint minima. The authors should report whether end-to-end joint optimization was attempted and how it compared, or provide loss curves showing that the alternating scheme monotonically decreases the joint objective in practice.
**Concern 3 (Baselines):** The flow architectures (10-16 coupling layers) and MDN components are standard, but explicit learning rate schedules or architecture width sweeps are not reported. While likely fair, a sentence confirming that baselines were tuned to convergence would satisfy rigorous ICLR standards.

*Section 6.3 & 6.4 (Object Detection & Redshift):* The application to astronomical datasets (BLISS framework) is a strong real-world validation. The use of fixed B-splines for redshift estimation is well-justified by convexity. 
**Concern:** The NLL improvement over MDN/NSF on the DC2 dataset (Table 2) is $\sim 1800-6500$ total over 153,000 samples, translating to $\sim 0.01-0.04$ per sample. While statistically significant given the low variance, the cosmological significance of this gain is not discussed. Does this improvement meaningfully reduce bias in downstream $p(z)$ cosmological inference? Citing how this NLL delta maps to parameter bias in dark energy equations of state would elevate the impact beyond pure ML metrics.

**Limitations & Discussion (Section 7)**
The limitation regarding sampling difficulty is honestly acknowledged. However, the text somewhat downplays how restrictive this is. Inverse transform sampling is only tractable in $d=1$; Langevin dynamics require careful step-size tuning and mixing diagnostics. This effectively caps LBF-NPE at $\leq 3\text{--}4$ dimensions for practical use without significant sampling overhead. The authors should explicitly state this dimensional regime in the limitations rather than vaguely deferring high-dimensional extensions to "future work." Additionally, the computational cost of approximating the normalizer integral (grid or MC with 22,500 samples in Appendix D.5) is non-trivial. Table 6 shows faster *convergence*, but per-step cost is higher. A clearer discussion of the trade-off between optimization stability (fewer steps) and per-step computational overhead would help practitioners decide when to use LBF-NPE over standard flows.

### Overall Assessment
This paper presents a well-motivated and empirically promising approach to amortized neural posterior estimation using latent basis expansions. The core idea—parameterizing the log-posterior as a neural exponential family to leverage automatic marginalization and improved optimization landscapes—is novel and theoretically grounded in the fixed-basis regime. The experiments span synthetic 2D benchmarks and a realistic astronomical case study, consistently showing strong performance against MDNs and normalizing flows.

However, several technical and empirical points need clarification before the paper fully meets ICLR's bar for theoretical and experimental rigor:
1. The proposal distribution $r(z)$ used in the SNIS gradient estimator is underspecified and critical for stability; its choice and sensitivity must be detailed.
2. The adaptive variant breaks the convexity guarantees highlighted in the Introduction; the rotational degeneracy of the stereographic projection and the convergence properties of the alternating optimization scheme require justification or empirical backing.
3. KL divergence estimates rely on grid integration which may be insufficiently precise for thin manifold posteriors; sensitivity analysis is recommended.
4. The sampling limitation inherently restricts applicability to very low-dimensional spaces ($d \lesssim 3$); this should be explicitly stated as a boundary condition rather than a minor footnote.

If the authors can address the proposal specification, clarify the optimization dynamics of the adaptive variant, and provide a brief sensitivity analysis for the KL metrics, this paper would be a solid contribution to the likelihood-free inference community. The method fills a genuine niche for stable, interpretable, low-dimensional amortized inference where flows struggle with local optima.

# Neutral Reviewer
## Balanced Review

### Summary
This paper introduces Latent Basis Function Neural Posterior Estimation (LBF-NPE), a novel amortized variational inference method for likelihood-free inference that parameterizes the log-posterior density as a linear combination of latent basis functions. By training a neural network to map observations to basis coefficients, and optionally learning the basis functions adaptively, the approach constructs a highly expressive exponential family that retains favorable optimization properties, including marginal convexity. Extensive experiments on synthetic and scientific benchmarks demonstrate that LBF-NPE achieves more stable training and superior posterior approximations for multimodal targets compared to standard NPE families like mixture density networks and normalizing flows.

### Strengths
1. **Rigorous Theoretical Grounding of Optimization:** The paper proves that the forward KL objective is marginally convex in both the amortization network and the basis function network (Proposition 1). This theoretical insight directly explains the empirically observed stability and avoidance of shallow local minima, contrasting sharply with the notoriously difficult optimization landscapes of MDNs and flows.
2. **Expressive yet Computationally Efficient Variational Family:** LBF-NPE captures complex multimodal geometries (e.g., bands, ring, spiral) with remarkably few basis functions ($K=20$), achieving order-of-magnitude improvements in FKL and RKL over MDNs and NSF (Table 1). The local basis variant (B-splines/wavelets) further improves optimization by inducing sparsity and simplifying gradient updates.
3. **Comprehensive Evaluation & Transparency:** The authors provide thorough baselines (MDN, RealNVP, NSF, EigenVI, SMNEF), multiple evaluation metrics, and clear ablations on basis dimensionality and normalization (Table 5, Figures 12-13). Training protocols, hyperparameters, and computational costs are explicitly detailed in Appendix D, and the promise of open-sourced code ensures strong reproducibility.
4. **Practical Mitigation of Identifiability Issues:** The stereographic projection reparameterization (Section 4.4) effectively resolves scaling degeneracies inherent to inner-product-based losses. The visual comparison in Appendix E.1 clearly demonstrates how normalization yields sharper, more interpretable basis partitions, directly linking the theoretical fix to empirical gains.

### Weaknesses
1. **Scalability to High-Dimensional Sampling:** As noted in Section 7, sampling from the unnormalized density is a core limitation. While inverse transform sampling and Langevin dynamics work well for 1D/2D tasks, the 50D experiment (Appendix E.4) only evaluates 2D marginals via MC integration and does not demonstrate efficient joint sampling. This restricts the method's utility for downstream tasks that require full joint posterior draws in dimensions $d > 3$.
2. **Underexplored Sensitivity to Numerical Integration:** The gradient estimator relies on self-normalized importance sampling or grid-based integration (Algorithm 1) to approximate the log-normalizer. The paper does not analyze how the choice of proposal distribution $r(z)$, particle count $P$, or grid resolution affects gradient bias, variance, and final posterior accuracy. This omission makes it difficult for practitioners to robustly tune these components on new problems.
3. **Overstated Convexity Guarantees in Practice:** While Proposition 1 establishes marginal convexity at the functional level, the actual optimization employs finite-width neural networks. The infinite-width NTK convergence guarantees (Appendix B.1) do not strictly apply to the practical architectures used. The framing could lead readers to mistakenly assume the empirical loss landscape is globally convex, rather than empirically smoother and better conditioned.
4. **Limited Baseline Coverage for Modern SBI Methods:** The comparisons focus on classical amortized VI (MDNs, coupling-based flows). The paper would benefit from contextualizing LBF-NPE against recent advances in simulation-based inference, such as conditional flow matching, neural score estimation, or diffusion-based amortized inference, which have seen rapid adoption in the SBI community.

### Novelty & Significance
**Novelty:** High. The synthesis of basis expansions with amortized NPE, framed within an adaptive exponential family, is a fresh and theoretically well-motivated contribution. The use of stereographic projection to stabilize joint optimization and the explicit exploitation of NPE's marginalization properties for low-dimensional scientific targets are novel and non-trivial.
**Clarity:** Excellent. The mathematical derivations (gradient computation, convexity proofs, sampling strategies) are clearly presented and well-organized. The transition from theory to fixed/adaptive variants, followed by systematic empirical validation, follows a logical and highly readable structure.
**Reproducibility:** Strong. Detailed network architectures, training schedules, optimizer choices, and dataset descriptions are provided across Appendices D and E. The promise of open-source code and the use of publicly available simulation benchmarks (LSST DC2) further ensure that results can be independently verified.
**Significance:** High. LBF-NPE addresses a critical bottleneck in simulation-based inference: the trade-off between variational flexibility and optimization stability. Its ability to reliably capture multimodal posteriors with minimal basis functions makes it highly relevant for scientific ML applications where likelihoods are intractable and posterior projections are low-dimensional but scientifically crucial.

### Suggestions for Improvement
1. **Develop or Benchmark a Scalable Sampling Strategy:** To address the high-dimensional limitation, explore a practical joint sampling pipeline (e.g., parallel tempering MCMC or learned importance sampling proposals guided by the basis functions). Provide a brief benchmark of sampling throughput vs. accuracy trade-offs for dimensions $d=5,10$ to better guide practitioners.
2. **Ablate Integration Hyperparameters:** Conduct a sensitivity analysis on the importance sampling step (number of particles $P$, choice of proposal $r(z)$, or grid density). Report how these choices impact gradient variance, KL divergence, and training stability, providing concrete default recommendations in the main text or appendix.
3. **Clarify Theoretical vs. Practical Convexity:** Temper the "convex optimization" claims by explicitly stating that convexity holds at the functional level (and in the infinite-width limit), while finite-width networks introduce a well-conditioned but non-convex landscape. Adding a loss landscape visualization or Hessian trace comparison over training would ground the theoretical claims in empirical reality.
4. **Broaden the Related Work & Baselines Context:** Add a discussion comparing LBF-NPE to modern amortized inference techniques like conditional flow matching (CFM) or score-based generative modeling in the SBI space. Even a qualitative positioning statement or a small experimental comparison would significantly strengthen the paper's relevance to the current ICLR/SBI landscape.

## How to Improve This Paper

### Missing Experiments (top 3-5 only)
1. **Add simulation-based calibration (Expected Coverage Plots / Rank Histograms)** because NLL alone masks over/under-confident posteriors, and without calibration analysis, claims of reliable uncertainty quantification for scientific inference are unproven.
2. **Run baselines (NSF, RealNVP, MDN) to matched total FLOPs or wall-clock time** because showing convergence in fewer steps without a Pareto analysis fails to prove LBF-NPE is computationally superior when the true metric is accuracy per unit compute.
3. **Evaluate on realistic $d \geq 10$ benchmarks (e.g., SLCP, SIR, or multi-parameter astrophysical models)** because relying on 2D grids and a synthetic 50D marginal leaves the curse of dimensionality claim unverified for standard NPE workflows.

### Deeper Analysis Needed (top 3-5 only)
1. **Quantify SNIS gradient variance and proposal sensitivity** because the training relies entirely on a biased estimator where a mismatched proposal $r(z)$ triggers catastrophic weight collapse, which directly undermines claims of stable, reliable optimization.
2. **Analyze the theoretical impact of stereographic projection** because this reparameterization explicitly breaks the marginal convexity structure proven in Proposition 4.2, creating a critical disconnect between the stated convergence theory and the adaptive implementation actually used.
3. **Bound the bias magnitude of the SNIS gradient vs. proposal sample count $P$** because practitioners cannot trust the optimization trajectory or select hyperparameters without knowing the minimum $P$ required to keep gradient bias negligible in high-curvature regions.

### Visualizations & Case Studies
1. **Provide Q-Q plots and marginal CDF overlays for actual draws via Langevin/IT sampling** to prove the method produces statistically indistinguishable posterior samples, not just accurate density evaluations on fixed evaluation grids.
2. **Show explicit failure cases (e.g., heavy-tailed distributions, sharply disconnected 3D+ modes)** to delineate where local bases or grid-based integration degrade, preventing overestimation of the method's robustness.
3. **Visualize the spatial evolution of $s_\psi(z)$ across training steps** to demonstrate that adaptive bases genuinely partition space according to posterior topology rather than collapsing into degenerate or memorized configurations.

### Obvious Next Steps
1. **Integrate a low-bias gradient estimator (e.g., doubly reparameterized gradients or control variates)** to remove the Jensen gap bias inherent to SNIS, which is a known bottleneck for stable exponential family training.
2. **Engineer a tractable, closed-form sampling head** (e.g., a lightweight conditional flow or analytical rejection bounds) so the method fits naturally into standard NPE pipelines that strictly require fast, exact sample generation for posterior predictive checks.
3. **Formalize the link between forward KL minimization and downstream calibration** or incorporate coverage penalties into validation, because minimizing FKL does not inherently guarantee well-calibrated scientific uncertainty intervals.

# Final Consolidated Review
## Summary
This paper introduces LBF-NPE, an amortized neural posterior estimation method that parameterizes the variational log-density as a linear combination of neural latent basis functions. By leveraging the forward KL objective and NPE's automatic marginalization, the approach targets low-dimensional posterior projections of scientific interest. The method achieves highly stable optimization, particularly through its convex formulation with fixed bases, and empirically outperforms mixture density networks and normalizing flows on multimodal synthetic benchmarks and astronomical inference tasks.

## Strengths
- **Theoretically grounded optimization stability:** The paper establishes marginal convexity of the forward KL objective in both the amortization network and basis functions (Proposition 1). This provides a rigorous explanation for LBF-NPE's consistent convergence on multimodal problems where standard baselines (MDNs, flows) frequently collapse into shallow local minima, as demonstrated in the sinusoidal toy task (Fig. 1).
- **Expressive yet efficient low-dimensional modeling:** LBF-NPE captures complex 2D posterior geometries (bands, ring, spiral) using remarkably few basis functions ($K \approx 20$). The combination of adaptive basis learning with stereographic projection effectively mitigates scale degeneracy while maintaining a compact parameterization, yielding order-of-magnitude improvements in FKL/RKL over NSF and MDN baselines (Table 1).
- **Strong alignment with scientific NPE workflows:** By explicitly scoping the method to low-dimensional projections of interest and exploiting NPE's likelihood-free marginalization, LBF-NPE sidesteps the curse of dimensionality inherent in full joint posterior modeling. The successful application to astronomical object detection and photometric redshift estimation on the LSST DC2 dataset demonstrates practical utility in real-world simulation-based inference pipelines.

## Weaknesses
- **Unspecified proposal distribution and gradient estimator robustness:** The training procedure relies on self-normalized importance sampling (SNIS) or grid-based integration to compute gradients of the log-normalizer (Sec. 3.2, Algorithm 1). However, the paper does not specify the proposal distribution $r(z)$ used in practice, nor does it analyze how the choice of proposal, particle count $P$, or grid density impacts gradient bias and variance. For thin, high-curvature manifolds (e.g., the spiral case), a mismatched or coarse proposal can induce high SNIS variance or systematic bias, which directly threatens the claimed optimization stability and reproducibility across new problem classes.
- **Potential evaluation bias on thin posterior manifolds:** Quantitative metrics in the 2D experiments are computed via numerical integration over a fixed $100 \times 100$ grid (Appendix D.2). For distributions with sharp ridges or intricate topology, fixed-grid discretization can smear probability mass and artificially inflate or deflate KL divergence estimates. Without a sensitivity analysis to resolution or an alternative evaluation protocol (e.g., importance sampling aligned with the true posterior geometry), the reported magnitude of improvement over baselines may partially reflect evaluation artifacts rather than pure model fidelity.

## Nice-to-Haves
- **Empirical validation of the adaptive optimization landscape:** While the paper correctly notes that stereographic projection breaks the strict convexity guarantees of the fixed-basis regime (Sec. 4.4), adding empirical diagnostics such as loss trajectory comparisons across multiple seeds, or an ablation against simpler $L_2$ normalization, would help practitioners understand the practical trade-offs of the adaptive variant.
- **Simulation-based calibration (SBC) analysis:** Reporting expected coverage or rank histograms would verify that the improved density estimates translate to well-calibrated posterior uncertainty, which is especially relevant for the stated scientific applications where accurate credible intervals are critical.
- **Explicit computational trade-off guidelines:** Table 6 shows higher per-step cost but faster convergence. A brief discussion or rule-of-thumb for when LBF-NPE's optimization stability justifies the increased per-iteration overhead would aid practitioner adoption.

## Novel Insights
LBF-NPE reframes amortized neural posterior estimation through the lens of neural exponential families, effectively turning the forward KL objective's natural affinity for sufficient statistics into a computational advantage. By decoupling observation-dependent coefficients from latent-space basis functions, the method inherits marginal convexity in each component a rarity in amortized VI that provides a rare theoretical anchor for stable training. The adaptive variant with stereographic projection cleverly sidesteps scale degeneracy without sacrificing expressivity, positioning LBF-NPE as a principled, interpretable middle ground between rigid parametric families and opaque normalizing flows specifically for low-dimensional scientific inference where stability and multimodality dominate.

## Suggestions
- Explicitly detail the choice of proposal distribution $r(z)$ (or grid sampling strategy) used across experiments, and provide a sensitivity analysis showing how variations in $r(z)$, particle count $P$, or grid resolution affect gradient variance, training stability, and final KL/NLL metrics.
- Validate the 2D KL results with a higher-resolution grid (e.g., $200 \times 200$ or $400 \times 400$) or a Monte Carlo-based KL estimator to rule out discretization artifacts, particularly for the spiral and band cases where probability mass concentrates on thin manifolds.
- Consider including a brief simulation-based calibration (SBC) evaluation or expected coverage analysis on at least one synthetic task to confirm that the learned variational families produce statistically reliable uncertainty intervals, strengthening the method's suitability for the targeted scientific downstream applications.
