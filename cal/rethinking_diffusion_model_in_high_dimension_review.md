# Harsh Critic Review
## Section-by-Section Critical Review

**Title & Abstract**
The title appropriately signals a re-examination of high-dimensional diffusion dynamics. However, the abstract makes two claims that are overstated relative to the paper's content. First, the claim that the objective function's degradation "hinders the model’s ability to effectively learn essential statistical quantities" conflates finite-sample approximation limits with fundamental learning barriers. Second, stating that the inference framework "involves no statistical concepts" is mathematically inaccurate. The framework explicitly enforces that the sum of signal coefficients equals $\sqrt{\bar{\alpha}_t}$ and noise coefficients equal $\sqrt{1-\bar{\alpha}_t}$, which are direct consequences of the variance-preserving/flow matching statistical schedules. These phrases should be tempered to reflect that the framework *reframes* rather than *eliminates* statistical foundations.

**Introduction & Motivation (Section 1)**
The introduction effectively frames a perceived paradox between high-dimensional data sparsity and diffusion model success. However, the core claim—*“diffusion models do not learn these statistical quantities; instead, they operate via a different mechanism”*—sets up a false dichotomy. The theoretical objective of diffusion models (score matching, flow matching, or denoising) is designed to learn population-level statistical quantities. The paper's later argument hinges on the behavior of the *empirical* loss given a finite dataset. The introduction must clearly distinguish between the population objective (which learns the true distribution/scoring) and the finite-batch empirical approximation. Without this distinction, the premise mischaracterizes why diffusion models work: neural networks possess strong inductive biases (smoothness, parameter sharing) that explicitly allow them to interpolate between finite samples and approximate continuous statistical fields, even in high dimensions.

**Background (Section 2)**
This section accurately derives the equivalences between posterior mean prediction, score matching, and flow matching, showing they all target $\mathbb{E}[x_0|x_t]$ or affine transforms thereof. This is mathematically correct but well-established in the literature (e.g., Karras et al., 2022; Lipman et al., 2022). The presentation is clear, but it would benefit from acknowledging prior unifications rather than treating these equivalences as newly revealed stepping stones. The mathematical derivations themselves are sound and serve as a proper foundation.

**Impact of Sparsity on Objective Function (Section 3)**
This section contains the paper's central theoretical argument but suffers from a critical conflation between population and empirical distributions.
- **Dirac Delta Assumption:** In Eq. (14), the paper models $p(x_0)$ as $\frac{1}{N}\sum_i \delta(x_0 - X_0^{(i)})$. This is the *empirical data distribution*, not the true underlying distribution $p_{\text{data}}(x_0)$. Analyzing the posterior under this assumption necessarily yields a discrete mixture of Gaussians. Concluding that diffusion models "cannot effectively learn the hidden probability distribution" based on this setup is circular: you assume the data is a set of point masses, derive that the target is point-mass dominant, and conclude the model can't learn a continuous distribution. In reality, diffusion models succeed precisely because the parameterized network $f_\theta$ acts as a smooth function approximator that interpolates between these discrete training points, effectively learning the population score despite finite data.
- **Degradation Metric (Tables 1 & 2):** The definition of degradation ($p(x_0=X'_0|x_t) > 0.9$) essentially measures whether a noisy sample $X_t$ lies within a high-density Gaussian basin around a single training point. This is a geometric nearest-neighbor dominance effect inherent to high dimensions with limited $N$. Presenting this as a failure of the objective function, rather than a known property of finite-sample estimation in high dimensions, overstates the issue. The methodology for computing these percentages is also under-specified (e.g., how is the integral over the unknown $p(x_0)$ handled if not using the Dirac assumption?).
- **Frequency Spectrum Argument (Section 3.3):** The discussion linking the objective to spectral filtering and information enhancement is intuitive and aligns with recent work by Dieleman (2023, 2024). However, it is presented qualitatively without mathematical formalization or experimental validation (e.g., Fourier analysis of intermediate generations).

**A Unified Inference Framework-Natural Inference (Section 4)**
- **Mathematical Validity vs. Novelty:** The "Natural Inference" framework algebraically unrolls the sampling recurrence to express each $x_t$ as a linear combination of past model predictions $y_\tau$ and noise terms $\epsilon_\tau$. This is mathematically correct. However, it is largely a change-of-basis / recurrence unrolling rather than a fundamentally new mechanism. The fact that the summed coefficients approximate $\sqrt{\bar{\alpha}_t}$ and $\sqrt{1-\bar{\alpha}_t}$ is a *necessary consistency condition* for any valid diffusion sampler; it is not a surprising discovery. Existing high-order solvers (DPM-Solver, DEIS) already enforce these marginal properties by construction.
- **Self Guidance (Section 4.1):** Drawing an analogy between linear combinations of intermediate predictions and Classifier-Free Guidance is intriguing but conceptually strained. CFG scales the difference between conditional and unconditional *scores* (directions), whereas "Self Guidance" here mixes *predicted images* from different time steps. The mathematical equivalence shown in Appendix B is algebraically trivial (any linear combination of two vectors can be rewritten as a convex/affine combination) and does not establish a meaningful theoretical link to CFG's guidance mechanism.
- **Lack of Empirical/Algorithmic Utility:** Section 4.4 claims the framework could lead to more optimal parameter configurations, but the paper provides zero experiments exploring this. Without demonstrating a new sampler, improved convergence, or better sample quality derived from the framework, it remains a descriptive reformulation. ICLR standards typically require either strong theoretical novelty with rigorous proofs or empirical demonstration that a new perspective translates to tangible improvements.

**Writing & Clarity**
The paper is generally readable but suffers from repetitive emphatic phrasing (e.g., heavy use of bold text) and occasionally imprecise terminology. The claim that the framework "maintains training-testing consistency" is standard in modern $x_0$-prediction diffusion models and not unique to this formulation. Several derivations in the appendices are correct but could be condensed; the main text should focus more on interpretation and less on restating standard sampler recursions.

**Limitations & Broader Impact**
This section is entirely missing. A rigorous version of this work must explicitly acknowledge: (1) the reliance on the finite Dirac-delta assumption and how smooth NN parameterizations mitigate its implications; (2) that the unifying framework, while elegant, is descriptive and has not yet yielded algorithmic improvements; (3) the absence of generative quality metrics (FID, precision/recall, etc.) to support the central claims. Without this, the paper reads as conclusory rather than analytical.

---

### Overall Assessment

This paper raises an interesting observation about the geometry of finite-sample posterior distributions in high-dimensional diffusion settings and provides a clean algebraic unrolling of existing samplers into a "Natural Inference" framework. However, the contribution currently falls short of ICLR's acceptance bar due to significant overclaiming and a lack of empirical or algorithmic substantiation. The core theoretical argument conflates the empirical distribution (Dirac deltas) with the true data distribution, leading to the unsupported conclusion that diffusion models do not learn statistical quantities. In reality, finite-sample degradation is a known high-dimensional statistical phenomenon that neural networks overcome via smooth interpolation and implicit regularization, which the paper does not adequately address. Furthermore, the unified inference framework, while mathematically sound, is largely a descriptive reformulation of existing solvers whose coefficient properties are enforced by design. The paper lacks experiments on generative quality, fails to propose a concrete improved sampler from its framework, and omits a limitations section. To meet ICLR standards, the authors must rigorously distinguish population vs. empirical objectives, contextualize the high-dim sparsity claims within existing manifold/spectral analysis literature, and provide empirical validation demonstrating that the proposed perspective yields measurable analytical or practical benefits.

# Neutral Reviewer
## Balanced Review

### Summary
This paper challenges the conventional assumption that diffusion models learn underlying statistical quantities (posterior, score, or velocity fields) in high-dimensional spaces, arguing instead that data sparsity causes the training objective to degenerate from a weighted average of samples to predicting a single training sample. Building on this observation, the authors propose the "Natural Inference" framework, which reformulates standard sampling algorithms (DDPM, DDIM, DPM-Solver, DEIS, etc.) as iterative linear combinations of past $x_0$ predictions and noise, independent of statistical interpretations. The framework is supported by coefficient matrix derivations, symbolic computation tools, and qualitative visualizations of the generation process.

### Strengths
1. **Clear Derivation of High-D Posterior Behavior:** Section 3 rigorously derives the form of $p(x_0|x_t)$ under an empirical data measure and demonstrates how distance concentration in high dimensions causes the posterior to be dominated by a single nearest neighbor. Tables 1 and 2 provide systematic empirical statistics across ImageNet resolutions and noise schedulers, quantifying the degradation phenomenon.
2. **Unified Algebraic View of Samplers:** The "Natural Inference" framework correctly shows that first- and higher-order ODE/SDE solvers can be unrolled into explicit linear combinations of past model outputs and noise. The provided coefficient matrices (Tables 3–11) and the use of symbolic computation for higher-order methods offer a transparent, debug-friendly perspective on iterative sampling.
3. **Intuitive Visualization for Practitioners:** Figures 15 and 16 map the intermediate signal/noise mixing and guidance steps to concrete image outputs at each stage. This aligns the inference process with familiar signal processing concepts (e.g., unsharp masking, frequency completion), which can aid debugging and educational understanding.

### Weaknesses
1. **Overstated Core Claim Contradicted by Empirical Reality:** The paper asserts that diffusion models "do not learn" complex statistical quantities due to objective degradation (Sec 1, 3.2). However, the widespread empirical success of diffusion models in generating diverse, out-of-distribution samples strongly suggests that inductive biases, parameter sharing, and iterative refinement enable generalization beyond nearest-neighbor memorization. The paper provides no theoretical or empirical evidence ruling out learning; rather, it characterizes a finite-sample property of the empirical objective.
2. **Incremental Theoretical Novelty:** The degradation to a single sample is a manifestation of well-known high-dimensional phenomena (distance concentration, empirical measure approximation, and memorization regimes). Furthermore, showing that iterative samplers can be expressed as linear combinations of past predictions is an algebraic consequence of unrolling recursive update rules. While mathematically sound, it does not constitute a new theoretical framework or reveal deeper insights into optimization landscapes, generalization bounds, or probability flow geometry.
3. **Missing Empirical Validation & Practical Utility:** ICLR typically requires analytical insights to be paired with tangible methodological or performance gains. The paper does not derive a new sampler, improve convergence/quality metrics, or demonstrate computational advantages. The coefficient matrices and visualizations are descriptive rather than prescriptive, limiting the work's impact on the state-of-the-art.
4. **Weak Logical Bridge Between Sections:** Section 3 concludes that the objective degenerates to predicting a single $X_0$. Section 4 introduces a framework where each step relies on a weighted combination of *multiple* past predictions. The paper does not explain why this multi-step linear structure remains effective if the training signal supposedly collapses to single-sample targets, nor does it show how the framework mitigates or exploits the degradation phenomenon.

### Novelty & Significance
**Novelty:** Low to moderate. The analysis of posterior concentration under an empirical distribution is correct but aligns with existing high-dimensional statistical learning theory and recent discussions on diffusion model memorization vs. generalization. The unification of samplers via unrolled linear combinations is a valid reformulation but lacks fundamental mathematical novelty beyond standard recurrence expansion. The frequency-based interpretation closely mirrors prior community analyses (e.g., Dieleman, 2024).
**Significance:** Limited by current ICLR standards. While the pedagogical reframing and visualization are useful for intuition, the paper does not advance theoretical understanding in a way that yields new algorithms, training objectives, or provable generalization guarantees. For acceptance at ICLR, re-interpretations must typically enable measurable improvements in sample quality, sampling efficiency, training stability, or provide rigorous theoretical bounds; this submission remains primarily descriptive.

### Suggestions for Improvement
1. **Reframe and Temper Claims:** Adjust the central claim from "models do not learn statistical quantities" to "finite-batch high-dimensional objectives exhibit nearest-neighbor concentration, yet models generalize through inductive biases." Cite and discuss literature on memorization thresholds, manifold learning, and how neural networks overcome empirical measure limitations.
2. **Establish Theoretical Guarantees:** Replace empirical degradation rates with theoretical analysis. For example, derive bounds on how generalization error scales with dimensionality, batch size, network width/depth, and time-step discretization. Connect the framework to existing convergence analyses of ODE solvers in diffusion models.
3. **Derive Actionable Methodological Improvements:** Leverage the coefficient matrix structure to propose a novel scheduling strategy, adaptive weighting of past predictions, or a modified sampling rule. Rigorously evaluate against baselines on standard benchmarks (e.g., FID, IS, sampling steps to reach target quality) to demonstrate practical utility.
4. **Clarify the Section 3 → Section 4 Connection:** Explicitly articulate why the linear combination of multiple past predictions works despite the claimed single-sample degradation. Does the autoregressive signal mixing effectively approximate an expectation over a learned manifold? Provide a formal or empirical justification linking the "degraded" objective to the stability/accuracy of the Natural Inference unrolling.
5. **Position Within Related Work:** Add a dedicated discussion comparing this framework to recent analyses of diffusion dynamics (e.g., spectral autoregression, rectified flow trajectories, neural tangent kernel regimes, and solver-specific error analyses). This will better contextualize the contribution and highlight what is genuinely new versus what is a repackaging of existing concepts.

# Spark Finder Review
## How to Improve This Paper

### Missing Experiments (top 3-5 only)
1. The "degradation" metric is computed using a single training mini-batch, completely ignoring that diffusion models are trained over millions of iterations on the full dataset. Add experiments measuring the actual learned predictions across the full training corpus to prove the network approximates a continuous field rather than memorizing the current batch's nearest neighbors.
2. The claim that the proposed framework offers practical utility is unsupported by empirical results. Add a quantitative study showing a sampling failure (e.g., ODE stiffness, mode collapse, or artifacts) that your coefficient-matrix representation directly diagnoses and fixes, but which the standard statistical view cannot.
3. The spectral hypothesis in Section 3.3 lacks quantitative validation. Compute the actual frequency-domain reconstruction error of the model's $x_0$ predictions across different noise schedules $t$ to rigorously verify the claim that the objective acts as a low-to-high frequency filter.
4. You claim the framework unifies stochastic and deterministic samplers, but you never test if manipulating these unified coefficients yields a superior sampler. Implement a search over the coefficient space to find configurations that strictly improve FID or NFE compared to DDIM/DDPM baselines.

### Deeper Analysis Needed (top 3-5 only)
1. The paper conflates discrete mini-batch approximations with the continuous ground-truth distribution, leading to the incorrect conclusion that diffusion models cannot learn statistical quantities. Analyze how the neural network's inductive biases (e.g., smoothness, weight normalization, architectural priors) regularize the sparse targets into a valid score/velocity field.
2. The algebraic expansion of existing solvers into autoregressive combinations of prior predictions is mathematically guaranteed by their update rules, not a new discovery. Analyze the numerical error bounds when equivalent coefficients deviate from theoretical marginal levels and explain under what conditions your representation diverges from standard numerical ODE/SDE integration theory.
3. The degradation threshold ($>0.9$ probability mass on one sample) is arbitrary and disconnected from actual model performance. Add a sensitivity analysis correlating degradation rates with standard generalization metrics (FID, IS) to prove that the observed statistic actually harms generation quality.

### Visualizations & Case Studies (top 3-5 only)
1. The provided inference visualization only shows a standard, high-quality SD3 generation. Visualize a pathological failure case and show whether the Natural Inference framework reveals failure mechanisms (e.g., signal degradation, coefficient explosion) that are invisible in standard score-based trajectory plots.
2. Visualize the actual $I_{bad}$, $I_{good}$, and resulting $I_{out}$ images for Fore, Mid, and Back Self Guidance. Demonstrate clearly whether the linear combinations genuinely recover structural information or simply perform naive interpolation of noise patterns.
3. Provide a side-by-side comparison showing the standard ODE solver trajectory vs. your coefficient accumulation trajectory. Highlight where the two views align and where they conceptually diverge to prove your framework is genuinely additive rather than tautological.

### Obvious Next Steps (top 4 only)
1. If the training objective is truly equivalent to frequency-dependent denoising, propose and test a modified loss function with explicit frequency weighting. Show whether optimizing for this behavior improves convergence speed or sample fidelity.
2. Design and evaluate a sampler that directly optimizes the Natural Inference coefficient matrix instead of relying on standard discretizations. Prove the framework enables discovering better sampling paths, otherwise it remains a mathematical re-description without utility.
3. Test the degradation hypothesis on non-image modalities (e.g., tabular data, 3D point clouds, or audio) where the manifold geometry differs significantly from natural images to prove the high-dimensional sparsity claim generalizes beyond vision.

# Final Consolidated Review
## Summary
This paper investigates the behavior of diffusion models in high-dimensional spaces, arguing that data sparsity causes the training objective to degrade from learning a weighted average of samples to predicting a single nearest training point. Based on this analysis, the authors conclude that diffusion models do not learn true statistical distributions and instead propose the "Natural Inference" framework, which unifies existing samplers as autoregressive linear combinations of past $x_0$ predictions and noise.

## Strengths
- **Systematic empirical quantification of posterior collapse.** The paper rigorously derives the form of $p(x_0|x_t)$ under an empirical data measure and systematically measures the "nearest-neighbor dominance" phenomenon across ImageNet resolutions and noise schedules (Tables 1 & 2). This provides concrete, reproducible data on a known geometric property of high-dimensional diffusion training.
- **Transparent algebraic unrolling of sampling algorithms.** The "Natural Inference" framework correctly demonstrates how first- and higher-order ODE/SDE solvers (DDPM, DDIM, DPM-Solver, DEIS) can be mathematically expanded into explicit linear combinations of past $x_0$ predictions and noise terms. The derived coefficient matrices and use of symbolic computation provide a clear, debug-friendly view of iterative sampling trajectories.

## Weaknesses
- **Flawed central conclusion conflating empirical and population objectives.** The core claim that diffusion models "do not learn statistical quantities" is fundamentally undermined by the paper's reliance on an empirical Dirac delta assumption for $p(x_0)$. Analyzing the posterior under a discrete, finite-sample distribution naturally yields nearest-neighbor dominance. Concluding that models fail to learn population-level statistical properties (scores, velocity fields) from this setup ignores the well-documented role of neural network inductive biases (smoothness, parameter sharing) that explicitly interpolate between finite training points. This conclusion directly contradicts the empirical reality of diffusion models generating highly diverse, out-of-distribution samples rather than merely memorizing training data.
- **Lack of theoretical novelty and practical utility in the proposed framework.** The "Natural Inference" formulation is an algebraic unrolling of existing recursive update rules rather than a new theoretical mechanism. The fact that aggregated coefficients approximate the theoretical marginal signal/noise levels ($\sqrt{\bar{\alpha}_t}$ and $\sqrt{1-\bar{\alpha}_t}$) is a necessary consistency condition engineered into any valid diffusion sampler, not an emergent discovery. The paper remains purely descriptive, failing to demonstrate how this perspective yields improved sample quality, reduced function evaluations, or tighter convergence bounds. Without empirical validation or a novel, optimized sampler derived from the framework, it functions as a mathematical re-parameterization with limited standalone impact.
- **Unresolved logical disconnect between training degradation and inference success.** Section 3 argues that the training target collapses to isolated, single-sample predictions. Section 4, however, relies on recursively combining *multiple* past predictions to construct high-quality samples. The paper does not explain why this multi-step linear interpolation remains effective if the underlying training signal supposedly provides only sparse, single-points targets. A rigorous bridge connecting the claimed "degraded" objective to the stability and accuracy of the multi-step autoregressive unrolling is entirely missing.

## Nice-to-Haves
- Quantitatively validate the spectral filtering hypothesis (Section 3.3) by computing actual Fourier-domain reconstruction errors of $x_0$ predictions across varying noise schedules $t$, rather than relying on qualitative spectral diagrams.
- Provide a sensitivity analysis to determine if the observed "degradation rates" actually correlate with practical generation metrics (e.g., FID, precision/recall), ensuring the statistic is predictive of performance rather than merely a geometric artifact.

## Novel Insights
The paper provides a valuable pedagogical reframing by treating diffusion sampling trajectories as explicit Autoregressive accumulations of past $x_0$ predictions and noise, rather than as abstract score-following or flow-based paths. This perspective demystifies the iterative process, making it amenable to standard signal processing intuitions (e.g., unsharp masking, progressive frequency completion) and yields coefficient matrices that could aid practitioners in visualizing and debugging sampler trajectories. However, the core theoretical insight regarding objective "degradation" is a formalized restatement of known high-dimensional distance concentration effects under an empirical measure, rather than a novel discovery about the fundamental learning mechanisms of diffusion models.

## Suggestions
- Reframe the central narrative: Replace the claim that models "fail to learn statistical quantities" with a characterization of "finite-sample nearest-neighbor concentration," and shift the focus to how architectural inductive biases and implicit regularization allow models to generalize beyond this limitation.
- Bridge the theoretical gap: Explicitly derive or empirically demonstrate why the autoregressive combination of multiple past predictions in Natural Inference successfully approximates the continuous probability flow, despite the claimed single-sample degradation during training. Explain how the multi-step linear combination mitigates the noise of isolated training targets.
- Provide empirical validation of the framework's utility: Utilize the coefficient matrix structure to propose and evaluate a modified sampling strategy (e.g., adaptive weighting of past predictions or optimized coefficient schedules). Benchmark this against standard methods using established metrics (FID, NFE) to prove the framework offers tangible methodological benefits beyond theoretical unrolling.
