## ABSTRACT 

> The accuracy-first perspective of differential privacy addresses an important short- coming by allowing a data analyst to adaptively adjust the quantitative privacy 

> bound instead of sticking to a predetermined bound. Existing works on the accuracy- 

> first perspective have neglected an important property of differential privacy known 

> as post-processing immunity, which ensures that an adversary is not able to weaken the privacy guarantee by post-processing. We address this gap by determining which existing definitions in the accuracy-first perspective have post-processing immunity, and which do not. The only definition with post-processing immunity, pure ex-post privacy, lacks useful tools for practical problems, such as an ex-post analogue of the Gaussian mechanism, and an algorithm to check if accuracy on separate private validation set is high enough. To address this, we propose a new 

> definition based on Rényi differential privacy that has post-processing immunity, and we develop basic theory and tools needed for practical applications. We 

> demonstrate the practicality of our theory with an application to synthetic data 

> generation, where our algorithm successfully adjusts the privacy bound until an 

> accuracy threshold is met on a private validation dataset. > 1 INTRODUCTION > As the volume of data collection and capabilities of machine learning models increase, the need for 

> methods to use the data while maintaining the privacy of data subjects grows ever larger. Differential 

> privacy (DP; Dwork et al., 2006b) has become the gold-standard definition of privacy to address this 

> need. 

> However, differential privacy is a very strict definition. First, it requires protection against a worst-case adversary with very strong capabilities, which necessitates necessitates very strong protection that can 

> heavily decrease the utility of an analysis. Second, it requires specifying a numerical privacy bound 

> before running the algorithm, which precludes workflows such as reducing the privacy bound over 

> time in case the data becomes less sensitive, using different bounds for different audiences (Rosenblatt 

> et al., 2024), and addressing low utility by decreasing the privacy protection afterwards (Ligett et al., 2017; Nanayakkara et al., 2024). 

Motivated by these issues, Ligett et al. (2017) proposed the _accuracy-first_ perspective to DP, where the analyst sets a minimum utility (accuracy) threshold, and finds the strongest privacy bound that empirically meets the threshold. As the privacy bound now depends on the private data, standard DP definitions, known as _ex-ante_ definitions, are not applicable. Instead, this perspective requires a new type of privacy definition that allows the privacy bound to depend on the private data, known as _ex-post privacy_ . Ligett et al. (2017) proposed the first ex-post privacy definition, pure ex-post privacy, and developed a _Laplace noise reduction_ mechanism that satisfies their definition. In subsequent work, Whitehouse et al. (2022) proposed a probabilistic relaxation, and developed the _Brownian mechanism_ , an analogue of the Gaussian mechanism (Dwork et al., 2006a) of ex-ante DP providing higher utility. In parallel to our work, Ghazi et al. (2025) developed a hyperparameter tuning algorithm for the accuracy-first perspective, and proposed ex-post privacy definitions based on _approximate DP_ (ADP, Dwork et al., 2006a) and _Rényi DP_ (RDP; Mironov, 2017). However, these works have neglected an important aspect that any privacy definition should have, known as _post-processing immunity_ (Dwork & Roth, 2014). Post-processing immunity is the 

1 

requirement that any processing of the output of a private algorithm cannot decrease the privacy guarantee. It is clear why any sensible privacy definition (Mironov, 2017; Dong et al., 2022) should have post-processing immunity: without it, an adversary could use post-processing to infer private information more easily than promised, making the guarantee weaker than promised (Meiser, 2018). **058 059 Contributions** Our goal is to address this gap by determining whether previous ex-post privacy defi- nitions have post-processing immunity, and by developing additional theory that supports mechanisms for practical problems under ex-post privacy guarantees with post-processing immunity. 1. We propose a new notation for ex-post privacy definitions that makes the promised privacy bound a part of the output of the algorithm, instead of making it a separate function of the output. The new notation allows defining post-processing immunity in an elegant way, while we were not able to find a satisfactory definition with the old notation, and neither has any previous work (Ligett et al., 2017; Whitehouse et al., 2022; Ghazi et al., 2025) that we know of. We then formulate the existing definitions to use our new notation. See Section 3.1 for details. 2. After the translation to the new notation, we prove that the pure ex-post privacy definition of Ligett et al. (2017) has post-processing immunity, while the probabilistic relaxation of Whitehouse et al. (2022) does not have it. This motivates the development of an alternative relaxation that both has post-processing immunity, and supports the Brownian mechanism. See Section 3.2 for details, and Table 1 for a summary. 3. We investigate an ex-post privacy definition based on RDP, which has been proposed in parallel by Ghazi et al. (2025). We prove that this definition has post-processing immunity, along with many basic properties that are useful in constructing practical mechanisms. In particular, we provide an algorithm that checks whether accuracy on a private validation dataset is high enough, whereas previous works only provide algorithms to check accuracy on the training set (Ligett et al., 2017; Whitehouse et al., 2022). See Section 4. 4. We prove that the Brownian mechanism satisfies ex-post RDP in Section 5. This is the first time the Brownian mechanism has been proven to satisfy an ex-post definition with post-processing immunity. Our proof uses a more elementary view of the Brownian mechanism than Whitehouse et al. (2022), that does not require working with stochastic processes, which may be of interest to future work. 5. We give a practical example of using the Brownian mechanism and our theory in Section 6. We generate synthetic data, and ensure that the accuracy of a classifier trained on the synthetic data on a private validation dataset is high enough on a minimal privacy budget. We find that our implementation is able to achieve high accuracy while keeping a tight privacy budget. We give another example in Appendix E, where we fine-tune an image classifier, ensuring a minimum accuracy while minimising the privacy budget. ## 1.1 RELATED WORK

3 POST-PROCESSING IN EX-POST PRIVACY In ex-ante DP, post-processing immunity requires that processing the output of a DP mechanism without further access to the input data cannot weaken the DP guarantee (Dwork & Roth, 2014). Any sensible privacy definition should have post-processing immunity (Mironov, 2017; Dong et al., 2022), since if an adversary can weaken a privacy guarantee by simply post-processing, that guarantee was not real in the first place (Meiser, 2018). Indeed, common ex-ante DP definitions have post-processing immunity (Dwork et al., 2006a;b; Bun & Steinke, 2016; Mironov, 2017; Dong et al., 2022). In addition, post-processing immunity is extremely useful in constructing DP mechanisms to solve practical problems, as it allows choosing an intermediate quantity to release with a simple DP mechanism, and using that to solve the problem without needing to consider DP further. Many DP algorithms for solving complex problems, such as DP-SGD (Rajkumar & Agarwal, 2012; Song et al., 2013; Abadi et al., 2016), and DP synthetic data generation (Hardt et al., 2012), use this design. In contrast to ex-ante DP, we are not aware of prior work proving that any ex-post privacy definition has post-processing immunity. To rectify this problem, we start with a very basic issue: the notation used in ex-post privacy definitions (Section 3.1). We then determine whether pure ex-post privacy and _δ_ -probabilistic ex-post privacy have post-processing immunity (Section 3.2). ## 7 DISCUSSION AND CONCLUSION 

**508 509 Interpreting Ex-Post Privacy Bounds** Since allowing a data-dependent privacy bound in ex-post privacy is a major change from ex-ante privacy, it is important to discuss how ex-post privacy bounds should be interpreted. This applies equally to all ex-post privacy definitions, including ones not using our notation from Section 3.1. > For one run of an ex-post mechanism, the returned privacy bound should be reported, along with the 

> details of how it is computed. The value can then be interpreted in a similar way as the corresponding ex-ante privacy bound, see Appendix B for ex-post RDP and Ligett et al. (2017) for pure ex-post privacy. Values from different runs can easily be compared: the smaller privacy bound is more private. 

> The more difficult case is comparing mechanisms, in which case the privacy bound is a random 

> variable. A major difficulty is that this random variable depends on the private data, so comparisons 

> using the private data may leak privacy. To circumvent this, it may be possible to use a prior for the 

> private data, and compare the distributions of the privacy bounds under that prior. The distributions can be compared for example by examining their CDFs, though it will not be possible to always establish that one mechanism is more private than the other. We leave an in-depth investigation of this to future work. **526 Limitations** Ex-post RDP inherits many of the limitations of RDP. First, RDP is not as easy to interpret as _f_ -DP or Gaussian DP (GDP; Dong et al., 2022), which are defined in terms of optimal performance of a distinguisher. RDP has a Bayesian interpretation (Mironov, 2017), which we extend to ex-post RDP in Appendix B, but these are not as clear as _f_ -DP or GDP. Second, the conversion from RDP to approximate DP or _f_ -DP is lossy in the ex-ante setting, which we expect to also hold in the ex-post setting. For both reasons, we expect developing ex-post GDP or _f_ -DP, and determining further properties of approximate ex-post privacy, to be a promising directions for future research. **532 533 534 Conclusion** In this work, we determined which ex-post privacy definitions have post-processing immunity and which do not. As the one definition with post-processing immunity does not support the Brownian mechanism, we filled this gap by proposing a new ex-post privacy definition with post-processing immunity, the Brownian mechanism, and supporting theory that enables application to practical problems. We demonstrated one such application: tabular synthetic data generation, where our mechanism successfully met a downstream classification accuracy threshold while minimising the spent privacy budget.

> **1404** E ACCURACY-FIRST DIFFERENTIALLY PRIVATE OPTIMIZATION **1405 1406** In this section, we implement a variant of differentially private stochastic gradient descent (DP-SGD; 

> **1407** Rajkumar & Agarwal, 2012; Song et al., 2013; Abadi et al., 2016), specifically DP-Adam, in the 

> **1408** accuracy-first setting. We apply the algorithm to fine-tune an image classifier. After an initial 

> **1409** fine-tuning run, we check the validation accuracy of the classifier, and then fine-tune longer if the 

> **1410** accuracy is not high enough. As every iteration of fine-tuning is RDP, Theorem 4.5 gives an ex-post **1411** bound for a data-dependent number of iterations with public validation data, and Theorem 4.6 gives **1412** an ex-post bound with private validation data. 

> **1413** To compute RDP bounds for each iteration of fine-tuning, we use the Opacus library (Yousefpour 

> **1414** et al., 2021). We convert ex-post RDP bounds to ex-post ADP with the conversion theorem of Ghazi 

> **1415** et al. (2025). For this conversion, Opacus computes ex-post RDP bounds for a set of _α_ values, finds 

> **1416** the _ϵ_ adp at a given _δ_ corresponding to each RDP bound, and takes the minimum _ϵ_ adp. With public **1417** validation data, each ex-post RDP bound is valid by Theorem 4.5, and each converted ex-post ADP **1418** bound is valid by the conversion theorem of Ghazi et al. (2025), so the minimum _ϵ_ adp is valid. 

> **1419** Note that by default Opacus uses another conversion theorem (Balle et al., 2020) that is valid in the 

> **1420** ex-ante setting and gives smaller ADP bounds. Future work should investigate if this conversion also 

> **1421** holds in the ex-post setting. **1422** With a private validation set, we use the plain Gaussian mechanism to check the accuracy threshold. 

> **1423** The privacy accounting with this mechanism has five steps: **1424** 

**1425** 

> **1425** 1. Find the optimal ( _α[∗] , ϵ[∗]_ rdp[)][ pair that gives the smallest] _[ ϵ]_[adp][ for the initial optimisation run.] **1426 1427** 2. Set the accuracy check noise variance so that the composition of all checks is ( _α[∗] , ϵ[∗]_ rdp[)][-RDP.] **1428** 3. Compute the ( _α[∗] , ϵ[∗]_ rdp[)][ pairs of the accuracy check for all other values of] _[ α]_[ in the set used] **1429** for privacy accounting. 

> **1430** 4. Take the maximum of the optimisation and accuracy check _ϵ_ rdp:s for each _α_ . **1431** 5. Convert those to ADP with the conversion of Ghazi et al. (2025). **1432** 

> **1433** Each ex-post RDP bound is valid by Theorem 4.6, so the conversion to ex-post ADP is valid. **1434 1435** As the specific setting, we fine-tune the last layer of a vision transformer (ViT-B-16, pre-trained on ImageNet-21K; Dosovitskiy et al., 2021) on CIFAR-10 (Krizhevsky, 2009), replicating an experiment **1436** from (Tobaben et al., 2023). To make the task harder, we only use 50 datapoints per class for training 

> **1437** data, and another 50 datapoints per class for validation data. We use the standard CIFAR-10 test set. 

> **1438** We set the initial _ϵ_ adp = 1 _._ 5, and use the same _δ_ = 1 _/n_ train as Tobaben et al. (2023). Our initial _ϵ_ adp 

> **1439** is larger than the smallest one ( _ϵ_ adp = 1) used by Tobaben et al. (2023) to compensate for the weaker 

> **1440** conversion theorem of Ghazi et al. (2025). We use Adam (Kingma & Ba, 2015) as the optimiser, **1441** implemented with DP in Opacus (Yousefpour et al., 2021). 

**1442** We tune hyperparameters for _ϵ_ = 1 _._ 5 using Optuna (Akiba et al., 2019), running 20 trials. We use the **1443** best hyperparameters for the rest of the experiment, except we divide the learning rate by 10 after the **1444** initial run of fine-tuning, since we expect the algorithm to have gotten reasonably close to a minimum **1445** at that point. We ignore the privacy cost of hyperparameter tuning, as is common in DP deep learning **1446** literature (Tobaben et al., 2023). Note that Tobaben et al. (2023) tune hyperparameters separately for **1447** every value of _ϵ_ , so we do not reach the same accuracy they do with other values of _ϵ_ . 

**1448** With public validation data, we check the accuracy threshold every 50 epochs, and allow a maximum **1449** of 10 releases, including the initial one. With private validation data, we check accuracy every 100 **1450** epochs and allow 5 releases. **1451 1452** Results are plotted in Figures 4 and 5. They show that our ex-post private fine-tuning is able to reach **1453** the accuracy threshold while minimising _ϵ_ adp, with both public and private validation data. The threshold check has noise with private validation data, meaning that the accepted validation accuracy **1454** is sometimes below the threshold, but the distributions of final test accuracies are similar with both **1455** public and private validation data. **1456**
