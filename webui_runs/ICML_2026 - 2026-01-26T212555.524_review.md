## Section-by-Section Critical Review

### Title & Abstract
- **Title accuracy**: The title accurately reflects the contribution—an attack method (FaceLinkGen) that exposes identity leakage through identity extraction rather than pixel reconstruction.
- **Abstract clarity**: The abstract clearly states the problem (reconstruction-centric evaluation failure), the method (FaceLinkGen for linkage and regeneration), and key results (98.5% matching, 96% regeneration, robust in zero-knowledge setting).
- **Unsupported claims**: The abstract claims are supported by experimental results in the paper.

### Introduction & Motivation
- **Problem motivation**: The problem is well-motivated. The critique that PSNR/SSIM fail to capture identity leakage is compelling and timely.
- **Gap identification**: The gap is clearly identified—prior work assumes preventing pixel-level reconstruction equates to privacy, which the paper demonstrates is false.
- **Contributions**: Contributions are clearly stated: (1) exposing misalignment in evaluation paradigms, (2) introducing identity-centric attack, (3) demonstrating SOTA vulnerability. These are accurate and appropriately scoped.
- **Over-claiming**: The introduction is appropriately scoped and does not over-claim.

### Related Work
- **Relevant baselines**: The paper cites and evaluates three relevant SOTA systems: PartialFace (ICCV 2023), MinusFace (CVPR 2024), and FracFace (NeurIPS 2025). This is appropriate.
- **Positioning**: The distinction from Model Inversion Attacks (Section 1, lines 100-118) and face anonymization (lines 119-132) is fair and necessary.
- **Missing references**: The paper could benefit from citing more cryptographic PPFR approaches (e.g., homomorphic encryption-based methods) in the main comparison, though they are mentioned in Section 7. Additionally, the paper does not engage with cancelable biometrics literature beyond brief mentions.

### Method / Approach
- **Clarity and reproducibility**: The method is clearly described. The distillation framework (Equation 1) is standard and reproducible. The student model is trained to align protected template embeddings with the teacher's ArcFace embeddings.
- **Assumptions stated**: The key assumption—that protected templates retain identity-discriminative information—is stated and justified (lines 163-170).
- **Information-theoretic framework**: Section 4.3 provides a formal framework, but Equation 6 contains unclear notation: "h is a implictily function conversion the noise to non-identity information." This is not mathematically precise and reads like a hand-waved justification rather than a rigorous formulation.
- **Failure modes**: The paper does not discuss cases where the attack might fail or when it would be inapplicable (e.g., if identity information is genuinely not preserved).

### Experiments & Results
- **Testing claims**: The experiments directly test the paper's claims. The linkage attack (Section 5.1) and regeneration attack (Section 5.2) demonstrate that identity can be extracted without pixel reconstruction.
- **Baselines**: The comparison against prior evaluation metrics (Table 6, comparing FracFace's claimed protection rates vs. actual regeneration success) is appropriate and striking.
- **Missing ablations**: The paper lacks ablation studies on:
  1. The effect of training data size on attack success
  2. The necessity of using ArcFace vs. other embedding models
  3. The effect of different student model architectures
- **Error bars / statistical significance**: **Critical omission.** No error bars, confidence intervals, or statistical significance tests are reported. For a paper making strong quantitative claims ("over 98.5% matching accuracy"), this is a significant weakness. Results are reported as single numbers without any measure of variance.
- **Dataset appropriateness**: The use of CASIA-WebFace, LFW, and TPDNE is appropriate. However, the linkage upper bound (88%) is attributed to dataset noise (line 293), but this explanation is not quantified—how much of this is truly dataset noise vs. attack limitation?
- **Cherry-picking concerns**: The Amazon API results in the zero-knowledge setting (Table 7: 44-57%) are notably lower than Face++ results (94-96%), but this discrepancy is attributed to the API being "more strict or sensitive to AI-generated images" without evidence. This is a weak explanation for a substantial gap.
- **Cross-validation**: The use of both Face++ and Amazon APIs for validation is good practice.

### Writing & Clarity
- **Confusing sections**: The information-theoretic framework (Section 4.3) is more confusing than illuminating due to imprecise notation. Equation 6 is particularly problematic.
- **Figures and tables**: Figures are informative. Figure 1 shows convincing regeneration examples. Tables are clear, though Table 4 could benefit from a clearer label indicating these are recall@1 scores.
- **Notation issues**: In Equation 1, the summation symbol and cosine similarity function are clear, but the notation "N X k=1" appears to be a parsing artifact.

### Limitations & Broader Impact
- **Acknowledged limitations**: Section 7 discusses future directions, including the need for key-based hardening and cryptographic methods.
- **Missing limitations**: 
  1. The paper does not acknowledge that all three tested systems use frequency-domain obfuscation—the attack's generalizability to fundamentally different protection approaches is not tested.
  2. The zero-knowledge setting explicitly exploits the shared "high-frequency preservation" property—this is a significant constraint not sufficiently emphasized.
  3. The paper claims to show "structural vulnerability" but only tests one family of methods.
- **Appendix E**: The appendix mentions "Tests on Non-Frequency Based PPFR Systems" but the content is empty (line 770-771 shows only a heading). This is a critical omission—this analysis would directly address the generalizability concern.

### Overall Assessment
This paper makes an important contribution by demonstrating that current PPFR evaluation practices are fundamentally flawed. The core insight—that identity leakage can occur without pixel reconstruction—is well-supported by experiments showing >90% regeneration success rates. The zero-knowledge setting experiment (Section 6) adds practical relevance.

However, the paper has notable weaknesses: (1) **No error bars or statistical significance testing**, which undermines confidence in the quantitative claims; (2) **Limited scope**—only frequency-domain PPFR methods are tested, and the appendix section promising tests on non-frequency-based systems is empty; (3) **Unexplained discrepancies**—the Amazon API results (44-57%) vs. Face++ results (94-96%) in zero-knowledge setting are not adequately explained. The contribution survives these flaws because the central claim (pixel metrics don't measure identity leakage) is convincingly demonstrated, but the paper's claims about "structural vulnerability" are overstated given the limited method diversity tested.

## Balanced Review

### Summary
This paper introduces FaceLinkGen, an identity extraction attack against Privacy-Preserving Face Recognition (PPFR) systems. The authors argue that existing evaluation paradigms using pixel-level metrics (PSNR, SSIM) fail to capture true privacy risks, as preventing pixel reconstruction does not prevent identity leakage. Their attack extracts identity embeddings from protected templates via distillation and demonstrates high success on linkage (identity matching) and regeneration (synthesizing identity-consistent faces) across three recent PPFR systems, including in a constrained "near-zero knowledge" setting.

### Strengths
1. **Compelling conceptual reframing**: The paper correctly identifies a fundamental misalignment between reconstruction-centric evaluation and actual privacy threats. Table 1 and Figure 2 provide concrete evidence that high pixel similarity (SSIM=0.841) can correspond to low identity similarity (FS=0.008), while different images of the same person show the inverse pattern—validating the core argument.

2. **Strong empirical results with practical significance**: The attack achieves 98.5%+ matching accuracy and 96%+ regeneration success on three recent PPFR methods (PartialFace ICCV'23, MinusFace CVPR'24, FracFace NeurIPS'25). Even in the near-zero knowledge setting (only 30 known pairs, no access to conversion process), results remain above 92% matching and 94% regeneration (Table 7).

3. **Thorough evaluation methodology**: Testing across three datasets (CASIA-WebFace hold-out, LFW, TPDNE), using two commercial verification APIs (Face++, Amazon) with multiple FAR thresholds (1e-3 to 1e-5), and reporting both linkage and regeneration metrics provides comprehensive evidence of vulnerability.

4. **Meaningful threat model clarification**: The distinction from Model Inversion Attacks (MIAs) is well-articulated—this attack targets protected templates before recognition processing, operates offline without server queries, and exploits structural vulnerabilities rather than learned embedding invertibility.

### Weaknesses
1. **Limited methodological scope undermines generalization claims**: Only three PPFR methods are tested, all frequency-domain based. The paper claims to expose "a structural gap" in "transformation-based PPFR" broadly, but this overgeneralizes from frequency-obfuscation methods. Section E ("Tests on Non-Frequency Based PPFR Systems") appears in the appendix heading but contains no content.

2. **The near-zero knowledge attack relies on an unvalidated assumption**: The claim that high-pass filtering serves as a "universal proxy" because all tested methods "preserve high-frequency information" lacks theoretical justification. Why should this generalize? The paper provides no frequency analysis demonstrating this property holds universally across PPFR approaches.

3. **Reproducibility is explicitly compromised**: The authors state they "withhold full attack code, trained models, and specific implementation details." While citing security concerns, this fundamentally conflicts with scientific reproducibility requirements. The proposed "verified request" mechanism is insufficient for independent validation.

4. **Missing quantitative comparison with related semantic attacks**: The paper mentions Yue et al. (2023) for federated learning semantic recovery but provides no quantitative comparison. Without such comparison, it is unclear whether the attack's success is specific to PPFR or reflects broader vulnerabilities in semantic-level inversion.

5. **Incomplete analysis of why these protections fail**: The paper demonstrates THAT frequency-domain methods fail but provides shallow analysis of WHY identity information specifically persists. A frequency spectrum analysis showing what identity-carrying frequencies survive protection would strengthen the theoretical contribution.

### Novelty & Significance
The conceptual contribution—shifting from reconstruction-centric to identity-centric privacy evaluation—is significant and timely. The attack method itself is intentionally simple (distillation + Arc2Face generation), which the authors frame as a strength (showing vulnerability requires no sophisticated techniques). However, this simplicity also limits technical novelty. The primary value lies in exposing flawed evaluation assumptions in a growing research area. The generalization claims are premature given the limited methodological scope tested.

### Suggestions for Improvement
1. **Expand empirical scope**: Include at least one non-frequency-based PPFR method to validate whether the vulnerability is structural to all transformation-based approaches or specific to frequency-domain obfuscation.

2. **Provide theoretical analysis**: Include frequency-domain visualization showing which components carry identity information and why high-pass filtering recovers them. This would justify the near-zero knowledge attack's generalization claim.

3. **Compare with semantic inversion baselines**: Quantitatively compare with Yue et al. (2023) or similar semantic recovery methods to contextualize the attack's novelty and effectiveness.

4. **Adopt responsible disclosure with reproducibility**: Consider releasing code through an institutional review process or providing anonymized attack outputs for verification. The current approach blocks independent scientific validation entirely.

5. **Soften overgeneralized claims**: Revise claims about "transformation-based PPFR" broadly to accurately reflect the tested scope (frequency-domain obfuscation methods).

## How to Improve This Paper

### Missing Experiments

1. **Comparison with Model Inversion Attack (MIA) baselines**: The paper explicitly distinguishes itself from MIAs but provides no empirical comparison. Include experiments using recent MIA methods (e.g., diffusion-based inversion from Wang et al. 2025) on the same PPFR systems to demonstrate that FaceLinkGen achieves superior or complementary results. Without this, readers cannot assess whether the claimed "structural vulnerability" is truly novel or just a variant of known attack surfaces.

2. **Additional PPFR systems**: The paper mentions DuetFace (Mi et al., 2022) and FaceObfuscator (Jin et al., 2024) in the introduction but does not evaluate them. Testing on these systems would strengthen the claim that the vulnerability is "structural" rather than method-specific. If source code is unavailable, contact authors or implement the methods—ICLR reviewers will question why only 3 of 5+ cited methods were tested.

3. **Ablation studies on distillation**: The paper uses a "simple, standard distillation process" but provides no ablation on: (a) effect of training data size (what if attacker only has 1K images instead of 90K?), (b) choice of teacher model (ArcFace vs. other face recognition models), (c) student architecture variations. Without these, the claim that vulnerability is "in the representation itself" cannot be disentangled from optimization details.

4. **Demographic subgroup analysis**: PPFR systems may have differential vulnerability across demographic groups. Break down attack success rates by race, gender, and age using FairFace labels. If certain groups are more vulnerable, this has significant ethical implications that ICLR reviewers will expect addressed.

5. **Intermediate knowledge settings**: The paper jumps from "full oracle access" to "30 pairs only" with no intermediate points. Test settings with 50, 100, 200, 500 known pairs to understand the scaling behavior of attack success vs. attacker knowledge—this would make the "minimal assumption" results more informative.

6. **Defense experiments**: The paper critiques current PPFR but offers no empirical test of potential defenses. Even a simple experiment adding noise to templates before the attack, or using different embedding models, would demonstrate whether the vulnerability is truly fundamental or can be mitigated.

### Deeper Analysis Needed

1. **Formal information-theoretic characterization**: Section 4.3 provides an intuitive framework (H(X) >> H(T)) but does not formalize it. Derive bounds on mutual information I(Identity; Template) vs. I(Identity; Reconstructed) to make rigorous the claim that identity information is preserved while pixel information is lost. Without formalization, the "conceptual framework" is hand-wavy for ICLR standards.

2. **Recognition-privacy trade-off analysis**: Quantify the relationship between the PPFR system's verification accuracy and the attack success rate. If there exists a fundamental lower bound on leakage given a target recognition performance, this would be a significant theoretical contribution currently absent.

3. **Analysis of failure cases**: The paper reports 98.5% success but does not analyze the 1.5% failures. What characteristics make certain identities harder to extract? Is it image quality, pose, occlusion, or identity-specific factors? Understanding failure modes would strengthen claims about when the attack generalizes.

4. **Convergence and sample complexity**: The distillation process trains for "under two hours" but no analysis of convergence behavior, sample efficiency, or minimum data requirements. Provide training curves and analysis of how attack success scales with training iterations and data.

### Untapped Applications

1. **Other biometric modalities**: The attack framework should generalize to fingerprint, iris, or voice biometrics with similar privacy-preserving transformations. Even a preliminary test on one additional modality would significantly broaden impact and demonstrate the "structural" nature of the vulnerability.

2. **Federated learning with face data**: The connection to federated learning attacks (Yue et al. 2023) is mentioned but not explored. Test whether protected templates used in federated face recognition settings leak identity information to malicious participants.

3. **Cross-database linkage attacks**: The paper tests within-dataset linkage but not cross-database scenarios (e.g., linking a protected template from a banking database to leaked social media profiles). This real-world attack scenario is highly relevant to the stated threat model.

### Visualizations & Case Studies

1. **Embedding space visualization**: Include t-SNE/UMAP plots showing: (a) original face embeddings, (b) student model outputs from protected templates, (c) their alignment. This would visually demonstrate the claimed embedding alignment and help readers intuitively understand the attack.

2. **Failure case gallery**: Show examples where regeneration fails, with analysis of why (pose extremes, occlusions, unusual attributes). This transparency about limitations is expected at ICLR.

3. **Attention/saliency analysis**: Visualize what regions of protected templates the student model attends to. This would reveal whether the model exploits specific structural patterns (confirming high-frequency leakage hypothesis) or learns more complex mappings.

4. **Soft-identity attribute visualization**: Beyond the table in Appendix C, show visual examples of regenerated faces alongside original faces with matched/mismatched attributes (race, age, gender) to make the soft-biometric leakage concrete.

### Natural Next Steps

1. **Propose and evaluate identity-centric defense mechanisms**: The paper convincingly shows current methods fail but does not propose solutions. A natural follow-up would be to design PPFR systems that explicitly minimize I(Identity; Template) while maintaining verification utility, perhaps using adversarial training against the distillation attack.

2. **Develop standardized identity-leakage benchmarks**: The paper argues for new evaluation standards but does not release a benchmark. Create and release a standardized dataset/protocol for identity-centric PPFR evaluation to catalyze the field.

3. **Formalize the privacy-utility trade-off**: Building on the information-theoretic framework, derive theoretical limits on what protection is achievable for any PPFR system that maintains recognition utility. This would transform the paper from an attack demonstration to a foundational contribution.
