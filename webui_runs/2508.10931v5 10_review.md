## Section-by-Section Critical Review

### Title & Abstract
- **Does the title accurately reflect the contribution?**  
  Yes. The title is aligned with the core idea in Section 3: a simple negative-guidance mechanism based on flipping the sign of attention values, aimed at few-step generation models.

- **Does the abstract clearly state the problem, method, and key results?**  
  Largely yes. The abstract identifies the problem (negative prompt guidance in few-step models), states the proposed method (value sign flip in attention values), and summarizes results.

- **Are any claims in the abstract unsupported by the paper?**  
  A few claims are somewhat stronger than the evidence provided:
  - The abstract claims effectiveness for **“image and video generation models”** and mentions integration with **“cross-attention-based models like Wan.”** In the paper, the strongest quantitative evidence is on **SD 3.5 Turbo**; **Wan** is only shown qualitatively in Appendix K, and the video claim is not validated with a quantitative benchmark.
  - The claim of **“small computational overhead”** is only partially supported. Table 6 shows that on SD3.5, runtime goes from **2.14s (baseline)** to **3.00s (VSF)**, which is not negligible relative to the unguided baseline, even if it is still better than CFG-like two-pass methods.
  - The headline numerical superiority is demonstrated only on the authors’ own benchmark (**NegGenBench**) and primarily with **MLLM-based evaluation**, which the appendix itself acknowledges is imperfect (Appendix D, F, O). For ICLR, that makes the strongest abstract claims feel somewhat under-validated.

### Introduction & Motivation
- **Is the problem well-motivated? Is the gap in prior work clearly identified?**  
  Yes. Section 1 clearly motivates why negative prompting matters, especially for few-step distilled models where standard CFG is unstable or unusable. Figure 2 is a useful concrete example of failure under forced CFG. The gap relative to NASA and NAG is also stated clearly: fixed-strength subtraction in attention output space versus a more adaptive mechanism.

- **Are the contributions clearly stated and accurate?**  
  The contributions are stated explicitly at the end of Section 1:
  1. a new negative-guidance method,
  2. a new benchmark dataset,
  3. collected labeled generations and a fine-tuned evaluator.
  
  These are accurate as a description of what the paper contains. However, the third contribution is not as integrated into the main paper as the first two; the evaluator is mostly justified and analyzed in the appendix.

- **Does the introduction over-claim or under-sell?**  
  It somewhat **over-claims generality** relative to the evidence. The introduction frames VSF as broadly applicable to few-step image and video generation models, but the main quantitative story is much narrower: mostly one image backbone family (SD3.5 Turbo), with lighter support for Flux and only qualitative support for Wan.
  
  It also somewhat **understates the evaluation risk**: the paper’s conclusions depend heavily on a synthetic benchmark and model-based judges, which is a material issue for ICLR-level confidence.

### Related Work
- **Are the most relevant baselines and prior work cited?**  
  For the immediate problem setting, yes. The paper discusses CFG, NASA, NAG, and dynamic negative guidance works such as Ban et al. (2024), Schramowski et al. (2023), and Koulischer et al. (2025). That is the right core set for few-step negative guidance.

- **Is the positioning against prior work fair and accurate?**  
  Mostly yes. The characterization that NASA and NAG use fixed-strength subtraction/extrapolation in attention output space, while VSF aims for a more adaptive token-level effect via value flipping, is fair. The discussion in Section 2.4 of NASA and NAG is reasonably balanced.
  
  One caveat: the statement in Section 2.2 that studies on dynamic negative guidance are “very limited (only …)” feels somewhat narrow. Even if direct few-step negative-guidance papers are few, the broader literature on attention-space intervention, safety steering, and prompt-based control is larger than this framing suggests. That does not invalidate the method, but the paper could position itself more carefully in that broader landscape.

- **Are there obvious missing references?**  
  No glaring omission among the most direct baselines. For ICLR, though, I would have liked somewhat clearer positioning relative to the broader family of **attention intervention / prompt editing / diffusion steering** methods, since VSF is also an intervention on attention internals rather than a wholly new training or sampling paradigm.

### Method / Approach
- **Is the method clearly described and reproducible?**  
  The core idea in Section 3.1 is clear and elegant: concatenate positive and negative keys, flip the sign of negative values, and let softmax determine the local strength dynamically (Eq. 9). The Appendix pseudocode is helpful for reproducibility.
  
  That said, there are still some reproducibility gaps for an ICLR submission:
  - The paper does not fully clarify **where** VSF is applied across different architectures: all attention blocks, only joint attention, only text-conditioned blocks, etc.
  - The implementation differences across **SD3.5, Flux, and Wan** are scattered across Appendix G rather than presented centrally.
  - The main method actually used in SD3.5 includes **duplication, masking, bias, and padding removal** (Sections 3.2 and Appendix C), but the exact rationale and interaction of these components are more empirical than principled.

- **Are key assumptions stated and justified?**  
  The main assumption is that **flipping negative prompt values while keeping the keys unchanged** allows the model to match undesired regions and push them away through cancellation. This is plausible and intuitively appealing.
  
  However, the justification is only partial:
  - The proof in Appendix B shows equivalence between Eq. 9 and a weighted subtraction formulation in a simplified cross-attention setting.
  - But the **actual deployed MMDiT version** in Section 3.2 is more complex due to duplicated negative tokens, masking, and bias. That full version is not theoretically analyzed in the same way.
  - The central intuition is therefore supported by algebra for a simplified case, but not for the exact version that drives the main results.

- **Are there logical gaps in the derivation or reasoning?**  
  There is one important ambiguity in Section 3.1:
  - The text introduces **\(W\)** as a **“per-token weight”** in Eq. 6–7.
  - But Eq. 8 defines \(W\) via sums over attention terms, and Appendix B treats it effectively like a scalar normalizer for the positive/negative branches.
  
  As written, it is not fully clear whether \(W\) is meant to be **per query token, per head, per spatial location, or global within an attention call**. That ambiguity matters because the paper’s central adaptivity claim depends on the granularity of \(W\).

- **Are there edge cases or failure modes not discussed?**  
  Some failure cases are discussed in Appendix L, which is good. But for the main paper, a few failure modes deserve more explicit treatment:
  - When the negative concept is **semantically inseparable** from the positive object (e.g., “house without roof”), VSF may either fail to remove it or distort the object.
  - When positive and negative prompts **overlap strongly** (as in the abstract-art examples in Figure 5 and Appendix N), the method may partly erase the main object rather than selectively suppress a property.
  - The paper should discuss more directly when VSF becomes “object deletion” rather than fine-grained negation.

- **For theoretical claims: are proofs correct and complete?**  
  The algebra in Appendix B for the simplified cross-attention case appears fine. The issue is not correctness so much as **scope**: the proof does **not** cover the full MMDiT mechanism used in the main SD3.5 experiments.

### Experiments & Results
- **Do the experiments actually test the paper's claims?**  
  They test the main claim **on one primary model family** (SD3.5 Turbo) and show promising results. Table 2 and Figure 6 suggest VSF offers a better tradeoff between negative adherence and quality than NASA/NAG on the proposed benchmark.
  
  However, for an ICLR paper, the evaluation is not yet strong enough to support all claims:
  - The benchmark (**NegGenBench**, Section 4.1) is **synthetic and author-created**.
  - The main metric uses **LLaMA as judge** (Section 4.2), and the paper later acknowledges nontrivial judge limitations (Appendix D, F, O).
  - Generality claims across **image/video architectures** are not tested with comparable quantitative rigor.

- **Are baselines appropriate and fairly compared?**  
  The baseline selection is reasonable: **NASA, NAG, None, CFG**, plus external systems in Table 1.
  
  Fairness concerns remain:
  - **NASA is reimplemented** because official code was unavailable (Section 4.2). This is understandable, but for ICLR it introduces uncertainty, especially since NASA looks clearly worse in Table 2 and Figure 6.
  - The hyperparameter sweep in Section 6.1 is not obviously balanced: **66 runs for VSF**, **287 for NAG**, and only **10 for NASA**. Since the paper’s main argument is about tradeoff fronts, the tuning budget should be symmetric or better justified.
  - The CFG comparison in Table 2 is with **28 steps**, not the same regime as the few-step models, so it is informative but not a clean apples-to-apples baseline.

- **Are there missing ablations that would materially change conclusions?**  
  The paper does provide meaningful ablations in Section 6.3 and Figure 6 (WEF, no mask, no bias, no duplication). That is a strength.
  
  Still, two additional ablations would materially strengthen the paper:
  - A cleaner comparison between **the simplified Eq. 9 cross-attention VSF** and the **full MMDiT version** with duplication/masking/bias, to isolate which part really drives gains.
  - A stronger quantitative ablation on **which layers/blocks** benefit from VSF, since applying the mechanism everywhere may not be necessary.

- **Are error bars / statistical significance reported?**  
  No. This is a notable weakness by ICLR standards. Table 2 reports point estimates only. Figure 6 has no confidence intervals. Since the main metric is judge-based and the dataset size is modest (**200 prompts × 2 seeds**), some uncertainty quantification is needed.

- **Do the results support the claims made, or are they cherry-picked?**  
  The quantitative results in Table 2 do support the claim that VSF improves negative adherence on the proposed benchmark. The tradeoff plots in Figure 6 are also directionally convincing.
  
  But the paper makes some claims that are stronger than the evidence:
  - **Video support** is not quantitatively demonstrated.
  - **Highest performance among open-source methods** (Table 1) is interesting but depends heavily on heterogeneous systems and the same judge-based metric.
  - Claims about **maintaining quality** should be softened, because the quality scorer is acknowledged to be overly generous (Appendix D, Figure 11), and human quality evaluation is limited.

- **Are datasets and evaluation metrics appropriate?**  
  The dataset idea is relevant: hard positive-negative pairs are exactly the right stress test for this problem. But for ICLR:
  - Relying primarily on a **new synthetic benchmark** without evaluation on at least one external benchmark reduces confidence.
  - The **MLLM-judge protocol** is a weak point. Table 4 shows negative-label F1 of **0.667**, which is not high enough to treat small score differences as decisive.
  - Human validation in Table 3 is too small to fully backstop the automatic metric, and it omits some central methods such as **VSF Strong** and **CFG**.

### Writing & Clarity
- **Are there sections that are confusing or poorly explained?**  
  The paper is generally readable and the high-level idea is easy to follow. Figure 3 and the pseudocode are helpful.
  
  The main clarity issues are conceptual rather than stylistic:
  - Section 3.1 is ambiguous about the granularity of **\(W\)** and how exactly the “adaptive” weighting should be interpreted.
  - The distinction between the **simple derivation in Eq. 9** and the **actual MMDiT mechanism in Section 3.2** is not emphasized enough; these can feel like one method, but the latter introduces several extra design choices.
  - Experimental protocol details crucial for judging fairness—especially **baseline tuning**, **judge reliability**, and **external baseline setup**—are pushed deep into the appendix.

- **Are figures and tables clear and informative?**  
  Mostly yes.
  - **Figure 2** effectively motivates why CFG is problematic in few-step models.
  - **Figure 4** is a useful mechanistic visualization, though it is still more illustrative than diagnostic.
  - **Figure 6** is one of the strongest pieces of evidence in the paper.
  - **Table 2** is central and clear, but it would benefit from uncertainty estimates.
  
  Overall, the paper communicates the main idea well enough, but the evaluation story needs more transparency in the main text for ICLR review standards.

### Limitations & Broader Impact
- **Do the authors acknowledge the key limitations?**  
  Partially. The paper does acknowledge:
  - evaluation limitations of MLLM judges (Appendix D, F, O),
  - some failure cases (Appendix L),
  - incomplete efficiency due to implementation constraints (Appendix H).
  
  These are good signs.

- **Are there fundamental limitations they missed?**  
  A few important ones are not foregrounded enough:
  - The method’s strongest evidence is on **one main model family**; claims of architectural generality are ahead of the evidence.
  - The benchmark is **self-created**, making independent calibration difficult.
  - The main evaluation depends on **model-based judges with imperfect negation understanding**, which is especially problematic because the paper’s contribution is itself about negation.

- **Are there failure modes or negative societal impacts not discussed?**  
  The broader impact discussion is underdeveloped for ICLR expectations.
  - On the positive side, VSF could improve **content control, safety, moderation, and user customization**.
  - On the risk side, a stronger negative-guidance method could be used to **circumvent intended generation constraints**, suppress safety-relevant concepts, or facilitate style/content removal in ways that raise policy or rights questions.  
  The appendix touches on style avoidance and “anti-aesthetics,” but there is no serious discussion of when such control is beneficial versus when it could be misused.

### Overall Assessment
This is a neat and plausible idea: the core mechanism in Eq. 9 is simple, intuitive, and likely useful, and the SD3.5 results suggest there is a real signal here. The paper also includes thoughtful ablations and some effort toward mechanistic interpretation. However, against the **ICLR bar**, the work currently feels **empirically under-validated for the breadth of its claims**. The main weaknesses are the reliance on a self-constructed benchmark, heavy dependence on imperfect MLLM judges, limited human validation, unclear fairness around some baselines (especially the NASA reimplementation), lack of uncertainty estimates, and only light quantitative evidence beyond the main image model. Because the methodological novelty is moderate rather than major, ICLR would typically expect especially strong and rigorous experiments; that is the main gap. My view is that the contribution is promising and probably real, but the paper would need stronger evaluation and tighter calibration of its claims to clear a confident ICLR acceptance threshold.

## Balanced Review

### Summary
This paper proposes Value Sign Flip (VSF), a simple negative-guidance mechanism for few-step diffusion/flow-matching generation that flips the sign of negative-prompt values inside attention rather than applying CFG-style extrapolation at the output. The paper also introduces a new benchmark, NegGenBench, and reports comparisons against NAG, NASA, CFG, and several proprietary/open-weight image generators, claiming improved negative-prompt adherence with minimal quality loss and low runtime overhead.

### Strengths
1. **Practical problem with clear relevance to ICLR**
   - The paper tackles a real limitation in modern few-step generative models: negative prompting is often ineffective or destabilizing under CFG-style guidance. This is an important practical issue for controllable generation, and the paper motivates it well with examples and comparison to recent methods.

2. **Simple mechanism with an intuitive implementation**
   - VSF is conceptually straightforward: flip the sign of negative-prompt values in attention while leaving keys intact, plus optional masking/duplication for MMDiT-style architectures. The implementation appears lightweight and compatible with several architectures, including SD3.5-style MMDiT, Flux Schnell, and Wan, which strengthens the practical appeal.

3. **Architecture-aware adaptation**
   - The paper does more than propose a single formula; it explains how the method must be adapted for cross-attention models versus MMDiT-style models, and it provides pseudocode and an equivalence proof in the appendix. That attention to architectural details is valuable for reproducibility and deployment.

4. **Evidence of broad applicability**
   - The authors test VSF on both image and video generation models and also on style avoidance / anti-aesthetics settings. This suggests the method may generalize beyond the narrow “remove object X” setting, which is a plus for significance.

5. **A benchmark and human cross-checks are included**
   - The paper contributes NegGenBench and reports human validation alongside MLLM-based evaluation. While imperfect, the authors at least attempt to triangulate results with more than one evaluator, which is better than relying solely on a single automatic metric.

### Weaknesses
1. **Novelty is somewhat incremental relative to prior guidance work**
   - The core idea—modulating attention with negative guidance and adapting strength over time/space—is closely related to NASA, NAG, and prior dynamic guidance work. VSF’s main move is sign-flipping values and adding masking/duplication in MMDiT, which is clever but may be viewed as a relatively small algorithmic step rather than a major conceptual advance expected for ICLR.
   - The paper itself notes that VSF is equivalent to token-weighted subtraction; that equivalence makes the method elegant, but also raises the question of whether the method is fundamentally new or mainly a reparameterization.

2. **Benchmark and evaluation are vulnerable to bias and overfitting**
   - NegGenBench is constructed with prompts generated by ChatGPT o3, and the paper’s main evaluation relies heavily on MLLM judges that also generate the questions. This creates a risk that the benchmark and evaluator share similar biases or prompt styles.
   - Because the benchmark is custom and not yet public, it is hard to assess whether the gains reflect genuine robustness or optimization to the dataset/evaluator pipeline.

3. **Automatic evaluation is not fully convincing for the claimed quality claims**
   - The paper acknowledges that LLaMA Maverick can overestimate quality, and later introduces NegAwareQwen trained partly on its own generated data. This makes the evaluation stack somewhat circular: generated samples are scored by models whose judgment is being adapted to this exact task.
   - Human validation is limited to only 10 prompts with 2 seeds, which is too small to support strong conclusions about quality/negation trade-offs.

4. **Experimental comparisons are not always apples-to-apples**
   - Some baselines are re-tuned for stronger negation, while others are compared under different settings, and external baselines include proprietary models with different generation interfaces and runtimes. This makes the comparison useful but not fully controlled.
   - For example, the paper compares VSF to GPT-4o, Nano Banana, Janus-4o, and Qwen-Image, but these systems differ substantially in architecture, training data, and objective; the reported ranking is informative but not definitive.

5. **Claims of superior quality are not strongly substantiated**
   - The paper repeatedly claims competitive or higher quality, but the quality metric itself is based on MLLM judgments that the authors admit are imperfect and sometimes uncorrelated with human judgments in the high-quality regime.
   - The “quality score” can be hard to interpret quantitatively, especially when the paper also states that scores below 0.9 may already indicate degradation. This weakens the strength of claims about preserving image quality.

6. **Some important ablations remain incomplete**
   - The paper does include ablations for masking, duplication, attention bias, and whole-embedding flip, but the analysis is not exhaustive. In particular, it would be helpful to isolate which component drives most of the gains and whether simpler token-level weighting methods can match VSF under carefully tuned settings.
   - The method’s sensitivity to prompts, model families, step counts, and negative-prompt length is only partially explored.

7. **Reproducibility is only moderate at present**
   - The paper says code, dataset, and models will be released after publication, but they are not available in the submission. Because results depend on proprietary models, custom evaluation prompts, and a finetuned evaluator, independent reproduction will be difficult until full artifacts are released.
   - Several implementation details are deferred to the appendix, and exact comparison settings across models are not always fully standardized in the main text.

### Novelty & Significance
From an ICLR perspective, the paper is **practical and well-motivated**, but the novelty appears **moderate rather than strong**. VSF is a neat and potentially useful adaptation of negative guidance to few-step and MMDiT-style models, yet it is close in spirit to prior attention-space guidance methods and, as written, may not clearly clear the higher novelty bar ICLR typically expects for acceptance unless the empirical advantages are confirmed more rigorously.

In terms of significance, the problem is important and the method could be valuable for real-world controllable generation. However, the strength of the empirical evidence is tempered by custom evaluation, limited human annotation, and reliance on model-based judges, so the paper’s claims currently feel stronger than the evidence fully supports.

### Suggestions for Improvement
1. **Strengthen the evaluation with a larger, independently annotated human study**
   - Expand human evaluation well beyond 10 prompts and include multiple annotators with agreement statistics. This would provide a more credible assessment of both negative adherence and quality, especially for the challenging “missing essential component” cases.

2. **Release NegGenBench and the evaluation protocol before publication or in an artifact appendix**
   - Provide the full prompt set, generation settings, judging prompts, and annotation rubric. Since the benchmark is central to the paper’s claims, reproducibility depends on transparent access to the exact evaluation pipeline.

3. **Add stronger, more controlled baselines**
   - Compare VSF against carefully tuned variants of simpler token-weighting or attention-scaling approaches, not only NAG/NASA. This would clarify whether the gains come from the sign flip itself, the masking/duplication strategy, or merely better hyperparameter tuning.

4. **Report more rigorous statistical analysis**
   - Include confidence intervals, significance tests, and per-prompt variance across seeds. Since the benchmark is small and the method is stochastic, statistical reliability would materially improve the credibility of the reported gains.

5. **Clarify the scope of the quality claims**
   - Distinguish clearly between “quality as judged by MLLM,” “human preference,” and “visual realism.” If the method intentionally creates unusual outputs such as abstract or anti-aesthetic art, the paper should separate these from standard quality metrics rather than treating them uniformly.

6. **Deepen the ablation study**
   - Quantify the contribution of each component: sign flip, duplication, masking, bias, padding removal, and negative-prompt length handling. A cleaner component-wise analysis would make it easier to understand what is essential and what is incidental.

7. **Discuss limitations more candidly**
   - The paper would benefit from a more explicit discussion of failure modes, especially cases where semantically entangled concepts, strong co-occurrence priors, or ambiguous negative prompts still defeat VSF. This would make the contribution more balanced and aligned with ICLR expectations for rigorous empirical work.

## Strengthening Opportunities

### Missing Experiments
1. **A broader benchmark suite beyond NegGenBench would make the empirical case much stronger for ICLR.** In addition to the proposed dataset, the paper would benefit from evaluation on established instruction-following and compositional T2I benchmarks such as **T2I-CompBench**, **TIIF-Bench**, and prior **negation-focused CLIP/T2I benchmarks** (e.g., benchmarks used by NegationCLIP / “Know No Better”). This would show whether VSF’s gains transfer from the authors’ challenging part-removal setting to broader negation and instruction-following scenarios.

2. **A larger-scale human study would substantially strengthen the evaluation story.** For ICLR, a study with **multiple raters per image**, **pairwise A/B preferences**, and **confidence intervals** over the full 200-prompt benchmark (or a stratified subset) would be especially valuable. It would directly validate the core claim that VSF improves negative adherence without unacceptable quality loss, which is central given the known brittleness of MLLM-as-a-judge.

3. **More model diversity would strengthen the “universal” claim.** The paper already tests SD3.5 Turbo, Flux Schnell, and Wan/CausVid; it would be even stronger with experiments on additional few-step backbones such as **SDXL Lightning**, **PixArt/DiT variants**, or other **1-step / 2-step distilled models**. That would help show whether VSF is broadly useful across architectures, text encoders, and distillation recipes rather than primarily effective for the chosen backbones.

4. **A step-count sweep would be very informative.** Since the paper’s positioning is few-step generation, an experiment varying **1 / 2 / 4 / 8 steps** on the same backbone would clarify where VSF helps most relative to NASA, NAG, and forced CFG. This would show whether the benefit grows as step count becomes more constrained, which is an important ICLR-style empirical insight.

5. **Category-level analysis on NegGenBench would add rigor.** The dataset appears to include multiple challenge types: essential-part removal, style avoidance, attribute suppression, and more abstract anti-aesthetic cases. Reporting performance broken down by categories such as **part-whole negation**, **co-occurring object suppression**, **style negation**, **scene attribute negation**, and **high semantic entanglement prompts** would show where VSF is most effective.

6. **Baselines from related dynamic-guidance work would make the comparison more complete.** The paper would be stronger with implementations or approximations of **temporal-scheduling negative guidance** from Ban et al. and **dynamic step-wise scaling** from Koulischer et al., even if adapted imperfectly to few-step settings. This would help isolate whether VSF’s main advantage comes from token-level adaptivity, value-space intervention, or simply better guidance scheduling.

7. **Matched-compute comparisons would be useful.** Since efficiency is a key claim, it would help to compare methods at **equal wall-clock budget** or **equal number of attention evaluations**, especially for VSF vs NASA/NAG vs generate-then-edit. A latency-quality-negative-adherence Pareto plot would align well with ICLR expectations for methods pitched as both simple and efficient.

8. **Seed robustness experiments would strengthen reliability.** The main table uses 2 seeds; expanding to **5–10 seeds on a representative subset** and reporting mean/std would help demonstrate that VSF’s gains are not seed-specific, especially in a stochastic generation setting.

9. **A control experiment with irrelevant or weakly related negative prompts would be valuable.** For example, using prompts where the negative concept is absent or unrelated (“a bicycle” with negative prompt “moon”) would test whether VSF preserves quality and positive adherence when suppression should ideally do little. This would clarify whether VSF is selectively suppressive rather than broadly disruptive.

10. **A comparison against training-based negation improvements would be insightful.** Even if not few-step-native, combining VSF with **negation-aware text encoders** or **prompt encoder adaptations** would show whether the gains are complementary to representation-side approaches, which could broaden the paper’s impact.

### Deeper Analysis Needed
1. **A more formal derivation of how VSF relates to NASA and NAG would deepen the contribution.** The paper already proves equivalence to a token-weighted subtraction form; it would be even stronger to explicitly show when VSF reduces to **NASA-like constant weighting**, how it differs from **NAG’s normalized extrapolation**, and why the adaptive weighting emerges naturally from the shared softmax denominator.

2. **A perturbation/norm analysis of the attention output would help explain stability.** Since one motivation is that forced CFG causes oversaturation while VSF remains stable, it would be valuable to derive bounds or approximations on the change in attention output under sign flipping, especially compared with NASA/NAG. Even a first-order analysis could clarify why VSF tends to avoid the harsher distortions of output-space extrapolation.

3. **A layer-wise and timestep-wise analysis would greatly help understanding.** The paper would be stronger with measurements of where VSF is actually doing work: e.g., average negative-attention mass, suppression strength, or performance contribution by **early vs late layers**, **early vs late denoising steps**, and **different attention blocks**. This would help readers understand whether VSF acts mainly as early semantic steering or late local suppression.

4. **A head/token-level analysis could reveal whether VSF is semantically localized.** Measuring which attention heads or token groups carry most of the suppressive effect could support the claim that VSF adaptively targets undesired content. For ICLR, this kind of mechanistic analysis often meaningfully elevates a methods paper.

5. **An explicit analysis of computational complexity would sharpen the efficiency claim.** The runtime table is useful; adding exact complexity in terms of sequence lengths—for example, the added cost from duplicating negative tokens and masking—would make the argument more principled. It would also help separate algorithmic overhead from implementation overhead due to FlashAttention limitations.

6. **A stronger study of hyperparameter sensitivity would improve practical clarity.** The current α/β analysis is helpful; it would be even more informative to report **robust operating regions**, default settings that transfer across models, and whether α scales predictably with prompt length, step count, or model size. This is especially important for ICLR, where reviewers often ask whether a method is easy to tune.

7. **An error taxonomy would deepen the paper substantially.** The failure cases suggest several distinct modes: semantic entanglement, object deformation, statue-like substitutions, and missed suppression. Grouping failures into a taxonomy and quantifying their frequency would make the contribution more insightful and actionable.

8. **Dataset analysis for NegGenBench would strengthen the benchmark itself as a contribution.** Reporting prompt statistics such as **object categories**, **negative prompt lengths**, **part-whole relation types**, **lexical overlap**, **semantic hardness**, and **prompt generation templates** would make the dataset more reusable and better aligned with ICLR expectations for benchmark contributions.

9. **Judge-model reliability analysis could be expanded.** Since evaluation is central, it would help to compare LLaMA judge, NegAwareQwen, and human ratings on the same held-out subset using **rank correlation**, **pairwise agreement**, and **confidence intervals**. This would make the evaluation foundation much more convincing.

10. **A theoretical discussion of why value-only sign flipping is preferable to key/value flipping would be illuminating.** The WEF ablation hints at this; a more explicit explanation in terms of matching vs anti-matching would help readers understand the design choice and likely generalize the insight to other architectures.

### Untapped Applications
1. **Safety and moderation use cases could be explored more directly.** VSF seems naturally suited for **NSFW suppression**, **violence avoidance**, **logo/watermark removal**, or **copyright-style avoidance** at inference time. Testing on a moderation-oriented prompt set would broaden the impact beyond creative control.

2. **Video generation deserves a more complete treatment.** The Wan examples are promising; a stronger ICLR version would include quantitative video results on metrics such as **temporal consistency**, **frame-level negative adherence**, and **flicker under suppression**, perhaps on a curated video negation benchmark.

3. **Image editing and inpainting are natural extensions.** Because VSF already behaves like a lightweight suppressive editing mechanism, experiments in **masked inpainting**, **instruction-based image editing**, or **object removal with text control** would showcase an especially practical application.

4. **Bias mitigation and controllable de-stereotyping would be a compelling direction.** For prompts involving professions, demographics, or culturally stereotyped co-occurrences, VSF could test whether unwanted associations can be reduced at inference time. That would connect the work to a broader ICLR audience interested in responsible generation.

5. **Scientific or medical imaging could be a useful high-precision setting.** In domains where exclusion matters—e.g., “cell without nucleus staining artifact” or “x-ray without support device”—VSF-style suppression could offer a compelling controlled-generation tool, even if only as a preliminary transfer experiment.

6. **Typography and layout control may be another strong application.** Negative prompts like “without watermark,” “without extra text,” or “without logo” are very practical. Demonstrating gains there would appeal to users of modern multimodal generation systems.

7. **Training-time unlearning or concept erasure could be linked more directly.** The style-avoidance results suggest that VSF may serve as an inference-time alternative or complement to model editing / concept erasure methods. Exploring that connection could significantly expand the paper’s relevance.

### Visualizations & Case Studies
1. **More systematic attention visualizations would help readers understand when VSF works.** The current example is useful; it would be even stronger to show **successful and failed cases**, across **multiple layers and timesteps**, with overlays on the generated image indicating where negative-prompt attention concentrates.

2. **A side-by-side trajectory comparison with NASA/NAG would be very valuable.** Showing intermediate denoising states for the same seed under all three methods would make the mechanism concrete: Does VSF suppress undesired content earlier, more locally, or with less collateral distortion?

3. **Case studies on semantic entanglement would be especially informative.** Prompts like “house without roof” or “cat without whiskers” are where the problem is hardest. A focused analysis of such prompts could reveal whether VSF succeeds by replacement, omission, abstraction, or structural deformation.

5. **Prompt-to-outcome scatter plots could reveal useful patterns.** For example, plotting negative score against prompt properties such as **negative token length**, **part-whole frequency**, **object familiarity**, or **positive/negative lexical overlap** could identify conditions under which VSF is especially strong.

### Natural Next Steps
1. **An adaptive VSF variant with learned or scheduled α/β would be a strong next step.** Since the paper already motivates adaptivity, learning guidance strength per **layer, timestep, or token** could further improve quality-negative trade-offs while keeping the core idea intact.

2. **Combining VSF with NAG-style normalization could be promising.** A hybrid that preserves VSF’s adaptive suppression while adding norm control may further stabilize extreme settings and could be an attractive follow-up for ICLR-level depth.

3. **A fused attention kernel would directly strengthen the efficiency contribution.** Implementing VSF in a custom FlashAttention-compatible kernel could convert the paper’s algorithmic efficiency into clear practical speedups, which would make the “simple and efficient” claim even more compelling.

4. **A more comprehensive public benchmark and judge model release could become a community resource.** NegGenBench plus NegAwareQwen is already a strong start; formalizing it as a benchmark with standard splits, categories, and evaluation protocols would increase the paper’s long-term impact.

5. **Extending VSF to non-attention or autoregressive generators is a natural research direction.** The paper mentions Janus-4o/Qwen-Image; pursuing a generalized formulation for multimodal autoregressive models could turn VSF from an attention trick into a broader principle for negative control.

6. **Inference-time suppression for concept editing or model unlearning could be developed further.** A next paper could position VSF as a practical alternative to expensive retraining-based unlearning, especially for fast deployment scenarios.

7. **A training-aware version of VSF could be interesting.** Distilling models with VSF-like behavior baked in, or training them to anticipate sign-flipped negative values, may improve both robustness and speed in ultra-low-step generation.

8. **Video-native VSF is an especially promising follow-up.** Extending the method to account for temporal attention, consistency, and object persistence under suppression could yield a strong standalone contribution for future work.

9. **Automatic prompt rewriting paired with VSF could improve usability.** Since negation phrasing is often ambiguous, coupling VSF with an LLM-based prompt normalizer that rewrites “no X” into a clearer suppressive description may further improve adherence in real-world use.

10. **A principled study of when inference-time negative guidance should replace training-time alignment would broaden the paper’s significance.** Framing VSF as part of a larger design space—prompt engineering, inference-time control, post-editing, and retraining—could make the work more influential for the ICLR audience.
