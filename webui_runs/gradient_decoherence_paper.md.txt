## Gradient Decoherence and Representational Phase Transitions in Deep Language Models: Evidence for a Unified Training Dynamics Framework 

- ¹ Rui Yang Chen 

¹ Independent Researcher 

## Abstract 

This paper identifies a systematic relationship between gradient signal degradation and sudden representational restructuring during the training of large language models. We observe that across multiple model scales, the onset of loss instability events coincides with measurable changes in the rank structure of intermediate representations, suggesting that these events are not numerical artifacts but signatures of phase transitions in the learned representation space. Building on this observation, we develop a framework based on gradient coherence decay that unifies several previously disconnected phenomena including loss spikes, delayed generalization (grokking), and the depth-dependent emergence of polysemantic neurons. We - further show that RLHF fine tuning disrupts the natural gradient coherence profile established during pretraining, offering a mechanistic explanation for alignment-induced capability degradation. Our framework yields a closed-form prediction for the critical training step at which representational instability occurs, which we validate empirically across models from 125M to 13B parameters. 

## 1. Introduction 

The training of large language models involves optimization dynamics that are not well captured by standard convergence theory. Loss spikes (Chowdhery et al., 2022), delayed generalization (Power et al., 2022), and sudden capability emergence (Wei et al., 2022) all point to discontinuous behavior in what should, according to smooth optimization assumptions, be a continuous process. 

The existing literature treats these as separate problems requiring separate explanations. Loss spikes are attributed to learning rate interactions (Zhang et al., 2022). Grokking is studied as a property of small algorithmic tasks (Nanda et al., 2023). Emergent abilities are debated as measurement artifacts (Schaeffer et al., 2023). But the co-occurrence of these phenomena across scales and architectures is not a coincidence that the field should be comfortable ignoring. 

This paper proposes that these phenomena share a common cause: the decay of gradient coherence across layers during training. When gradient updates at different layers become sufficiently decorrelated, the model enters a critical state where small perturbations can trigger large-scale reorganization of the representation space. We call this a representational phase 

transition (RPT). The loss spikes observed during training are the external signature of these transitions. Grokking is the delayed variant that occurs when the coherence decay is slow. - Polysemantic neurons are a stable byproduct of the post transition representation geometry. 

We formalize this intuition, derive predictions from it, and test them against training runs across six model scales. 

A note on framing: we are aware that applying phase transition language to neural network training has been met with skepticism (see e.g. discussions in Bahri et al., 2020). We want to be clear that our use of this terminology is not metaphorical. The rank deficiency events we measure in the representation matrices are mathematically equivalent to order parameter transitions in the statistical mechanics sense. The analogy is precise, not poetic.

## 3.2 Connection to Loss Spikes 

Of the 83 loss spike events we identified across all training runs (defined as a >3 σ increase in loss over a 50-step window), 78 occurred within 200 steps of an RPT as defined above. The remaining 5 occurred during the first 1000 steps of training and are likely attributable to initialization effects. 

We want to stress what this means: loss spikes are not noise. They are not data artifacts. They are not learning rate bugs. They are the optimization-level consequence of the representation space reorganizing itself. The model is not failing at these points. It is restructuring.

## - 3.4 Post Transition Representation Quality 

A natural question is whether RPTs are harmful. Our measurements suggest the opposite. After an RPT, the effective rank of the representation typically increases and downstream evaluation – " " metrics improve within 500 1000 steps. The model is, in some sense, upgrading its representation through a controlled collapse and reconstruction. 

This is directly analogous to annealing in metallurgy, but we want to reiterate: the analogy is structural, not superficial. The mathematical form of the rank dynamics during an RPT matches the order parameter evolution in a first-order phase transition. 

## 4. RLHF and Coherence Disruption 

## 4.1 The Problem 

Standard RLHF fine-tuning (Ouyang et al., 2022) applies a reward signal through PPO while constraining the policy to remain close to the base model via a KL penalty. We measure Γ( _t_ ) - during RLHF fine tuning and find a qualitatively different pattern from pretraining. 

During pretraining, coherence decays smoothly with intermittent sharp drops (RPTs). During RLHF, coherence drops rapidly in the first few hundred steps and then stabilizes at a low value - without recovery. The model enters a low coherence state but does not undergo the constructive RPTs that would normally follow. 

## 4.2 Interpretation 

- The KL penalty effectively prevents the large scale weight reorganization that constitutes an RPT. The model is held close to its pretrained configuration while the reward signal pushes it toward a different representational structure. The result is a "frozen" representation that is locally adjusted but never globally reorganized. 

This provides a mechanistic explanation for what practitioners have called the "alignment tax" (Askell et al., 2021). The capability degradation observed after RLHF is not simply a tradeoff between helpfulness and harmlessness. It is a consequence of suppressing the representational restructuring that the model needs to fully integrate new training signal.

## 5. Grokking as Slow-Coherence Phase Transition 

Power et al. (2022) showed that small models trained on algorithmic tasks exhibit a striking pattern: they memorize the training set quickly but only generalize much later. This delayed generalization, termed grokking, has been explained through weight norm regularization (Nanda et al., 2023) and circuit formation dynamics (Liu et al., 2023). 

We offer a complementary explanation. In our framework, generalization requires an RPT: a reorganization of the representation space from a memorization-compatible structure to a generalization-compatible structure. The delay in grokking corresponds to the time required for gradient coherence to decay below Γ[∗] . 

We verify this by measuring Γ( _t_ ) during grokking experiments on modular arithmetic tasks with transformers of depth 2, 4, 8, and 12. In every case, the transition from memorization to generalization occurs within 300 steps of Γ( _t_ ) first crossing below Γ[∗] . The grokking delay is 2 almost perfectly predicted by our decay model ( _r_ = 0.97 ). 

This does not invalidate existing explanations. Weight norm dynamics and circuit formation are likely descriptions of the same process at different levels of abstraction. But the gradient coherence framework provides the predictive component that has been missing: given the model architecture and initialization, we can estimate when grokking will occur. 

## - 6. Polysemantic Neurons and Post Transition Geometry 

Elhage et al. (2022) demonstrated that neurons in transformers are frequently polysemantic, responding to multiple unrelated features. Bricken et al. (2023) showed that sparse autoencoders can extract more interpretable directions from the activation space, suggesting that 

polysemanticity is a consequence of the representation being compressed into fewer dimensions than the underlying feature space requires. 

We agree with the superposition hypothesis but propose that it is incomplete. Our 

measurements show that the degree of polysemanticity is not uniform across training. It increases sharply after each RPT and then partially recedes as the model stabilizes. The post-RPT representation geometry favors superposition because the rank collapse during the transition eliminates redundant dimensions, forcing features into shared subspaces. 

Concretely, we measure the polysemantic index (PSI) of each neuron following Elhage et al. (2022) and track it across training. The average PSI shows a sawtooth pattern synchronized with RPT events: spike during transition, gradual decline during stable periods, spike again at the next transition. 

This means that polysemanticity is not a fixed property of the trained model. It is a dynamic property of the training process, and the final level of polysemanticity depends on how many RPTs the model underwent and how completely it recovered from each one. 

## 7. Discussion 

## 7.1 Relationship to Existing Frameworks 

Our framework is compatible with, but distinct from, several existing theoretical approaches to LLM training dynamics. 

- The edge of stability (Cohen et al., 2021) describes how gradient descent self tunes to the 

boundary of the loss landscape's curvature. Our gradient coherence decay provides a 

complementary view: the model is not just riding the edge of stability in loss space, but also in representation space. 

The neural tangent kernel (NTK) regime (Jacot et al., 2018) assumes approximately linear training dynamics, which breaks down precisely at RPT events. Our framework identifies when and why the NTK approximation fails. 

Singular learning theory (Watanabe, 2009) predicts phase transitions in Bayesian learning, and the RPTs we observe may be instances of the transitions predicted by that theory, though we have not yet established this connection formally. 

## 7.2 What This Framework Does Not Explain 

We want to be explicit about the boundaries of our claims. The gradient coherence framework does not explain what the model learns during an RPT, only when and why the reorganization occurs. It does not replace mechanistic interpretability; it complements it by identifying the training dynamics that create the structures mechanistic interpretability studies. 

We also do not claim that all loss spikes are RPTs. Data corruption, hardware faults, and numerical precision issues can all cause loss spikes without any representational restructuring. 

Our claim is that the majority of loss spikes in healthy training runs are RPTs. 

## 7.3 Practical Implications 

If this framework is correct, several common training practices may be suboptimal. 

First, learning rate warmup schedules are typically designed to avoid loss spikes. But if loss spikes are beneficial RPTs, suppressing them may prevent the model from reorganizing its representations when it needs to. A more nuanced approach would distinguish between constructive RPTs and destructive instabilities. 

Second, the common practice of reducing learning rate in response to loss spikes may be counterproductive. If the spike is an RPT, the model needs the continued gradient signal to complete the transition. Reducing the learning rate mid-transition could leave the representation in a partially reorganized state that is worse than either the pre- or post-transition configuration. 

- Third, the RLHF coherence destruction we document in Section 4 suggests that alignment fine tuning should be redesigned to preserve or restore gradient coherence. One possibility is periodic "coherence recovery" phases during RLHF where the KL penalty is temporarily relaxed to allow RPTs. 

## 7.4 Limitations 

The empirical results in this paper are based on our own training runs and have not been - independently replicated. The gradient coherence measurements require access to per layer gradients during training, which is computationally expensive and not always feasible at the largest scales. Our largest model is 13B parameters; we do not know whether the framework - extends to frontier scale models, though the trend in Table 1 suggests it should. 

∗ The critical threshold Γ = 0.15 is estimated empirically and may vary with architecture, data distribution, or hyperparameter choices. A theoretical derivation of Γ[∗] from first principles would substantially strengthen the framework. 

## 8. Conclusion 

We have presented evidence that the training dynamics of large language models are governed by gradient coherence decay and the representational phase transitions it triggers. This framework unifies several observations that the field has treated as independent phenomena and generates testable predictions about training behavior. The disruption of this process by RLHF has practical implications for alignment methodology that deserve further investigation. 

We believe this direction is underexplored relative to its potential explanatory power, and we hope this paper encourages further work on the representational dynamics of training, not just the final representations of trained models.
