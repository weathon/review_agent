ABSTRACT


The accuracy-first perspective of differential privacy addresses an important shortcoming by allowing a data analyst to adaptively adjust the quantitative privacy
bound instead of sticking to a predetermined bound. Existing works on the accuracyfirst perspective have neglected an important property of differential privacy known
as post-processing immunity, which ensures that an adversary is not able to weaken
the privacy guarantee by post-processing. We address this gap by determining
which existing definitions in the accuracy-first perspective have post-processing
immunity, and which do not. The only definition with post-processing immunity,
pure ex-post privacy, lacks useful tools for practical problems, such as an ex-post
analogue of the Gaussian mechanism, and an algorithm to check if accuracy on
separate private validation set is high enough. To address this, we propose a new
definition based on R√©nyi differential privacy that has post-processing immunity,
and we develop basic theory and tools needed for practical applications. We
demonstrate the practicality of our theory with an application to synthetic data
generation, where our algorithm successfully adjusts the privacy bound until an
accuracy threshold is met on a private validation dataset.


1 INTRODUCTION


As the volume of data collection and capabilities of machine learning models increase, the need for
methods to use the data while maintaining the privacy of data subjects grows ever larger. Differential
privacy (DP; Dwork et al., 2006b) has become the gold-standard definition of privacy to address this
need.

However, differential privacy is a very strict definition. First, it requires protection against a worst-case
adversary with very strong capabilities, which necessitates necessitates very strong protection that can
heavily decrease the utility of an analysis. Second, it requires specifying a numerical privacy bound
before running the algorithm, which precludes workflows such as reducing the privacy bound over
time in case the data becomes less sensitive, using different bounds for different audiences (Rosenblatt
et al., 2024), and addressing low utility by decreasing the privacy protection afterwards (Ligett et al.,
2017; Nanayakkara et al., 2024).

Motivated by these issues, Ligett et al. (2017) proposed the _accuracy-first_ perspective to DP, where
the analyst sets a minimum utility (accuracy) threshold, and finds the strongest privacy bound that
empirically meets the threshold. As the privacy bound now depends on the private data, standard
DP definitions, known as _ex-ante_ definitions, are not applicable. Instead, this perspective requires a
new type of privacy definition that allows the privacy bound to depend on the private data, known as
_ex-post privacy_ . Ligett et al. (2017) proposed the first ex-post privacy definition, pure ex-post privacy,
and developed a _Laplace noise reduction_ mechanism that satisfies their definition. In subsequent work,
Whitehouse et al. (2022) proposed a probabilistic relaxation, and developed the _Brownian mechanism_,
an analogue of the Gaussian mechanism (Dwork et al., 2006a) of ex-ante DP providing higher utility.
In parallel to our work, Ghazi et al. (2025) developed a hyperparameter tuning algorithm for the
accuracy-first perspective, and proposed ex-post privacy definitions based on _approximate DP_ (ADP,
Dwork et al., 2006a) and _R√©nyi DP_ (RDP; Mironov, 2017).

However, these works have neglected an important aspect that any privacy definition should have,
known as _post-processing_ _immunity_ (Dwork & Roth, 2014). Post-processing immunity is the


1


requirement that any processing of the output of a private algorithm cannot decrease the privacy
guarantee. It is clear why any sensible privacy definition (Mironov, 2017; Dong et al., 2022) should
have post-processing immunity: without it, an adversary could use post-processing to infer private
information more easily than promised, making the guarantee weaker than promised (Meiser, 2018).


**Contributions** Our goal is to address this gap by determining whether previous ex-post privacy definitions have post-processing immunity, and by developing additional theory that supports mechanisms
for practical problems under ex-post privacy guarantees with post-processing immunity.


1. We propose a new notation for ex-post privacy definitions that makes the promised privacy bound
a part of the output of the algorithm, instead of making it a separate function of the output. The
new notation allows defining post-processing immunity in an elegant way, while we were not able
to find a satisfactory definition with the old notation, and neither has any previous work (Ligett
et al., 2017; Whitehouse et al., 2022; Ghazi et al., 2025) that we know of. We then formulate the
existing definitions to use our new notation. See Section 3.1 for details.

2. After the translation to the new notation, we prove that the pure ex-post privacy definition of Ligett
et al. (2017) has post-processing immunity, while the probabilistic relaxation of Whitehouse et al.
(2022) does not have it. This motivates the development of an alternative relaxation that both has
post-processing immunity, and supports the Brownian mechanism. See Section 3.2 for details,
and Table 1 for a summary.

3. We investigate an ex-post privacy definition based on RDP, which has been proposed in parallel by
Ghazi et al. (2025). We prove that this definition has post-processing immunity, along with many
basic properties that are useful in constructing practical mechanisms. In particular, we provide an
algorithm that checks whether accuracy on a private validation dataset is high enough, whereas
previous works only provide algorithms to check accuracy on the training set (Ligett et al., 2017;
Whitehouse et al., 2022). See Section 4.

4. We prove that the Brownian mechanism satisfies ex-post RDP in Section 5. This is the first time
the Brownian mechanism has been proven to satisfy an ex-post definition with post-processing
immunity. Our proof uses a more elementary view of the Brownian mechanism than Whitehouse
et al. (2022), that does not require working with stochastic processes, which may be of interest to
future work.

5. We give a practical example of using the Brownian mechanism and our theory in Section 6. We
generate synthetic data, and ensure that the accuracy of a classifier trained on the synthetic data
on a private validation dataset is high enough on a minimal privacy budget. We find that our
implementation is able to achieve high accuracy while keeping a tight privacy budget. We give
another example in Appendix E, where we fine-tune an image classifier, ensuring a minimum
accuracy while minimising the privacy budget.


1.1 RELATED WORK


The closest related works are those by Ligett et al. (2017), Whitehouse et al. (2022) and Ghazi et al.
(2025) that have proposed several ex-post privacy definitions. Follow-up work (Rogers et al., 2023)
has studied compositions involving ex-post private mechanisms. None of these have proven that any
ex-post privacy definition has post-processing immunity.

Another line of related work is known as privacy odometers (Rogers et al., 2016). A privacy
odometer allows running a sequence of mechanisms with adaptively selected privacy bounds and
keeps track of the cumulative privacy budget that has been spent. Odometers have been developed for
several DP definitions (Rogers et al., 2016; Feldman & Zrnic, 2021; L√©cuyer, 2021; Koskela et al.,
2023; Whitehouse et al., 2023) and more general sequences of mechanisms (Haney et al., 2023).
While odometers do not provide ex-ante DP guarantees, they can provide ex-post guarantees. See
Appendix A for more details.

3 POST-PROCESSING IN EX-POST PRIVACY


In ex-ante DP, post-processing immunity requires that processing the output of a DP mechanism
without further access to the input data cannot weaken the DP guarantee (Dwork & Roth, 2014). Any
sensible privacy definition should have post-processing immunity (Mironov, 2017; Dong et al., 2022),
since if an adversary can weaken a privacy guarantee by simply post-processing, that guarantee was
not real in the first place (Meiser, 2018). Indeed, common ex-ante DP definitions have post-processing
immunity (Dwork et al., 2006a;b; Bun & Steinke, 2016; Mironov, 2017; Dong et al., 2022).

In addition, post-processing immunity is extremely useful in constructing DP mechanisms to solve
practical problems, as it allows choosing an intermediate quantity to release with a simple DP
mechanism, and using that to solve the problem without needing to consider DP further. Many DP
algorithms for solving complex problems, such as DP-SGD (Rajkumar & Agarwal, 2012; Song et al.,
2013; Abadi et al., 2016), and DP synthetic data generation (Hardt et al., 2012), use this design.

In contrast to ex-ante DP, we are not aware of prior work proving that any ex-post privacy definition
has post-processing immunity. To rectify this problem, we start with a very basic issue: the notation
used in ex-post privacy definitions (Section 3.1). We then determine whether pure ex-post privacy
and _ќі_ -probabilistic ex-post privacy have post-processing immunity (Section 3.2). See Table 1 for a
summary of the results.

5 EX-POST RDP FOR THE BROWNIAN MECHANISM


The Brownian mechanism (Whitehouse et al., 2022) is the accuracy-first analogue of the Gaussian
mechanism (Dwork et al., 2006a). The idea of the mechanism is to add Gaussian noise to each
release of a function _f_ : _X_ _вЖТ_ R _[d]_ like the Gaussian mechanism, and reduce the variance of the
noise if the privacy budget is increased for the next release. The added noises at each release are

appropriate correlations are obtained from a stochastic process called _Brownian motion_ . We defer the
mathematical definition to Definition C.3 in the Appendix.

7 DISCUSSION AND CONCLUSION


**Interpreting Ex-Post Privacy Bounds** Since allowing a data-dependent privacy bound in ex-post
privacy is a major change from ex-ante privacy, it is important to discuss how ex-post privacy bounds
should be interpreted. This applies equally to all ex-post privacy definitions, including ones not using
our notation from Section 3.1.

For one run of an ex-post mechanism, the returned privacy bound should be reported, along with the
details of how it is computed. The value can then be interpreted in a similar way as the corresponding
ex-ante privacy bound, see Appendix B for ex-post RDP and Ligett et al. (2017) for pure ex-post
privacy. Values from different runs can easily be compared: the smaller privacy bound is more private.

The more difficult case is comparing mechanisms, in which case the privacy bound is a random
variable. A major difficulty is that this random variable depends on the private data, so comparisons
using the private data may leak privacy. To circumvent this, it may be possible to use a prior for the
private data, and compare the distributions of the privacy bounds under that prior. The distributions
can be compared for example by examining their CDFs, though it will not be possible to always
establish that one mechanism is more private than the other. We leave an in-depth investigation of
this to future work.


**Limitations** Ex-post RDP inherits many of the limitations of RDP. First, RDP is not as easy to
interpret as _f_ -DP or Gaussian DP (GDP; Dong et al., 2022), which are defined in terms of optimal
performance of a distinguisher. RDP has a Bayesian interpretation (Mironov, 2017), which we extend
to ex-post RDP in Appendix B, but these are not as clear as _f_ -DP or GDP. Second, the conversion
from RDP to approximate DP or _f_ -DP is lossy in the ex-ante setting, which we expect to also hold in
the ex-post setting. For both reasons, we expect developing ex-post GDP or _f_ -DP, and determining
further properties of approximate ex-post privacy, to be a promising directions for future research.


**Conclusion** In this work, we determined which ex-post privacy definitions have post-processing
immunity and which do not. As the one definition with post-processing immunity does not support
the Brownian mechanism, we filled this gap by proposing a new ex-post privacy definition with
post-processing immunity, the Brownian mechanism, and supporting theory that enables application
to practical problems. We demonstrated one such application: tabular synthetic data generation, where
our mechanism successfully met a downstream classification accuracy threshold while minimising
the spent privacy budget.

A PRIVACY ODOMETERS AND EX-POST PRIVACY


A closely related line of work to ex-post privacy is the so-called fully adaptive composition that
studies _privacy filters_ and _odometers_ (Rogers et al., 2016; Feldman & Zrnic, 2021; L√©cuyer, 2021;
Koskela et al., 2023; Whitehouse et al., 2023; Haney et al., 2023). While ex-ante DP composition
theorems require specifying the privacy bounds at each step in advance, fully adaptive composition
considers the case where the privacy bounds can also be chosen adaptively, based on results from
previous iterations. As filters and odometers have been developed for many DP definitions, we use
_ќ≥_ _вИИ_ ќУ to denote a generic privacy bound when discussing filters and odometers in general.

Privacy filters require specifying an upper bound _ќ≥c_ on the privacy loss. The filter keeps track of the
accrued privacy loss during the iterations, and halts the computation before the accrued privacy loss
exceeds the upper bound. The filter guarantees ex-ante DP with the bound _ќ≥c_ .

Privacy odometers do not require specifying an upper bound beforehand. Instead, an odometer keeps
track of the accumulated privacy loss, and returns the value of the accumulator at the end of the
computation. Interpreting the value returned by the odometer is not as simple as interpreting a filter.
While a filter guarantees an ex-ante definition of DP with parameter _ќ≥c_, an odometer does not do so.

We give two reasons why an odometer cannot provide an ex-ante DP guarantee. [3] The first is that the
value _ќ≥_ ( _X_ ) returned by the odometer can depend on the data _X_ . However, ex-ante definitions of
DP do not permit data-dependent privacy bounds. The second reason is more concrete. Consider
a pathological mechanism _MP_ that releases the input _X_ with probability [1] 2 [, and releases nothing]

The ex-post privacy of RDP odometers (Feldman & Zrnic, 2021; L√©cuyer, 2021) is not as clear.
They are constructed by running multiple privacy filters with different upper bounds, and setting
the accumulated privacy loss to be the smallest loss of the filters whose upper bound has not been
exceeded. This construction results in some discretisation error in the odometers, since there is
only a finite number of filters. L√©cuyer (2021) provides a conversion of their odometer to a valid
probabilistic-DP odometer in the sense of Rogers et al. (2016), so Theorem A.1 can be applied to
it. We have not been able to determine whether either of these odometers implies ex-post RDP
(Definition 4.1). However, Theorem 4.5 supersedes an answer to this question, since it essentially
provides an odometer that guarantees ex-post RDP without any discretisation error.

Ex-post privacy for the Gaussian DP (GDP; Dong et al., 2022) odometer of Koskela et al. (2023) is
even less clear. It is constructed in a similar way to the RDP odometers by running multiple GDP
filters. The natural questions to ask is: does this odometer satisfy some sense of ex-post GDP?
However, we are not aware of any work giving a definition of ex-post GDP, so this question cannot
be answered at this time.

**Synthetic** **Data** **Details** The summaries of real data we use are _marginal_ _queries_, which count
how many rows of a discrete dataset have given values for given variables. While marginal queries
are very simple, the Private-PGM algorithm (McKenna et al., 2019) is able to generate high quality
synthetic data based on only noisy values of marginal queries (Tao et al., 2021; Chen et al., 2025). To
choose the marginal queries, we use the MST algorithm (McKenna et al., 2021), to obtain an initial
set, and add the pairwise marginal queries between the classification target and all other variables.

As we randomly split the data into train and validation sets, we use Theorem 4.6 to prove that our
synthetic data generator is ex-post RDP. Since we already know the Brownian mechanism is ex-post
RDP, we only need to ensure the accuracy check is RDP. Both check mechanisms depend on the
sensitivity of the accuracy, which is вИЖacc = 1 _/n_ validation under add-remove neighbourhood.


**Plain Gaussian Details** The plain Gaussian mechanism simply releases the accuracies with Gaussian noise. To account for multiple checks we divide the privacy budget with the maximum number
of checks, which is _m вИТ_ 1 if there are _m_ possible _ѕµ_ values. This ensures that the composition of all
checks is _ѕµ_ check-RDP. This means the noise variance is _[ќ±]_ [вИЖ] 2acc [2] _ѕµ_ check [(] _[m][вИТ]_ [1)] .

be applicable, but found that they have too large overheads in privacy cost to be useful in our setting.

E ACCURACY-FIRST DIFFERENTIALLY PRIVATE OPTIMIZATION


In this section, we implement a variant of differentially private stochastic gradient descent (DP-SGD;
Rajkumar & Agarwal, 2012; Song et al., 2013; Abadi et al., 2016), specifically DP-Adam, in the
accuracy-first setting. We apply the algorithm to fine-tune an image classifier. After an initial
fine-tuning run, we check the validation accuracy of the classifier, and then fine-tune longer if the
accuracy is not high enough. As every iteration of fine-tuning is RDP, Theorem 4.5 gives an ex-post
bound for a data-dependent number of iterations with public validation data, and Theorem 4.6 gives
an ex-post bound with private validation data.

To compute RDP bounds for each iteration of fine-tuning, we use the Opacus library (Yousefpour
et al., 2021). We convert ex-post RDP bounds to ex-post ADP with the conversion theorem of Ghazi
et al. (2025). For this conversion, Opacus computes ex-post RDP bounds for a set of _ќ±_ values, finds
the _ѕµ_ adp at a given _ќі_ corresponding to each RDP bound, and takes the minimum _ѕµ_ adp. With public
validation data, each ex-post RDP bound is valid by Theorem 4.5, and each converted ex-post ADP
bound is valid by the conversion theorem of Ghazi et al. (2025), so the minimum _ѕµ_ adp is valid.

Note that by default Opacus uses another conversion theorem (Balle et al., 2020) that is valid in the
ex-ante setting and gives smaller ADP bounds. Future work should investigate if this conversion also
holds in the ex-post setting.

With a private validation set, we use the plain Gaussian mechanism to check the accuracy threshold.
The privacy accounting with this mechanism has five steps:


1. Find the optimal ( _ќ±_ _[вИЧ]_ _, ѕµ_ _[вИЧ]_ rdp [)][ pair that gives the smallest] _[ ѕµ]_ [adp][ for the initial optimisation run.]

2. Set the accuracy check noise variance so that the composition of all checks is ( _ќ±_ _[вИЧ]_ _, ѕµ_ _[вИЧ]_ rdp [)][-RDP.]

3. Compute the ( _ќ±_ _[вИЧ]_ _, ѕµ_ _[вИЧ]_ rdp [)][ pairs of the accuracy check for all other values of] _[ ќ±]_ [ in the set used]
for privacy accounting.

4. Take the maximum of the optimisation and accuracy check _ѕµ_ rdp:s for each _ќ±_ .

5. Convert those to ADP with the conversion of Ghazi et al. (2025).


Each ex-post RDP bound is valid by Theorem 4.6, so the conversion to ex-post ADP is valid.

As the specific setting, we fine-tune the last layer of a vision transformer (ViT-B-16, pre-trained on
ImageNet-21K; Dosovitskiy et al., 2021) on CIFAR-10 (Krizhevsky, 2009), replicating an experiment
from (Tobaben et al., 2023). To make the task harder, we only use 50 datapoints per class for training
data, and another 50 datapoints per class for validation data. We use the standard CIFAR-10 test set.
We set the initial _ѕµ_ adp = 1 _._ 5, and use the same _ќі_ = 1 _/n_ train as Tobaben et al. (2023). Our initial _ѕµ_ adp
is larger than the smallest one ( _ѕµ_ adp = 1) used by Tobaben et al. (2023) to compensate for the weaker
conversion theorem of Ghazi et al. (2025). We use Adam (Kingma & Ba, 2015) as the optimiser,
implemented with DP in Opacus (Yousefpour et al., 2021).

We tune hyperparameters for _ѕµ_ = 1 _._ 5 using Optuna (Akiba et al., 2019), running 20 trials. We use the
best hyperparameters for the rest of the experiment, except we divide the learning rate by 10 after the
initial run of fine-tuning, since we expect the algorithm to have gotten reasonably close to a minimum
at that point. We ignore the privacy cost of hyperparameter tuning, as is common in DP deep learning
literature (Tobaben et al., 2023). Note that Tobaben et al. (2023) tune hyperparameters separately for
every value of _ѕµ_, so we do not reach the same accuracy they do with other values of _ѕµ_ .

With public validation data, we check the accuracy threshold every 50 epochs, and allow a maximum
of 10 releases, including the initial one. With private validation data, we check accuracy every 100
epochs and allow 5 releases.

Results are plotted in Figures 4 and 5. They show that our ex-post private fine-tuning is able to reach
the accuracy threshold while minimising _ѕµ_ adp, with both public and private validation data. The
threshold check has noise with private validation data, meaning that the accepted validation accuracy
is sometimes below the threshold, but the distributions of final test accuracies are similar with both
public and private validation data.
